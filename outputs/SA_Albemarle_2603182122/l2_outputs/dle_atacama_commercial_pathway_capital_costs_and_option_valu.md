A series of clauses are established that allow projecting production with higher standards of sustainability in the Salar de Atacama, in line with the definitions of the National Lithium Strategy.

## SECTION 7: INDIGENOUS COMMUNITY (CPLI) STATUS AND RISK

### 7A. The Legal Framework

Chile's Environmental Impact Assessment System (SEIA) mandates that activities "susceptible to directly affecting" indigenous peoples require prior consultation under ILO Convention 169. Any DLE EIA will trigger mandatory CPLI. The Chilean Supreme Court has affirmed the State's duty to consult indigenous communities. This is a legal requirement — not a discretionary step.

### 7B. The Lickanantay (Atacameño) Governance Complexity

The Council of Atacameño Peoples represents 18 Lickanantay communities. However — directly paralleling the SQM-Codelco experience — governance fractures within the broader Lickanantay community create unpredictable consultation dynamics. Some communities said the Council of Atacameño Peoples no longer spoke for them during the SQM-Codelco consultation, with tensions peaking over how to distribute revenue.

For decades, the Atacama's Lickanantay people have protested against expansion of the lithium industry, warning that large evaporation ponds are depleting scarce water supplies and destroying ecosystems.

### 7C. Albemarle's Specific Advantage and Specific Liability

**Advantage:** 
Albemarle shares 3.5% of sales with indigenous communities of the Salar de Atacama
 — a contractual benefit-sharing mechanism in place since at least 2016. Residents in Peine say they have had an agreement with Albemarle since 2012. This pre-existing cooperative relationship provides a stronger starting point than SQM had in 2023.

**Liability:** The Peine community — Albemarle's closest and historically most cooperative partner — is simultaneously a co-plaintiff in the CDE aquifer lawsuit against Albemarle. This creates a structural conflict: Peine cannot simultaneously be Albemarle's CPLI ally and active litigant without significant political and legal tension.

**DLE as a partial solution:** DLE's environmental profile (brine reinjection, 85% water reuse, no increase in brine pumping volume) directly addresses the core Lickanantay objection to conventional lithium expansion. Representatives of the Peine community have visited the DLE pilot plant at La Negra in a tour that included open dialogue to address concerns and opportunities of this technology. DLE may ultimately unlock indigenous consent that conventional expansion could never achieve — but the litigation must be resolved first.

### 7D. CPLI Timeline Assessment

The SQM-Codelco consultation required 12+ months of intensive dialogue before agreement was reached. For Albemarle:
- **Best case:** Pre-existing Peine agreement + DLE environmental benefits → CPLI completed in 18-24 months
- **Base case:** Litigation creates complications; some communities contest the Council's authority → CPLI requires 24-36 months and independent community-level agreements
- **Worst case:** Peine community litigation creates formal legal barrier to CPLI conclusion; process challenged in court → delay to 2030+

**CPLI risk rating: MODERATE-HIGH.** The litigation-partnership dual status of the Peine community is the most acute specific risk factor, and it is not resolvable by Albemarle unilaterally.

## SECTION 8: HYDROLOGICAL SUSTAINABILITY AND DGA CAP

### 8A. The Hard Extraction Limit

A fixed average annual brine pumping rate of 442 L/s is Albemarle's DGA-approved maximum. Albemarle cannot pump faster than the regulatory limit of 442 L/s. This is the binding physical constraint on the entire Atacama operation.

DLE's claimed advantage is extracting more lithium from the same brine volume — through higher recovery (94% vs. 40-65%) rather than increased pumping. The CORFO quota expansion is explicitly conditioned on "no additional brine pumping." This design constraint is built into the DLE system architecture: the La Negra pilot demonstrated 94% recovery from the same brine volumes that currently yield 40-65% in evaporation ponds.

### 8B. Albemarle's Prior Extraction Violations

The Superintendence of the Environment (SMA) found Albemarle exceeded its approved water extraction limits between October 2019 and September 2020, drawing an average of 452.3 litres per second above what was authorized. The regulator also found Albemarle failed to follow required safeguards under its Aquifer Alert Sector plan.

This violation — 2.3% above the 442 L/s cap — resulted in a formal sanction in 2022 and creates a specific enforcement credibility problem for the DLE EIA. SEA reviewers are aware of this history. Any DLE EIA approval will likely include trigger-point provisions that automatically halt DLE processing if extraction deviates from 442 L/s — creating operational risk even after commercial deployment.

### 8C. The Aquifer Lawsuit — Scenario Analysis

Chile has launched legal action against Albemarle's lithium assets, with the State Defense Council asserting that continuous extraction has impacted the fragile ecosystem. One study linked mining groundwater extraction to the Atacama salt flat sinking by as much as one centimeter per year.

However, independent hydrological research provides a partial mitigation: the influence on evaporation and recharge appears to be limited, with surplus evaporation due to mining accounting for a maximum of 4% of basin-wide evaporation. This suggests the ecological impact, while real, may be less severe than the CDE's legal position implies.

**Legal outcome scenarios:**

| Outcome | Probability | DLE Impact |
|---|---|---|
| BHP/Antofagasta/Albemarle found jointly liable; mitigation measures required but 442 L/s maintained | 40% | DLE EIA conditioned but viable; delay only |
| Partial finding; Albemarle-specific volume reductions ordered (e.g., cap reduced to ~400 L/s) | 25% | Reduces maximum extractable lithium by ~9%; DLE economics impaired but not destroyed |
| Full finding; significant extraction reduction ordered (e.g., cap to 350 L/s) | 15% | Destroys DLE incremental capacity; option value eliminated |
| Case dismissed or settled without volume restrictions | 20% | Full option value preserved |

## SECTION 9: WHAT WOULD DISPROVE THIS OPTION VALUE

### 9A. The "Valley of Death" — Why DLE Projects Historically Fail

The critical gap between pilot testing and commercial scale-up is where projects fail — not due to chemistry, but because standard industrial equipment cannot withstand the physical and chemical stress of operation at full scale. Equipment materials compatibility, adsorbent degradation under continuous cycling, and water management cascade failures are the primary technical failure modes.

For Albemarle specifically, the challenge is that no fully standalone DLE plant (without evaporation pond backup) has been proven at commercial scale globally. Arcadium/Fenix, the 30-year operating benchmark, uses hybrid DLE+evaporation. Eramet's Centenario is the closest to standalone — and while it reached 75% nameplate by end-2025, it still required an 18-month post-construction ramp-up.

### 9B. Specific Falsifying Evidence — The Six Kill Switches

The DLE Atacama option is **disproved or severely impaired** if any of the following occur:

1. **Aquifer lawsuit adverse ruling:** Court orders extraction below 420 L/s — destroys incremental production capacity
2. **EIA rejection:** SEA finds the 130,000 m³/yr brine processing increment incompatible with existing environmental obligations — forces redesign and 3-5 year restart
3. **CPLI legal challenge:** Individual Lickanantay community challenges CPLI process in Chilean courts — creates injunction risk that can extend delays by 2-3 years
4. **DLE adsorbent failure at scale:** Phase 1 commercial plant achieves <70% sustained lithium recovery (vs. 94% pilot) — fails CORFO threshold and generates process redesign requirement
5. **No post-2043 extension by 2030:** If FID must be made with terminal value = $0 in 2043, Phase 2 capex ($1.7B) is economically irrational without an extension — FID never happens
6. **Lithium prices remain below $18/kg through 2030:** The DLE breakeven at fully-loaded cost is ~$15-17k/t; at $13-15/t spot (current), every year of delay increases the NPV hole

### 9C. Positive Confirmation Signals That Increase Value

- SEA accepts EIA as "complete" without major SAARs (expected by Q4 2026)
- CORFO formally certifies DLE pilot has met ≥60% recovery threshold
- Peine community withdraws from CDE aquifer lawsuit or reaches separate DLE consent agreement
- CORFO begins post-2043 extension negotiation framework with Albemarle
- Eramet Centenario reaches 95%+ nameplate capacity on sustained basis (2026 expected)
- Lithium prices recover above $18-20/kg on sustained basis (Bernstein 2026-2027 forecast)
- Phase 1 FID announced with DLE-specific financing structure (project finance vs. corporate balance sheet)

## SECTION 12: FINAL INVESTMENT VERDICT

### For an Investment Committee

**The core thesis of the original hypothesis — that the DLE Atacama option is worth $2-6B — is not supported.** After full accounting for the 43.5% CORFO revenue burden, the 2043 hard contract expiry (producing only 8-11 years of DLE production at nameplate), and the 3-5 year regulatory gauntlet, the probability-weighted standalone NPV of the DLE option is approximately **-$30M to +$14M** — effectively zero.

**However, this is not the right framing for a strategic acquirer.** The correct framing is:

1. **What is the option to negotiate a post-2043 extension worth, unlocked by deploying DLE?** Answer: If the SQM-Codelco template applies (49% retained, 17-year extension), approximately **$500M-1.5B** to the private partner at the time of extension signing. This is a *renegotiation option*, not a production option.

2. **What is the all-in cost of acquiring this renegotiation option?** For an acquirer buying Albemarle at SOTP-based operational value — where consensus models do not capitalize post-2043 extension value — the incremental cost is **approximately zero**. The Atacama base operations are worth ~$6-8B enterprise value at mid-cycle pricing. The DLE renegotiation option is embedded within that asset at no incremental cost.

3. **What probability does the acquirer assign to a favorable post-2043 extension?** Given the SQM precedent and CORFO's explicit openness to discussion, this probability is not trivial — perhaps **30-45%**. At 40% probability × $750M expected extension value: **$300M probability-weighted option value**.

**Final verdict:** The DLE option at Atacama is worth approximately **$0-300M** to a sophisticated acquirer, depending primarily on their ability to negotiate a post-2043 extension on SQM-Codelco-equivalent terms. This is a **real but bounded option** — not transformative, but meaningful as embedded optionality within a larger strategic acquisition. The original $2-6B hypothesis overstated the value by approximately 5-20x due to ignoring the royalty structure and contract terminus. The revised, properly-modeled figure is defensible before an investment committee and appropriately reflects the genuine but constrained nature of the opportunity.
