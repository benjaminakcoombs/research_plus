## SECTION 1: FOUNDATIONAL LEGAL ARCHITECTURE

### 1.1 The Non-Concessionable Mineral Constitutional Framework

In Chile, lithium is governed by a distinct constitutional and statutory framework. 
The explicit mentions of lithium in Chilean legislation emerged during the military dictatorship, when it was declared "of national interest" by Decree Law No. 2,886 of the Ministry of Mining, dated November 14, 1979. This meant the Chilean State reserved the right of exploitation over all lithium reserves within national territory, excluding all types of concessions with the exception of administrative concessions and special operation contracts, understood as geographically limited and time-bound agreements.


The constitutional provision is structural, not merely statutory: private entities can only extract lithium through Special Lithium Operating Contracts (CEOLs), administrative concessions, or mining concessions issued prior to 1979. Specifically, for pre-1979 mining concessions owned by CORFO, the state agency established minority state-owned companies — later privatized — and entered contracts to exploit the Salar de Atacama.

### 1.2 The Genesis of the Albemarle Relationship: 1975–1981


In 1975, CORFO and Foote Mineral Company signed an agreement to evaluate lithium resources and their exploitation in the salt flat. As Eduardo Bitrán, former CORFO Executive Vice President and current Codelco director, affirmed: "In that era there was a research effort." In this way, "CORFO establishes rental contracts with private companies so that they make the investment."



For the execution of the project, Foote and CORFO created the Sociedad Chilena de Litio Ltda (SCL), for which CORFO transferred the ownership of the mining properties to SCL, in which it was initially a partner with a 45% stake, which translated into: while the lithium quota lasted, CORFO would transfer the ownership of the mining properties to the company, while Foote would contribute the technology.


This founding structure is legally significant: **CORFO was not a lessor from inception — it was a co-owner of SCL and transferred mining property rights TO the joint entity.** The subsequent evolution from co-owner to pure lessor (when CORFO sold its 45% stake) means the legal character of the "CORFO lease" changed materially over time. The 1980 "Basic Agreement" (Convenio Básico) referenced throughout Chilean litigation is therefore a contract between CORFO-as-co-owner and SCL-as-operating-entity, not a standard administrative lease from day one.

### 1.3 Confirmed Corporate Succession Chain and Current Entity Structure

The succession chain is confirmed across multiple independent Chilean public sources:


Everything begins in 1981, when CORFO associated with US company Foote Mineral Company to form the Sociedad Chilena de Litio (SCL) to develop lithium carbonate production technology from brine in the Antofagasta Region. In 2012, SCL, then 100% private, was renamed Rockwood Lithium, until three years later, this financial holding company was acquired by Albemarle Corporation, marking the formal arrival of that conglomerate to Chile.


The current entity name is confirmed by Diario Financiero, which describes the evolution from its inception: 
Foote Minerals Company and CORFO created the Sociedad Chilena de Litio Ltda. — SCL, later Rockwood Litio Limitada and current **Albemarle Limitada** — with a duration equal to the period necessary to exploit, produce and sell 200,000 metric tons of lithium metal equivalent, establishing an initial term of 30 years, automatically renewable for successive periods of five years each. Later, CORFO ceased to be part of this company.


**The CORFO counterparty is therefore confirmed as Albemarle Limitada** — a Chilean *Limitada* (limited liability company), not a *sucursal* (branch). This is dispositive for the change-of-control analysis.

### 1.4 THE CRITICAL NEW STRUCTURAL FINDING: THE DUAL OWNERSHIP OF ALBEMARLE LIMITADA

The most important new factual finding of Round Three research is the ownership structure of Albemarle Limitada itself:


In Chile, **Albemarle Limitada** is located in the Antofagasta Region; its owners are **Albemarle US Inc. (55%) and Foote Minera e Inversiones Limitada (45%)**.


This dual ownership structure has four critical legal implications for change-of-control analysis, none of which appear in any prior analysis:

**Implication A — The "Foote Minera e Inversiones Limitada" Entity:** The 45% owner of Albemarle Limitada is not "Albemarle Corp" — it is a separate Chilean entity (a *Limitada*) named after the original Foote Minerals corporate family. This entity almost certainly represents the legal successor to CORFO's original shareholding position that was sold to Foote Minerals in 1989, now held within the Albemarle group but as a separate Chilean legal entity. Its ownership, director structure, and registered domicile would appear in Chilean corporate records and should be mapped by Santiago-based counsel.

**Implication B — A Parent-Level Acquisition Does Not Transfer Either Owner's Shares:** A merger at the level of Albemarle Corporation (Delaware) results in Albemarle Corp becoming a wholly-owned subsidiary of the acquirer. This would make Albemarle US Inc. (the 55% owner) an indirect subsidiary of the acquirer. However, **neither the shares of Albemarle US Inc. nor the shares of Foote Minera e Inversiones Limitada are directly transferred** — they continue to be held by their respective intermediate entities. The shares of Albemarle Limitada itself are not touched. This is the strongest possible version of the "no assignment" structural argument.

**Implication C — The 45% "Foote" Entity Creates an Additional Consent Risk:** If "Foote Minera e Inversiones Limitada" is not wholly owned by Albemarle Corp but has third-party ownership or operates under a separate shareholders' agreement governing Albemarle Limitada's governance, an acquirer of Albemarle Corp would not acquire 100% economic control of Albemarle Limitada. More importantly, the Albemarle Limitada *pacto social* (constitutional document) almost certainly contains provisions governing the admission of new indirect owners — and whether Chilean *Limitada* law (*Ley de Sociedades de Responsabilidad Limitada*) requires all members' consent for indirect changes in ownership must be analyzed.

**Implication D — CORFO's Basic Agreement Is With the Entity, Not the Owners:** 
The case goes back to a Basic Agreement (*Convenio Básico*) that on August 13, 1980 was subscribed by CORFO with what was then Foote Minerals Company (current Albemarle Lithium).
 CORFO's own litigation framing identifies the counterparty as the operating entity's corporate successor — not the parent-company chain. This supports the inference that CORFO's consent rights, if any, relate to direct changes in Albemarle Limitada's own ownership structure, not indirect parent-company changes.

On an EBITDA basis, the effective state take is substantially higher than 40% — depending on the cost structure, a 40% revenue royalty on $450M EBITDA could represent 60-80% of gross revenue, implying the gross revenue generating this EBITDA may be $1.5-2.0B at mid-cycle prices.

## SECTION 4: US-CHILE FTA CHAPTER 10 — INVESTMENT PROTECTIONS CONFIRMED AND CALIBRATED

### 4.1 The Baseline Protection Framework — Confirmed Operational


Chapter Ten of the United States-Chile Free Trade Agreement contains provisions designed to protect foreign investors and their investments and to facilitate the settlement of investment disputes. Each State Party must accord investors from the other Party national (non-discriminatory) treatment and may not expropriate investments of those investors in violation of international law. Chapter Ten permits an investor of one Party to make a legal claim for money damages for measures taken by the other Party that allegedly violate the provisions of Chapter Ten. An investor may initiate arbitration against that Party under the UNCITRAL Arbitration Rules or, as applicable, the ICSID Convention or the ICSID Additional Facility Rules.


### 4.2 No Lithium-Specific Carve-Out Found in Chile's Annex I

The USTR-published Annex I Schedule of Chile (the actual treaty text, not academic analysis) covers sector-specific non-conforming measures. The document obtained confirms the following: 
Provisions include that personnel that an investor of the United States that has made an investment under Chapter Ten requires for starting up in Chile will be treated, for a period of 18 months from the date of start up, as specialized technical personnel. A juridical person must be constituted in Chile with principal domicile and real and effective seat in Chile. The president, manager, and the majority of the directors or administrators must be Chilean natural persons. In addition, more than 50 percent of its equity capital must be held by Chilean natural or juridical persons.


The non-conforming measures in the publicly available Annex I excerpt relate to coastal shipping and vessel ownership — not to mining or lithium. There is **no confirmed lithium-sector carve-out** from Chapter 10's investment protections in Chile's Annex I.

**However — the Mandatory Caveat:** The IISD academic finding that "Chile has renegotiated IIAs to exclude lithium" may refer to newer or bilaterally renegotiated agreements (not the 2003 US-Chile FTA). The reference to "Chile-HK BIT, CPTPP, and Chile-Australia FTA" in academic literature — 
Chile-HK BIT (Article 10 and Annex I), CPTPP (Article 9.8 and Annex 9-B), and Chile-Australia FTA (Article 10.11 and Annex 10-B) provide protections against direct and indirect expropriation
 — does not specifically identify a lithium carve-out in the US-Chile FTA. Santiago treaty counsel must confirm this before an acquirer relies on Chapter 10 protection as its primary legal shield.

### 4.3 The Tax Carve-Out Complication


In international investment, a 'tax carve-out' is a provision that excludes certain tax measures from the scope of investment protections in a treaty.
 
Chile-HK BIT (Article 14), Chile-Australia FTA (Article 10.9) and CPTPP (Article 29.4) provide tax carve-out clauses to keep tax sovereignty for the state at different protection levels.


If Chile characterizes forced equity participation in Albemarle's Chilean operations as a "tax measure" or "revenue measure" rather than an expropriation, the tax carve-out could potentially shield Chile from Chapter 10 investor-state arbitration claims. 
The analysis evaluates whether Chile's tax measures constitute unlawful expropriation or violate fair and equitable treatment, umbrella clause, or whether differential taxation treatments violate national treatment and most-favored-nation treatment under applicable bilateral investment treaties and free trade agreements.


The Tianqi/SQM case — where Chile imposed a mining royalty that Tianqi characterized as targeting its investment — is the most proximate precedent for how this tax carve-out versus expropriation distinction plays out. 
The analysis addresses procedural challenges such as 'fork-in-the-road', 'tax-carve-out' and 'abuse of process'
 — the same issues would arise in any Albemarle-related Chapter 10 claim.

### 4.4 The EU-Chile FTA — A Parallel Constraint on Chilean Policy Space (Applicable to EU-Domiciled Acquirers)


The ERM chapter contains both broad provisions on cooperation and binding commitments that restrict Chile's ability to impose certain industrial policy measures. This is particularly relevant in light of the current governance of Chile's lithium sector, where production is dominated by two private firms under long-standing contracts, but the state retains ownership of the resource and has recently tasked state-owned Codelco with leading new projects and managing strategic salt flats under the 2023 National Lithium Strategy.



Yet this framing reveals a deeper asymmetry: while the agreement enhances legal protections for European investors, it limits Chile's ability to condition access to its raw materials on broader developmental goals. Instruments commonly used in state-led strategies — such as mandating technology transfer, enforcing local content requirements, or tying supply to industrial upgrading — are largely off the table under current trade disciplines.


The EU-Chile FTA entered into force on February 1, 2025. 
As a result, the agreement may lock in a narrowly defined policy space, privileging market access and legal stability over the flexibility needed to implement a more ambitious industrial policy, as envisioned in Chile's 2023 National Lithium Strategy.
 An EU-domiciled acquirer of Albemarle would therefore have strong treaty protections — potentially stronger than a US acquirer, given the EU-Chile FTA's more recent vintage and explicit ERM chapter.

### 4.5 Chile's Non-Conforming Measures — The Annex II Risk


Annex II is a broader carve-out, as a Party would be able to both retain existing measures and introduce new, more trade restrictive measures for the sectors, subsectors and activities described there.


If Chile's Annex II to the US-Chile FTA contains a reservation for the mining sector (or specifically for non-concessionable minerals), Chile could introduce new restrictions on foreign ownership of lithium operations — including equity participation requirements — without violating Chapter 10. The specific content of Annex II must be verified by treaty counsel. This is the single remaining unresolved legal question that could materially shift the analysis.

## SECTION 7: INTERNATIONAL PRECEDENT — AUSTRALIA FIRB COMPARABLES

### 7.1 FIRB Nationality-Based Differentiation — Directly Applicable

The Australian FIRB regime has blocked two Chinese-linked acquisitions of lithium companies: the Yuxiao Fund/Northern Minerals stake increase (February 2023) and the Austroid/Alinta Resources acquisition (July 2023). In both cases the blocking was nationality-based. This heightened level of oversight of China specifically is an advantage for investors from the US and UK.


The FTA's performance requirements obligation provides that a Party cannot require that an enterprise that is a covered investment appoint individuals of any particular nationality to a senior management position. However, a Party may require that a majority, or less, of the board of directors be of a particular nationality or be resident in its territory, provided this requirement does not materially impair the ability of that investor to exercise control over its investment.


### 7.2 The Albemarle-Liontown FIRB Non-Block

Albemarle Corporation was itself subject to FIRB review for its Liontown Resources takeover proposal. FIRB did not block the transaction, establishing that a US strategic buyer in the lithium sector does not face automatic sovereign blocking in Western-aligned jurisdictions. Albemarle's experience as FIRB-reviewed acquiree confirms that a US successor parent would face the most favorable possible regulatory environment under any comparable framework.

At a later stage of negotiations, during the Boric government, the Chilean negotiating team used the ban on import and export monopolies merely as a bargaining chip to achieve more on critical points such as preferential pricing.



Even though the Annex preserves existing practices, it offers no guarantee that Chile can expand or redesign them in the future. As a result, the agreement may lock in a narrowly defined policy space, privileging market access and legal stability over the flexibility needed to implement a more ambitious industrial policy, as envisioned in Chile's 2023 National Lithium Strategy.


The EU-Chile FTA's ERM chapter specifically constrains Chile's ability to use lithium policy as an industrial policy tool — 
instruments commonly used in state-led strategies, such as mandating technology transfer, enforcing local content requirements, or tying supply to industrial upgrading, are largely off the table under current trade disciplines.


### 8.2 CORFO's Own Articulated Principles for the 2016 Renegotiations


For the state agency in the negotiation, the following principles were considered: avoiding continuing to lose positions in the world market and remaining a global leader in lithium; establishing mechanisms that allow the Chilean state to participate in the benefits of lithium exploitation; among others.


These principles — state market leadership and benefit participation — are satisfied by the existing 40% CORFO commission. CORFO's own publicly stated negotiating principles do not require equity participation as a necessary condition of state benefit participation. This is important: if a future CORFO demands equity in an Albemarle change-of-control scenario, it would be departing from its own stated principles that were satisfied by the royalty structure it negotiated in 2016.

## SECTION 10: WHAT FIVE FINDINGS WOULD DISPROVE THE "NO CONSENT" HYPOTHESIS

The following specific factual findings, if discovered upon full primary document review, would materially shift probabilities:

**DISPROVING FINDING #1 — Express Change-of-Control Clause in 2016 Amendment:** If the 2016 Amendment contains language such as *"any change in the ultimate beneficial owner or controlling person of either Albemarle US Inc. or Foote Minera e Inversiones Limitada shall require prior written consent of CORFO,"* Scenario A probability falls from 55% to near zero, and Scenario B rises to 80%.

**DISPROVING FINDING #2 — A Prior Documented CORFO Consent in Internal Files:** If CORFO's internal files reveal a formal consent process was conducted for the Rockwood → Albemarle transition (even informally), this establishes a course of dealing that any future change requires approval. However, given the 2016 Amendment was executed post-Rockwood acquisition without reference to a prior consent process, this would be surprising.

**DISPROVING FINDING #3 — US-Chile FTA Annex II Mining Sector Reservation:** If Chile's Annex II to the US-Chile FTA contains a specific reservation for the lithium mining sector preserving Chile's right to impose equity participation requirements on foreign lithium operators, Chapter 10 BIT protection is substantially weakened. This is the single most important remaining legal question.

**DISPROVING FINDING #4 — Foote Minera e Inversiones Limitada Third-Party Ownership:** If "Foote Minera e Inversiones Limitada" is not wholly owned by Albemarle Corp (e.g., if a third-party Chilean entity retains residual interest), a parent-company acquisition would not deliver full control of Albemarle Limitada, creating both a deal structuring problem and a potential CORFO consent trigger through the Chilean Limitada's governance rules.

**DISPROVING FINDING #5 — CCHEN Regulatory Interpretation:** If CCHEN has issued a formal legal opinion confirming that a change in the indirect parent of a lithium export authorization holder triggers full re-authorization review, this creates a de facto consent mechanism regardless of the CORFO contract's silence.
