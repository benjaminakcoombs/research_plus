CONTEXT:
Albemarle operates what is arguably the world's lowest-cost lithium brine operation at the Salar de Atacama in Chile through a CORFO lease originally granted in 1980 to Foote Minerals Company. This lease has survived four corporate ownership changes: Foote Minerals → Cyprus Minerals → SCM Chemicals → Rockwood Holdings → Albemarle (2015). Albemarle's current lease runs through 2043 and was modified by a 2016 Amendment and a 2024 Annex, the latter expanding Albemarle's production quota up to 240,000 MT LCE conditional on deploying direct lithium extraction (DLE) technology. The Chilean operations generate approximately $450M in estimated EBITDA at mid-cycle lithium prices.

In December 2023, SQM (the other major Atacama operator) reached a restructuring agreement with state-owned Codelco that established a stark new precedent: Codelco takes 50%+1 equity in the combined entity, the Chilean state receives 70% of operating margin from 2025-2030, rising to 85% after 2031. If this economic framework were applied to Albemarle's operations at lease renegotiation or upon a change-of-control event, Albemarle's acquirable Chilean EBITDA would drop from ~$450M to ~$135M — a $2.4B enterprise value reduction at 8x EBITDA.

Critically, there is conflicting evidence on whether CORFO consent is even required for a parent-level acquisition of Albemarle. CORFO's own 2024 Annex press release identifies the original counterparty as "Foote Minerals Company, today Albemarle US Inc." — formally acknowledging the full chain of corporate succession without referencing any prior consent process. The Albemarle-Rockwood merger (closed January 2015) listed only EU and China antitrust as required approvals — CORFO consent was absent. And Albemarle's 10-K risk disclosures do not specifically identify CORFO change-of-control consent as a material risk.

HYPOTHESIS TO INVESTIGATE:
The deal community widely assumes any Albemarle acquisition requires Chilean government consent, pricing in a significant "Chile discount." Three independent pieces of evidence suggest no mandatory consent requirement exists for parent-level acquisitions. If confirmed, this eliminates the most-feared regulatory blocker and compresses the Chilean risk discount by 500-1,000 basis points in an acquirer's IRR model. Conversely, if the SQM/Codelco precedent is applied, the Chilean assets are worth $2.4B less in M&A than standalone DCF implies.
