# DELIVERABLE 1: COMPANY SITUATION MAP

## What Albemarle IS

Albemarle is the world's largest lithium producer and the #1 or #2 global bromine producer (alongside ICL, with ~70% combined market share). As of March 2026, it is a freshly simplified, pure-play lithium and specialty chemicals company — having completed the divestiture of its Ketjen refining catalysts business to KPS Capital Partners on March 2, 2026.

**Physical footprint and what it actually owns and operates:**

- **Chile (Atacama/La Negra):** A sovereign exploitation contract (not a concession, not a property right) granted by CORFO in 1980, extended through 2043 via a 2016 amendment. Produces ~70,000-85,000 MT LCE/year of lithium carbonate and chloride from the Salar de Atacama — among the lowest-cost lithium production globally ($3,500-5,500/MT AISC, first quartile). The La Negra III/IV chemical plant (~80,000 MT/yr capacity) is Albemarle-owned. The brine extraction infrastructure sits on CORFO-owned land. CORFO retains a latent option to purchase extraction assets at depreciated replacement cost at contract expiry. Approximately 1,100 Chilean employees.

- **Australia (Greenbushes — 49% JV):** Minority stake in the world's highest-grade, lowest-cost hard-rock lithium mine, held through Talison Lithium. JV partner: 51% held by TLEA (Tianqi Lithium 26.01%, IGO 24.99%). Life of mine extended to 2045. CGP3 came online Q1 2025; CGP4 planned for Q1 2027. SEC-filed SRK technical report: Albemarle's 49% attributable NPV = **$4.34B** at $1,500/t SC6 (10% discount rate). Greenbushes generates **68% EBITDA margins at trough spodumene prices** (~$1,135/t). Albemarle holds a right of first refusal over any direct Talison interest sale. The Tianqi-Albemarle 2026 transaction cap is set at 950,000 tonnes of concentrate annually.

- **Australia (Wodgina — 50% JV):** 50% ownership of a major hard-rock spodumene mine. JV partner: MinRes (50%, operator). POSCO acquired an indirect 15% interest via a $765M deal with MinRes in November 2025. POSCO is now Albemarle's indirect co-stakeholder.

- **Australia (Kemerton — 100%):** Two-train lithium hydroxide conversion plant, fully idled and in care-and-maintenance as of February 2026. Replacement cost >$1B. Management confirmed restart requires lithium prices above $25,000/MT sustained. Currently EBITDA-accretive when idle (meaning it was cash-negative when operating). ~$100M cash cost to complete the idling.

- **United States (Silver Peak, NV):** The only operating lithium mine in the U.S. Small scale (~5,000 MT LCE/yr) but strategically significant as FEOC-clean domestic production. IRA 45X production tax credit eligible.

- **United States (Kings Mountain, NC — development):** Former lithium mine being restarted. DoD DPA Title III grant ($90M) + DOE BIL grant ($150M) = **$240M in active federal funding**. Permitting advancing (MOA with NC SHPO executed October 2025). Timeline pushed to ~2027-2028. This is the MP Materials template for lithium.

- **China (Meishan, Qinzhou, Chengdu):** Three lithium conversion plants serving the Chinese domestic market. Albemarle *expanded* its Chinese footprint in 2025 by acquiring a lithium recycler. China = 36-39% of total revenue. These plants are FEOC-classified — a strategic liability for IRA-qualifying sales but operationally valuable for Chinese domestic market access.

- **Jordan (JBC — 50% JV):** Jordan Bromine Company. Core of the Specialties segment. Near-monopoly bromine position alongside ICL. Suffered a major flooding event in January 2026; returned to full operations but 2026 production will be impacted.

**Revenue composition (FY2025 actual, pre-Ketjen):**

| Segment | Revenue (est.) | Adj. EBITDA | Margin |
|---|---|---|---|
| Energy Storage (Lithium) | ~$3.5B | $697M | ~20% |
| Specialties (Bromine + Li Specialties) | ~$1.3B | $276M | ~21% |
| Ketjen (divested) | ~$0.6B | $150M | ~25% |
| Corporate | — | -$112M | — |
| **Total** | **$5.1B** | **$1,098M** | ~20% |

Post-Ketjen pro-forma EBITDA: ~$950M-$1,000M. This is the correct denominator for any forward multiple — not the $1.098B headline. At current lithium prices (~$17-24/kg in Q1 2026), the 2026 run-rate is likely $1.2-1.7B EBITDA, but management guided conservatively for "flat to down" EBITDA vs. 2025 using a $10/kg base case.

## Who OWNS It and What That Means

**Public company.** NYSE: ALB. Market cap ~$19.1B at $162.44/share (March 2026). 52-week high of $206 reached February 2026 before pulling back 21%. ~117.85M shares outstanding. Enterprise value ~$20-21B.

**Institutional ownership is dispersed:** Vanguard ~12%, BlackRock ~7.7%. No activist on file. No 13D filings. Korean sovereign entities (KIC and NPS) are **not** accumulating — KIC sold 87.9% of its position in Q2 2025; NPS holds a trivial $103K. **No evidence of a live M&A process, banker engagement, or strategic review.**

**However, the governance cleanup is textbook pre-transaction preparation:**

- Two new independent directors appointed February 26, 2026: Michelle Collins (40+ years Deloitte audit/advisory) and Mark Widmar (former First Solar CEO — the company that perfected the IRA domestic-content playbook).
- Ketjen divestiture completed, Eurecat sold, $670M proceeds received and immediately deployed to retire $650M in 2027 notes and tender for $650M more in long-dated notes.

**Key signal:** Albemarle is **not** a forced seller. $3.2B liquidity, $692M FY2025 FCF, covenant relief period ending Q2 2026. Management has runway. But the Moody's negative outlook and negative interest coverage at trough prices (-$367M operating income vs. $208M interest expense) mean the lithium recovery is not optional for the credit profile — it is required.

## How It MAKES MONEY and How That's Changing

**Revenue quality is deteriorating on multiple fronts:**

1. **Contract book erosion:** Long-term contracted salts volume has declined from ~75-80% (2023) → ~67% (2024) → ~50% (2025) → **~40% guided for 2026**. The shift to 60% spot exposure dramatically increases earnings volatility and reduces the "utility-like" revenue premium some analysts have assigned.

2. **China concentration is stuck:** 36-39% of net sales to China for three consecutive years, despite management rhetoric about geographic diversification. Chinese domestic lithium prices are increasingly bifurcated from Western prices due to FEOC/IRA dynamics — creating a situation where Albemarle's most profitable customers (IRA-qualifying Western OEMs) want non-Chinese supply, while its largest volume customers (Chinese battery makers) want the cheapest price.

3. **The $350M prepayment distortion:** A $350M customer prepayment received January 2025 — counterparty undisclosed through 14+ months of earnings calls — inflated FY2025 OCF to 117% conversion. In 2026, $88M of this will be recognized as EBITDA **with no corresponding cash inflow**, creating a permanent EBITDA-cash disconnect. This is the longest sustained counterparty non-disclosure for a transaction of this magnitude in Albemarle's filing history.

4. **Margin trajectory depends entirely on lithium price:** Management confirmed $10/kg = $1.1B EBITDA; $20/kg = $1.6-1.8B EBITDA; $30/kg = $3.9-4.1B EBITDA. The EBITDA leverage to lithium price is extreme — roughly $80-100M of EBITDA per $1/kg change. Current spot (~$17-24/kg in Q1 2026) puts run-rate EBITDA at $1.3-1.8B, but contract lags mean realization trails spot by 6-12 months.

5. **Bromine provides the floor:** Specialties at $276M EBITDA represents a near-indestructible cash flow floor — oligopoly position, 8-12% annual price increases sustained through the 2022-2024 period, diversified end-markets. This segment alone, at specialty chemical comps (10-12x EBITDA), is worth $2.8-3.3B — roughly 15% of enterprise value that is invisible when the market prices Albemarle as a pure lithium play.

## What the ENVIRONMENT Is Doing

**The lithium market has turned.** Lithium carbonate spot prices surged from ~$8,260/MT (June 2025 trough) to ~$20,800/MT (February 2026) — a near-doubling driven by supply curtailments (Zimbabwe export ban removing ~165-183kt LCE, 27 Jiangxi mining permits cancelled, CATL Jianxiawo suspended) and demand acceleration (global BESS installations +51% YoY, storage reaching 40% of total battery cell output). The supply-demand balance is tightening toward structural deficit by 2026-2027.

**The competitive landscape has consolidated dramatically.** Rio Tinto acquired Arcadium Lithium for $6.7B (closed March 2025), creating the #3 global lithium producer. Anglo American merged with Teck Resources (September 2025). The Western lithium producer universe has compressed to: Albemarle (independent), SQM (Chile/Codelco partnership), Pilbara Minerals (pure spodumene), and Rio Tinto/Arcadium. **Albemarle is the last large independent Western lithium platform.**

**IRA/FEOC is creating a structural Western price premium.** The "One Big Beautiful Bill" expanded FEOC provisions. Management explicitly stakes the company's competitive strategy on this premium: "Management hopes that the FEOC provisions...will result in Western lithium prices commanding a premium over Chinese prices." If this bifurcation materializes ($1-3/kg sustained premium), Albemarle's non-Chinese production (Chile, Australia, US) captures an annuity-like policy-driven margin enhancement worth $850M-$3.6B in enterprise value.

**Chilean sovereign risk is the most consequential external factor.** The SQM/Codelco deal (confirmed terms: 70% of operating margin to the Chilean state 2025-2030, rising to 85% after 2031, with 50%+1 Codelco equity control) establishes the precedent for what Chile will demand at Albemarle's 2043 contract renegotiation — and potentially upon any acquisition-related change of control. This precedent reduces the M&A-adjusted value of Chilean operations by 25-70% depending on scenario.

## What's CHANGED Recently (Last 12-18 Months)

1. **Ketjen divestiture completed March 2, 2026** — $670M proceeds, transforms Albemarle into a pure-play lithium + bromine company. Simplifies acquisition profile. Removes EU antitrust overlap for potential BASF acquisition.

2. **Kemerton fully idled February 2026** — Both trains in care-and-maintenance. CEO confirmed "recent lithium price improvements alone are not enough to offset the challenges facing Western hard-rock lithium conversion operations." The $4-5/kg cost gap vs. Chinese converters is structural.

3. **POSCO became an indirect Wodgina co-stakeholder (November 2025)** — $765M for 30% of MinRes lithium JV = indirect 15% of Wodgina. Changes the M&A dynamics: POSCO is now simultaneously Albemarle's customer, JV co-stakeholder, and the most strategically motivated potential acquirer.

4. **Lithium price recovery** — From $8,260/MT trough to ~$17-24/kg in Q1 2026. Albemarle stock recovered from <$80 to $206 (52-week high) before pulling back to ~$162. The trough-buying opportunity in public equity has largely passed; the private market control premium is the remaining acquisition value.

5. **DLE pilot validation at Atacama** — 85% water reuse efficiency demonstrated; environmental permits for reinjection secured; 2028 commercial deployment target. This is potentially $6-11B in NPV that no analyst model quantifies.

6. **Rio Tinto's Glencore merger collapsed February 5, 2026** — Creates a precise 6-month window (until ~August 2026) where Rio Tinto is unconstrained, acquisitive, and under board pressure to demonstrate standalone lithium strategy.

7. **Supermajority removal proposed for May 5, 2026 AGM** — Removes the 75% vote requirement for affiliated transactions. Coincides with POSCO's new Wodgina co-stakeholder status. Whether causal or coincidental, the downstream M&A effect is real.

**The tension in one sentence:** Albemarle is simultaneously the most strategically valuable lithium company in the world, trading at what appears to be a full mid-cycle multiple (~12-14x current-price EBITDA), with a Chilean crown jewel asset that may be worth 25-70% less in any M&A transaction than it is as a standalone operating asset due to sovereign extraction risk that no sell-side model has properly quantified.

**PRE-MORTEM:** If this insight turned out to be wrong, the most likely reason would be that DLE at Atacama's specific brine chemistry proves significantly harder to scale commercially than the pilot suggests, or that Chile's water regulatory framework creates multi-year permitting delays that push deployment to 2031+.

## TENSION POINT 3 (RANK: #3)

**THE OBSERVATION:** Albemarle received a $350M customer prepayment in January 2025. The dual-product specification (spodumene + salts) requires a counterparty with its own conversion capacity — pointing to an Asian battery manufacturer (CATL, POSCO, Ganfeng).

**WHY IT MATTERS:** In any acquisition, the change-of-control provisions of this contract create a binary outcome: either the obligation transfers seamlessly ($0 impact) or the counterparty can demand repayment (~$340M immediate cash obligation) or exercise other rights (ROFR, consent-to-assignment, renegotiation). If the counterparty is a Chinese entity, CFIUS may require the supply commitment to be mitigated as a condition of any non-Chinese acquisition. The $340M figure is material — approximately 1.7% of total deal value at $20B EV. No sell-side analyst has asked management about CoC provisions in 14 months of earnings calls.

**THE QUESTION IT RAISES:** What are the exact change-of-control, assignment, and termination provisions of the supply agreement? Who is the counterparty, and would their nationality create a CFIUS complication for a non-Chinese acquirer? Does the Kemerton idling create a delivery specification breach if Kemerton-sourced hydroxide was designated?

**CATEGORY:** DILIGENCE FLAG
**POTENTIAL MAGNITUDE:** Major — $0-$340M binary cash exposure, plus potential CFIUS complication
**CONFIDENCE:** High on the existence and magnitude of the risk; Low on the direction (no contract text available)
**L2 RESEARCH QUESTION:** Obtain the full $350M customer prepayment supply agreement under NDA as the #1 diligence priority. Specifically review: change-of-control definition, assignment/consent provisions, delivery facility specifications, and counterparty-controlled drawdown rights.

**PRE-MORTEM:** If this insight turned out to be wrong, the most likely reason would be that the contract is a standard market-indexed supply agreement with no CoC provision (consistent with Albemarle's decision not to file it as a material definitive agreement under SEC rules), and the counterparty is a U.S. or allied-nation buyer where CFIUS creates no complication.

**THE OBSERVATION:** CORFO's official press release for Annex 2024 identifies the original contract counterparty as "Foote Minerals Company, today Albemarle US Inc." — formally acknowledging the full chain of corporate succession from the 1980 original through at least four ownership changes (Foote → Chemetall → Rockwood → Albemarle) without referencing any prior consent process, CCHEN authorization, or novation requirement. [Source: CORFO official press release, May 15, 2024] Additionally, the Albemarle-Rockwood merger agreement (closed January 2015) listed only EU and China antitrust as required regulatory approvals — CORFO consent did not appear in the closing conditions. **WHY IT MATTERS:** The market and deal community widely assume that any Albemarle acquisition requires Chilean government consent, pricing in a significant "Chile discount." Three independent pieces of evidence — CORFO's own official language, the Rockwood merger conditions, and the 10-K disclosure silence — all point toward no mandatory consent requirement for parent-level acquisitions. If correct, this eliminates the most feared regulatory blocker, potentially compressing the Chilean risk discount by 500-1,000 bps in an acquirer's IRR model. The gap between perceived and actual Chilean regulatory risk may be the single most actionable information asymmetry for a sophisticated buyer.

**THE QUESTION IT RAISES:** Did the 2016 Amendment (negotiated *after* the Rockwood acquisition) introduce new CoC language not present in the original 1980 contract? The 2016 Amendment was a comprehensive renegotiation — CORFO may have used the opportunity to insert prospective CoC protections.

**CATEGORY:** INFORMATION ARBITRAGE
**POTENTIAL MAGNITUDE:** Major — $1-3B swing in Chilean asset valuation discount
**CONFIDENCE:** Medium — The evidence is strong but circumstantial; the contract text has not been reviewed
**L2 RESEARCH QUESTION:** Obtain the 2016 Amendment text from the Chilean Comptroller General's public database (the CGR approved the amendment and its toma de razón resolution should be publicly accessible), and compare the assignment/CoC provisions to the original 1980 contract terms.

**PRE-MORTEM:** If this insight turned out to be wrong, the most likely reason would be that the 2016 Amendment did in fact introduce an express change-of-control clause — a natural action for CORFO to take given Chile's evolving National Lithium Strategy — and CORFO's acceptance of the Foote-to-Albemarle succession was merely historical tolerance, not a precedent for future acquiescence.

## TENSION POINT 7 (RANK: #7)
### The Market Has Already Priced the Bull Case — Creating Asymmetric Downside for an Acquirer

**THE OBSERVATION:** Albemarle's stock climbed to a 52-week high of $206 in February 2026, recovering 155% from its mid-2025 trough below $80. At $162 current price, the stock implies ~12-14x EV/EBITDA on the $20/kg mid-cycle scenario (~$1.5-1.7B EBITDA). The SOTP bull case at $164/share barely exceeds current trading. [Source: ALB stock price data, management FY2026 scenario guidance, SOTP analysis from Workstream 2] Meanwhile, the SOTP mid-case is $98/share — a 40% discount to current trading. The market is pricing in a combination of lithium price recovery AND multiple expansion simultaneously; historically, at least one of these assumptions proves false.

**WHY IT MATTERS:** An acquirer paying a 20-25% premium ($195-202/share) is paying essentially the February 2026 all-time high — which the market itself already corrected away from. At current price, there is ~40% upside to the bull SOTP but ~40% downside to the mid SOTP. # DELIVERABLE 3: RESEARCH GAPS & CLASSIFICATION CHALLENGE

## a) ARCHETYPE CHALLENGE

The L0 classification of EXTRACTOR/GROWER captures Albemarle's lithium segment but fundamentally mischaracterizes the whole. A buyer would see Albemarle as at least **three different businesses requiring three different valuation frameworks:**

**1. Infrastructure Concession (Chile/CORFO):** The Atacama brine operation is a 17-year government-granted exploitation right over a sovereign mineral resource. The correct comps are toll roads, airports, and water concessions — assets with defined-term revenue streams backed by sovereign counterparties. Infrastructure funds value these at 13-18x EBITDA; the market applies 6-8x. The gap on the same asset stream is 2-3x. Reframing Chile from "commodity mining" to "infrastructure concession" is the single most value-accretive analytical contribution available to a banking team.

**2. Oligopoly Specialty Chemical (Bromine/JBC):** A near-monopoly position (70% global share with ICL) demonstrating sustained pricing power (8-12% annual increases) through commodity cycles. This is a textbook specialty chemicals business — not a cyclical commodity — and should be valued at 12-15x EBITDA rather than the 6-8x blended multiple the market assigns. The classification as "Specialties" within a lithium company actively suppresses this segment's achievable multiple.

**3. Strategic Resource Platform (US/Australia):** Kings Mountain, Silver Peak, Greenbushes ROFR, Kemerton restart option, and DLE technology constitute a portfolio of strategic options whose value is primarily geopolitical (FEOC compliance, DoD supply chain security, Western price premium) rather than purely economic. The correct comp is MP Materials (defense-strategic rare earths) or GlobalFoundries (last independent US semiconductor foundry), not Livent or Arcadium. If Albemarle's most valuable asset is the combination of its geographic position across US/Australia/Chile, non-FEOC status, and sovereign concession rights — then starting valuation from the commodity price is backward. Starting from the strategic premium for irreplicable critical mineral supply chain position would produce a fundamentally different (higher) number.
