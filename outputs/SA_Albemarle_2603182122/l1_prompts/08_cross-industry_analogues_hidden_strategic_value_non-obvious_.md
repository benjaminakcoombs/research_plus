ROLE: You are an innovation-focused senior investor at a multi-strategy fund that specializes in identifying hidden value in traditional businesses by applying cross-industry pattern recognition. You have made your career finding companies that are undervalued because the market is analyzing them through the wrong lens. Your task is to identify the non-obvious value, the unconventional buyers, and the strategic frameworks that would make a senior investment banker sound genuinely original in a client meeting about Albemarle Corporation.

CONTEXT: Albemarle Corporation (NYSE: ALB, ~$22B EV) is classified as a specialty chemicals company, covered by chemicals analysts, and valued using chemicals multiples. But its actual business model — extracting a critical mineral from sovereign-controlled deposits under long-term exploitation contracts, converting it into a battery-essential input, and selling it to the global automotive and energy storage supply chain — doesn't fit neatly into any single industry framework. The company's key assets include:

1. A 2043 CORFO exploitation contract for lithium in Chile's Salar de Atacama — a fixed-term extraction right with escalating royalties, from a sovereign counterparty, on a constitutionally designated "strategic mineral"
2. JV stakes in two of the world's largest hard-rock lithium mines (Greenbushes 49%, Wodgina 50%)
3. An idled conversion plant (Kemerton) with >$1B replacement cost
4. A bromine business with oligopolistic market position
5. A DoD grant for domestic lithium production at Kings Mountain
6. ~$5.1B revenue, ~85% from lithium post-Ketjen divestiture

Post-Ketjen, the market treats this as a pure-play commodity stock. But the sum of parts and the strategic dynamics suggest there's more going on.

RESEARCH TASKS:

1. CROSS-INDUSTRY STRUCTURAL ANALOGUES
For each of the following analogues, research specific companies and outcomes that illuminate hidden value in Albemarle's structure:

a) CONCESSION-BASED INFRASTRUCTURE: The CORFO contract is structurally a government-granted concession with a fixed term, escalating payments, and sovereign counterparty risk — similar to toll road concessions (Transurban, Vinci), airport concessions (ADP), or spectrum licenses (wireless carriers). Research how concession-based assets are valued in infrastructure transactions: what multiples do concession-based infrastructure assets command? How do investors price the "remaining contract life"? Apply this framework to Albemarle's 17-year remaining CORFO term.
   - Specific question: infrastructure investors (Brookfield, Macquarie, IFM, GIP) typically pay 12-20x EBITDA for concession-based assets with 15-25 year remaining terms. Would an infrastructure investor see the CORFO contract through this lens? Research whether any infrastructure fund has ever acquired or bid on a mining concession.

b) DEFENSE-PRIME SUPPLY CHAIN: The DoD grant and "friendly nation" lithium supply position are structurally analogous to defense-prime contractor supply chains. Research how defense-critical suppliers are valued when they have a national security designation — companies like Aerojet Rocketdyne (before L3Harris acquisition), Howmet Aerospace, or rare earth processors (MP Materials, Lynas). Do these companies command a "strategic premium" above what their financial metrics alone would justify?
   - Specific question: would the U.S. Department of Defense (or DARPA, or the Defense Logistics Agency) support or even facilitate a transaction that keeps Albemarle under U.S. or allied-nation ownership? Research the CHIPS Act analogy — has the U.S. government intervened in the ownership of critical supply chain assets?

c) MOTHBALLED INDUSTRIAL CAPACITY: Kemerton is a fully constructed plant sitting idle — similar to mothballed semiconductor fabs, steel mills, or refineries. Research how mothballed industrial assets have been valued and transacted: at what discount to replacement cost? Who are the typical buyers? What is the typical timeline from care-and-maintenance to restart?
   - Specific question: when Intel or Samsung mothball a semiconductor fab, the market often assigns zero value — then a strategic buyer acquires it at 30-50% of replacement cost and restarts it as demand recovers. Is Kemerton in this category?

d) OLIGOPOLY CHEMICALS: The bromine business (Albemarle + ICL = ~70% of global supply) is a textbook oligopoly analogous to industrial gases (Air Liquide, Linde, Air Products), crop protection chemicals, or specialty coatings. Research the valuation premium that oligopolistic market structures command in chemicals M&A. What multiples have oligopoly chemicals businesses fetched in recent transactions?

For each of the following, develop a specific strategic thesis and assess feasibility:
