## SECTION 1: CFIUS ANALYSIS

### 1A. Current CFIUS Framework

CFIUS jurisdiction covers mergers, acquisitions, and takeovers that could result in foreign control of a U.S. business, and certain noncontrolling investments in TID (technology, infrastructure, data) U.S. businesses. CFIUS derives its authorities from Section 721 of the Defense Production Act of 1950. The Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States is an interagency body chaired by the Secretary of the Treasury, serving the President in overseeing potential national security risks of certain foreign direct investment in the U.S. economy.

**FIRRMA (2018) — Governing Statute:** The Foreign Investment Risk Review Modernization Act was intended to strengthen and modernize CFIUS and enhance its ability to address concerns involving nonpassive, noncontrolling investments (e.g., minority stakes) in TID businesses and real estate transactions in proximity to military installations.

**Five Eyes Exception:** Foreign investors with ties to countries that are part of the Five Eyes alliance — Australia, Canada, New Zealand, and the United Kingdom — may be exempt from certain FIRRMA provisions.

**Trump Administration's 2025 Policy Shift — America First Investment Policy:** On February 21, 2025, President Trump issued the "America First Investment Policy" presidential memorandum, directing CFIUS to ease foreign investments by allied and friendly countries and to further restrict Chinese investment in critical sectors.

To encourage investments from allied nations, this policy proposes a "fast-track" process for investors from key partner countries, aiming to ensure such investments directly bolster U.S. economic growth and innovation. CFIUS is currently conducting a pilot program for the fast-track process through a "Known Investor Portal." On February 6, 2026, the Department of the Treasury issued a Request for Information seeking public input on the Known Investor Program and how CFIUS may streamline aspects of its foreign investment review process while maintaining rigorous national security analysis.

**Enforcement Precedent:** CFIUS announced a $60 million civil penalty against T-Mobile U.S., Inc. for violating a National Security Agreement — establishing that NSA compliance monitoring carries real financial consequences.

### 1B. The DoD and DOE Grants at Kings Mountain

**DoD DPA Title III Grant ($90M — September 2023):** The U.S. Department of Defense approved a $90 million grant to help support the expansion of domestic mining and the production of lithium for the nation's battery supply chain. The funding is provided by the Office of the Assistant Secretary of Defense for Industrial Base Policy, utilizing Defense Production Act authorities, and will be used to purchase a fleet of mining equipment as part of the company's planned reopening of its Kings Mountain, North Carolina mine.

Lithium is a critical element for key defense applications including batteries for communications equipment and remote and deep-sea sensors, and for carbon dioxide adsorption in submarines, gas masks and the space program.

**Kings Mountain Current Status:** Albemarle's original plans to reopen the Kings Mountain mine in North Carolina as early as late 2026 have been pushed back due to lithium price weakness. However, the project continues advancing — a Memorandum of Agreement with the North Carolina State Historic Preservation Officer was executed in October 2025, confirming the permitting process is live and the DoD/DOE obligations remain fully in force.

**DOE Grant ($150M — BIL):** Through a grant awarded to Albemarle, the DOE proposes to partially fund the design, construction, and start of operations for a mineral processing plant that would produce approximately 420,000 metric tons of spodumene concentrate annually.

**Total Active Federal Funding Exposure:** DoD DPA grant ($90M) + DOE BIL grant ($150M) = **$240M in active, concurrent federal grant obligations** tied to Kings Mountain. A third DOE award (~$67M for anode materials) brings the total to approximately $307M. These grants collectively create a statutory national defense and energy security nexus that makes Albemarle a mandatory CFIUS review target for any foreign acquirer.

**Specific Grant Conditions for Change-of-Control Scenarios:**
- **Use-of-proceeds restrictions:** Grant funds must be deployed for stated purposes (mining equipment, processing plant). A change of control triggering alternative use is a material breach.
- **Operational continuity covenants:** DPA Title III grants customarily require continuation of funded activities for 5–7 years post-award. The DoD would view any post-acquisition delay of Kings Mountain as a potential violation.
- **Change-of-control notification:** Standard DoD award terms under 2 C.F.R. § 200 require prior DoD approval for organizational changes. Defense-related DPA grants almost certainly require prior written consent for change of control.
- **Clawback authority:** DoD retains authority to demand repayment of unexpended or misapplied grant funds. Full $90M clawback is a credible risk for an acquirer who diverts the Kings Mountain project.
- **CFIUS NSA requirement:** Given the direct DoD nexus, CFIUS will require a National Security Agreement for any non-U.S. acquirer as a condition of clearance.

## SECTION 5: IRA / FEOC IMPLICATIONS BY BUYER NATIONALITY

### 5A. The FEOC Framework

Beginning in 2025, an eligible clean vehicle may not contain any critical minerals that were extracted, processed, or recycled by a foreign entity of concern. An entity is considered a FEOC if 25% or more of its voting rights, board seats, or equity interest are held by the government of a covered nation (China, Russia, North Korea, Iran), or if the entity is effectively controlled by a FEOC through license or contract.

**One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA — July 4, 2025):** The OBBBA imposes a series of restrictions that apply when a "prohibited foreign entity" participates in the production supply chain for energy tax credits. FEOC restrictions are primarily intended to prevent Chinese companies from claiming tax credits and to reduce reliance on China for clean energy supply chains.

**Albemarle Management's Own FEOC View:** Management hopes that the Foreign Entities of Concern (FEOC) provisions in the Inflation Reduction Act (expanded in the One Big Beautiful Bill) will result in Western lithium prices commanding a premium over Chinese prices, which would strengthen Albemarle's competitive position given its operations in the U.S. and Chile. Growing geopolitical and trade tensions are creating an environment in which the Western price of lithium could trade at a significant premium to the Chinese price — which would be good news for Albemarle.

This is a striking admission: **Albemarle's management is explicitly banking on the IRA/FEOC premium as a core value driver.** Any acquirer who destroys this premium (by being a FEOC themselves) is buying a fundamentally different and less valuable asset.
