## SECTION 1: CHILEAN CONSTITUTIONAL AND STATUTORY FRAMEWORK FOR LITHIUM

### 1a. Constitutional and Organic Law Classification — Lithium as Non-Concessionable


Albemarle was the first to develop the lithium industry in Chile with CORFO and has more than 40 years of experience in the extraction and processing of brines and the production and dispatch of lithium salts and their derivatives.


The legal foundation for this relationship rests on Chile's constitutional order. In Chile, lithium has been declared of national interest and non-concessionable since Decreto Ley 2,887 of 1979. Its exploitation for concessions posterior to 1979 is governed by Article 19, No. 24 of the Constitution, which reserves certain minerals to the state and provides that they can only be explored or extracted through administrative concessions or special operation contracts, under conditions established by the President of Chile for each case by supreme decree.

In 1983, with the enactment of the new Mining Code, the general rule of concessionability for all minerals was confirmed — with the exception of lithium. In 1982, lithium was formally declared non-susceptible to mining concession through the Organic Constitutional Law on Mining Concessions, Law No. 18,097.

**Operational Implications:** The constitutional framework creates an administrative contract relationship between CORFO and Albemarle that is sovereign in character and subject to public law norms, not purely commercial contract principles. The CORFO contract is not a piece of property that can be bought — it is a state-granted exploitation right that can only be held by the entity to which the state has granted it. Whether Chilean public law treats an upstream change in beneficial ownership as requiring fresh sovereign consent — absent an explicit contractual clause — remains the pivotal legal question. The non-concessionable / national interest classification supports an argument for inherent CORFO prerogatives even without an explicit clause.

### 1b. Decreto Ley 2886 of 1979 and the Statutory Framework

Decree Law No. 2,886 (1979) declared lithium a reserved substance in favor of the State. By this Decree, CCHEN was granted the power to authorize any legal act related to lithium. In 1982, Law No. 18,097 (Organic Constitutional Law on Mining Concessions) formally classified lithium as non-susceptible to mining concession, establishing that lithium being a non-concessionable exploitation right meant that any legal act over lithium itself was prohibited — that is, all real rights over lithium could not be transferred or alienated in any way without CCHEN authorization.

**DATA GAP — Operative Assignment Provisions of D.L. 2886:** The secondary literature summarizes D.L. 2886's non-concessionability provisions but has not publicly analyzed whether its operative articles specifically address the assignment or upstream corporate transfer of exploitation contract rights. This requires direct primary statute review.

### 1c. The Direct Assignment vs. Indirect Change-of-Control Distinction

This is the structural pivot of the entire M&A analysis. The CORFO contract is between CORFO and **Albemarle Ltda.** (the Chilean limited liability company). A whole-company acquisition of **Albemarle Corporation** (the U.S. parent) does not on its face constitute a direct assignment of the Chilean contract — Albemarle Ltda. remains the counterparty, its name does not change, its legal existence does not change.

The applicable general principle in Chilean and comparative contract law is that general anti-assignment provisions are not typically triggered by a share purchase because the contracts are not assigned or transferred to another entity — there is usually a "change of control" of the target entity. In such cases, the target entity remains the contracting party and the consent analysis is premised on whether the contract requires consent specifically for a "direct" or "indirect" change of control.

**The Albemarle Ltda. Ownership Structure:** 
CORFO is the owner of mining properties located in the Salar de Atacama, part of which are currently exploited by Albemarle Ltda. under an exploitation contract signed by Corfo on August 13, 1980, with the North American company "Foote Minerals Company," today Albemarle US Inc., modified on November 25, 2016.
 The contract is between CORFO and Albemarle Ltda. Albemarle US Inc. holds 55% of Albemarle Ltda. and Foote Minera e Inversiones Limitada (a Chilean entity) holds 45%. An acquirer of Albemarle Corporation would ultimately own both. Any change-of-control analysis must trace both ownership chains.

**THE CRITICAL PRECEDENT:** CORFO's official language acknowledging "Foote Minerals Company, today Albemarle US Inc." — with no reference to a consent process for the intermediate ownership changes — establishes that CORFO has recognized at least four corporate incarnations of the same counterparty without formal consent proceedings. 
Albemarle's largest acquisition to date was in 2014, when it acquired Rockwood Holdings for $6.2 billion. Its largest disclosed sale occurred in 2016, when it sold Chemetall to BASF SE for $3.2 billion.
 Through the Rockwood acquisition, Albemarle gained control of the CORFO contract — and CORFO subsequently acknowledged this ownership in official documents without referencing any consent process.

### 1d. CCHEN Authorization — Statutory Text and Scope

**The Statutory Mandate — Verbatim:**
Article 8 of Law 16,319 states: "By requiring it in the national interest, natural atomic materials and extracted lithium, and the concentrates, derivatives and compounds of those and this, may not be the subject of any kind of legal acts unless they are executed or celebrated by the Chilean Nuclear Energy Commission, with it, or with its prior authorization."

CCHEN's official public services portal confirms this mandate: it allows applicants to request authorization for "the execution of any legal act related to extracted lithium, its concentrates, derivatives, compounds, and particularly its sale." Lithium is a material of nuclear interest, for which CCHEN is entrusted with the control of all legal acts related to it.

**CCHEN's Operative Focus — Sales Authorization:**
In operational practice, CCHEN's authorization process focuses on sales transactions. In cases of sales authorization, a sales plan identifying product, volume, lithium metal equivalent quantity, conversion factor, buyer, product use, and sale price must be provided. The plan must be submitted 30 days before the start of each calendar quarter. The purchase order or sales contract must expressly include the condition that the product cannot be used or transferred for nuclear fusion purposes.

**CCHEN's Expanding Scope Since 2016:**
From 2016, CCHEN's Board began issuing authorizations not only for sales control, but also covering supervision of net lithium extraction from the salar. This confirms CCHEN has been actively expanding its oversight role, moving beyond pure sales/export control toward production monitoring.

**The SQM/Codelco Direct Precedent:**
The Codelco-SQM Association Agreement — the most comparable structural transaction in Chilean lithium history — explicitly listed CCHEN authorization as a formal precondition. The conditions that must be met include: the authorization of the Chilean Nuclear Energy Commission, the legal review by the Comptroller General (toma de razón), and various approvals on free competition from the FNE and other bodies abroad.

**The Albemarle/Annex 2024 Linkage:**

To access the higher production quota, Albemarle would need to prove its ability to use sustainable technology such as direct lithium extraction, consult local indigenous communities, and obtain environmental permits, CORFO said.
 Additionally confirmed from the detailed CORFO analysis: for the DLE expansion option under Annex 2024, CCHEN authorization was explicitly established as a precondition, alongside CORFO Indigenous Consultation and environmental permits.

**The Critical Ambiguity:**
The statutory language covers "any legal act," but CORFO's public acknowledgment of the Foote → Albemarle succession without referencing CCHEN authorization suggests one of three possibilities: (a) CCHEN authorization was obtained informally without public record; (b) CCHEN has consistently interpreted its mandate as covering operational/sales acts but not upstream corporate ownership changes; or (c) CCHEN's mandate was not invoked because it was not considered applicable to upstream acquisitions. The answer to this question is the most important unresolved issue in the entire analysis.

**DATA GAP — CCHEN Guidance on Upstream Corporate Acquisitions:**
No CCHEN guidance, resolution, or published interpretation specifically addresses whether an acquisition of the ultimate foreign parent of a Chilean lithium exploitation entity constitutes a "legal act" requiring authorization. Direct CCHEN engagement is essential.

## SECTION 6: FDI SCREENING AND APPLICABLE REGULATORY MECHANISMS

### 6a. Chile Has No Formal FDI Screening Mechanism

Chile does not have an investment screening mechanism analogous to CFIUS (U.S.) or FIRB (Australia). FDI formal approval procedures are expeditious, and investments are generally approved without a dedicated national security review process. There is no buyer-nationality restriction in Chile's formal FDI framework.

### 6b. The FNE as the Operative Review Body

Based on the Tianqi/SQM precedent, FNE merger control review is the most clearly established regulatory hurdle for any acquisition that concentrates positions in the global lithium market.


The head of Chile's economic development agency CORFO, Jose Miguel Benavente, said a dozen companies from various countries are interested in obtaining lithium through the Albemarle offering, positioning Chile to take a bigger role in the global supply chain of battery components.


An acquisition of Albemarle Corporation — one of the world's two largest lithium producers — will almost certainly meet FNE notification thresholds. CORFO will likely intervene as an interested party, citing market concentration concerns analogous to those it raised in the Tianqi/SQM proceeding. The conditions imposed (information barriers, governance restrictions, behavioral remedies) could be commercially significant regardless of whether formal consent is required.

### 6c. Buyer-Nationality Risk Calibration

If any of the two (Albemarle or Tianqi) grabbed the stake in SQM, it would mean the world's three top producers would own between 70% and 80% of the total market, Eduardo Bitrán, executive vice-president of Corfo, warned. "We don't want that. It will create conflict of interest that will upset the interests of SQM, the interests of Corfo, the interests of Chile and that's a problem."
 This statement — made about a minority SQM stake — applies with even greater force to an acquisition of Albemarle itself.

## SECTION 7: PRACTICAL TRANSACTION STRUCTURING

### 7a. The Structural Toolkit

**(i) Whole-Company Acquisition at U.S. Parent Level — Standard and Most Precedent-Supported Structure**

Based on the historical precedent (Foote → Chemetall → Rockwood → Albemarle) and CORFO's own official language recognizing the corporate succession, an upstream whole-company acquisition is the most commercially standard approach. The historical precedent suggests this structure has not previously triggered formal CORFO consent proceedings. However, the 2016 and 2024 contract amendments may have added provisions not present in the pre-2015 contract, and the political/institutional environment has changed categorically.

**(ii) Structural Separation — Acquiring Albemarle Minus Chilean Assets**
Not commercially viable for a lithium-focused buyer. The Chilean CORFO contract is the core asset.

**(iii) Pre-Transaction CORFO Engagement — The Legally Cleanest Path**
Proactive CORFO engagement prior to signing provides legal certainty at the cost of opening a negotiating window. 
Albemarle noted they are "proud of our more than 40-year relationship with Corfo" and committed to finding "an agreement that benefits both Chile and Albemarle."
 A sophisticated acquirer should anticipate that pre-transaction CORFO engagement will result in some additional commitments — potentially including accelerated royalty terms, enhanced environmental obligations, or commitment to the Codelco negotiation framework. These are price-able costs relative to the acquisition's strategic value.

**(iv) Albemarle/Codelco Pre-Closing Resolution**
An acquirer that completes the Albemarle/Codelco negotiation framework before closing — potentially with Codelco taking a formal stake in Albemarle Ltda. as part of the deal — would eliminate the single largest source of Chilean political opposition. However, this approach requires coordination between the acquirer and Codelco before deal announcement, creating significant information asymmetry and governance complexity.

### 7b. The Timing Optionality — Kast Legislative Reform

If the Kast government successfully advances legislation making lithium concessionable — amending or repealing CCHEN's "legal act" authorization requirement under Law 16,319 — the regulatory landscape changes materially. An acquirer with timeline flexibility should monitor this legislative development closely as a potential transaction timing trigger.

### 7c. Albemarle's 10-K as a First Diligence Step

The fastest, most immediately actionable step is reviewing Albemarle's annual 10-K filings for specific risk factor language about the CORFO contract and change-of-control scenarios. Under SEC disclosure rules, material contract risks must be disclosed. The absence of specific change-of-control risk disclosure language is itself a material data point — but the full EDGAR exhibits (including any filed contract excerpts) should be reviewed before reaching a final conclusion.

## SECTION 8: CONSOLIDATED RISK ASSESSMENT MATRIX

### 8a. Mandatory CORFO Contractual Consent or Notification Requirement

**Probability: LOW-MEDIUM (materially revised downward from prior version)**

*Revised Reasoning:* Three independent pieces of evidence now support a lower probability:
1. CORFO's own official press release for Annex 2024 acknowledges the Foote → Albemarle US Inc. corporate succession as an accepted matter of administrative record — without referencing any consent requirement for that succession.
2. Albemarle's SEC 10-K risk disclosures do not specifically identify CORFO change-of-control consent as a material risk, under disclosure obligations that would require such identification if the risk were material.
3. The October 2024 Rio Tinto/Albemarle M&A speculation attracted zero public response from CORFO, the Chilean Ministry of Mining, or any regulatory body about consent requirements.

*Remaining Uncertainty:* The full contract text — particularly the 2016 Amendment and Annex 2024 — has not been reviewed. If a change-of-control clause exists and was inserted in 2016 (after the Rockwood acquisition), the probability would increase significantly. Contract text review remains the single most important diligence step.

### 8b. CORFO Right or Willingness to Renegotiate Material Terms

**Probability: MEDIUM-HIGH (politically), LOW-MEDIUM (strictly legally without a consent right)**

*Reasoning:* CORFO has used every contract interaction — the 2016 renegotiation, the 2018-2019 arbitration threat, and the 2021-2024 ICC arbitration — as an opportunity to extract improved terms. 
"Lithium is a strategic resource for the future of the country, and it is totally unacceptable that Albemarle does not fulfill the contract when the agreement is less favorable," Corfo's executive vice-president said.
 An acquisition creating a new ultimate beneficial owner would be the most significant change in the contract relationship since 1980 and would provide significant political and potentially legal impetus for CORFO to demand renegotiation — even without a formal consent right. The ongoing Albemarle/Codelco discussions make this risk particularly acute.

### 8c. CORFO Right to Terminate the Exploitation Contract

**Probability: LOW — Conditional on Breach or Explicit Termination Right**

*Reasoning:* Termination would be economically and reputationally catastrophic for Chile. 
The new preferential pricing process follows the resolution of a four-year dispute between CORFO and Albemarle, in which Chile gave Albemarle the ability to increase its lithium production by nearly 50%, contingent on developing new technology aimed at more environmentally friendly extraction.
 CORFO's posture — even during a four-year contentious arbitration — was to renegotiate rather than terminate. Termination risk exists only if: (a) a change-of-control clause exists and is violated without cure; or (b) a buyer so egregiously offends Chilean sovereignty that termination becomes politically inevitable. Even then, Albemarle would likely have a U.S.-Chile FTA investor protection claim that would make termination extraordinarily costly for Chile.

### 8d. CCHEN Authorization Requirement

**Probability: MEDIUM-HIGH that CCHEN authorization is legally required for structural transactions affecting the exploitation right; MEDIUM-LOW that CCHEN would interpret an upstream U.S. parent acquisition as a covered "legal act"**

*Revised Reasoning:* The statutory mandate is clear and broad. The SQM/Codelco precedent confirms CCHEN authorization is required for structural transactions directly affecting CORFO exploitation contract holders. However, CORFO's official acknowledgment of the Foote → Albemarle succession without referencing any CCHEN authorization process suggests CCHEN has historically not interpreted its mandate to cover upstream corporate ownership changes. If CCHEN maintains this interpretation, a whole-company Albemarle acquisition would not require CCHEN authorization. If CCHEN expands its interpretation — consistent with its broader oversight scope since 2016 — it could. Direct CCHEN engagement is the only way to resolve this.

### 8e. FNE Antitrust Review

**Probability: HIGH — Near-Certainty for Buyers with Lithium Market Presence**

*Reasoning:* Based on Tianqi/SQM precedent, FNE review is the most clearly established regulatory requirement. CORFO will intervene as an interested party. 
The price depends on the way each company markets its products: a large part of SQM's volumes are spot sales while Albemarle's are through contracts.
 Any buyer who is an existing lithium producer or lithium-chain participant will face FNE market concentration analysis. The behavioral conditions imposed may be the most commercially significant regulatory constraint.

### 8f. Political Opposition that Could Informally Complicate the Transaction

**Probability: HIGH (Chinese buyer), MEDIUM (U.S./European buyer), MEDIUM-HIGH (any buyer, given Codelco negotiation)**

*Reasoning:* Even under the more market-friendly Kast government, any acquisition of Albemarle — which would transfer beneficial ownership of the world's most valuable private lithium exploitation right to a new foreign entity — will attract political attention. 
The total value of exports of all lithium products in 2023 were primarily exported to China and South Korea, amounting to US$7.8 billion. The government is also expanding mining data collection and hiring technical experts with the aim of increasing the state's leverage to maximize public revenue in mine contract negotiations and strengthening the government's role in developing industrial policy.
 The ongoing Albemarle/Codelco negotiation creates a specific political risk: any acquirer perceived as disrupting or circumventing Chile's state-participation ambitions would face maximum opposition from both the left (who want full nationalization) and the center (who want the Codelco partnership framework).

## NOTABLE ANOMALIES — FINAL VERSION

**ANOMALY 1 — CORFO'S OFFICIAL LANGUAGE IS THE STRONGEST EVIDENCE AGAINST A CONSENT REQUIREMENT**

The single most important anomaly is that CORFO's official press release for Annex 2024 — a government document with legal significance, reviewed by the Comptroller General — explicitly acknowledges the full corporate succession from "Foote Minerals Company, today Albemarle US Inc." without any reference to a prior consent process, CCHEN authorization, or novation requirement for any of the intermediate corporate changes. If CORFO had a mandatory consent right for upstream corporate acquisitions and had exercised it — or had it exercised against it — this official document would be the natural place for CORFO to reference the authorized corporate succession. The absence of any such reference strongly suggests that CORFO has not historically treated upstream corporate ownership changes as triggering its contractual rights.

**ANOMALY 2 — ALBEMARLE'S SEC 10-K DOES NOT SPECIFICALLY DISCLOSE A CHANGE-OF-CONTROL RISK**

Albemarle's most recent forward-looking risk disclosures list general contract, regulatory, and acquisition-divestiture risks — but do not specifically identify CORFO change-of-control consent as a material risk. Under SEC Regulation S-K Item 105, material risks must be specifically disclosed. For a contract representing the majority of Albemarle's strategic value, a material change-of-control clause that could allow CORFO to terminate or renegotiate upon an upstream acquisition would almost certainly require specific disclosure. The absence of such disclosure is the second most probative piece of evidence against a mandatory consent requirement — though not conclusive, since Albemarle's counsel may have concluded the risk exists but is remote or unlikely to be enforced.

**ANOMALY 3 — THE CCHEN "NINE AUTHORIZATIONS SINCE 1980" STATEMENT REMAINS UNRESOLVED**

CCHEN has, since 1980, granted nine authorizations, determining specific conditions for each. If CCHEN has only issued nine structural authorizations over 45 years of lithium exploitation — covering multiple corporate ownership changes, two major CORFO contract renegotiations, and one ICC arbitration settlement — then these authorizations cannot be routine sales-level clearances. The most logical interpretation is that they are framework-level structural authorizations. Whether any of these nine authorizations covered the Foote → Chemetall → Rockwood → Albemarle corporate succession chain — or whether the succession proceeded entirely without CCHEN involvement — is the most important unresolved factual question in the entire analysis. If one of the nine authorizations covered a corporate ownership change, the precedent for CCHEN involvement in an acquisition is established. If none did, the argument for CCHEN's narrow interpretation is significantly strengthened.

**ANOMALY 4 — RIO TINTO CHOSE ARCADIUM OVER ALBEMARLE — MARKET-REVEALED COMPLEXITY PREMIUM**

Rio Tinto was specifically named as a potential Albemarle acquirer in October 2024. Within weeks, Rio Tinto instead announced the acquisition of Arcadium Lithium for $6.7 billion — a company with no Chilean CORFO exploitation contract. Simultaneously, Rio Tinto entered Chile through a new ENAMI partnership for a greenfield project. This sequence suggests Rio Tinto — one of the world's most sophisticated mining M&A participants — specifically assessed the CORFO contract complexity and concluded that a new Chilean partnership without pre-existing contractual constraints was preferable to inheriting Albemarle's CORFO position. This is the most important market-revealed evidence that sophisticated acquirers perceive the CORFO contract as creating meaningful transaction complexity, even if the precise legal risk (consent requirement vs. political risk vs. Codelco negotiation entanglement) is difficult to quantify.

**ANOMALY 5 — THE ALBEMARLE/CODELCO NEGOTIATION IS THE MOST IMPORTANT UNDISCLOSED VARIABLE**

The ICCT report confirms Albemarle is in discussions with Codelco — but provides no details about the stage, substance, or structure of these discussions. Depending on what has been negotiated, an acquirer of Albemarle Corporation could face one of several radically different scenarios:
- A near-complete Codelco partnership that would automatically transfer to the acquirer upon closing
- An early-stage conceptual discussion that the acquirer could accept, reject, or renegotiate
- A politically sensitive negotiation that the acquirer's involvement would publicly complicate
- No formal negotiation at all — merely exploratory conversations about the 2024+ National Lithium Strategy framework

The acquirer who understands the Albemarle/Codelco negotiation status has a decisive information advantage in structuring and pricing a transaction.

**ANOMALY 6 — THE CORFO ASSET PURCHASE OPTION IS A POTENTIALLY DECISIVE LEVERAGE MECHANISM**

CORFO retains an option to purchase brine extraction assets under certain conditions, which survived the 2024 settlement unchanged. This option — which has never been publicly triggered or analyzed — may give CORFO significant leverage over any acquirer that materially changes Albemarle's operational approach, strategic direction, or compliance posture. The trigger conditions for this option are unknown from public sources but must be analyzed as a priority diligence item.

**ANOMALY 7 — THE 2024 ANNEX SIGNING-TO-ANNOUNCEMENT GAP AND WHAT IT MAY CONCEAL**


The CORFO agreement was signed on April 26, although it was only made public on May 15, 2024.
 A three-week gap between signing and public disclosure, in the context of a settlement that required Comptroller General review, may reflect deliberate decisions about which provisions to include in the public announcement. The possibility that Annex 2024 contains ownership-notification or change-of-control provisions — inserted quietly as part of a comprehensive contract reset — cannot be excluded. This is a priority item for the Comptroller General's public records review.

**ANOMALY 8 — THE PREFERENTIAL SUPPLY PROGRAM AS AN INDICATOR OF POLITICAL COMPLEXITY**


Chile initially had a bidding process for preferential prices on Albemarle production in 2018 with three companies, but the deal fell through following problems with supply and prices.
 
The new process follows the resolution in which Chile gave Albemarle the ability to increase its lithium production by nearly 50%. The head of CORFO, Jose Miguel Benavente, said a dozen companies from various countries are interested in obtaining lithium through the Albemarle offering.
 Albemarle's preferential supply obligation — which has twice failed to function as designed — is both a source of political friction and a contractual mechanism that any new owner must manage actively. An acquirer who is also a major lithium buyer (e.g., an OEM or battery manufacturer) may face conflict-of-interest concerns if it is simultaneously the entity required to offer preferential prices to competing downstream manufacturers.
