## AGENT 1: BUYER UNIVERSE & PRECEDENT TRANSACTIONS

**JUSTIFICATION:** Bloom Energy's July 2023 dual-class sunset makes it fully takeover-accessible for the first time in its history, while its $43B market cap, 119x forward P/E, and recent Oracle-driven volatility create a complex buyer universe that must be mapped with precision — the range of plausible acquirers spans infrastructure funds, hyperscalers, industrial conglomerates, and sovereign wealth, each with radically different price tolerance and strategic logic.

**PRIORITY:** Critical

```
You are a senior M&A investment banker who has covered the power infrastructure, clean energy, and industrial technology sectors for 15 years. You have executed transactions involving distributed generation assets, fuel cell companies, and infrastructure-as-a-service businesses. You are preparing the buyer universe section of a Strategic Situation Assessment for Bloom Energy Corporation (NYSE: BE), a $43B market cap solid-oxide fuel cell manufacturer that has become a central power infrastructure supplier to AI data centers.

CRITICAL CONTEXT: Bloom Energy's dual-class supervoting structure sunsetted in July 2023. The company is now a single-class, one-share-one-vote entity with ~12% insider ownership. Founder/CEO KR Sridhar cannot unilaterally block a hostile bid. This company is mechanically and legally takeover-accessible. The stock trades at $153.68 (March 2026), has a beta of 5.34, and experienced a 15.5% single-day decline on March 6, 2026 after Oracle/OpenAI cancelled a Texas data center project. FY2025 revenue was $2.02B (+37.3% YoY), with $20B in backlog. The company has $2.5B in zero-coupon convertible notes due 2030 with a conversion price of $194.97 — currently 21% out of the money.

YOUR RESEARCH TASK — DELIVER ALL THREE SECTIONS WITH FULL SOURCING:

SECTION A: STRATEGIC BUYER MAPPING (Identify 10-15 specific companies)

For each potential strategic acquirer, provide:
1. Company name, current market cap or enterprise value, and relevant business unit
2. Specific strategic rationale for acquiring Bloom Energy — not generalities, but how Bloom fills a specific gap in their portfolio, technology stack, or customer base
3. Recent M&A activity (last 3 years) — deals completed, sizes, sectors, stated strategic priorities
4. Financial capacity estimate: cash on hand + undrawn credit facilities - near-term maturities = available acquisition firepower
5. Cultural/structural fit assessment: would this buyer retain KR Sridhar? Could they integrate Bloom's manufacturing operations?

Prioritize these categories and research SPECIFIC companies in each:
- **Infrastructure asset managers** (Brookfield, KKR Infrastructure, Global Infrastructure Partners/BlackRock, Macquarie, EQT): What is their thesis on distributed power? Have they acquired power generation OEMs before, or only operating assets?
- **Hyperscaler vertical integration** (Oracle, Microsoft, Google, Amazon/AWS, Meta): Would any hyperscaler buy their power supplier? Research whether any hyperscaler has EVER acquired a power generation equipment manufacturer. What precedent exists for vertical integration of power supply in the tech sector?
- **Industrial conglomerates** (GE Vernova, Siemens Energy, Caterpillar, Cummins, Mitsubishi Power, Doosan): Which of these has an active fuel cell or distributed generation strategy? Research private equity and infrastructure funds that could finance a take-private of a $43B+ market cap company:

3. Has any PE firm or infrastructure fund ever taken a company of this market cap private in the energy technology sector? What are the 3-5 closest precedents by deal size?
4. Research Brookfield's specific track record: How many preferred-provider or strategic-partnership relationships has Brookfield converted into outright acquisitions? What was the typical timeline from partnership to acquisition? What premium did Brookfield pay relative to pre-partnership valuations?
5. Could a consortium bid work? Identify 2-3 plausible consortium combinations (e.g., Brookfield + SK Group, KKR + a hyperscaler)

SECTION C: PRECEDENT TRANSACTIONS

Research and compile a detailed comp table of 10-15 precedent transactions involving:
- Fuel cell companies (any type — SOFC, PEM, molten carbonate)
- Distributed generation equipment manufacturers

- Mission-critical industrial OEMs with long-tail service contracts (analogous business models)

For each transaction, provide:

Pay special attention to:

## AGENT 2: CAPITAL STRUCTURE, CONVERTIBLE NOTE MECHANICS & M&A SCENARIO MODELING

**JUSTIFICATION:** The $2.5B zero-coupon convertible notes due 2030 with a conversion price of $194.97 are the single most complex financial instrument in Bloom's capital structure and create acute deal-mechanics questions — how does a change-of-control trigger work on these notes, what happens at various stock price scenarios, and does the convert create seller urgency or buyer opportunity? This lens is deal-critical and has no standard template.

**PRIORITY:** Critical

```
You are a senior convertible bond analyst and capital structure specialist at a bulge-bracket investment bank. You have spent 15 years structuring and analyzing convertible securities, and you advise M&A teams on how convertible instruments affect deal mechanics, merger consideration, and transaction feasibility. You are analyzing Bloom Energy Corporation's (NYSE: BE) capital structure for a Strategic Situation Assessment focused on M&A feasibility.

KNOWN CAPITAL STRUCTURE:
- $2.5 billion in 0% Convertible Senior Notes due November 15, 2030
- Conversion price: $194.97/share (52.5% premium to $127.85 close on October 30, 2025)
- Callable by issuer after November 20, 2028 if stock trades above 130% × $194.97 = $253.46 for specified period
- Prior 2028 and 2029 convertible tranches retired (exchanged for cash + Class A shares in November 2025)
- $600 million Senior Secured Revolving Credit Facility (December 2025, multicurrency, permits "permitted acquisitions")
- Current stock price: $153.68 (March 16, 2026) — 21.2% below conversion price
- All-time high: $174.77 (February 25, 2026)
- Stock beta: 5.34
- Market cap: $43.27B
- Debt-to-equity ratio: 3.89
- Current ratio: 5.98
- Altman Z-Score: 6.88

YOUR RESEARCH TASK:

SECTION A: CONVERTIBLE NOTE DEEP DIVE

1. Research the SPECIFIC terms of Bloom's November 2025 convertible note indenture. The notes were issued under a standard convertible note indenture — find and analyze:
   - **Change-of-control provisions**: What happens to the $2.5B notes upon a merger, acquisition, or change-of-control event? Standard convertible indentures typically include a "fundamental change" put right allowing holders to require repurchase at par + accrued interest. Confirm whether Bloom's notes include this provision and at what price (par? par plus make-whole?).
   - **Make-whole fundamental change**: Does the indenture include additional share consideration upon a make-whole fundamental change? If so, what is the make-whole table (additional shares per $1,000 note based on stock price and effective date)?
   - **Merger consideration adjustment**: If Bloom is acquired for cash, does the conversion right convert into a right to receive cash consideration? Or do notes become puttable at par?
   - **Anti-dilution provisions**: What adjustments occur upon stock splits, dividends, or other dilutive events?

2. Research the SPECIFIC terms of the prior 2028 and 2029 convertible notes that were retired in November 2025. How were they exchanged? What was the settlement mix (cash vs. shares)? What was the implied premium or discount to par at time of exchange? This tells us how Bloom's treasury team handles convert rollovers — critical intelligence for modeling 2030 maturity scenarios.

3. Research the $600M revolving credit facility:
   - What are the financial covenants?
   - Are there change-of-control provisions that would trigger acceleration?
   - What cross-default provisions exist with the convertible notes?
   - The facility permits "permitted acquisitions" — what are the defined limitations?

SECTION B: M&A SCENARIO MODELING

Model the following acquisition scenarios and calculate the financial impact on each capital structure layer:

**Scenario 1: Cash acquisition at 25% premium ($192/share, ~$54B equity value)**
- What happens to the $2.5B converts? (Below conversion price of $194.97 — notes likely puttable at par)
- Total acquisition cost: equity + convert retirement + revolver paydown = total EV
- How much cash does the acquirer need?

**Scenario 2: Cash acquisition at 40% premium ($215/share, ~$60.5B equity value)**
- Now ABOVE conversion price — what happens? Notes convert to shares → acquirer must acquire more shares → total diluted share count increases
- Calculate the fully diluted share count including convert shares
- Total acquisition cost at this price
- Is it cheaper for the acquirer to buy at a price below conversion ($194.97) to avoid convert dilution, or above it?

**Scenario 3: Stock-for-stock merger (acquirer issues own stock)**
- How do the converts adjust? Conversion into acquirer stock?
- What governance/voting complications arise from $2.5B in convertible securities that now convert into acquirer equity?

**Scenario 4: No acquisition — stock remains below $194.97 through 2030 maturity**
- Bloom must repay $2.5B in cash with no prior coupon payments
- Current cash position and projected free cash flow through 2030
- Can Bloom self-fund this repayment? What refinancing would be required?
- At what stock price does this maturity become a balance sheet crisis vs. a manageable refinancing?

**Scenario 5: Hostile tender offer at $170/share during a stock correction**
- Stock at $130-140 after another Oracle-type event; bidder offers $170
- Convert holders: put at par ($2.5B) or hold for potential upside?
- How does the convert holder base (identify top holders if possible) likely behave?
- Is the convert an impediment or an accelerant to a hostile bid?

SECTION C: ORACLE WARRANT INTERACTION

The Oracle warrant (3.53M shares at $113.28 strike, ~$143M in-the-money) has terms that were "subject to negotiation" as of the October 2025 8-K. Research:
1. Has a definitive warrant agreement been filed as an 8-K exhibit since October 2025?
2. Standard warrant change-of-control provisions in comparable commercial warrant agreements — what typically happens?
3. If Oracle has a right to exercise upon change of control, what is the dilutive impact? (3.53M additional shares at acquisition price minus $113.28 strike = net cost to acquirer)
4. If Oracle has consent rights or anti-assignment provisions, how does this affect deal mechanics?

SECTION D: COMPARABLE CAPITAL STRUCTURE ANALYSIS

Research 3-5 precedent M&A transactions involving targets with large outstanding convertible notes:
- How were the converts handled in each deal?
- Did the convert provisions affect the acquisition price or structure?
- Were there cases where the convert created a "sweet spot" price range for the acquirer (just below conversion to avoid dilution)?
- Specific examples: Tesla's convertible note history, MicroStrategy's BTC converts and how they'd interact with a hypothetical acquisition, any clean energy company with outstanding converts that was acquired

SECTION E: CREDIT & REFINANCING ANALYSIS

1. What is Bloom's current credit rating (if rated)? If unrated, what implied rating would the capital structure suggest?
2. Could Bloom issue new debt to refinance the $2.5B converts before maturity? At what interest rate?
3. How does the zero-coupon convert structure affect Bloom's reported interest expense and GAAP earnings vs. cash flow?
4. The Altman Z-Score of 6.88 suggests financial stability — but the debt-to-equity of 3.89 suggests high leverage. Reconcile: is this a healthy or stressed capital structure?

DELIVERABLE FORMAT:
- Convert term sheet summary table
- Five acquisition scenario models with total cost calculations
- Timeline of convert-related pressure events (call date, maturity, stock price thresholds)
- Precedent transaction table showing how converts were handled
- All financial calculations shown with methodology
- Sources cited for all indenture terms and financial data

NOTABLE ANOMALIES: Flag anything unusual about the convert structure — particularly if the change-of-control provisions are more or less favorable to holders than standard market terms. Flag if the convert holder base is concentrated in hedge funds (who might be short the stock as a hedge) vs. long-only investors (who might prefer conversion). Flag if the $194.97 conversion price creates a specific "acquisition price ceiling" that a sophisticated buyer could exploit — i.e., buying below conversion price to minimize total deal cost. This is potentially the single most important tactical insight in the entire Situation Assessment.

## AGENT 3: BACKLOG QUALITY, CUSTOMER CONCENTRATION & REVENUE DURABILITY

**JUSTIFICATION:** Bloom's $20B backlog is the single most-cited metric in the bull case, but two Oracle project failures in three months (Michigan financing crisis December 2025, Texas cancellation March 2026) have directly challenged backlog monetizability. A quality-of-earnings-style decomposition of what's actually in the backlog — firm funded orders vs. contingent commitments vs. LOIs — is the highest-value diligence intelligence for any acquirer.

**PRIORITY:** Critical

```
You are a quality-of-earnings (QoE) analyst at a top-tier transaction advisory firm. You specialize in revenue quality assessment for industrial technology companies with large backlog-dependent business models. You have conducted QoE analyses for 20+ M&A transactions involving companies with long-cycle government, utility, and enterprise contracts. You are conducting a pre-LOI revenue quality assessment of Bloom Energy Corporation (NYSE: BE) for a potential acquirer.

CRITICAL CONTEXT:
- Bloom Energy reported $20B in backlog as of Q4 2025, with $6B added in Q4 alone
- FY2025 revenue: $2.02B. FY2026 guidance: $3.1-3.3B
- Backlog implies ~6-10 years of revenue visibility at current run rates
- Two Oracle-related project disruptions occurred in 3 months: (1) Blue Owl pulled out of the $10B Oracle Michigan facility financing in December 2025; (2) Oracle/OpenAI cancelled their Texas data center expansion on March 6, 2026, causing Bloom's stock to fall 15.5%
- Key named customers: Oracle, Amazon/AWS (via AEP), Equinix, CoreWeave, Brookfield-backed projects, SK ecoplant (Asia distributor)
- The AEP contract ($2.65B, 900 MW, 20-year, near Cheyenne, Wyoming) routes through a regulated utility
- SK ecoplant Preferred Distributor Agreement covers 500 MW through 2027
- Bloom sells both equipment (upfront) and 20-year service contracts (recurring)

YOUR RESEARCH TASK:

SECTION A: BACKLOG DECOMPOSITION

Research every available public disclosure about Bloom Energy's backlog composition:

1. **Backlog definition**: How does Bloom define "backlog" in its SEC filings? Search the 10-K risk factors and MD&A sections. Is backlog defined as (a) firm signed contracts with unconditional payment obligations, (b) signed contracts subject to conditions precedent (like customer project financing), (c) framework agreements or volume commitments without specific delivery schedules, or (d) a combination? The exact definition is critical.

2. **Cancellability provisions**: Research Bloom's disclosed contract cancellation terms. Can customers cancel backlog orders? Under what circumstances? What penalties apply? Search 10-K disclosures, earnings call transcripts, and any analyst day presentations for specific language about cancellation rights.

3. **Backlog stratification attempt**: Using all available public information, estimate what percentage of the $20B backlog falls into each category:
   - **Tier 1 — Firm, funded, scheduled**: Contracts with binding delivery dates and unconditional payment terms (e.g., AEP contract through regulated utility)
   - **Tier 2 — Signed but contingent**: Contracts dependent on third-party project financing, permitting, or site readiness (e.g., Oracle-dependent projects)
   - **Tier 3 — Framework/option**: Volume commitments, preferred provider agreements, or option exercises (e.g., portions of Brookfield $5B, SK ecoplant 500 MW)
   Show your methodology. If you cannot determine exact percentages, provide a reasoned range with supporting evidence.

4. **Historical backlog conversion**: Research Bloom's historical backlog-to-revenue conversion rate. How much backlog existed 1, 2, and 3 years ago, and how much revenue was subsequently recognized? Has backlog ever been written down, restated, or reduced other than through revenue recognition? This is the single most important backward-looking data point.

SECTION B: CUSTOMER CONCENTRATION DEEP DIVE

   - The Oracle AI factory delivered in 55 days
   - The Texas project (cancelled March 6, 2026)
   - The Michigan project (financing pulled December 2025)
   - Any other Oracle projects announced or in progress
   - The Oracle warrant (3.53M shares) — does the warrant correspond to a specific volume commitment?

2. **SK ecoplant concentration**: SK ecoplant accounted for ~55% of Q3 2025 revenue. Research:
   - Is this concentration declining or increasing quarter-over-quarter?
   - The Preferred Distributor Agreement expires in 2027 — what happens to the Asia distribution channel post-2027?
   - Does the agreement contain change-of-control provisions? (Search original 2021 SEC filing for the agreement terms)
   - The 80 MW Korea deployment (world's largest fuel cell installation) — is this under the existing agreement or a separate contract?

3. **Amazon/AEP contract analysis**: The $2.65B AEP contract (900 MW, 20 years) is described as the most structurally durable anchor:
   - Is this a firm, fully contracted commitment or does it have option/exercise features?
   - What is the revenue recognition timeline? (20-year service implies $130M+/year in service revenue alone)
   - Does AEP (a regulated utility) face any regulatory approvals that could delay or modify the contract?
   - Has Amazon/AWS publicly confirmed this specific Bloom engagement?

4. **Brookfield pipeline**: The $5B preferred provider relationship — how much of this is contracted vs. pipeline?
   - Has any specific Brookfield project been announced with contracted Bloom systems?
   - The European AI inference data center "planned for announcement by end of year" — has this been announced?
   - What is Brookfield's typical timeline from preferred provider selection to actual procurement?

5. **Other named customers**: Research Equinix, CoreWeave, and the unnamed "global gas provider" for:
   - Specific Bloom deployments confirmed
   - Contract values if disclosed
   - Any public statements about Bloom as a supplier

SECTION C: REVENUE QUALITY METRICS

1. **Product vs. service revenue split**: Research the quarterly trend over the last 8 quarters. Is the service revenue percentage growing as the installed base scales?
2. **Recurring revenue percentage**: What percentage of total revenue is contractually recurring (service contracts) vs. non-recurring (product sales)?

4. **Geographic revenue concentration**: What percentage of revenue is domestic vs. international? How much is Korea-dependent (through SK ecoplant)?

SECTION D: BOOK-TO-BILL & BACKLOG TREND ANALYSIS

**Base case**: 65% of $20B backlog converts; some Oracle/project-finance dependent orders fall through; run-rate of ~$1.6B from existing backlog
**Bear case**: 45% of $20B backlog converts; multiple hyperscaler project cancellations; Oracle concentration fully haircut; run-rate of ~$1.1B from existing backlog

For each, calculate the implied valuation range using a 3-5x EV/Revenue multiple (infrastructure precedent range).

NOTABLE ANOMALIES: Flag any instances where (a) backlog figures don't reconcile with reported revenue recognition patterns, (b) management language about backlog quality has shifted between earnings calls, (c) specific contracts were cited as backlog additions but no corresponding revenue has appeared, (d) the $6B Q4 2025 backlog addition seems concentrated in a single customer or contract type. Pay particular attention to whether the Oracle warrant issuance corresponds to a specific Oracle backlog commitment — if the warrant was given in exchange for a volume commitment that is now being scaled back, the commercial substance of the warrant relationship has eroded even as its financial value persists. This would be a material finding.

## AGENT 4: OBBBA/ITC REGULATORY DYNAMICS, FEOC COMPLIANCE & POLITICAL TIMING RISK

**JUSTIFICATION:** The OBBBA's new 30% ITC for fuel cells is arguably the most important external valuation driver for Bloom — it cuts the installed cost of every system by 30% while simultaneously removing subsidies from competing wind/solar technologies. But FEOC compliance risk could void this advantage entirely, and the 2028 midterm election cycle creates a political "sell-by date" on the legislative moat. This lens is essential for deal timing analysis.

**PRIORITY:** High

```
You are a regulatory and tax policy specialist who advises energy companies and their acquirers on investment tax credit (ITC) structures, FEOC (Foreign Entity of Concern) compliance, and the interaction between tax policy and M&A valuations. You have deep expertise in the Inflation Reduction Act, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), and Section 48/48E clean energy tax credits. You are preparing a regulatory risk assessment for a potential acquirer of Bloom Energy Corporation.

CRITICAL CONTEXT:
- The OBBBA (signed July 4, 2025) provides a 30% ITC for "fuel cell property" under Section 48E beginning construction after December 31, 2025, regardless of emissions levels
- The OBBBA simultaneously reduced or eliminated ITC eligibility for wind and solar projects
- FEOC rules effective January 1, 2026 prohibit ITC eligibility for components sourced from "covered nations" (primarily China)
- Bloom Energy manufactures solid-oxide fuel cells using ceramic electrolyte materials, rare-earth elements, and specialty metal interconnects
- Bloom's primary manufacturing facility is in Newark, Delaware (200,000 SF)
- Bloom has international operations in 9 countries, including South Korea (SK ecoplant partnership)
- 46% of publicly announced data center builds are in EPA non-attainment zones, creating air permitting risk for gas-fed generation
- Bloom's fuel cells run primarily on natural gas, with hydrogen and biogas capability

YOUR RESEARCH TASK:

SECTION A: OBBBA TAX CREDIT ANALYSIS — BLOOM-SPECIFIC

1. Research the EXACT statutory text of the OBBBA's Section 48E provisions as they apply to fuel cells:
   - What is the definition of "fuel cell property" under the new law?
   - Is there a capacity threshold (minimum kW) for eligibility?
   - Does the 30% rate apply to both the fuel cell hardware AND installation/balance-of-plant costs?
   - Are 20-year service contracts eligible for any separate tax treatment?
   - Is the credit transferable (Section 6418) — can Bloom or its customers sell the credit to a tax equity investor?

2. Calculate the financial impact of the 30% ITC on Bloom's unit economics:
   - Bloom Energy Servers reportedly cost $7,000-10,000 per kW installed
   - For a 10 MW installation (~$70-100M), the 30% ITC = $21-30M credit
   - How does this change the payback period for a data center customer?
   - Show the comparison: Bloom with 30% ITC vs. comparable diesel generator, gas turbine, and grid-connected solar + battery

3. Research how the OBBBA affected the competitive landscape:
   - Which specific clean energy technologies LOST their ITC or PTC eligibility under OBBBA?
   - Which competitors are disadvantaged? (Specifically: solar developers, wind developers, battery storage manufacturers)
   - Has any sell-side analyst quantified the competitive advantage the OBBBA creates for Bloom specifically?

SECTION B: FEOC COMPLIANCE — THE EXISTENTIAL DILIGENCE QUESTION

1. Research Bloom Energy's supply chain for FEOC exposure:
   - Solid-oxide fuel cells require yttria-stabilized zirconia (YSZ) ceramic electrolytes, lanthanum strontium manganite (LSM) cathodes, and nickel-based cermet anodes
   - Where are these ceramic and rare-earth materials primarily sourced globally? What percentage of global supply comes from China?
   - Has Bloom disclosed its supply chain compliance status for FEOC purposes in any SEC filing, earnings call, or investor presentation?
   - Search specifically for: Bloom Energy 10-K supply chain disclosures, risk factor language about "foreign entity of concern," FEOC, or "covered nation" sourcing

2. Research the FEOC compliance requirements:
   - What is the exact standard for FEOC compliance under the current Treasury/IRS guidance?
   - Is it a component-level test (any single component from a covered nation voids eligibility) or a percentage-of-value test?
   - What documentation does a manufacturer need to maintain?
   - Is there a self-certification process or does the IRS audit compliance?

3. Assess the risk:
   - If Bloom's ceramic stack components are sourced from FEOC-covered entities, what is the business impact? (30% ITC voided = effective 30% cost increase for customers = potential order cancellations or renegotiations)
   - Are there non-Chinese sources for YSZ ceramics, LSM, and nickel cermet at commercial scale?
   - How long would it take Bloom to re-source its supply chain if FEOC non-compliance is discovered?
   - Has any competitor (FuelCell Energy, Plug Power, GE Vernova) publicly confirmed FEOC compliance?

SECTION C: AIR PERMITTING RISK

1. Bloom's fuel cells run primarily on natural gas, producing NOx emissions (though significantly less than diesel gensets or gas turbines):
   - Research EPA non-attainment zone regulations as they apply to stationary fuel cell installations
   - Are solid-oxide fuel cells classified differently from combustion sources under the Clean Air Act?
   - The 46% of data center builds in non-attainment zones statistic — verify this and assess: does Bloom's lower-emission profile give it a permitting advantage in these zones?
   - Research any specific instances where Bloom installations have faced air permitting delays or denials

2. Research state-level permitting:
   - In Bloom's top 5 deployment states (likely California, Texas, Wyoming, Virginia, Ohio), what are the air permitting requirements for a 50+ MW fuel cell installation?
   - Are there states where permitting is a significant timeline risk (6+ months)?

SECTION D: POLITICAL CYCLE & LEGISLATIVE DURABILITY

1. The OBBBA was signed July 4, 2025. Research:
   - Can the 30% fuel cell ITC be reversed or modified by a future Congress?
   - What is the "beginning of construction" safe harbor — if a project starts construction before any legislative change, does it lock in the credit?
   - Historical precedent: has a clean energy tax credit ever been retroactively revoked?

2. 2028 midterm election risk assessment:
   - If the political landscape shifts in 2028, what is the probability and mechanism for OBBBA fuel cell provisions to be modified?
   - How much of Bloom's backlog could be deployed before end of 2028, locking in credits under safe harbor provisions?
   - Is there a scenario where a new administration or Congress eliminates the fuel cell ITC — and what is the timeline risk?

3. M&A timing implication:
   - Make the explicit case: the OBBBA creates a 2-3 year legislative moat that could close in 2028. An acquirer who buys Bloom in 2026-2027 and deploys aggressively captures full subsidy benefit. Quantify: how much incremental value does the ITC create over a 3-year deployment window?

DELIVERABLE FORMAT:
- ITC financial impact model (per-unit and fleet-wide)
- FEOC compliance risk matrix (component-by-component, sourcing country, alternative suppliers)
- Air permitting risk map by state
- Political timeline and legislative durability assessment
