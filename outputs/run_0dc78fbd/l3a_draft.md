## I. THE OPPORTUNITY

Bloom Energy's reported $20B backlog breaks down as $6B product and $14B service. What the market has not priced: the AEP Wyoming $2.65B order — the largest fuel cell procurement in history — is confirmed product-only [Source: AEP 8-K, January 2026; BMO Capital Markets]. The associated 20-year service contract on 900 MW is separately negotiated, undisclosed, and absent from the $14B figure. At the SK ecoplant contractual benchmark of $300K/MW/year [Source: Bloom 8-K, October 2021], this hidden service stream carries ~$2.9B in NPV at 7% — roughly 6.5% of Bloom's ~$45B enterprise value, entirely unrecognized.

This matters because the identity of the buyer determines which valuation framework applies. An infrastructure fund (Brookfield, which already has a $5B partnership and manages $550B in critical assets) values 10-20 year contracted service streams at 6-8% discount rates. Under that lens, the service annuity alone — including contracted backlog plus prospective AEP-type streams — is worth $8-11B, or 20-25% of current enterprise value. The hardware business carries the remainder at ~10x its own forward revenue — rich, but defensible for a 58% grower with the world's only commercial-scale solid oxide fuel cell manufacturing capability. Meanwhile, Korea's 400 MW installed base (deployed 2018-2022) enters mandatory stack replacement now, providing the first large-scale commercial proof point for the annuity thesis. That event reprices the entire company.

## II. COMPANY SITUATION

Bloom Energy is the world's sole commercial-scale manufacturer of solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) power systems — modular 250 kW "Energy Servers" that convert natural gas into electricity at ~55% efficiency through electrochemistry, not combustion, deployable on a concrete pad behind a customer's meter. The company reported $2.02B in FY2025 revenue (+37.3% YoY), decomposing as ~$1.5B product (74%), ~$257M installation (13%), ~$229M service (11%), and ~$40M legacy PPA electricity (2%) [Source: Bloom FY2025 10-K]. Service revenue is contracted at 10-20 year terms with 100% attachment on every hardware sale. Production runs from two US facilities — Fremont, CA (multi-GW capacity, ~2,000 employees, $200M expansion) and Newark, DE (~715 employees) — plus a Korean JV facility in Gumi consolidated as a VIE. The installed base is ~1.5 GW across 1,200+ sites in 9 countries. Bloom has guided FY2026 revenue of $3.1-3.3B (midpoint +58% YoY), underpinned by a capacity doubling from ~1 GW to 2 GW by year-end 2026 [Source: Q4 2025 earnings call, February 5, 2026].

Geographic mix shifted materially: US revenue surged from ~54% (FY2024) to ~65% (FY2025) as AI data center demand materialized domestically, while Korea declined to ~32%. Customer concentration remains acute — SK ecoplant represented ~23% of FY2024 revenue and ~55% of Q3 2025 quarterly revenue ($285M of $519M), though the 2026 guide implies Korea drops to ~11% of the revenue base even if SK ecoplant fully performs its contracted volumes [Source: Bloom 10-K; Q3 2025 earnings].

The capital structure is deliberately complex and deal-relevant. Bloom carries **$2.5B in zero-coupon convertible senior notes due 2030** (conversion price ~$195/share, 52.5% premium at issuance) — the largest 0% US convert above $2B in history — and **$518M in 3.0% green convertible notes due 2029** ($20.84 conversion price, deeply in-the-money). Cash at year-end was ~$2.45B, substantially funded by the convert proceeds. The 2030 notes bear zero cash interest; an acquirer faces a binary choice at closing: retire at $2.5B par (put option) or allow conversion with make-whole premium that generates $1.5-1.9B in additional equity dilution. The 2029 notes would almost certainly convert to ~24.9M shares in any transaction [Source: Bloom 8-K, November 2025 indenture].

Ownership is dispersed — BlackRock 9.4% (passive), SK ecoplant ~4.7% (actively selling; disposed 12.6M shares in July-August 2025 for ~$386M), D.E. Shaw 3.4%, KIA 1.38%. Founder-CEO KR Sridhar holds ~1.7% (~4.4M shares, ~$750M) and sold $34M at $170/share in February 2026 — his largest discretionary sale — while simultaneously deferring 300K vested PSUs to 2030 [Source: SEC Form 4; 8-K filings].

The last 12-18 months represent a coordinated strategic repositioning. Aaron Hoover — former Morgan Stanley Global Co-Head of Energy Investment Banking with $180B in completed M&A — was hired as Head of Corporate Development in August 2025. Fifteen days earlier, Jim Hagemann Snabe — Chairman of Siemens AG and board member of Temasek (~$400B AUM) — joined Bloom's board. This occurred while the company operated without a permanent CFO (a vacancy now exceeding 10 months), three months before issuing the $2.5B convertible. Marquee customer wins followed: AEP's 1 GW supply agreement, Brookfield's $5B AI factory partnership, Oracle's deployment agreement with an equity warrant (3.53M shares at $113.28/share) [Source: Bloom press releases; SEC filings].

The narrative-vs-reality gap that matters most: management's "100 MW in 90 days" deployment claim — the pillar of the AI data center thesis — is explicitly conditioned on "gas supply and permits being in place" [Source: Bloom marketing materials]. For greenfield sites without high-pressure gas infrastructure, pipeline interconnection adds 12-24 months. The $3.1-3.3B revenue guide beat Street consensus by 35-43%, but the non-GAAP operating income range of $125-475M — a $350M spread on a $3.2B revenue base — signals genuine execution uncertainty that the market has largely ignored. GAAP EPS was -$0.37 in FY2025 despite a $45B market cap; GAAP operating cash flow of $114M yields 0.25% on enterprise value. This is a company the market is valuing on 2029 cash flows while the 2026 P&L remains GAAP-negative [Source: Bloom FY2025 earnings release].

## III. KEY FINDINGS

**The AEP Wyoming Service Stream: ~$2.9B in NPV Exists in No Backlog Figure, No Analyst Model, and No Market Price**

AEP's January 2026 exercise of its option for a Wyoming data center fuel cell plant was disclosed as a $2.65B unconditional equipment purchase for ~900 MW — confirmed as product-only by both the 8-K filing and BMO Capital Markets, which noted the "disclosed revenue opportunity is below its base-case assumptions without an additional service contract" [Source: AEP 8-K, January 4, 2026; BMO analyst note]. The 900 MW figure is independently verified by BFC Power's (a Tallgrass Energy subsidiary) air permit application filed with the Wyoming DEQ in September 2025 [Source: Wyoming DEQ filing]. The end-user chain is Tallgrass/Crusoe Energy Systems, whose 1.2 GW Oracle/OpenAI campus in Abilene, TX is already operational. AEP separately executed a 20-year power purchase agreement with an unnamed "high investment grade" counterparty. Applying the SK ecoplant contractual benchmark of $300K/MW/year in service revenue (derived from the $3.0B service commitment over 20 years on 500 MW, per the October 2021 PDA) [Source: Bloom 8-K, October 2021], the AEP Wyoming 20-year service contract implies ~$5.3B face value, or ~$2.9B in NPV at a 7% discount rate.

**The Deal Implication:** An infrastructure buyer performing sum-of-parts analysis should add ~$2.9B to the stated $14B service backlog NPV — a 25-35% increase to the service annuity's contribution to enterprise value. When combined with prospective service streams from the remaining $6B product backlog (at the confirmed 2:1 lifetime service-to-product revenue ratio), the total service annuity approaches $20-21B face value, or $11-16B in NPV. This is the asset that makes Bloom an infrastructure story, not a hardware story.

**Key Uncertainty:** The AEP Wyoming service contract has not been publicly disclosed or executed. If hyperscaler customers negotiate self-maintenance rights or cost-plus pricing on service renewals, the $300K/MW benchmark overstates the achievable rate.

**The $2.5B Zero-Coupon Convertible Was Designed as an M&A Defense — and It Adds 12.5-13.3% to Any Acquisition Price**

At every offer price tested between $180 and $240/share, the total acquisition cost exceeds the headline equity price by a remarkably consistent 12.5-13.3% — a structural "convertible friction premium" that cannot be negotiated away [Source: Bloom 8-K, November 2025; indenture analysis]. At $195/share (the 2030 note conversion price, where make-whole friction peaks), total acquisition cost is $61.7B — an effective price of $219.40 per basic share versus a $195 headline. At $210/share, total cost is $66.6B, effective $236.90/share. The math: $2.5B in 2030 notes settled at par, ~$518M in deeply in-the-money 2029 notes converting to ~24.9M shares, plus ~10M dilutive RSU/option shares, offset by $2.45B in on-hand cash [Source: indenture terms; Bloom balance sheet]. A quality-of-earnings adjustment compresses FY2025 sustainable EBITDA from the reported $271.6M non-GAAP to ~$219M (a 19% haircut, primarily from $65-70M in recurring SBC that non-GAAP treatment excludes) [Source: Bloom FY2025 earnings; QofE reconstruction]. At $219M sustainable EBITDA and $60B+ total acquisition cost, implied leverage exceeds 270x — making any leveraged buyout structurally impossible. This is an all-equity or all-stock transaction only.

**The Deal Implication:** The convertible structure eliminates financial sponsors from the buyer universe and forces strategic acquirers to fund $57-76B with equity or cash on hand. Brookfield's BAIIF ($10B equity target) falls ~$16B short of a solo acquisition at $210/share even at aggressive 5x leverage. Only Microsoft ($2.5T+ market cap), Alphabet, or a multi-party consortium can execute at these levels without prohibitive dilution. The capital structure was assembled — $2.5B convert issued three months after hiring Morgan Stanley's former energy IB co-head — to maximize Bloom's leverage in any negotiation.

**Key Uncertainty:** In an all-stock deal, convertible dilution is passed to the acquirer's shareholders rather than paid as cash — reducing cash flow impact but not economic cost. The exact make-whole table in the November 2025 indenture (Exhibit 4.1) should be verified in diligence.

**SK Ecoplant's IPO Is Legally Blocked as of March 15 — But Bloom Holds an Unexercised $311M Financial Call That Changes the Negotiation Dynamic**

Korea's March 15, 2026 dual-listing regulations prohibit new listings by subsidiaries of large business groups, making SK ecoplant's planned KOSPI IPO legally impossible without a "future industry" exemption that has not been applied for [Source: Seoul Economic Daily, March 15, 2026]. SK ecoplant has a 233% debt ratio and owes ~KRW 1T ($725M) to pre-IPO financial investors facing a July 2026 redemption deadline [Source: KED Global; Korean media]. The company representing ~$640M in annual Bloom revenue and $4.5B in total committed value (product + service) is simultaneously exiting its equity position (reduced from ~10.5% to ~4.7% through $386M in share sales) while deepening commercial dependence [Source: SEC Schedule 13D/A]. The finding most analysis misses: Bloom's Q3 2023 10-Q discloses a Shareholders' Loan Agreement granting Bloom the right to draw up to $311M from SK ecoplant if SK ecoplant "reduced any portion of its holdings of Class A common stock" [Source: Bloom 10-Q, November 2023]. The August 2025 share sales appear to have triggered this right. No 8-K has disclosed either a draw or a waiver — meaning Bloom may hold a $311M financial call on a counterparty with a 233% debt ratio, creating mutual financial deterrence against PDA non-performance.

**The Deal Implication:** An acquirer should apply a probability-weighted EV haircut of ~$680M (central estimate within $620M-$1.1B range) to reflect the near-certain need for a PDA renegotiation in late 2026. However, this risk is structurally declining: at $3.2B 2026 guided revenue, Korean product revenue (~$350M/year) represents only ~11% of total — down from 32% in FY2025. Even zero Korean revenue would reduce 2026 guidance to ~$2.85B, still 41% growth, with blended margins improving as Korean product revenue (29% gross margin) is replaced by US volumes (37% gross margin). The SK ecoplant risk is real but self-hedging.

**Key Uncertainty:** The 2023 PDA Extension's operative language — particularly whether "committed to purchase" preserved the take-or-pay obligation and whether change-of-control termination rights exist — has never been filed as a standalone SEC exhibit. This is a non-negotiable pre-LOI diligence condition.
