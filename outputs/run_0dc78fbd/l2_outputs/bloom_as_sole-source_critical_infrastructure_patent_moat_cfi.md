## SECTION 3: CFIUS ANALYSIS — DEFINITIVE FRAMEWORK

### 3.1 Statutory and Regulatory Evolution

Executive Order 14083 identified clean energy technology, microelectronics, semiconductors, quantum technologies, biotechnology, and AI as particularly sensitive to national security, directing CFIUS to consider each covered transaction's effect on supply chain resilience. FIRRMA (2018) expanded CFIUS authority to include non-controlling investments in TID US businesses — those involved in critical technologies, sensitive personal data, or critical infrastructure.

In 2023, 21% of CFIUS filings led to binding mitigation agreements (up from 11% in 2021); over 200 agreements are under active supervision; and four civil monetary penalties were issued — more than all prior years combined. CFIUS has explicitly expanded scrutiny to transactions involving even US allies in sensitive sectors.

### 3.2 The Borqs/HHE Energy Storage Precedent (Most Directly Applicable)

CFIUS required Borqs (China-based) to divest its 51% stake in Holu Hou Energy LLC — a US-based producer of energy storage systems for *residential multi-dwelling units* — because CFIUS determined that Chinese government access to basic residential energy storage technology constituted a "critical technology." If residential energy storage is critical technology, the world's sole commercial-scale SOFC manufacturer providing firm power to AI data centers classified as critical national infrastructure is, by logical extension, a higher-order critical technology. A foreign acquisition of Bloom would almost certainly trigger mandatory CFIUS notification and the highest level of scrutiny.

### 3.3 Nippon Steel/US Steel — The Allied-Nation Mitigation Template

President Biden blocked Nippon Steel/US Steel on January 3, 2025. President Trump subsequently permitted the acquisition to close on June 18, 2025, conditioned on a National Security Agreement requiring: domestic production commitments, trade-related commitments, governance provisions, and a US government "golden share" with veto authority over corporate decisions and the right to appoint one of three board members.

This establishes the minimum mitigation template for any allied-nation acquirer of a US critical infrastructure company. For Bloom Energy — a *technology* company rather than a *commodity* company like US Steel, with active federal investment relationships — the mitigation requirements would exceed the Nippon Steel NSA, likely requiring:
- US-majority board of directors
- No transfer of core SOFC IP (stack chemistry, manufacturing processes) outside the US
- Fremont manufacturing facility must remain US-based and US-staffed
- DOE/DOD right of first refusal on technology in event of distress
- Possibly a US government golden share in the SOFC manufacturing subsidiary

### 3.4 Federal Investment Nexus

Bloom received up to $75 million in tax credits under the DOE/Treasury/IRS Qualifying Advanced Energy Project 48C initiative — selected for expanding domestic fuel cell and electrolyzer manufacturing at Fremont, California. This active federal investment relationship means any CFIUS review will assess whether a foreign acquisition would impair the government's investment and the national manufacturing interest it was designed to serve. This federal nexus is a *presumptive* CFIUS critical technology classification trigger.

### 3.5 The AI Data Center Critical Infrastructure Reclassification


The unprecedented power demand from AI has collided with multi-year grid interconnection delays, making traditional power procurement unviable. Fuel cells, particularly Bloom Energy's SOFCs, have transitioned from a "nice-to-have" green alternative to a "must-have" enabling technology for rapid deployment.



By 2030, 38% of facilities are expected to incorporate primary onsite generation, up from 13% a year ago; 27% of facilities expect being fully powered by onsite generation by 2030, up from just 1% a year ago.


As AI data centers become the operational backbone of the US economy and AI leadership becomes a national security imperative, the company that provides *the only available commercial-scale firm power solution* for those data centers takes on a national security classification that CFIUS must account for in any foreign acquisition review.

### 5.3 The Market Bifurcation — Bloom vs. GE Vernova Territory

GE Vernova and Bloom are serving *different segments* of the same market today — GE Vernova at 200MW+ co-located scale (2–5 year deployment), Bloom at 5–100MW behind-the-meter scale (90 days). GE Vernova's SOFC program, when commercialized in 5–8 years, would directly threaten Bloom's segment. Until then, they are complementary rather than directly competitive.
