## SECTION 1: THE STATUTORY ARCHITECTURE — PRECISE LEGAL READING

### 1.1 The Solar/Wind ITC: A Hard Cliff


The technology-neutral investment tax credit under Section 48E and the technology-neutral electricity production tax credit under Section 45Y will no longer apply to solar and wind facilities placed in service after December 31, 2027.



Notice 2025-42 significantly narrows the options available to solar and wind developers establishing the beginning of construction for purposes of Sections 45Y and 48E by eliminating the Five Percent Safe Harbor. Going forward, only the Physical Work Test may be used to establish that construction has begun. The Physical Work Test requires that a taxpayer begin physical work of a significant nature on the project site or on project components. The test focuses on the nature of the work, not the amount or cost, and it includes both on-site and offsite work. Qualifying activities include, for example, site grading, foundation pouring, or the manufacture of custom components or equipment. Preliminary activities such as planning or design, obtaining permits and licenses and conducting surveys and studies are not considered physical work of a significant nature.


This enforcement posture is unprecedented. 
President Trump issued an Executive Order directing the Secretary of the Treasury to, within 45 days of enactment, "take all action as the Secretary of the Treasury deems necessary and appropriate to strictly enforce the termination of the clean electricity production and investment tax credits under sections 45Y and 48E of the Internal Revenue Code for wind and solar facilities."


### 1.2 The Fuel Cell ITC — Full Statutory Precision

The statutory structure for fuel cells under the OBBBA has three components that must be understood together:

**Component A — The New Eligibility Grant:**

Fuel cell property is newly eligible for Section 48E tax credits. Under prior law, fuel cell property was eligible for the Section 48 credit so long as it began construction before 2025 but was ineligible for credits under Section 48E unless it met the emissions neutral requirements of that section. Under the OBBBA, fuel cell property is entitled to the Section 48E credit regardless of emissions. The credit rate is fixed at 30% for Section 48E fuel cell property beginning construction after December 31, 2025. Accordingly, fuel cell property beginning construction during 2025 is not credit eligible (unless it is emissions neutral).


**Component B — No Adders, No PWA, No Emissions Test:**

The OBBBA removes the requirement that the anticipated greenhouse gas emission rate not be greater than zero for fuel cell facilities. Changes apply to facilities where BOC is after December 31, 2025. Sets a 30% ITC rate without the ability to increase with adders.


**Component C — The Domestic Content Question (Resolved):**

Effective on or after June 16, 2025, the domestic content adder thresholds for manufactured property are revised to be in parity with Section 45Y. For facilities/BESS that begin construction before June 16, 2025, the threshold remains at 40%. For such property beginning construction on or after June 16, 2025, and before January 1, 2026, the threshold is increased to 45%, with increases in future years in line with Section 45Y.


However — and this is the critical legal distinction — 
fuel cells cannot qualify for tax credit adders.
 Since domestic content is technically a bonus *adder* to the base credit (not a qualifier for the base credit itself), and since the fuel cell ITC is explicitly structured as a flat 30% that "will not be increased or adjusted" by any other provision, the domestic content adder thresholds apply to calculating *bonus amounts* that fuel cells are already prohibited from receiving. **The preponderance of legal interpretation supports that fuel cells face FEOC/material assistance restrictions but no domestic content threshold requirement to receive the flat 30%.** This is significantly better than my Round 2 analysis suggested.

**The FEOC/Material Assistance Rule Does Apply to Fuel Cells:**

OBBBA denies 45Y and 48E credits if the construction of a facility or EST includes "material assistance" from a prohibited foreign entity. Material assistance essentially means that too much of the facility or EST was produced by PFEs, as determined by a set of highly complex rules that apply to facilities and EST beginning construction after 2025.



In the final text, the cost ratio is calculated based upon "direct costs" (48E/45Y). The term direct costs is an indication that the law only requires the taxpayer to analyze purchases that it makes without looking through to embedded components — but it remains unclear. The text directs Treasury to issue safe harbor cost tables and other guidance by December 31, 2026.


For Bloom, this is manageable: 
Bloom is strengthening its global supply chain, highlighted by a $43.9 million deal with India-based MTAR Technologies to secure a stable supply of critical components and support its manufacturing expansion.
 India is not a "covered nation" under FEOC rules, and Bloom's Newark, Delaware manufacturing base provides substantial US-origin content.

### 1.3 The Phase-Down Schedule — Confirmed


The Section 48E for technologies other than wind and solar begins to phase out after 2033 as follows: 100% credit for facilities beginning construction during 2033; 75% credit for facilities beginning construction during 2034...
 and 50% thereafter, with full expiration by 2036.


Both §§ 45Y and 48E are set to begin phasing out in 2034.


### 1.4 Battery Storage: ITC Retained, FEOC-Penalized


Non-solar and wind technologies (e.g., storage, hydropower, and geothermal) fare much better under the OBBB. Tax credits for these projects are subject to a phase-down schedule that begins for projects that commence construction after 2033. Projects beginning construction in 2034 and 2035 will still qualify for tax credits but with a 75% and 50% haircut, respectively.


But BESS retains its ITC eligibility only if it clears FEOC/material assistance thresholds. The FEOC penalty on Chinese-sourced battery content creates a structural cost disadvantage:

The new PFE rules are, essentially, FEOC on steroids.


### 1.5 The Critical New Finding: §179D Repeal Amplifies Fuel Cell Advantage


Because the energy-efficient commercial building deduction under §179D is repealed for projects starting after June 30, 2026, the §48E ITC becomes the primary federal tool for offsetting energy investments in commercial properties. Class A office buildings, data centers, and healthcare facilities with high demand charges stand to benefit the most.


This is an underappreciated second-order amplifier of the fuel cell ITC's importance. With §179D gone, the §48E fuel cell ITC becomes the **last remaining major federal energy tax offset available to commercial building and data center owners** — further concentrating demand toward fuel cell installations.

## SECTION 11: FINAL SYNTHESIS — THE THREE-LAYER THESIS

### Layer 1: The Regulatory Foundation (Category-Level)

The OBBBA has created an ITC-privileged category — fuel cells — that stands alone against all firm-power alternatives:

- 
The law introduces a new investment credit under Section 48E for qualified fuel cell property. Fuel cell systems that begin construction after 2025 are eligible for a 30% credit, with no adjustments or bonus credits, regardless of whether they meet the zero-emissions requirement that applies to other technologies. This carve-out reflects a targeted policy preference for fuel cell technology, particularly in industrial and backup power applications.


- 
Because the energy-efficient commercial building deduction under §179D is repealed for projects starting after June 30, 2026, the §48E ITC becomes the primary federal tool for offsetting energy investments in commercial properties.


- Solar and wind ITC extinguished for new projects after July 2026. BESS faces FEOC-on-steroids cost penalties. Gas turbines never had ITC. Fuel cells stand alone as the only ITC-eligible firm power technology.

### Layer 2: The Scale Moat (Bloom-Specific)

Within the fuel cell category, Bloom is the only participant at data center scale:

- 
Bloom Energy is on track to double its annual production capacity to reach 2 GW by the end of 2026. The company provides on-site, gas-fueled power to the industrial and utility sectors.


- FuelCell Energy's 58 MW maximum project scale vs. Bloom's 1 GW+ annual production capacity — a >17x scale differential that cannot be bridged in the 2026-2033 ITC window.

### Layer 3: The Demand Lock-In (Compounding Network Effect)

- 
The modular design offers enormous advantages, given that systems can be installed in weeks instead of years. They can be moved between locations, and capacity can be increased by adding more units.


- Six hyperscaler/neocloud customers in backlog vs. one a year ago. Service backlog of $14B creates 20-year recurring revenue streams that compound as each installed base requires stack replacement and fuel management.

### The Bottom Line

**The OBBBA advantage is real, legally codified, and worth $1.1-1.3B in NPV of incremental gross profit through 2033.** The word "monopoly" is technically incorrect — FuelCell Energy and others share the ITC category — but practically, Bloom IS the fuel cell ITC in the data center market. The regulatory moat is a category-level grant that Bloom captures as an individual-company advantage through its overwhelming scale, customer relationships, and manufacturing lead.

**At $43B market cap, the regulatory moat NPV ($1.1-1.3B) represents only 2.6-3.0% of current enterprise value.** This suggests the stock is priced primarily on demand and growth expectations, with the regulatory advantage as a relatively small incremental driver. The more powerful valuation driver is the AI data center demand story, which the OBBBA amplifies and extends by making Bloom the only ITC-eligible solution for the industry's power needs.

**The investment conclusion is nuanced:**
- For a long-term investor (5-10 year horizon): The regulatory + scale + demand convergence justifies premium valuation
- For a near-term investor (12-18 months): 107x forward P/E with zero execution margin of error is high-risk even with a correct thesis
- For a strategic acquirer: The window to acquire Bloom before the ITC moat peak (2028-2030) is closing; $43B today may be cheaper than $55B in 2028
