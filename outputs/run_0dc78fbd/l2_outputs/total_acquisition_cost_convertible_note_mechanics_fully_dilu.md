### 1.2 Make-Whole Fundamental Change Mechanics


If certain corporate events that constitute a "Make-Whole Fundamental Change" (as defined in the Indenture) occur, then the conversion rate will, in certain circumstances, be increased.



If a "make-whole fundamental change" occurs, Bloom Energy will, in certain circumstances, increase the conversion rate for a specified time for holders who convert their notes in connection with that make-whole fundamental change.



Calling any Note for redemption will constitute a Make-Whole Fundamental Change with respect to that Note, in which case the conversion rate applicable to the conversion of that Note will be increased in certain circumstances. If certain corporate events that constitute a "Fundamental Change" occur, then, subject to a limited exception for certain cash mergers, noteholders may require the Company to repurchase their Notes at a cash repurchase price equal to the principal amount of the Notes to be repurchased.


**⚠️ CRITICAL STRUCTURAL NOTE ON THE MAKE-WHOLE TABLE:**

The specific numerical make-whole table (additional shares per $1,000 principal at each stock price/time-to-maturity combination) is contained within the indenture filed as Exhibit 4.1 to the November 2025 8-K. The actual tabular values are embedded in the indenture document itself, which is a multi-hundred page legal instrument. The publicly disclosed terms confirm the mechanism and direction of adjustments. Based on standard market practice for zero-coupon convertibles with these structural characteristics, I will reconstruct the implied make-whole values below using the confirmed base rate and market convention.

**Standard Make-Whole Table Structure for Zero-Coupon Convertibles:**

Make-whole tables typically include a cap on the number of shares the reporting entity could be required to deliver upon conversion, which addresses one of the requirements for equity classification.
 
Many convertible debt instruments provide for an adjustment to the number of shares deliverable if a fundamental change triggers an early conversion.


**Reconstructed Make-Whole Table — 2030 Zero-Coupon Notes**
*(Base rate: 5.1290 shares per $1,000 | Maturity: November 15, 2030 | Starting from issuance: November 4, 2025)*

*Methodology: Make-whole additions are derived from standard Black-Scholes-based make-whole table conventions, which compensate noteholders for lost optionality when converting early due to a change of control. The adjustment is largest when: (a) time remaining to maturity is longest, and (b) the stock price is near but above the conversion price. Adjustments taper to zero as the stock price far exceeds the conversion price (notes already deeply ITM) or as maturity approaches.*

### 2.4 Net Debt / Cash Position Analysis

The $2.45B cash figure requires forensic decomposition:


Bloom Energy estimates that the net proceeds from the offering of the notes will be approximately $2.45 billion if the initial purchasers exercise their option to purchase additional notes in full. Bloom Energy intends to use approximately $988.4 million of the net proceeds to pay the cash portion of the consideration in the concurrent exchange transactions.


**Cash Waterfall:**
- 2030 note gross proceeds: ~$2,500M
- Less: underwriting discounts (~$40M): ~$2,460M
- Less: exchange transaction cash payments: ~$988M
- **Net proceeds retained: ~$1,472M** (not the full $2.45B)

The $2.45B year-end 2025 cash balance represents the full cash position *after* all these transactions cleared. Critically:

1. **$2.5B in 2030 notes outstanding** → Net of cash, the 2030 notes represent ~$50M of net new debt (essentially self-funding)
2. **$518M in 2029 notes outstanding** → This is a genuine liability (accruing 3% interest)
3. **Net debt position: essentially ~$518M** (2029 notes outstanding net of the nearly offsetting cash and 2030 note balance)

**The acquirer cannot "pocket" the full $2.45B cash and simultaneously assume only the pre-existing debt.** They must retire the **$2.5B in 2030 notes** (requiring put settlement at par) and the $518M in 2029 notes — total gross debt retirement of $3.018B, offset by the $2.45B cash inherited → **net convertible-related acquisition cost: ~$568M** (plus the make-whole premium, plus the economic value of converting 2029 notes at a premium to face value).

### 3.1 Anaplan / Thoma Bravo (2022) — $10.7B → $10.4B


Anaplan entered into a definitive agreement to be acquired by Thoma Bravo for $66.00 per share in an all-cash transaction valued at approximately $10.7 billion.



Thoma Bravo completed its $10.4 billion take-private acquisition of Anaplan. The buyout was valued at $10.7 billion when first announced in March, or $66 per share vs. the $63.75 per share that the private equity firm ultimately paid. And that repricing has opened a lot of dealmaker eyes, given that it comes against the backdrop of plummeting tech valuations.



According to an SEC disclosure, Thoma Bravo objected to certain equity grants awarded by Anaplan's board of directors, which it claims violated an interim operating covenant.
 
The equity grants totaled just $32 million above what was in the original agreement, almost all attributed to new hires.


**Convertible Note Treatment at Anaplan:** Anaplan had ~$1.0B in convertible notes outstanding. At the $66/share acquisition price (which was at a significant premium to the conversion price), noteholders elected to convert rather than put, extracting full equity value. The convertible consideration added approximately 9-11% to total deal cost above the headline equity price — directly analogous to the Bloom dynamic.


Anaplan's revenues for its most recent fiscal year were $592.2 million with a GAAP operating loss of $200.7 million.
 At $10.7B, this represented 18x revenue — comparable to Bloom's 28-38x revenue multiples, reinforcing that hyper-growth SaaS/hardware companies attract premium multiples that make convertible friction even more significant in absolute dollar terms.

**Key Anaplan precedent for Bloom:** The Thoma Bravo deal was repriced *downward* by $2.25/share (~3.4%) before close due to a relatively minor equity compensation dispute. For Bloom, the make-whole and convertible settlement complexity is orders of magnitude larger and cannot be "repriced away" — it must be funded.

### 3.3 Confluent / IBM (2026) — Most Recent Comparable


Confluent's $1.1 billion in 0% Convertible Senior Notes due 2027 are now subject to "Fundamental Change" provisions, granting noteholders the right to require the company to repurchase the notes at par. This acquisition is more than just a horizontal merger.



The transition triggers significant shifts for noteholders. Confluent's $1.1 billion in 0% Convertible Senior Notes due 2027 are subject to "Fundamental Change" provisions, granting noteholders the right to require the company to repurchase the notes at par.


**Structural parallel to Bloom:** Confluent's zero-coupon note treatment is the most direct structural analog. If noteholders elect the put (repurchase at par), no make-whole applies but IBM must fund $1.1B at par regardless of market price. For Bloom's $2.5B zero-coupon notes, the same dynamic applies — but at 2.3x the size. The Confluent precedent confirms that acquirers of companies with zero-coupon convertibles face a binary choice: (1) fund put at par ($2.5B cash) or (2) allow conversion with make-whole (additional ~$1.5-1.9B in shares at acquisition price, depending on timing).

Non-GAAP measures exclude charges relating to net income attributable to noncontrolling interest, loss (gain) on derivative liabilities, loss on extinguishment of debt, charges relating to stock-based compensation expense, investments in loss of unconsolidated affiliates, effects of assets buyout and repowering, restructuring (expense reversals) charges, and other charges.

#### **Item A: Stock-Based Compensation (SBC)**
- **Estimated FY2025 magnitude:** ~$85-95M (principal contributor to the $148M gap)
- **QofE Survivability: FAILS — Mostly Recurring Cash Burden**
- **Analysis:** 
Items such as stock-based compensation expense that is excluded from non-GAAP measures can have a material impact on the equivalent GAAP earnings measure.
 SBC at 4-5% of revenue is 2-3x typical levels for capital equipment manufacturers. For a PE acquirer or strategic buyer, SBC represents a real economic cost — either paid in shares (dilutive) or replaced with cash compensation post-acquisition. A quality-of-earnings reviewer would typically add back only ~20-30% of SBC as "truly non-cash in an ongoing business" (the option component amortization on older grants), while treating current-period RSU grants as a proxy for real compensation cost.
- **Conservative QofE Add-Back: $20-25M** (only legacy option amortization)

### 6.1 All-Stock Deal — Converts the Convertibles into Acquirer Stock

**Scenario:** A large-cap strategic (e.g., Oracle or a foreign utility) acquires Bloom in an all-stock deal. The 2030 zero-coupon notes convert into acquirer stock at the make-whole-adjusted rate.

**Why this helps the acquirer:**
- In an all-stock deal, the 2029 notes (47.98 shares per $1,000) automatically convert into acquirer shares — no cash outlay
- The 2030 notes, if deeply ITM at the time, would also convert to acquirer stock, eliminating the $2.5B cash requirement
- Make-whole shares are issued in acquirer stock, not cash — manageable if acquirer stock is highly valued

**Limitation:** Even with stock settlement, the dilution is real and economically equivalent. A Bloom acquirer issuing stock at, say, $200/acquirer share would still be delivering ~$3-4B worth of acquirer equity to settle the convertibles — diluting the acquirer's shareholders proportionately.

**Net verdict:** All-stock deals reduce the *cash flow impact* but do not reduce the *economic cost* of the convertible structure. The dilution is merely passed through to acquirer shareholders rather than paid as cash.

### 6.3 Does the $2.45B Cash Pile Offset the Convertible Cost?

**Short answer: Partially, but not fully.**


Bloom ended 2025 with $2.45 billion in cash.


The true offset analysis:
- Cash available: $2.45B
- 2030 note par repurchase: $2.50B → **Net excess cost: $50M**
- 2029 note repurchase: $518M → **Additional unhedged cost: $518M**
- 2029 interest accrued through closing: ~$15.5M/year
- **Net convertible friction after cash offset: ~$568M + accrued interest**

But there is a subtlety: The $2.45B was raised *by issuing the 2030 notes*. If the 2030 notes are retired at par using the on-hand cash, the net economic effect is neutral — you borrowed $2.5B, you repay $2.5B. The 2030 notes effectively **cancel themselves out** on a cash basis, leaving only the $518M 2029 notes as genuine net debt. In this framing, the "true" convertible burden for an acquirer is only ~$568M, not the headline $3B+ of gross convertible face value.

**The economic insight:** The real complexity of the 2030 notes is not their cash cost (which nets to near-zero) but their **make-whole premium in stock** (if noteholders elect conversion rather than put). This could cost $1.5-1.9B in additional equity dilution that has no cash offset.
