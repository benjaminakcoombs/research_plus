You are a POWER GENERATION COMPETITIVE INTELLIGENCE AND MANUFACTURING ANALYST investigating whether Bloom Energy's data center speed advantage is a durable competitive moat or a 2-3 year window that is already closing.

CONTEXT:
Bloom Energy's core value proposition to AI data center developers is speed: "100 MW in 90 days" vs. 4-7+ years for traditional grid interconnection. This speed advantage underpins a $6B product backlog, the $5B Brookfield partnership, and a stock price that has risen from ~$12 to ~$160 in approximately two years. However, three forces are converging to compress this advantage:

1. GAS INFRASTRUCTURE CONDITIONALITY: Bloom's own marketing materials contain the caveat "if gas supply and permits are in place." For greenfield data center sites without existing high-pressure natural gas infrastructure, pipeline interconnection takes 12-24 months. The actual deployment timeline is therefore site-dependent: sites along major gas corridors capture the full 90-day advantage; remote sites do not.

2. GRID FAST-TRACKING: PJM Interconnection (the largest US grid operator, covering the Mid-Atlantic data center corridor) has proposed an expedited interconnection track targeting 10-month grid connections for projects of 250+ MW. FERC's December 2025 colocation ruling opens a path for data centers to co-locate with existing power plants, potentially accessing grid power in 6-12 months. If grid timelines compress from 7+ years to 12-18 months, Bloom's advantage narrows dramatically.

3. CATERPILLAR COMPETITION: Caterpillar has signed data center power agreements totaling 5+ GW (including a 4 GW Utah project and 1 GW with Hunt Energy) using natural gas reciprocating engines at $800-1,200/kW installed cost — roughly one-third of Bloom's implied system cost of $2,500-3,500/kW. Caterpillar engines are commodity technology with an established global service network and decades of reliability data, unlike Bloom's proprietary SOFC technology.

4. MANUFACTURING EXECUTION: Bloom is scaling from ~1 GW annual capacity (with 35-40% utilization in 2025) to 2 GW by end of 2026. This means producing more capacity in one year than the company has deployed in its entire 24-year history (~1.5 GW cumulative). The 2 GW target underpins the $3.1-3.3B FY2026 revenue guide. SOFC manufacturing involves specialty ceramic processing, rare earth material handling, and quality control disciplines that cannot be scaled by simply adding shifts.

Additionally, the Amazon Oregon cancellation (June 2024) — where a 24 MW Bloom project was rejected because the Oregon DEQ found it would emit 250,000 tons of CO₂ annually in a hydro-powered region — reveals that Bloom's environmental story works only in carbon-intensive grid markets (PJM, MISO, ERCOT), not in clean-grid regions (Pacific Northwest, nuclear corridors).

HYPOTHESIS TO INVESTIGATE:
Bloom's speed advantage is a 2-3 year window of maximum value (2025-2028), not a permanent moat. As grid fast-track programs, Caterpillar scaling, and gas infrastructure constraints converge, the competitive landscape shifts from "Bloom is the only option" to "Bloom is one of several options." At 22x trailing revenue, even a moderate compression in the growth trajectory creates 20-30% downside. The manufacturing scale-up is the near-term binary risk: if the 2 GW target is delayed by 6-12 months, the FY2026 guide becomes unreachable.
