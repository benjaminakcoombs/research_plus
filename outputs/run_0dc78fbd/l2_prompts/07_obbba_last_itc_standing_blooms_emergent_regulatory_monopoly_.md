CONTEXT:
The OBBBA, signed into law in July 2025, made dramatic changes to the US clean energy tax credit landscape:

1. SOLAR AND WIND ITC/PTC: Terminated for projects placed in service after December 31, 2027, unless construction began by July 4, 2026. This effectively ends the ITC/PTC regime that has driven renewable energy deployment for two decades.

2. FUEL CELL ITC: Granted a fixed 30% Investment Tax Credit under §48E through at least 2033, exempt from greenhouse gas emissions tests and prevailing wage requirements. This is an extraordinary carve-out — fuel cells receive a higher, more durable credit than any other energy technology.

3. BATTERY STORAGE FEOC RULES: Battery energy storage systems (BESS) face Foreign Entity of Concern (FEOC) content requirements: 55% of battery components must be non-FEOC sourced (rising to 75% by 2030). Since the vast majority of global BESS manufacturing is in China (a "covered nation"), compliant BESS costs approximately 43% more than non-compliant alternatives. This creates a de facto tariff on the primary competitor to fuel cells for on-site firm power.

4. GAS TURBINES AND RECIPROCATING ENGINES: Have never qualified for ITC under any regime. No change under OBBBA.

The net effect: For a C&I customer or data center developer seeking on-site, 24/7 firm power generation with ITC benefits, Bloom's solid oxide fuel cells are now the ONLY qualifying technology. Solar loses ITC after 2027. Wind loses ITC after 2027. Battery storage faces FEOC cost penalties. Gas turbines and reciprocating engines (including Caterpillar) never had ITC. Bloom stands alone.

This "last ITC standing" status should theoretically translate into pricing power: Bloom can capture a larger share of the 30% ITC value in its own margins rather than passing it through to customers as a discount. Historical precedent exists — when solar companies had exclusive ITC access in the early 2010s, leading manufacturers (First Solar, SunPower) achieved gross margins of 25-35%, well above their post-ITC-democratization margins of 15-20%.

HYPOTHESIS TO INVESTIGATE:
The OBBBA has created a structural regulatory advantage for Bloom that most equity research and sector analysis has not fully modeled. This advantage is worth 3-5 percentage points of gross margin improvement over the 2026-2033 ITC window, translating to $90-165M in incremental annual gross profit at $3B revenue. Additionally, the elimination of competing ITC-eligible technologies expands Bloom's effective TAM — projects that would have been solar+storage or BESS now favor fuel cells to access ITC benefits. This regulatory alpha is the most underappreciated valuation driver in the Bloom thesis.
