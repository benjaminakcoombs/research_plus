# DELIVERABLE 1: COMPANY SITUATION MAP

## BLOOM ENERGY CORPORATION (NYSE: BE) — STRATEGIC SITUATION ASSESSMENT

---

### WHAT BLOOM ENERGY IS

Bloom Energy is the world's only commercial-scale manufacturer of solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) power systems. The company designs, manufactures, sells, and services stationary fuel cell "Energy Servers" that convert natural gas (or hydrogen/biogas) into electricity through an electrochemical process — not combustion — at ~54% electrical efficiency, near-zero NOx/SOx, and roughly half the CO₂ of the average US grid. The core product is modular: a single 250 kW "building block" can be stacked to multi-hundred-MW configurations deployed on a concrete pad behind a customer's meter, bypassing the utility grid entirely.

**Physical Footprint & Scale:**
- **Manufacturing:** Two US facilities — Fremont, CA (multi-GW capacity, 164,000 sq ft, $200M expansion, ~2,000 CA employees) and Newark, DE (~715 employees). One Korean JV facility in Gumi (Bloom SK Fuel Cell LLC, consolidated as a VIE), capable of "full assembly" but dependent on Bloom-approved components and Fremont-manufactured fuel cell stacks.
- **Installed Base:** ~1.5 GW deployed across 1,200+ sites in 9 countries. South Korea alone: ~600 MW.
- **Production Capacity:** ~1 GW/year at end-2025; targeting 2 GW/year by end-2026 — a doubling that would exceed lifetime cumulative deployments in a single year. This is the single largest execution bet in the company's history.
- **Headcount:** ~2,700+ (estimated from disclosed CA and DE figures plus international JV).
- **IP:** 1,000+ patents covering SOFC stack chemistry, manufacturing processes, system integration, and electrolyzer technology.

**Revenue Composition (FY2025 Actual: $2.02B, +37.3% YoY):**

| Segment | FY2025 Est. | % | Quality |
|---------|------------|---|---------|
| Product (hardware) | ~$1,499M | 74% | Point-of-acceptance; lumpy |
| Installation | ~$257M | 13% | Project-based; one-time |
| Service/Maintenance | ~$229M | 11% | Contracted 10-20 yr; recurring ★★★★★ |
| Electricity (PPA) | ~$40M | 2% | Recurring but shrinking legacy |

**Geographic Mix (FY2025 Est.):** US ~65%, South Korea ~32%, ROW ~3%. The US share surged from ~54% in FY2024 as AI data center demand materialized domestically.

**Customer Concentration (FY2024 confirmed):** Top 3 customers = 53% of revenue (SK ecoplant ~23%, two others at 16% and 14%). In Q3 2025, SK ecoplant-related revenue reached ~55% of quarterly revenue — an extreme single-quarter concentration driven by deployment timing.

### HOW IT MAKES MONEY AND HOW THAT'S CHANGING

**The Business is Transforming Along Three Vectors Simultaneously:**

**1. Hardware Growth (Currently Dominant):** Product revenue grew ~39% in FY2025 to ~$1.5B, driven by AI data center demand. The 2026 guide of $3.1-3.3B (up ~58% at midpoint) is almost entirely hardware-driven. The backlog of $6B in product orders provides 2+ years of revenue visibility. But this is point-of-acceptance revenue — lumpy, non-recurring, and subject to project timing. Gross margins on product: ~31-32%.

**2. The Hidden Service Annuity (Emerging, Vastly Underappreciated):** Every hardware sale generates a 10-20 year service contract. The $14B service backlog ($20B total minus $6B product) is the shadow asset. Current service revenue of ~$229M/year is only ~$157K/MW on a 1.5 GW installed base — early-stage and pre-repowering. As the 2018-2022 deployment cohort (~500-700 MW) enters its 5-7 year mandatory stack replacement cycle in 2025-2028, service revenue should step-change to $300-500K/MW/year. NPV of the contracted service stream from current installed base + backlog: ~$5-10B (7% infrastructure discount rate). Service margins have flipped from negative to ~17-20% non-GAAP in 6 quarters and are headed toward 25%+.

**3. The Repowering Business (Physics-Driven, Not Yet Visible):** SOFC stacks degrade over 5-7 years and require mandatory replacement. This is the energy equivalent of GE Aerospace's engine overhaul business — recurring, proprietary, and impossible to source from a third party given Bloom's 1,000+ patent moat. The Korean installed base (~600 MW, deployed 2018-2022) is entering this window now. This revenue stream does not yet appear as a separate line item.

**Margin Trajectory:** GAAP gross margin: 5-8% (2021) → 29.0% (2025). Non-GAAP operating income: negative → $221M (2025). But the GAAP-to-non-GAAP gap is large ($148M in FY2025, primarily SBC at ~$80-100M/year or ~4-5% of revenue) and growing. GAAP operating cash flow was $114M in FY2025 vs. $45B+ market cap — a 0.25% yield. The company is not yet generating cash flows that justify its valuation on any conventional basis; the market is pricing 2029-2032 cash flows.

### WHAT THE ENVIRONMENT IS DOING

**Tailwinds — Powerful and Converging:**
- **AI Data Center Power Crunch:** US data center power demand projected to grow from ~55 GW (2025) to 122 GW (2030) globally. Grid interconnection timelines in PJM stretch 7+ years. Bloom's "100 MW in 90 days" value proposition (conditional on gas supply) is the fastest path to power for grid-constrained hyperscalers.
- **Gas Turbine Sold Out:** GE Vernova's gas turbines are sold out through 2028 with <10% availability in 2029. Reciprocating engines take 12-24 months. Bloom is the only scaled option that can deliver in <6 months.
- **ITC Codified:** The OBBBA (July 2025) gives fuel cells a fixed 30% ITC through 2033, exempt from GHG emissions tests and prevailing wage requirements — *more favorable* treatment than under the original IRA. Solar/wind ITC terminates after 2027 for new projects.
- **FEOC Clean:** South Korea is not a "covered nation" under FEOC rules. Bloom's US manufacturing gives it a structural advantage over Chinese-dependent battery storage competitors.

**Headwinds — Real but Underappreciated:**
- **The 90-Day Claim is Conditional:** Equipment delivery is 90 days; *gas pipeline interconnection* can take 12-24 months. In markets without existing high-pressure gas infrastructure, Bloom's speed advantage compresses dramatically.
- **Caterpillar is Scaling Fast:** Caterpillar's 4 GW Utah data center deal and 1 GW Hunt Energy agreement signal a well-capitalized combustion competitor entering Bloom's market at scale. Recip engines cost $800-1,200/kW vs. Bloom's $2,500-3,500/kW.
- **Utility Fast-Track Programs Eroding Speed Gap:** PJM's proposed expedited interconnection track could compress grid timelines from 7+ years to 10-12 months for priority projects. FERC's December 2025 colocation ruling opens a faster path. Bloom's window of maximum relative advantage is 2025-2028.
- **Sustainability Tension:** Natural gas fuel cells emit ~350-400 lbs CO₂/MWh. In clean-grid markets (Pacific Northwest hydro, nuclear-heavy), Bloom is *carbon-additive* — the exact issue that killed the Amazon Oregon deal. Hyperscalers' net-zero commitments (2030 for Microsoft, Google, Meta) create a structural ceiling on willingness to deploy gas-fed systems.
- **Korea Policy Risk:** Pending transition from RPS (with favorable 2x REC multiplier for fuel cells) to government auction could undermine the economics of Korean deployments.

**Sector M&A Activity:**
- Data center infrastructure M&A is red-hot: BlackRock/NVIDIA/Microsoft's $40B Aligned Data Centers deal, Brookfield's Centersquare data center consolidation
- No precedent fuel cell company acquisitions at scale — Bloom is sui generis
- Distributed generation M&A typically 2-4x revenue ($4-8B for Bloom) — wildly below current market cap
- The market is applying a dual premium: AI infrastructure scarcity + monopoly technology

# DELIVERABLE 3: RESEARCH GAPS & CLASSIFICATION CHALLENGE

## A) ARCHETYPE CHALLENGE

The L0 classification of Bloom Energy as a "MANUFACTURER" captures only the current-state revenue composition (74% product revenue) and fundamentally misframes the asset for valuation purposes. Bloom is three businesses in one entity:

1. **A high-growth equipment manufacturer** (hardware: 74% of current revenue, 29-32% gross margin, 37-58% growth) — this is what the L0 classification sees.
2. **An emerging infrastructure annuity** (service + repowering: $14B contracted backlog, 100% attach rate, 10-20 year contracts with investment-grade counterparties) — this is what an infrastructure fund buyer sees.
3. **A technology monopoly with a patent moat** (1,000+ SOFC patents, the only commercial-scale SOFC manufacturer globally, a proprietary stack chemistry that no competitor can replicate in under a decade) — this is what a strategic acquirer or sovereign wealth fund sees.

If the company were valued starting from its most valuable asset, the analysis would begin with the **$14B service backlog** — a contracted, recurring cash flow stream that infrastructure investors would value at 6-8% discount rates, yielding $7-14B in NPV for the current base alone. The hardware business would then be valued as a "customer acquisition engine" that feeds the annuity, and the patent portfolio would be valued as the moat that makes the annuity proprietary. Under this framework, Bloom is an **infrastructure asset disguised as a manufacturer** — similar to how Rolls-Royce was misvalued as an "engine company" before the market recognized TotalCare's dominance of its economics. The "right" valuation lens depends on who is buying: an infrastructure fund sees $10-15B in contracted value plus a growth engine; a hyperscaler sees supply security worth a strategic premium; a traditional industrial buyer sees a technology acquisition at 22x revenue and walks away. The archetype that maximizes deal value is "**AI Infrastructure Platform with Embedded Annuity**" — not "Manufacturer."

## B) RESEARCH GAPS

- **Full text of the SK ecoplant PDA Restatement (2021) and PDA Extension (2023):** These are the most critical undisclosed documents for any transaction. They govern ~30% of revenue and contain potential change-of-control provisions, termination rights, and pricing terms that affect whether the SK relationship survives an acquisition. The SEC filings reference these agreements but the full texts are not filed as exhibits. This should have been the #1 diligence priority.

- **Brookfield 13F holdings and $5B framework agreement terms:** Whether Brookfield has already accumulated a below-5% equity position (visible only in 13F filings) would fundamentally change the escalation timeline analysis. The framework agreement's exclusivity, change-of-control, and equity escalation provisions are critical but not publicly available.
