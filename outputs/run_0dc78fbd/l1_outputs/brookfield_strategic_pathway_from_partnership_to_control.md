## PART A: BROOKFIELD'S ACQUISITION PLAYBOOK — HISTORICAL PATTERN ANALYSIS

### A.1 The Core Pattern: Brookfield's Three-Phase Escalation Model


Brookfield has codified what it calls a repeatable value-creation "playbook," integrating operating executives earlier in the deal cycle — even pre-acquisition — to assess and plan transformations.
 The firm's M&A behavior, across its infrastructure, renewable, and private equity arms, shows a consistent pattern: **enter through capital provision or commercial agreement → build informational and operational dependency → escalate to equity or control when the target is distressed, undervalued, or strategically necessary.**


Brookfield focuses on direct control investments in real estate, renewable power, infrastructure, credit, and private equity.
 
The firm acquires assets on a value basis to maximize long-term, risk-adjusted returns, recognizing that superior returns often require contrarian thinking.


**Key Structural Insight for the Bloom Analysis:** Unlike traditional private equity, Brookfield routinely uses **commercial partnerships as phase-one positioning**, particularly in capital-intensive sectors where operating relationships create informational advantages and switching costs.

### A.2 Case Studies: Eight Precedents for the Escalation Pattern

**CASE 1: WESTINGHOUSE ELECTRIC (Nuclear Services)**
*The Gold Standard Precedent*

**Phase 1 (2017–2018): Distressed Acquisition from Bankruptcy**

In 2018, Brookfield Business Partners (together with institutional partners) agreed to acquire 100% of Westinghouse from Toshiba for about $4.6 billion.
 At acquisition, Westinghouse was emerging from Chapter 11 bankruptcy — Brookfield acquired a deeply troubled asset, not a commercial partnership.

**Phase 2 (2018–2022): Operational Transformation**

Brookfield consolidated Westinghouse's facility footprint, renegotiated supplier contracts, and helped expand service offerings via eight add-on acquisitions of complementary businesses.
 
Over the course of Brookfield's ownership, Westinghouse's EBITDA reportedly increased by more than $350 million.


**Phase 3 (2022–2023): Intra-Brookfield Transfer to Strategic Arm**

The Cameco/Brookfield Renewable acquisition of Westinghouse followed the turnaround by Brookfield Business Partners, which acquired the business in 2018, refocused it on core nuclear services, reduced its cost base, and pursued complementary M&A.
 
When combined with distributions received, Brookfield's expected proceeds would equate to approximately 6x invested capital, a 60% IRR, and approximately $1.8 billion in proceeds from the sale of its 44% stake.


**Phase 4 (2023–present): Deepening Strategic Positioning**

In October 2025, the U.S. Government entered into a strategic partnership with Westinghouse, Brookfield, and Cameco — at least $80 billion of new reactors to be constructed using Westinghouse technology.


**Westinghouse Lesson for Bloom:** Brookfield's Westinghouse investment demonstrates an ability to hold a critical infrastructure technology asset across multiple fund vehicles and through successive stages of value creation. Critically, Brookfield **transferred Westinghouse from its private equity arm (BBU) to its renewable arm (BEP)** without losing control — exactly the kind of intra-Brookfield migration that could occur with Bloom (from BAIIF to BIP or BEP). No prior commercial partnership preceded the Westinghouse acquisition, however. This is a key difference from the Bloom situation.

**CASE 2: AUSNET SERVICES (Australian Energy Networks)**
*Persistent Pursuit Leading to Full Acquisition*


AusNet revealed that it had twice rejected Brookfield's approach before the company sweetened its offer. In earlier proposals, Brookfield had valued the business at "up to A$9.38 billion."
 Brookfield returned persistently.


On February 18, 2022, a consortium led by Brookfield acquired AusNet Services in a deal that marked the full privatization of Australia's electricity infrastructure sector, at a premium of 34% to prior trading levels.


**AusNet Lesson for Bloom:** Brookfield pursued AusNet as a **platform for a thematic strategy** (Australian energy transition), not because of a pre-existing commercial relationship. The persistence across multiple rejected bids is characteristic. More importantly, AusNet then became the **platform from which Brookfield attempted to acquire Origin Energy** — demonstrating that Brookfield uses infrastructure acquisitions as escalating footholds in a geography or sector.

**CASE 3: ORIGIN ENERGY (Australia's Largest Power Generator)**
*The "Adjacent Platform" Acquisition That Failed*


In November 2022, Origin Energy announced it had received a non-binding indicative offer of $9.00/share from a consortium led by Brookfield and EIG, following which the consortium was granted due diligence access and exclusivity.
 Brookfield had already acquired AusNet (2022) — the deal explicitly leveraged Brookfield's existing Australian infrastructure footprint.


Brookfield, through its Global Transition Fund, had an eye on Australia's energy sector — it acquired AusNet for A$10.2bn and tried to acquire AGL Energy, with a $3.5bn takeover offer. The failed AGL bid still resulted in Brookfield acquiring a 2.5% (A$200 million) stake in AGL Energy.


**This is the key precedent for Bloom:** After failing to acquire AGL outright, Brookfield **still took a 2.5% equity position** — keeping optionality alive. The final Origin bid, at $18.7 billion, was 
defeated when only 69% of shareholders backed the offer — below the 75% minimum required — primarily because AustralianSuper rejected the bid as a "low-ball offer."


**Origin Lesson for Bloom:** Brookfield shows a consistent pattern in Australia of: (1) establishing infrastructure footholds, (2) using those platforms to attempt full acquisitions of adjacent businesses, and (3) even when bids fail, securing equity footholds. The AGL residual stake is the direct analog to a scenario where Brookfield fails to acquire Bloom but acquires a meaningful minority position anyway.

**CASE 4: GRAFTECH (Graphite Electrodes)**

GrafTech was acquired in 2015 by Brookfield for $1.25 billion during a time of poor earnings and severe downturn in the steel industry. Brookfield repositioned the company by selling non-core assets, consolidating six manufacturing operations into three, and focusing on lean manufacturing techniques.



GrafTech completed a successful IPO in April 2018. As of December 2019, Brookfield still owned approximately 74% of the common stock.


**GrafTech Lesson for Bloom:** Brookfield has a playbook for taking a specialized materials/technology manufacturer, restructuring it, taking it public, and maintaining long-term control. Bloom is not distressed in the way GrafTech was, but the core pattern — acquiring a manufacturer of a critical industrial input, optimizing it, and holding controlling equity — is relevant.

**CASE 5: CLARIOS (Global Automotive Battery Leader)**
*Carve-Out at Scale, Long-Hold Strategy*


At the end of April 2019, Brookfield closed its acquisition of Clarios, the global leader in automotive battery technology. The transaction was funded with $3.0 billion of equity, of which BBU's share was $750 million for a 25% ownership interest.



Clarios is the world's leading provider of advanced low-voltage batteries, powering one in three cars on the road globally, with 80% of volumes from recurring aftermarket demand. Its technology, scale, and relationships with nearly all major automakers are unmatched.



Since the acquisition, annual profitability at Clarios has increased by more than $500 million, driven by investing in new product development, improving customer service levels, and expanding manufacturing capabilities.


**Clarios Lesson for Bloom:** Brookfield targets companies where it can own **global #1 positions in mission-critical technology with 80%+ recurring revenue streams**. Bloom's service contracts (reportedly a $9 billion backlog) look structurally similar to Clarios's aftermarket model. This is significant.

**CASE 6: INTEL SEMICONDUCTOR CO-INVESTMENT (SCIP)**
*Commercial Co-Investment as Ownership Model — Most Direct Structural Precedent*


Intel's agreement with Brookfield followed the two companies' MOU announced in February 2022. Under the terms of the agreement, they will jointly invest up to $30 billion in Intel's manufacturing expansion in Chandler, Arizona, with Intel funding 51% and Brookfield funding 49%.



Intel will retain majority ownership and operating control of the two new leading-edge chip factories.
 
The partnership was expected to enhance Intel's balance sheet by allowing Intel to tap a new pool of capital below its cost of equity, providing a $15 billion cumulative benefit to Intel's adjusted free cash flow.


**Intel SCIP Lesson for Bloom:** This is the **most structurally direct precedent** for the Bloom relationship. Intel SCIP was:
- A commercial co-investment, not a company acquisition
- A 49% stake in *assets* (the fab), not in Intel itself
- Structured to serve Intel's capital efficiency, while giving Brookfield infrastructure-like returns
- Preceded by a February 2022 MOU, then a definitive agreement six months later

The Bloom partnership is structured similarly — Brookfield invests in Bloom's *deployments* (the fuel cell infrastructure at AI factories), not directly in Bloom Energy Corp. This could evolve exactly like SCIP: Brookfield builds ownership in the *deployed asset pool*, and separately pursues an equity stake in Bloom itself.

## PART B: THE BLOOM-BROOKFIELD RELATIONSHIP — DETAILED STRUCTURE ANALYSIS

### B.1 What the $5 Billion Partnership Actually Means

**The Critical Structural Distinction:**


The partnership marks Brookfield's first investment through its dedicated AI Infrastructure strategy. This partnership marks the first phase of a joint vision to build AI factories capable of meeting the growing compute and power demands of artificial intelligence.



Brookfield has secured the Bloom Energy deal as a seed AI infrastructure investment for BAIIF — a $5 billion framework agreement with Bloom Energy to install up to 1 GW of behind-the-meter power solutions for data centers and AI factories.


This is **deployment capital, not equity in Bloom Energy Corp.** The $5 billion is Brookfield's investment in the *hardware deployed* at AI factories — Bloom manufactures and sells the fuel cells, Brookfield funds the *installation and ownership* of those fuel cells as infrastructure assets. This is structurally analogous to how a landlord funds the construction of a building (infrastructure capital) versus buying the construction company's stock (equity).

**Key unresolved question:** Who owns the deployed hardware? If Brookfield owns the fuel cell hardware installed at its AI factories (which is the infrastructure-ownership model), then:
1. Bloom receives revenue from hardware sales and long-term service contracts
2. Brookfield holds **physical infrastructure that is entirely Bloom-serviced and Bloom-dependent**
3. This creates a **switching cost** — replacing Bloom-manufactured SOFC systems with competing technology mid-deployment is technically and economically prohibitive
4. Brookfield has a **structural incentive to secure guaranteed supply** by acquiring an equity stake in Bloom


BAIIF will focus on investing in the physical infrastructure assets that underpin the delivery of AI, across four verticals: AI Factories primarily built on NVIDIA's DSX Vera Rubin-ready reference design; dedicated behind-the-meter power solutions; compute infrastructure; and strategic adjacencies. BAIIF will prioritize investments backed by highly creditworthy counterparties and contracted cash flows.


The framing here is critical: BAIIF is buying **infrastructure assets** — the fuel cells themselves — and Bloom's service contracts are what make those assets "backed by contracted cash flows."

### B.2 The BAIIF Fund Structure Amplifies the Stake Case


Brookfield announced the launch of a $100 billion global AI Infrastructure program in partnership with NVIDIA and KIA. The BAIIF targets $10 billion of equity commitments and has already received $5 billion from Brookfield, NVIDIA, and KIA.



BAIIF, together with co-investor capital and financing, will acquire up to $100 billion of AI infrastructure assets, deploying investment across every stage of the value chain — from energy and land to data centers and compute.


**The Bloom deal is explicitly described as BAIIF's "seed investment."** This means:
- Bloom is central to BAIIF's power infrastructure thesis
- BAIIF's LPs (NVIDIA, KIA, and future institutional investors) are implicitly co-investing in Bloom's deployment
- As BAIIF scales toward $100 billion, Bloom's fuel cell supply becomes a **fund-critical dependency**

When a fund manager's flagship product depends on a single technology supplier, the acquisition of that supplier becomes a **risk management imperative**, not merely an opportunistic investment.

### B.3 The November 2025 Convertible Notes — Brookfield Participation Assessment


On November 4, 2025, Bloom Energy issued $2.5 billion of 0% Convertible Senior Notes due 2030, with an option for initial purchasers to buy an additional $300 million, which was fully exercised. These notes are senior unsecured obligations convertible into cash or shares of Class A common stock.



The initial conversion rate is 5.1290 shares per $1,000 principal, representing an initial conversion price of approximately $194.97 per share — a premium of approximately 52.50% over Bloom's October 30, 2025 closing price of $127.85.


**Assessment:** The $2.5 billion convertible notes offering was placed via Rule 144A to qualified institutional buyers — a private placement. Public disclosures do not confirm or deny Brookfield participation in this offering. The conversion price at ~$195/share would represent significant upside for any holder given Bloom's current market cap (implied ~$200+ per share at $45-47B market cap with ~235M shares outstanding).

**Critical gap:** No 13F filing from any Brookfield entity discloses Bloom Energy common stock or convertible note holdings as of March 2026 based on available public records. However, 13F filings are filed quarterly and report holdings as of the prior quarter end — the Q4 2025 13F filings from Brookfield entities (due February 2026) are what would need to be searched. The absence of a public disclosure is not equivalent to the absence of a position, particularly for convertible notes held by offshore or non-filing Brookfield affiliates.

**Significant signal:** 
Management cited strong demand from AI data centers and strategic partnerships, including a $5 billion collaboration with Brookfield Asset Management, as key drivers of investor appetite for the upsized offering.
 This suggests the Brookfield partnership was actively used to market the convertible notes — a relationship that would create strong incentive for Brookfield to participate (as a strategic validation of the paper it effectively sponsored).

## PART E: DEAL MECHANICS & FEASIBILITY

### E.1 Financial Capacity


In late 2024, Brookfield's managed assets reached the US$1 trillion mark, making it one of the world's largest investment firms.



Brookfield Asset Management has completed 99 acquisitions with an average acquisition amount of $4.13 billion.
 At $45-47B current market cap, Bloom would be Brookfield's **largest single corporate acquisition by a factor of 5-6x.** However, this does not mean it's beyond Brookfield's capacity:

- The Origin Energy deal (failed) was structured at ~$18.7 billion
- The AusNet deal was ~$10.2 billion
- Brookfield routinely uses consortium structures — the Origin bid included GIC, Temasek, EIG
- BAIIF already has $5B in committed equity from Brookfield, NVIDIA, and KIA; sovereign co-investors could provide additional capacity

**Feasibility at different valuation levels:**

| Acquisition Structure | Valuation | Brookfield Equity Required | Feasibility |
|---|---|---|---|
| 15% minority stake | $50B market cap | ~$7.5B | **Highly feasible** from BAIIF |
| 25% stake with governance rights | $55B | ~$13.75B | Feasible via consortium |
| Majority stake (51%) | $60B + premium | ~$31B | Requires multi-fund coalition + debt |
| Full acquisition at premium | $65-75B | ~$65-75B (with leverage) | At limit, requires consortium |

**Most likely entry point:** A 10-20% stake acquisition through a combination of secondary market purchases and a negotiated PIPE (Private Investment in Public Equity), structured at a modest premium to market. This would cost $4.5-9.4 billion — well within BAIIF's capacity and consistent with Brookfield's preference for **co-controlling or blocking minority positions** (as seen in Intel SCIP, Westinghouse with Cameco, Compass co-controlling stake).

### E.2 Regulatory Considerations

**Antitrust:** A Brookfield acquisition of Bloom would create a vertically integrated entity controlling:
- Major data center operator (Centersquare, Compass, Data4)
- Primary on-site power supplier for AI data centers (Bloom)

This is structurally similar to the AusNet/Origin ACCC concern — 
the ACCC authorized the Origin deal despite finding a material risk that the vertical integration of Origin with AusNet would give rise to a likely substantial lessening of competition, particularly around Ausnet's incentive to discriminate in favor of Origin in the planning and operation of its electricity network assets.


An analogous concern: Would a Brookfield-owned Bloom preferentially supply fuel cells to Brookfield-owned data centers over competitor data centers? This is likely manageable through behavioral undertakings, similar to the AusNet/Origin structure, rather than a blocking issue.

**CFIUS:** 
Brookfield Asset Management, formerly headquartered in Toronto, transferred its headquarters to New York City in December 2024.
 As a now-US-headquartered entity, CFIUS exposure is reduced compared to a purely foreign acquisition. Bloom's SOFC technology has some dual-use characteristics (NASA heritage, military microgrid applications), which would warrant CFIUS review but is unlikely to be blocking for a US-aligned Canadian-American infrastructure fund.

**ITC Eligibility:** The 30% Investment Tax Credit for fuel cells under the Inflation Reduction Act is a significant value driver for Bloom. Change of ownership could theoretically affect ITC treatment on future deployments — this would require careful structuring but is a manageable tax complexity, not a deal-breaker.

### E.3 Alternative Structures

Given Bloom's $45-47B market cap and a founder-CEO focused on independence, **three non-control structures are worth modeling:**

**Structure A: Preferred Equity with Commercial Protections**
- Brookfield invests $3-5 billion in a privately negotiated convertible preferred instrument
- Conversion price set at 20-30% premium to market
- In exchange: right of first refusal on Bloom hardware for Brookfield AI factories, supply priority, board observer right
- Effectively converts Brookfield from a "commercial partner" to a "preferred strategic investor"
- Precedent: SK ecoplant invested in Bloom common equity; a Brookfield preferred instrument would be structurally superior

**Structure B: Asset JV + Minority Equity**
- Brookfield and Bloom create a joint venture (e.g., "Bloom-Brookfield AI Power JV") that owns the deployed fuel cell infrastructure
- Brookfield owns 80% of the JV; Bloom owns 20% plus provides manufacturing, installation, and service
- Separately, Brookfield acquires 10-15% of Bloom common equity on the open market
- This mirrors the Intel SCIP structure precisely: Brookfield owns 49% of the *assets*, Intel retains full corporate independence

**Structure C: Long-Term Exclusivity + Contractual Control Rights**
- Deepened commercial agreement with 10-year exclusivity provisions
- Change-of-control clauses in the partnership agreement (if Bloom is acquired by a competitor, Brookfield has consent rights or early termination with substantial penalties)
- Right of first offer if Bloom sells a controlling stake
- This is the least aggressive but establishes optionality without equity commitment

### ANOMALY 1: The "First Investment" Language Is Unusually Explicit
The Bloom partnership's official press release explicitly states it is 
"the partnership marks Brookfield's first investment through its dedicated AI Infrastructure strategy."
 Sophisticated infrastructure managers do not typically designate commercial partnerships as "investments" — this language suggests Brookfield internally classifies this as a financial commitment (infrastructure deployment capital) rather than a mere commercial agreement. The word "investment" has legal and accounting implications that distinguish it from a "contract" or "partnership."

### ANOMALY 2: BAIIF Used Bloom as Its Fundraising "Proof Point"

Brookfield specifically secured a "seed AI infrastructure investment for the Fund" — using the Bloom partnership as the anchor deal to attract NVIDIA and KIA as BAIIF investors.
 When a fund manager uses an asset as the fund's flagship marketing instrument to institutional LPs, the fund manager has a fiduciary and reputational obligation to ensure that asset's continuity and availability. This creates **implicit LP pressure** on Brookfield to secure equity-level control over Bloom's supply chain.

### ANOMALY 3: The AGL/Origin Precedent — Brookfield Takes Residual Stakes After Failed Bids

After Brookfield's AGL takeover bid was rejected, it still acquired a 2.5% (A$200M) equity stake in AGL Energy.
 This is a behavioral signal that Brookfield **never truly abandons a target after a failed commercial approach.** If the current commercial partnership represents Brookfield's Phase 1 positioning, and Sridhar resists an equity approach, Brookfield could still accumulate open-market shares quietly — without triggering 5% disclosure thresholds (which require Schedule 13D/G filings in the US).

### ANOMALY 4: Bloom's Stock Has Surged 291% in 2025 — Creating a Complex M&A Window

The context of Bloom's 291% gain in 2025 makes any immediate Brookfield acquisition structurally awkward at current valuations.
 However, history shows that Brookfield does not acquire at cyclical peaks — it is a patient, contrarian investor. The more likely scenario is that Brookfield uses the current period to establish a below-threshold equity toehold, then moves more aggressively if Bloom's stock corrects from current elevated levels.

### ANOMALY 5: KR Sridhar's Insider Selling at Scale

Over the past 90 days, CEO KR Sridhar has disposed of roughly 200,000 shares at an average price near $170, a transaction totaling about $34 million.
 This is meaningful — a founder-CEO monetizing at scale reduces his economic incentive to resist an acquisition. Sridhar's sales suggest he may be comfortable with the stock's risk/reward at current levels, which could signal openness to a structured transaction that includes founder-friendly elements (remaining CEO for a defined transition period, mission preservation language).

## CONCLUSIONS & INVESTMENT IMPLICATIONS

**The Bloom-Brookfield relationship is structurally unlike any of Brookfield's prior commercial partnerships — it is the *founding investment* of a $100 billion fund.** That designation alone creates institutional pressure toward equity escalation that does not exist in a standard commercial vendor relationship.

The most likely near-term (12-24 month) outcome is **a negotiated minority equity stake of 10-20%**, structured as a PIPE or convertible preferred, coinciding with Bloom's next major capital need (manufacturing scale-up, working capital for the $20 billion backlog). However, **within a 5-year horizon**, as Sridhar approaches succession age, as the AI infrastructure buildout matures, and as Bloom's contracted revenue base increasingly resembles an infrastructure asset rather than a growth tech company, the probability of full Brookfield control increases materially — potentially to 35-40%.

The most powerful signal to watch: **Does a Brookfield-affiliated entity appear on Bloom's 13F shareholder list in any quarter before Q3 2026?** If yes, the escalation clock has started. If Brookfield reaches 5% of Bloom's shares, it must file a Schedule 13D or 13G — and that public disclosure would be the clearest possible signal that the commercial Phase 1 has converted to the equity Phase 2 of Brookfield's documented acquisition playbook.
