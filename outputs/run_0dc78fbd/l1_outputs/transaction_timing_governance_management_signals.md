### A.3 — THE 10-MONTH VACANCY: IS THIS UNPRECEDENTED?

**Comparable situations:** A 10-month CFO vacancy at a company with >$2B revenue, a $45B+ market cap, actively executing a $2.5B capital raise, managing hyperscaler customer negotiations, and contemplating strategic transactions is **highly unusual**. The typical Fortune 500 CFO search takes 4–6 months. Extended vacancies (8+ months) at companies of this scale generally fall into three categories:

1. **The company is waiting for a specific individual** (a candidate finishing a contract, clearing a non-compete, or awaiting regulatory clearance);
2. **The company cannot attract qualified candidates** (distressed situation, governance red flags, or founder-CEO reputation for being difficult to work with);
3. **The vacancy is deliberate** — the company does not want to bring in a permanent CFO until a specific transaction or event crystallizes (because a permanent CFO hired into a transaction in progress is a distraction, and an Acting PFO with no equity stake asks fewer questions).

**Evidence from Earnings Calls:** 
On the Q3 2025 earnings call (October 2025), KR Sridhar personally addressed the CFO search: "It is a very important position. For us. We take it very seriously. And so we have a process in place. And that search is going on. We have a certain — we have a sense of urgency."
 This is a CEO who rarely addresses operational HR matters on earnings calls. The fact that the CFO question was asked — and that Sridhar answered it personally — indicates it was material enough for analysts to probe.

**Caldwell Partners / Search Firm:** 
For the Cameron replacement, the company retained Caldwell Partners.
 No search firm has been publicly named for the Berenbaum replacement, which itself may be significant — either the search is being run internally (unusual for a CFO at this scale) or a confidential engagement is underway.

**The "What Kind of CFO?" Signal — THE MOST IMPORTANT NEAR-TERM INDICATOR:**

This is the highest-signal forthcoming event in the entire analysis. Based on what we know:

- If Bloom hires a **CFO with energy M&A / capital markets background** (Goldman energy group, JPMorgan power team, Macquarie infrastructure) → This is the strongest possible transaction preparation signal. It signals the board is building a team to execute a major transaction (either M&A as acquirer, JV/partnership at scale, or managing an inbound acquisition process).

- If Bloom hires a **CFO from a strategic peer or potential acquirer** (GE Vernova, Siemens Energy, NextEra, Berkshire energy businesses) → This would be the most alarming signal of all — a direct channel to a potential strategic.

- If Bloom hires a **CFO with public company scaling / manufacturing background** (similar to Berenbaum profile) → Signals the vacancy was genuinely about finding the right operational fit, not a strategic event signal.

- If Bloom hires a **CFO with PE/infrastructure fund background** → Signals the company is preparing for sophisticated capital structure transactions (possibly infrastructure JVs, asset monetization, or a PE-backed consortium acquisition).

### D.3 — THE FAILED GOVERNANCE AMENDMENT


The amendment sought to add officer exculpation provisions and eliminate outdated references to Class B common stock. The failure to secure the necessary two-thirds majority vote means the current certificate of incorporation will remain unchanged.


**Why This Amendment Failed — And What It Means:**

The failure of an officer exculpation amendment at a mid/large-cap company is statistically rare. 
Tracking data reports that, as of early 2024, 271 Delaware public companies had held stockholder votes to approve officer exculpation amendments, with 85% of such proposals being approved. Ninety-four percent of the proposals by mid- and large-cap companies passed.



For companies that require supermajority approval under their governing documents, the higher vote threshold proved to be a hurdle to stockholder approval. Specifically, 18 of the 42 proposals that failed required a supermajority vote, 13 of which would have passed had the Delaware default applied.
 This is the most likely mechanical explanation for Bloom's failure: a 2/3 supermajority requirement combined with passive institutional votes and abstentions created a vote deficit.


Glass Lewis voted against a proposal that bundled amendments to the certification of incorporation, on the basis that shareholders should be allowed to judge each amendment on its own merits. This suggests that an officer exculpation amendment may have a greater chance of passing if evaluated separately.


**The Transaction Preparation Dimension:** Officer exculpation amendments are routinely added before transactions because they protect officers from liability claims arising from the *process* of evaluating and executing a sale. Without this provision, officers (including a CFO navigating a sale process) face greater personal exposure to shareholder litigation. The failure to pass this amendment means:
1. Officers executing a deal process face greater personal litigation risk;
2. This may *complicate* attracting a new permanent CFO who will be asked to manage a potential transaction;
3. The board will likely resubmit this amendment — its failure was likely procedural/proxy advisory, not substantive opposition.

**The Class B Cleanup:** The bundling of Class B reference cleanup with officer exculpation in the same amendment suggests the company was doing governance housekeeping — preparing the charter for a "clean" state. This is classic pre-transaction preparation.

## NOTABLE ANOMALIES

> *The following are the highest-priority unresolved questions that would require additional diligence in a live transaction context.*

### ANOMALY #1: THE 10-MONTH CFO VACANCY IS STATISTICALLY UNPRECEDENTED FOR A COMPANY OF THIS PROFILE

A company with $2B+ trailing revenue, a $45B+ market cap, executing a $2.5B capital markets transaction, managing hyperscaler customer negotiations, doubling manufacturing capacity, and being explicitly described by its own CEO as being at "a pivotal moment" — this company has been operating without a permanent CFO for 10 months. The comparable universe (Fortune 500 / S&P 500 companies of similar size and complexity) averages 4–6 months for CFO replacement. The extended vacancy either reflects: (a) a very high bar for a specific type of CFO that has not yet been found; (b) a founder-CEO who is not in a hurry because the acting CFO is providing stability while something else crystallizes; or (c) a deliberate holding pattern until a strategic event reduces the uncertainty in what the CFO's mandate will actually be. **This is the single most anomalous governance fact in the entire dataset.**

---

### ANOMALY #2: THE AUGUST 2025 DOUBLE-HIRE IS TOO COINCIDENTAL TO BE UNRELATED

Jim Snabe joined the board on **August 6, 2025.** Aaron Hoover was hired on **August 21, 2025.** Both are announced within 15 days of each other. Both are unambiguously deal-credentialed in ways the existing team was not. Both were described as arriving at "a pivotal moment." This simultaneous board/management upgrade in M&A capability — on top of a $2.5B capital raise three months later — strongly suggests a coordinated strategic initiative was underway by mid-2025.

### ANOMALY #3: THE SRIDHAR $34M DISCRETIONARY SALE IN FEBRUARY 2026

The August 2025 Sridhar sales were forced by option expiration — analytically benign. The **February 24, 2026 sale of 200,000 shares at $170 ($34M)** is not forced by any mechanical requirement. This is the largest single discretionary share sale by Sridhar on record. It occurred at the stock's near-52-week-high, shortly after the 300,000 PSU vesting was certified as achieved (suggesting strategic objectives are "done"). While this *can* be interpreted as normal founder liquidity at peak prices, it *can also* be interpreted as a founder who has concluded that the company's near-term strategic objectives are substantially complete — and is monetizing accordingly. Note that Sridhar *simultaneously* deferred 300,000 vested PSUs to 2030 — suggesting he is not fully liquidating, but rather optimizing his portfolio by selling *current* high-cost shares while locking in long-term exposure via deferred awards.

### ANOMALY #4: THE PSU STRATEGIC OBJECTIVES — WHAT WERE THEY?


Sridhar's 300,000 PSUs became earned and vested on February 27, 2026, after certification that he achieved specific objective criteria tied to strategic priorities before the stated deadline. The PSUs were eligible to be earned only if Sridhar KR met specific objective criteria tied to strategic priorities by December 31, 2027. On February 27, 2026, it was determined these objectives were achieved, triggering vesting into Class A common stock.


These were performance-based PSUs granted in December 2024, with a deadline of December 31, 2027 — and they vested *early* on February 27, 2026. What strategic objectives did Sridhar achieve in 13 months that were expected to take up to 36 months? The company has not disclosed the specific criteria. The most significant strategic milestones Bloom achieved in this period include: the Oracle partnership, the AEP/1GW deal, the Brookfield $5B partnership, the $2.5B capital raise, and the 2GW capacity expansion commitment. **If the criteria included securing a certain level of hyperscaler commitment, or a specific partnership threshold, the early vesting suggests those commitments are now locked in** — removing the *business* uncertainty from the near-term outlook while leaving the *strategic* question of what comes next fully open.

### ANOMALY #7: THE GOVERNANCE AMENDMENT FAILURE — WHO VOTED AGAINST?

The 2/3 failure is unexplained in terms of the specific voter composition. Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) has historically opposed officer exculpation amendments at *controlled companies or companies with poor governance track records*. 
ISS noted that in cases with negative recommendations, companies were often "controlled or close to controlled" companies where "stockholders have no practical ability to amend governing documents against the wishes of the controlling stockholder." Other reasons for negative recommendations included a company's poor track record on corporate governance issues or having a dual class structure with no time-based sunset provision.
 The fact that Bloom's amendment failed — in an era where 94% of comparable company proposals pass — suggests ISS or Glass Lewis may have issued a negative recommendation, possibly due to residual dual-class structure concerns (Class B references still in the charter) or Sridhar's combined Chairman/CEO role. This is a governance overhang that could complicate deal mechanics: if Bloom's charter has provisions that require supermajority votes for certain actions (beyond just the exculpation amendment), those thresholds could create structural complications in any M&A vote.
