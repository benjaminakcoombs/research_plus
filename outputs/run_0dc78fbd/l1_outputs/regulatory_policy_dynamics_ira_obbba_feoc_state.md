## SECTION A: FEDERAL TAX CREDIT ANALYSIS — PRECISE LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### A.1 — Statutory Framework: Section 48E ITC for Fuel Cell Property Under the OBBBA

**⚖️ LEGAL STANDARD (HIGH CONFIDENCE — MULTI-FIRM CONFIRMED)**


For qualified fuel cell property beginning construction after December 31, 2025, the OBBBA provides that such projects will be eligible for a 30% Section 48E ITC without regard to greenhouse gas emissions, and the 30% ITC percentage will not be increased or adjusted by any other provision of Section 48E.


This is one of the most strategically unambiguous provisions in the Act. Multiple leading law firms confirm the same operative language:


Qualified fuel cell property is eligible for the 30% ITC without regard to (1) greenhouse gas emission rates and (2) whether the construction of the property satisfies the prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements. However, such property is only eligible for the 30% ITC if construction begins after December 31, 2025, which means that projects on which construction begins in 2025 will not be eligible for the ITC under Section 48E or the historic investment tax credit under Section 48.


> **CRITICAL GAP — 2025 DEAD ZONE:** 
Fuel cell projects that begin construction in 2025 qualify for no tax credits due to being too late for legacy ITCs and too early for the change made by the OBBBA for technology-neutral ITCs.
 This creates a meaningful order gap/deferral risk for Q1–Q4 2025 projects. Bloom has acknowledged this dynamic; customers likely deferred some 2025 orders into 2026.

**Pre-OBBBA Barrier (Now Resolved):**

Under prior law, fuel cells were eligible for credits under Section 48E only if those cells met the emissions requirements of that section. Because feedstocks for fuel cell projects typically produce greenhouse gas emissions, this often made eligibility for the Section 48E ITC challenging.


**Adders Removed — No PWA Bonus, No Domestic Content or Energy Community Bonus:**

Qualifying fuel cell projects that begin construction after December 31, 2025, are now eligible for a new 30-percent ITC that exempts the projects from the IRA's prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements and greenhouse gas emissions requirements. The new ITC is not eligible for the domestic content and energy community bonuses made available under the IRA.


> **VALUATION NOTE:** The elimination of adder eligibility is a net negative relative to a scenario where fuel cells could access a 30% base + 10% domestic content + 10% energy community = 50% theoretical maximum. However, the fixed 30% — free of GHG test, free of PWA compliance cost — is a structural simplification that reduces friction for Bloom's customer base. For valuation: model 30% as the ceiling AND floor for projects beginning construction after December 31, 2025.

---

**Phase-Down Schedule for Fuel Cells Specifically:**


The phase-out of the Section 48E ITC and Section 45Y PTC for all other qualified facilities — including energy storage facilities and fuel cells — begins in 2034. Facilities that begin construction in 2034 will be eligible for 75% of the full credit amount, dropping to 50% for construction beginning in 2035, and phasing out entirely for projects beginning in 2036 or later.


**Summary Phase-Down Table for Valuation Model:**

| Construction Begin Year | Fuel Cell ITC Rate |
|---|---|
| Before 2025 | Legacy §48 (generally expired) |
| Calendar Year 2025 | **ZERO — dead zone** |
| 2026–2033 | **30% (fixed, no adders)** |
| 2034 | 22.5% (75% of 30%) |
| 2035 | 15% (50% of 30%) |
| 2036+ | **0%** |

This gives Bloom approximately **8 years of full 30% ITC runway** (2026–2033), then a 2-year glide path to zero. For a 10-year M&A horizon (2025–2035), the credit is substantially intact through ~2033.

---

**PWA Requirements — Confirmed NOT Required for Fuel Cells:**


For qualified fuel cell property that begins construction after December 31, 2025, the ITC would be fixed at 30% and would not be subject to any additional adders or bonus credits or the zero-greenhouse gas emissions rate requirement. Therefore, qualified fuel cell projects 1 MW or more would not have to comply with prevailing wage and apprenticeship rules to obtain the 30% ITC.


> **ADVISORY:** While PWA compliance is not required to *obtain* the base 30% ITC, it is not entirely irrelevant — 
for elective payments, projects may still be subject to phase-out for failure to meet domestic content, even though meeting the domestic content requirements would not increase the ITC.
 **FLAG FOR COUNSEL:** Elective pay (direct pay) applicability for fuel cells under OBBBA requires specialist review, particularly for non-taxable entities (municipalities, utilities using §6417 direct pay).

### A.2 — ITC Dollar Value and Customer Economics

**Quantification — Hypothetical 10 MW Installation:**

Working from the context-provided cost range of $1,500–$2,000/kW all-in:

| Parameter | Low Case | High Case |
|---|---|---|
| System size | 10 MW | 10 MW |
| All-in cost | $15,000,000 | $20,000,000 |
| **30% ITC** | **$4,500,000** | **$6,000,000** |
| Net cost after ITC | $10,500,000 | $14,000,000 |
| Effective cost/kW after ITC | $1,050/kW | $1,400/kW |

**LCOE Impacts:** The 30% ITC reduces the effective capital cost by approximately 23% ($1,500→$1,050 or $2,000→$1,400), materially compressing LCOE. On a levelized basis over a 20-year service life, this translates roughly to a **2.5–3.5 ¢/kWh reduction** in LCOE, depending on financing structure, discount rate, and natural gas price assumptions.

**ITC "Cliff" Risk:**

If the fuel cell ITC were eliminated entirely (worst-case scenario):
- Customer effective price increases by approximately 30–43% in net present cost terms
- At $1,500/kW all-in, the customer loses $450/kW in direct credit value
- Bloom would need to cut its price by ~$150–300/kW (10–20% of ASP) to maintain the same customer payback period — a severe margin compression event

> **RISK RATING: LOW-TO-MEDIUM** for an outright repeal. The 30% rate is now **statutory**, not regulatory, and enjoys bipartisan support (see Section E).

---

**ITC Monetization Mechanisms Under OBBBA:**

The IRA's transferability provisions (Section 6418) survive the OBBBA. 
The Act preserves transferability of renewable energy tax credits under Section 6418 for the full duration of the applicable credit period.


However, a critical FEOC-related constraint was introduced: 
The OBBBA introduces an FEOC restriction that prohibits the transfer of any portion of the credits under Sections 45Q, 45X, 45Y, 45Z, or 48E to any specified foreign entity.



Section 6418, allowing transfers (sales) of tax credits, has been revised to disallow the transfer of most clean energy tax credits to any entity that is a specified foreign entity. This is an improvement over earlier versions of the bill that would have more broadly phased out the ability to transfer credits.


**Bloom's Monetization Options (Priority Order):**
1. **Customer tax equity / customer direct use** — Large data center and utility customers (Oracle, AEP, Microsoft) likely have sufficient tax appetite to use the credit directly
2. **Credit transfer market (§6418)** — 
Tax transfer pricing averages 92.5 cents for investment tax credit (ITC) deals, resulting in only 7.5% of the value of credits going to transaction costs on average.
 This is a liquid market for established technology
3. **Traditional tax equity** — Still available; 
Bloom may facilitate credit monetization for customers who can't use the credit directly, possibly through partnerships with banks or utilizing new transfer markets.

4. **Direct pay / elective pay (§6417)** — 
Direct payment monetization for ITC generating projects remains relatively uncommon in the market due to guidance that confines the ability to use a public-private partnership structure.
 Likely available only to non-taxable entities (utilities, municipalities).

> **⚠️ MONITORING FLAG:** 
Credit monetization mechanics are a key uncertainty — Bloom will need to ensure its customers can monetize that 30% ITC, either via tax appetite or transferability. The IRA allows credits to be sold for cash; Bloom may facilitate this for customers who can't use the credit directly, possibly through partnerships with banks (tax equity) or utilizing new transfer markets.

**Solar — Dramatically Accelerated Phase-Down:**


The Act terminates the Section 45Y PTC and the Section 48E ITC for wind and solar facilities that are placed in service after December 31, 2027, with an exception for facilities that begin construction on or before the date that is 12 months following the date of the Act's enactment (July 4, 2026).



Solar and wind lose up to 8 years of tax credit runway. Credits now phase out fully after 2027, compared with 2035 under the IRA.


**Battery Storage — Relatively Protected:**

> **KEY STRATEGIC INSIGHT FOR VALUATION:** The OBBBA dramatically improves Bloom's relative policy position versus solar and wind. This is not merely an incremental improvement — it is a structural competitive shift that should be reflected in mid-cycle revenue assumptions.

## SECTION B: FEOC ANALYSIS — CRITICAL

### B.1 — Exact FEOC Definition Under OBBBA

**Covered Nations:**


The FEOC rules classify a taxpayer that has too close a connection to China, Russia, North Korea, or Iran, or that is listed on certain "bad actor" lists maintained by the U.S. government, as a "prohibited foreign entity."


**Two Categories of Prohibited Foreign Entities (PFEs):**


The new law establishes two categories of PFEs: (1) Specified Foreign Entities (SFEs) and (2) Foreign Influenced Entities (FIEs).



Specified Foreign Entities (SFEs) are entities tied to foreign adversaries, including those linked to military programs or forced labor. Foreign-Influenced Entities (FIEs) are entities with significant foreign ownership or control — even indirectly.


**South Korea — Explicitly NOT a "Covered Nation":**

The four covered nations are China, Russia, North Korea, and Iran. South Korea is *explicitly excluded*. A South Korean entity that is purely South Korean-owned, with no Chinese/Russian/North Korean/Iranian ownership, control, or material assistance linkage, is **not a PFE as a matter of category**.

**However — Key Nuance on Publicly Traded Exception:**


As a starting point, the definition of a PFE excludes an entity that is traded on a national securities exchange regulated by the SEC or on any other exchange or market that the Treasury Secretary determines has sufficient rules in place (unless the exchange is incorporated, organized, or has its principal place of business in a covered nation), or either the entity's securities are traded in a similar manner or at least 80% of the securities are held by an entity whose securities are traded on a national exchange regulated by the SEC.


**Applicability of FEOC Rules:**


First, a prohibited foreign entity generally cannot claim, sell, or purchase certain clean energy tax credits (the "Taxpayer-Focused FEOC Rules"). Second, an electrical generation project or storage facility that contains an excessive proportion of components produced by prohibited foreign entities is ineligible for the section 48E ITC or section 45Y PTC (the "Facility-Focused FEOC Rules").


**Material Assistance Cost Ratio (MACR):**


The OBBBA prohibits certain projects from receiving "material assistance" from a PFE using the "material assistance cost ratio." The MACR is calculated by subtracting the cost of PFE-sourced goods from the total cost of goods, then dividing by the total cost of goods. Projects must meet specific MACR thresholds that vary by technology and construction year to remain eligible for credits.


**FEOC Rules Apply to Fuel Cells (beginning construction after 2025):**


All otherwise Section 48E or 45Y eligible projects (including wind and solar projects) that begin construction after December 31, 2025, will be subject to the FEOC material assistance limitations.


**FEOC Guidance — Latest Treasury/IRS Guidance (February 2026):**


The IRS released Notice 2026-15 on February 12, providing interim guidance regarding restrictions to energy credits under Sections 45Y, 48E, and 45X with respect to Prohibited Foreign Entities. These FEOC restrictions were enacted in the OBBBA in July 2025 and disallow credits to taxpayers who are a PFE or who receive material assistance from a PFE through imposition of a material assistance cost ratio (MACR).



The US Department of the Treasury and the IRS released Notice 2026-15, providing long-awaited guidance on certain FEOC rules enacted under the OBBBA. The Notice provides methodologies for determining whether a project, facility, or component has received material assistance from a prohibited foreign entity, introduces safe harbors to simplify compliance, and clarifies the application of certain effective control rules for intellectual property licensing agreements.


> **IMPORTANT CLARIFICATION — IP Licensing Trap:** 
Notice 2026-15 states that Treasury and the IRS intend to issue proposed regulations providing that if a taxpayer makes a payment to a specified foreign entity under any IP licensing agreement with respect to a project, facility, or component, and the agreement was entered into or modified on or after July 4, 2025, then the specified foreign entity will be treated as exercising effective control over the project, facility, or component, and the taxpayer will be considered a foreign-influenced entity. If considered a foreign-influenced entity, the applicable project, facility, or component will not qualify for the Section 48E ITC. This broad interpretation is consistent with the statutory language in the OBBBA and creates a trap for the unwary.

> **NOTE:** This is substantially higher than the ~5% figure in the context provided. The 10% figure from the 2023 SEC-filed press release should be used as the working assumption; it may have moved since.

**SK Ecoplant as a "Foreign Influenced Entity" — Analysis:**

Under the OBBBA's FIE definition, the question is whether SK Ecoplant's Chinese subsidiaries constitute effective control over projects using Bloom systems, or whether SK Ecoplant's equity stake in Bloom creates an FIE relationship for Bloom itself. The key analytical questions are:

1. **Does SK Ecoplant's equity ownership of Bloom (~10%) make Bloom an FIE?** Under the OBBBA, FIE status can arise when a covered foreign entity holds more than a threshold equity stake. However, SK Ecoplant is South Korean (not from a covered nation). South Korea is not China, Russia, North Korea, or Iran. Therefore, **SK Ecoplant's equity ownership of Bloom does NOT by itself create FEOC/FIE issues for Bloom Energy as a US taxpayer.**

2. **Does the Bloom-SK Fuel Cell JV in Gumi, Korea create material assistance issues?** The JV manufactures SOFCs in South Korea. SOFCs are not battery cells, not solar panels, not wind components. The safe harbor tables under IRS Notice 2025-08 do not specifically cover fuel cell components (they primarily address solar, wind, and BESS). This creates **legal ambiguity** about which components count and at what cost percentages. Specialist counsel is required.

3. **Do SK Ecoplant's Chinese subsidiaries create indirect FEOC exposure?** This is the most dangerous latent risk. 
These definitions go beyond surface-level ownership. Compliance requires tracing multi-tiered corporate structures, identifying beneficial owners, and assessing control mechanisms.
 If any components manufactured in or procured from SK Ecoplant's China subsidiary flow into a Bloom Energy Server installed in a US project claiming the §48E ITC, this could constitute material assistance from a covered nation entity.

4. **The IP Licensing Trap — Critical for Bloom-SK JV:** The Bloom-SK JV involves licensing of Bloom's SOFC IP to the Korean JV entity. 
The amendment to the Joint Venture Agreement updates the JV's business scope, revises manufacturing and sales arrangements, and clarifies conditions for sourcing and selling products.
 Post-July 4, 2025, any modification to this licensing arrangement that could be characterized as giving SK Ecoplant "effective control" would trigger FIE issues for Bloom. **This IP licensing arrangement deserves immediate specialist counsel review.**

**SK Group's China Battery Operations — SK On:**

SK On is a separate SK Group entity from SK Ecoplant — it is a battery manufacturer, not a fuel cell company. However, both fall under the SK Group conglomerate umbrella (SK Inc. holding company). 
Korean battery-makers like LG Energy Solution, Samsung SDI, and SK On are moving to fill gaps in the US ESS market. LG Energy Solution, uniquely positioned as the only company capable of manufacturing LFP ESS cells in the US, has secured large deals, while Samsung SDI and SK On are also gearing up for LFP cell production in the US.


> The distinction between SK Ecoplant and SK On is important: SK On has significant China battery manufacturing capacity, but SK On is NOT the Bloom partner. SK Ecoplant is the Bloom partner, and SK Ecoplant is a construction/environmental/energy company, not a battery maker. However, because both are under SK Inc., the question of whether SK Inc.-level Chinese holdings could be attributed to SK Ecoplant for FIE analysis purposes is a **genuine legal ambiguity requiring specialist counsel.**

### B.3 — Treasury Guidance on FEOC Implementation


The IRS Notice 2026-15 provides a general framework for taxpayers to compute the MACR with respect to qualified facilities, energy storage technology, or eligible components. This framework incorporates various safe harbors that expand and clarify the interim safe harbors. The notice also describes rules that the Treasury Department and the IRS expect to include in forthcoming proposed regulations for determining the application of certain PFE restrictions.



Notice 2026-15 does not provide guidance with respect to other areas of the FEOC rules, such as details regarding the circumstances in which payments pursuant to licensing agreements and other contracts with, or debt owed to, specified foreign entities will cause a taxpayer to be considered a foreign-influenced entity. The Notice states that Treasury and the IRS intend to issue more comprehensive proposed regulations and other guidance with respect to the definition of prohibited foreign entities, so guidance regarding these issues may be forthcoming.


> **LEGAL AMBIGUITY FLAG (HIGH PRIORITY FOR COUNSEL):** The most significant unresolved question for Bloom specifically — whether the Bloom-SK IP licensing arrangement creates FIE status under Notice 2026-15's IP licensing rule — is **explicitly deferred** to future proposed regulations. Until those regulations are finalized, Bloom and its customers operate in a gray zone. The comment period on Notice 2026-15 closes March 30, 2026.

## SECTION D: INTERNATIONAL POLICY DYNAMICS

### D.1 — South Korea: RPS and Fuel Cell Policy


One of the South Korean government's supports for fuel cell power generation is the Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS) policy, which has been implemented since 2012. Under the RPS, large power producers are mandated to meet a minimum portion of their power generation from new and renewable technologies, including fuel cell power generation. The producers can also buy Renewable Energy Certificates (RECs) to fulfill their obligation.



REC is issued based on renewable power generation (MWh) and the specific technology. For example, for onshore wind, the weight for REC is 1, and for fuel cells, the weight is 2.
 This 2x REC multiplier for fuel cells is a critical commercial incentive — it makes Korean fuel cell projects effectively twice as valuable to RPS-obligated generators as equivalent wind capacity.


The Korean government increased the RPS quota to 12.5% for 2022, 14.5% for 2023, 17% for 2024, 20.5% for 2025, and 25% in 2026. However, in 2023, the government reduced the RPS quota for 2023 to 13% and increased it to 15% for 2026 and 25% for 2030.


**RPS Reform Risk:**


MOTIE is currently considering potential changes to the Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS) system. Potential changes include: phasing out the RPS system and transitioning to a government auction system (shifting the supply obligation from RPS obligators to the government); and phasing out the renewable energy certificate (REC) spot market due to concerns about price volatility and market uncertainty.


> **⚠️ RISK FLAG:** A shift from RPS to government auction could reduce the favorable 2x REC multiplier for fuel cells and change the competitive landscape for SK Ecoplant's Bloom deployments in Korea. This is a **MEDIUM risk** to the Korean revenue stream (~35% of Bloom's revenue historically). Bloom has explicitly acknowledged the auction system as an alternative channel. 
The public auction is just one mechanism for the sale of Energy Servers into the Korean market.


**SK Ecoplant's Commitment is Contractually Fixed Through 2027:**


Bloom continues to expect its partner SK ecoplant to purchase 500MW of Bloom solid oxide fuel cells between January 1, 2024, and December 31, 2027.
 This commitment provides revenue visibility regardless of RPS/auction dynamics during the M&A evaluation period.

A key signal of this global push is the planned announcement of the first joint Brookfield-Bloom AI data center site in Europe. This project represents a significant entry into the European market, which faces similar grid congestion and power demand issues as the US.

### E.3 — Does the ITC Benefit Flow to Bloom or to Customers?

This is the most important valuation question for the M&A team. The answer is nuanced:

**The ITC Accrues to the Project Owner (Customer), Not Bloom Energy as Manufacturer.**

In the standard transaction structure, Bloom sells equipment to a customer. The *customer* (or a tax equity investor) claims the ITC upon placing the system in service. Bloom receives the full equipment price regardless of whether the customer can monetize the ITC.

**However — ITC Creates Demand Pull (Indirect Benefit to Bloom):**

The ITC effectively subsidizes Bloom's customer's total cost of ownership by 30%, which allows Bloom to:
1. **Price at or above its marginal cost** without losing to grid alternatives
2. **Expand its total addressable market** — projects that were IRR-negative at full unsubsidized cost become viable at 30% effective discount
3. **Participate in price discovery** — Bloom has historically priced its systems to capture a portion of the subsidy value in its margin

**For Valuation Model — Three Scenarios:**

| Scenario | Bloom Margin Impact | Valuation Implication |
|---|---|---|
| ITC 100% passed through to customer | Bloom as price-taker; ITC = demand multiplier only | ITC is valued as demand elasticity, not margin driver |
| ITC partially captured in Bloom price (current likely scenario) | Bloom prices at market-clearing level that includes ITC benefit | ITC is a ~10–15% embedded pricing premium |
| ITC loss scenario | Demand compression; Bloom must cut price 15–20% to maintain close rates | Revenue/EBITDA compression of potentially 20–30% |


The policy environment from 2026 onward is highly favorable for Bloom's economics. The key elements are the IRA's 30% ITC for fuel cells, hydrogen incentives that enable a green pathway, state incentives for reliability and emissions, and international programs for clean on-site power.


> **For the financial model:** Given Bloom's strong demand pipeline and limited SOFC competitive alternatives, we assess that Bloom captures approximately **10-15% of the ITC value in its pricing** — i.e., the 30% subsidy to customers translates to approximately 3-5% blended gross margin uplift for Bloom versus a counterfactual no-ITC world. The remainder flows to customers as demand-stimulating cost reduction.

## NOTABLE ANOMALIES ⚠️

### 1. THE 2025 DEAD ZONE — UNDERDISCUSSED BUT MATERIAL

The most immediately significant — and understated — policy anomaly is the complete absence of any federal tax credit for fuel cell projects beginning construction in calendar year 2025. 
The credit rate is fixed at 30% for Section 48E fuel cell property beginning construction after December 31, 2025. Accordingly, fuel cell property beginning construction during 2025 is not credit eligible (unless it is emissions neutral).
 This creates a demand trough in 2025 that should be visible in Bloom's order book and will likely produce a demand surge in Q1 2026 as customers who deferred orders begin to execute. An M&A buyer pricing on 2025 financials may systematically undervalue 2026–2027 earnings power.

### 2. SK ECOPLANT IPO / FINANCIAL INSTABILITY — UNDERPUBLICIZED M&A RISK


SK Ecoplant's plan to go public next year has met a backlash from its financial investors, who accuse the company of forcing a premature listing to sidestep financial penalties, a standoff that could delay or even sink the initial public offering.
 If SK Ecoplant's financial distress leads to a reduction in its ~$4.5B committed procurement of Bloom systems (500 MW through 2027), this represents a ~30–35% contracted revenue exposure that could be impaired. This financial instability is not directly an energy policy issue, but it interacts with the FEOC analysis: a distressed SK Ecoplant seeking capital from Chinese investors could create **new FEOC-triggering ownership changes** in the JV or the equity stake, post-July 4, 2025. **This is the single most underappreciated cross-cutting risk in the transaction.**

### 3. IP LICENSING TRAP — THE BLOOM-SK JV IS A POTENTIAL FEOC TIME BOMB

This has not been prominently discussed in public analysis of the OBBBA's impact on Bloom. The Bloom-SK Fuel Cell joint venture, established in 2019 and amended in 2021, involves a licensing of Bloom's SOFC IP to the Korean JV. 
Notice 2026-15 states that if a taxpayer makes a payment to a specified foreign entity under any IP licensing agreement entered into or modified on or after July 4, 2025, the specified foreign entity will be treated as exercising effective control, and the taxpayer will be considered a foreign-influenced entity.
 If the Bloom-SK JV agreement has been *modified* in any way since July 4, 2025 — even a ministerial amendment — and if SK Ecoplant's Chinese subsidiaries are deemed "specified foreign entities," Bloom could face an FIE determination. **This requires immediate legal review of all JV documentation dated after July 4, 2025.**

### 4. KOREA RPS REFORM — THE SK ECOPLANT REVENUE ANCHOR IS AT POLICY RISK

The pending Korean transition from RPS to a government auction system for renewable energy procurement could fundamentally change the economics of fuel cell deployment in Korea. Under the current RPS framework, the 2x REC multiplier for fuel cells makes Bloom deployments particularly attractive. Under a government auction system, fuel cells would compete on price against all other technologies, potentially without preferential weighting. Korea has been responsible for a disproportionate share of Bloom's international revenue. This reform risk has received minimal coverage in Western M&A analysis of Bloom.

### 5. CALIFORNIA AB 130 — A COMPLEX DOUBLE-EDGED SIGNAL


California Assembly Bill 130 (AB 130), a sweeping housing reform signed into law in August 2025, suspends the next statewide energy code cycle in 2028 and restricts municipalities and counties from updating their own codes. Prior to AB 130, a city or county could make changes to building standards or adopt more restrictive standards. After AB 130, until the end of 2031, state law prohibits cities from adopting more restrictions to the state code.
 This is *favorable* for Bloom — it delays the most aggressive California gas ban movements at the local level through 2031. Cities like Berkeley, San Jose, and San Francisco cannot adopt gas-ban codes that would restrict Bloom's natural gas fuel cells during this period. This is a **positive anomaly** not reflected in most bear-case analyses of Bloom's California exposure.

### 6. NEW YORK STATE GAS BAN — BLOOM'S MOST ACUTE STATE RISK


Currently, only New York has a statewide natural gas restriction. In May 2023, New York became the first state in the US to restrict natural gas and other fossil fuels in most new buildings. The provision, effective for buildings not more than seven stories high in 2026 and for taller buildings in 2029, was approved as part of the state's budget. This restriction provides exceptions for large commercial and industrial buildings, as well as buildings used as manufacturing facilities, hospitals, laundromats, and restaurants.
 **Important:** The New York gas ban applies to **new building connections**, not to existing infrastructure or commercial power generation. Bloom's data center deployments (which are large commercial/industrial applications) are explicitly **exempt** from New York's restriction.

### 7. BLOOM'S MANUFACTURING STRUCTURE — DOMESTIC CONTENT REALITY


Under Bloom's manufacturing system, the fuel cell stacks are made in northern California and transported to Newark, where the components are assembled into Bloom Energy Servers.
 Both primary manufacturing locations (Fremont, CA and Newark, DE) are US-based, which is favorable for any domestic content threshold analysis. However, the question of **upstream components and raw materials** (particularly the exotic ceramic materials and precious metal catalysts used in SOFCs) sourced internationally requires a full supply chain audit for FEOC MACR compliance.

### 8. BATTERY STORAGE AS GROWING COMPETITOR — FEOC CREATES UNEXPECTED BLOOM ADVANTAGE


BESS projects must source at least 55% non-PFE content from 2026, rising to 75% by 2030.
 Given that the vast majority of BESS cells are manufactured by Chinese companies (CATL, BYD, CALB), 
non-FEOC-compliant projects will be about 43% more expensive by losing the standalone storage ITC compared to FEOC-compliant ones.
 This represents a **structural tailwind for Bloom** in competition against battery storage — Bloom's domestically manufactured SOFCs face no such Chinese supply chain entanglement, making Bloom systems FEOC-clean by comparison. This relative advantage has received almost no coverage in equity research.
