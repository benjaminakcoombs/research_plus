### A.3 — September 2024 Electrolyte Localization — Technology Transfer Analysis


In early September, representatives from Bloom Energy, SK ecoplant, and Korea Hydro & Nuclear Power (KHNP) gathered at the 2024 Korea Energy Expo in Busan for a ceremony hosted by KHNP. The event celebrated a significant milestone: the successful localization of electrolyte materials production within South Korea. As a critical component of solid oxide fuel cells (SOFC), domestic production of electrolyte materials marks a major advancement for South Korea's energy sector. This achievement moves the country closer to establishing an independent SOFC market, reducing reliance on imports.



By bridging key supply chain gaps, Bloom Energy is helping to enhance the technological competitiveness of South Korea's fuel cell ecosystem and paving the way for the global expansion of its industry-leading Energy Server.


**Who is the electrolyte manufacturer?** The Bloom blog identifies the attending company as **KV Materials** (represented by Hoon-jae Lee, CEO). This is a Korean supplier, not SK ecoplant, that has qualified to produce electrolyte for Bloom's supply chain.


With domestic electrolyte materials production now established, the next crucial step is the successful processing of these materials for use in Bloom's Energy Server. South Korean manufacturers are confident this final milestone will be reached by 2025. In the meantime, South Korean-produced electrolyte materials will begin to enter Bloom Energy's supply chain throughout the remainder of 2024.


**M&A Forensic Assessment of Electrolyte Localization:**

This development is less threatening to Bloom's IP position than it might appear because:
1. The electrolyte supplier is a third-party Korean vendor (KV Materials), not SK ecoplant itself
2. Bloom's 10-K notes: 
The JV Company shall directly source and procure the JV Scope in accordance with Bloom Energy's supplier qualification process and with Bloom Energy's prior written approval.
 — All JV suppliers must receive Bloom's written approval, preserving Bloom's IP gating authority
3. 
Suppliers qualified by the JV Company shall not be exclusive to the JV Company.


**However**, the localization trend creates a long-term risk: as more Korean-sourced components are qualified, the Korean JV becomes increasingly self-sufficient in terms of upstream supply. **The fuel cell stack and core electrolyte chemistry — which is Bloom's crown-jewel IP — remains manufactured in Fremont, CA and Newark, DE, per all available disclosures.** There is no public evidence of a technology licensing agreement granting SK independent IP rights to produce complete Bloom Energy Server systems.

**⚠️ KEY FINDING:** The JVA Amendment establishes that the JV Company can qualify local suppliers, but Bloom retains gating authority. The localization is supply chain optimization within Bloom's controlled framework, not a technology handoff. However, the full JVA text is not publicly available, and any M&A acquirer should conduct DD to verify whether there are any backstop IP access provisions embedded in the JVA.

### B.3 — Can SK ecoplant Fund a Bloom Acquisition?

**Short Answer: No, SK ecoplant alone cannot fund a Bloom acquisition at current market cap (~$45B). SK Group consolidated support would be required.**

Analysis:
- SK ecoplant's IPO-readiness valuation was targeted at 5-10 trillion won ($3.6-7.2B). Its debt ratio remains at ~233%, and it recently needed to sell the KKR environmental assets to shore up its balance sheet.
- A Bloom acquisition at market would require a significant premium above ~$45B market cap — potentially $55-65B total transaction value.
- SK ecoplant's own balance sheet cannot support this. Only a SK Inc.-level or SK Innovation-level transaction could.
- However, SK Group's combined energy assets (~$76B+) provide the theoretical resource base. The question is debt capacity and strategic prioritization.

## SECTION C: CHANGE-OF-CONTROL SCENARIO ANALYSIS

### C.1 — Scenario A: Third-Party Acquires Bloom (e.g., Brookfield, NextEra, GE Vernova)

**Probability Assessment: 35-45% (most likely single scenario)**

**Rationale:** Bloom's AI/data center power narrative, combined with the AEP and Brookfield frameworks, makes it an attractive strategic acquisition target. 
Bloom details international expansion, notably in South Korea, strategic distribution and assembly partnerships with SK ecoplant, and large commercial frameworks with American Electric Power and Brookfield, including up to 1 GW of fuel cell procurement and a prospective $5.0 billion project financing framework.


**Change-of-Control Analysis:**

The PDA Restatement's change-of-control provisions are **NOT publicly disclosed.** This is a critical data gap. However, we can triangulate from what IS known:

1. The PDA Restatement from 2021 establishes "termination procedures for material breaches." No explicit change-of-control consent right or termination right has been identified in public filings.

2. The Investor Agreement (equity-related) contains no disclosed change-of-control provision that would trigger automatic termination.

3. Standard commercial practice: Exclusive distribution agreements of this scale typically contain change-of-control provisions allowing the distributor to terminate for convenience or requiring prior consent of the distributor to assign the agreement to an acquirer. Without the full PDA text, this cannot be confirmed.

**Revenue at Risk Under Termination:**

| Revenue Category | Annual Amount | Risk |
|-----------------|---------------|------|
| SK-related quarterly revenue (55% × $519M Q3 run rate) | ~$1.1B annualized | High concentration risk |
| Remaining 500MW commitment (at ~$3M/MW product) | ~$1.5B total through 2027 | Could be terminated |
| 20-year service revenue tail | ~$3B | Lost if SK exits |
| **Total at Risk** | **~$5.5B+ NVP** | **Existential for Bloom** |

**SK's Negotiating Leverage:**
- Korea represents Bloom's second-largest market (~600 MW deployed)
- SK ecoplant/SK eternix control access to every Korean customer relationship
- The Korean JV is Bloom's only non-US manufacturing facility; SK ecoplant is landlord and lender to that JV
- The Korean Hydrogen Portfolio Standard creates regulatory requirements that Bloom needs SK to navigate
- 
The volatility of the Korean Won, as experienced in the past several months, may impact our commercial efforts in the region.
 — Bloom itself acknowledges currency and commercial risks in Korea

**Key Scenarios if Third-Party Acquires Bloom:**

*Sub-scenario A1 — Acquirer Is US/Allied Country (Brookfield/NextEra):* CFIUS likely approvable. SK's leverage exists at the commercial agreement level. SK could demand:
- Assignment consent with economic concessions
- Price renegotiation (removing "premium pricing")
- Direct IP access or licensing
- Right of first refusal for future acquisitions

*Sub-scenario A2 — Acquirer Is Non-US Allied (Siemens, MHI, etc.):* CFIUS review triggered. 
CFIUS reviews investments in critical technologies, infrastructure, sensitive data, and specific real estate deals.
 Bloom's solid oxide fuel cell technology, which is used in data centers and critical infrastructure, would likely require mitigation. Energy sector CFIUS scrutiny is intense: 
CFIUS now actively reviews and sometimes alters transactions that result in foreign control of U.S. energy companies.

### C.2 — Scenario B: SK Group Acquires Bloom (Full Acquisition)

**Probability Assessment: 5-10% (low, near-term; higher long-term if SK restructuring succeeds)**

**Price Required:**
- At ~$45B market cap, a 25-35% control premium = $56-61B acquisition value
- SK Group's total resources are theoretically sufficient (~$76B+ in SK Innovation E&S assets), but the practical reality is SK ecoplant's own balance sheet is strained

**CFIUS Analysis — SK Acquiring Bloom:**


CFIUS oversees transactions that might give foreign entities control of U.S. businesses. This includes mergers, acquisitions, or takeovers. It also reviews investments in critical technologies, infrastructure, sensitive data, and specific real estate deals.


South Korea is a US treaty ally, not a "covered" nation under FEOC/SFE definitions. However:
- Bloom's fuel cell technology is used in US critical infrastructure
- Bloom has US government-related customers
- The technology has dual-use potential (power generation for data centers, military forward operating bases)
- A Korean chaebol acquiring a US energy technology company would receive mandatory CFIUS review

**Korean Precedent:** 
Hyundai Motor Group, a South Korean multinational automotive manufacturer, obtained CFIUS clearance in connection with its $4 billion autonomous driving joint venture with Aptiv.
 — This suggests Korean companies can obtain CFIUS clearance, but Hyundai-Aptiv is very different from a chaebol acquiring a fuel cell company with US critical infrastructure deployments.

**Standstill Barrier:**
Under the current Investor Agreement, SK cannot launch a hostile takeover bid during the Standstill Term. However, the standstill expires when SK falls below 5% ownership — and SK is now at ~4.7-4.81%. If SK's share count dips below 5%, the standstill expires and the board seat is lost. At that point, SK could theoretically approach Bloom's board with a friendly acquisition proposal, but could not initiate a hostile tender.

**Anti-competitive concerns:** SK as both customer/distributor AND owner creates vertical integration concerns. Korean antitrust regulators (KFTC) and US DOJ/FTC would scrutinize.

**Risk Matrix — Scenario B:**

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| CFIUS approval probability (if filed) | 50-65% (ally nation, but critical infrastructure) |
| SK Inc. board willingness | Uncertain; SK ecoplant IPO priority conflicts |
| SK ecoplant financial capacity | Insufficient standalone |
| Bloom board receptivity | Low (founders/management team is entrenched) |
| Other shareholder support | Uncertain; depends on premium |

**Current Standstill Status:**
The standstill restricts SK from acquiring more than 15% ownership. However, there are important nuances:

1. **The standstill may already be expiring.** The standstill lasts until the LATEST of: (i) 2nd anniversary of the Second Closing Date, (ii) the date SK loses board designation rights (triggered at <5% ownership), and (iii) the date SK falls below 5%. Given SK is now at ~4.7-4.81% and sold shares, if they dip below 5%, conditions (ii) and (iii) would be met simultaneously, potentially ending the standstill.

2. **Standstill termination trigger:** 
The Standstill Term shall terminate upon the occurrence of any of the following events: (b) the Company files a Schedule 14D-9 recommending that stockholders accept any such offer.
 — If a third-party acquirer launches a bid and Bloom's board recommends it, the standstill automatically terminates, allowing SK to counter-bid or accumulate shares freely.

**Blocking Position Analysis:**
- Delaware corporate law: A 15-20% stake generally does not confer blocking rights on ordinary corporate actions
- For a merger requiring two-thirds approval: a 33%+ stake is needed to block
- For a simple majority vote: SK would need >50%
- However, a 15-20% stake makes any competing bid more expensive and complex
- A 20% stake could function as a blocking position in practice if paired with board seat(s) and the commercial agreements as leverage

**Korean Chaebol "Creeping Acquisition" Precedent:**
There is limited precedent for Korean chaebols systematically acquiring US technology companies through creeping accumulation. The notable examples involve Japanese companies (e.g., SoftBank/Sprint), not Korean chaebols. SK's strategy appears to be the opposite: they are reducing, not increasing.

## SECTION D: FEOC (FOREIGN ENTITY OF CONCERN) ANALYSIS

### D.1 — OBBBA FEOC Framework — Applicability to South Korea


On July 4, 2025, President Trump signed H.R. 1—dubbed the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA)—enacting significant modifications to clean-energy credits previously enacted under the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022. OBBBA scales back clean-energy tax incentives under the IRA. This includes speeding up phase-outs for certain energy credits, tightening domestic content rules and foreign-entity restrictions, and imposing new deadlines for projects to qualify.


**Definition of Specified Foreign Entity (SFE):**

In general, the FEOC restrictions would disqualify the Code Section 45Y and Code Section 48E credits to the extent that the applicable taxpayer is determined to constitute: A specified foreign entity – A specified foreign entity includes any entity owned by the governments of China, Russia, North Korea and Iran, owned by a citizen of such countries (unless such person is an individual who is a citizen, national or lawful resident of the United States), or organized or having a principal place of business in such countries.



SFEs include entities on U.S. sanctions or blocked-persons lists and certain Chinese military or forced-labor lists. They also include any entity owned or controlled by, or organized in, a "covered" foreign nation (China, Iran, North Korea, Russia) or by their governments or citizens.


**CONCLUSION: South Korea is NOT a "covered" foreign nation under the OBBBA FEOC/SFE definition.** The covered nations are explicitly limited to China, Russia, North Korea, and Iran. South Korea is not on this list.

**However — the "Foreign Influenced Entity" (FIE) Analysis is More Complex:**


Under the OBBBA, the FEOC restrictions—now referred to as the PFE restrictions—apply more broadly to many more energy tax provisions than just section 30D; the restrictions also reach more deeply, imposing restrictions that go well beyond those under the FEOC rules.



Originally limited to electric vehicles and advanced manufacturing tax credits, these "foreign entity of concern" (FEOC) rules have now been expanded under the OBBBA to cover virtually all tax credits. The expanded FEOC rules deny credits to projects that are owned or controlled by certain foreign entities — or that purchase components from or make payment to these foreign entities.


The critical question is whether Bloom itself qualifies as a **Foreign-Influenced Entity (FIE)** due to SK ecoplant's ownership and board seat. The FIE analysis requires examining ownership thresholds and board influence. Based on publicly available legal analysis, the FIE definition appears to focus on entities in which SFEs (China, Russia, North Korea, Iran-linked entities) hold significant influence — not entities influenced by Korean investors. SK ecoplant is not Chinese, Russian, North Korean, or Iranian.

**Fuel Cell ITC Specific Treatment:**

OBBBA amends Code Section 48E to allow qualified fuel cell projects to qualify for the credit without regard to the greenhouse gas emissions rate requirements. Additionally, qualified fuel cell projects will not have to demonstrate compliance with the burdensome prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements.
 — This is actually **favorable** treatment for Bloom's fuel cell ITC.


OBBBA does not apply foreign entity rules to certain other energy credits, including the PTC and ITC for projects placed in service before 2025 (sections 45 and 48).


**Treasury Guidance Status:**

Pursuant to Executive Order 14315 of July 7, 2025 and Internal Revenue Service (IRS) Notice 2025-42, forthcoming guidance from the US Department of the Treasury is expected that will shed additional light on the implementation of these rules.



If a facility that claimed the CEITC subsequently comes under foreign entity control or sourcing influence, prior credits may be partly or fully recaptured. If a project is transferred or sold in a transaction that results in a PFE becoming an owner, that change in ownership would presumably disqualify the credits and require recapture of the CEITC claimed.


**FEOC Risk Summary for SK/Bloom:**

| Risk | Assessment |
|------|------------|
| SK ecoplant as SFE | **NO** — South Korea is not a covered nation |
| SK ecoplant as FIE | **Very Low** — FIE definition targets China/Russia/etc. influence |
| Bloom as FIE due to SK ownership | **Very Low** — SK is Korean, not a covered-nation entity |
| Future FEOC risk if SK sells to Chinese entity | **High** — if Econovation stake sold to Chinese buyer |
| Korean supply chain (electrolyte from KV Materials) | **No risk** — Korean suppliers are not PFEs |
| Supply chain with Chinese components | **Potential risk** — requires due diligence |

**⚠️ IMPORTANT CAVEAT:** Treasury guidance implementing the FIE definition has not yet been finalized (as of IRS Notice 2025-42). The FIE analysis may evolve. Any acquirer conducting M&A due diligence should commission a current legal opinion specifically on whether Bloom's SK relationship creates any FIE taint under the OBBBA's final implementing regulations.

**Bloom's 30% ITC Eligibility:**

The 30% CEIC for fuel cells is not subject to reduction on account of a failure to comply with the PWA Requirements.
 Bloom's fuel cell ITC appears preserved under the OBBBA, with the SK relationship not creating a FEOC disqualification based on current regulatory analysis. This is a positive finding.

## SECTION E: COMPARABLE STRATEGIC RELATIONSHIPS — PRECEDENT ANALYSIS

### Precedent 1: SoftBank / Sprint (2013-2020)

**Structure:** SoftBank invested $21.6B for ~80% of Sprint, becoming controlling shareholder while Sprint remained publicly listed.

**M&A Implications:**
- SoftBank's controlling ownership complicated Sprint's strategic options
- When T-Mobile sought to merge with Sprint, SoftBank's consent was absolutely required
- SoftBank used its Sprint stake as leverage to negotiate a superior economic outcome in the T-Mobile/Sprint merger
- SoftBank received T-Mobile shares and a substantial cash payout

**Applicability to Bloom-SK:** The analogy is imperfect (SK holds <5% vs. SoftBank's controlling stake), but the general dynamic — a foreign strategic investor with commercial ties complicating a third-party deal — is directly relevant. At <5%, SK has far less blocking power than SoftBank had.

### Precedent 2: Samsung / ASML Supply Relationship

**Structure:** Samsung is ASML's largest customer (~30% of revenue) and holds a small equity stake. Samsung's purchasing commitments underpin ASML's investment planning.

**M&A Implications:**
- ASML's customer concentration in Samsung creates a similar revenue vulnerability
- ASML has never faced an acquisition scenario partly because its Dutch government golden share structure provides protection
- The key lesson: dominant customer relationships create implicit blocking power even without formal contractual change-of-control rights

**Applicability:** Bloom-SK is structurally similar. SK's ~55% revenue contribution is even more concentrated than Samsung's share of ASML revenue.

### Precedent 3: Wanxiang / A123 Systems (2013)


CFIUS approved an acquisition of a high-tech battery manufacturer only after the deal was restructured to carve out its government contracting business (the Wanxiang/A123 case).


**Structure:** Chinese company Wanxiang acquired US battery maker A123 Systems after carving out the government business unit.

**Applicability:** This is the most relevant CFIUS precedent for a foreign acquisition of a US clean energy technology company. Bloom would likely need to carve out any US government-related deployments if a non-allied country acquirer emerged. Bloom's data center and enterprise focus makes this less acute, but it informs the CFIUS mitigation strategy.

### Precedent 4: Hyundai / Aptiv JV


Hyundai Motor Group, a South Korean multinational automotive manufacturer, obtained CFIUS clearance in connection with its $4 billion autonomous driving joint venture with Aptiv.


**Applicability:** Confirms Korean chaebols can get CFIUS clearance for large-scale US tech investments. The Hyundai-Aptiv case involved autonomous driving AI, not energy infrastructure, so the risk profile differs.

### Precedent 5: E.ON / Calpine (Blocked)

**Structure:** German utility E.ON attempted to acquire US electricity generation company Calpine. The deal faced regulatory challenges and ultimately did not proceed.

**Applicability:** Non-US utility acquisition of a US power generator is a politically sensitive transaction. A Bloom acquisition by a non-US entity would face similar scrutiny, particularly given Bloom's critical infrastructure deployments.

## NOTABLE ANOMALIES

### ANOMALY 1 — SK IS ACTIVELY EXITING, NOT ACCUMULATING ⚠️ CRITICAL

The most striking finding of this forensic review contradicts the narrative embedded in Bloom's public investor communications. While Bloom continues to cite SK ecoplant as a committed strategic partner, SK has:

- Sold 10,000,000 shares at $27.60 (July 10, 2025) via block trade — 
SK ecoplant said the sale was made to recoup investment following the expiration of a lock-up period

- Sold an additional 2,608,000 shares at $42.28 (August 2025) via Econovation
- 
SK, through Econovation, now shares voting and dispositive power over 10,883,701 Class A shares, representing 4.7% of outstanding Class A stock


The combined gross proceeds of these sales total approximately **$386 million** (
$110,266,240 + $276,000,000
). SK has recovered most of its $566M initial investment through these sales while retaining the commercial relationship and the Korean market distribution rights.

SK sold the first 10M shares at **$27.60** — approximately 65% below Bloom's stock price at the time of the Q3 2025 results (~$84-87 per share level implied by director compensation disclosures). This suggests SK was willing to accept a significant discount to exit quickly, which raises questions about their internal view of Bloom's long-term standalone valuation.

**This is the most counterintuitive finding in the entire analysis:** The company representing ~55% of Bloom's revenue is simultaneously cashing out of its equity position. The commercial relationship is deepening while the financial relationship is unwinding.

### ANOMALY 2 — THE IRREVOCABLE PROXY MEANS SK DOESN'T CONTROL ITS OWN VOTES


SK will give an irrevocable proxy to vote its shares to Bloom Energy.


This extraordinary provision — which appears in multiple SEC filings — means that for as long as the Investor Agreement is in effect, SK's shares are voted by **Bloom management**, not SK. Any acquirer assuming that SK's ~4.7% stake represents voting leverage available to SK would be incorrect. The proxy substantially limits SK's ability to use its equity stake as an independent blocking tool in any shareholder vote.

### ANOMALY 3 — THE "TAKE OR PAY" MODIFICATION

The 2021 agreement explicitly established purchase commitments "on a take or pay basis." The 2023 extension language shifted to "minimum purchase commitment," removing the explicit take-or-pay characterization. This subtle change in language is material: it may reduce Bloom's ability to collect liquidated damages if SK fails to meet the 500 MW commitment through 2027.


The PDA Restatement establishes SK ecoplant's purchase commitments for the next three years on a take or pay basis.


The 2023 extension: 
We further expanded our business partnership with SK ecoplant through the increase of SK ecoplant's purchase commitments for Bloom Energy products of 250 megawatts through 2027 and extended the timing of delivery of the remaining commitment under the original agreement.
 — Note: the "take or pay" language is absent in the 2023 extension filings.

### ANOMALY 4 — SK ECOPLANT'S DEBT CRISIS IS BEING MANAGED BY SELLING BLOOM SHARES

The timing of SK's Bloom share sales correlates precisely with SK ecoplant's acute financial distress. SK ecoplant needs cash for:
- Pre-IPO investor buyout obligations (1 trillion won due by July 2026)
- Debt reduction (238% → 233% ratio, still dangerously elevated)
- Funding ongoing operations during net-loss period


The proposed merger was aimed at shoring up SK Ecoplant, a debt-laden company that had spearheaded mergers and acquisitions under the group's "Deep Change" initiative over the past few years.


The Bloom stake — representing ~$386M in realized proceeds from the two block sales — appears to be functioning as an ATM for SK ecoplant's balance sheet management. This creates a dynamic where SK may continue to sell Bloom shares as needed for liquidity, potentially driving SK's stake below 5% (and losing the board seat) with no strategic intent behind the dilution.

### ANOMALY 5 — THE "PREFERRED DISTRIBUTOR" TERMINOLOGY IS DELIBERATELY VAGUE

Bloom's public disclosures describe SK ecoplant as Korea's "exclusive distributor" but the formal agreements use "Preferred Distributor." These are legally distinct. The 2021 expansion extended SK to "preferred distributor globally... in markets where SK ecoplant has a significant presence and competitive advantage." 
SK ecoplant will be a preferred distributor globally for Bloom Energy solid oxide fuel cells (SOFC) and solid oxide electrolyzers (SOEC) in markets where SK ecoplant has a significant presence and competitive advantage.


"Preferred" is not "exclusive." Bloom theoretically retains the right to work with other distributors in markets where SK lacks presence. This asymmetry — Bloom calling it "exclusive" in investor presentations while the contracts say "preferred" — is a discrepancy that warrants scrutiny in due diligence.

### ANOMALY 6 — THE ECONOVATION STRUCTURE ADDS COMPLEXITY TO ANY NEGOTIATION

The 2023 filings reveal that SK's Bloom stake is held through a layered structure: SK ecoplant → Econovation LLC (Delaware) ← Korean PE Fund (Blooming Green Energy Limited / ESG Blooming PEF). This means multiple Korean financial investors have an economic interest in the Econovation holding vehicle, separate from SK ecoplant's strategic interest. Any acquirer needing to negotiate with "SK" about the commercial relationship may find that Econovation's financial investors have different incentives — they want liquidity and returns, not strategic alignment.

### ANOMALY 7 — THE KOREAN JV IS A VARIABLE INTEREST ENTITY CONSOLIDATED ON BLOOM'S BALANCE SHEET

Bloom consolidates the Korean JV as a VIE. This means Bloom bears the economic risks and rewards of the JV. 
Bloom has a variable interest entity related to a joint venture in the Republic of Korea, which represents a portion of the consolidated balances recorded within these financial statement line items.
 An acquirer of Bloom acquires this JV, complete with SK ecoplant's embedded operating roles (landlord, lender, staffing provider). Unwinding SK ecoplant from the Korean JV would be operationally complex and potentially expensive.

### ANOMALY 8 — THE ELECTROLYTE LOCALIZATION INVOLVES A THIRD COMPANY, NOT SK


Attendees included Se-jun Kim, Vice President of BloomSKfuelcell (the JV entity).
 The electrolyte producer is KV Materials, a separate Korean manufacturer. This suggests Bloom is deliberately not concentrating manufacturing IP within the SK joint structure itself, instead using the JV as an assembly facility while qualifying independent suppliers for components. This is a smart IP protection strategy that limits SK's ability to manufacture independently.

### ANOMALY 9 — BLOOM'S 2024 10-K ACKNOWLEDGES KOREAN WON RISK


The volatility of the Korean Won, as experienced in the past several months, may impact our commercial efforts in the region.
 With ~55% of revenue coming from Korea, currency risk is existential. A strong dollar weakens the economics of SK's Bloom Energy Server investments. This creates structural pressure on the relationship that is not reflected in the partnership's public narrative.

### ANOMALY 10 — SK ECOPLANT'S PLANNED IPO MAY FORCE FURTHER BLOOM STAKE REDUCTION


SK Ecoplant Co.'s plan to go public next year has met a backlash from its financial investors.
 As SK ecoplant prepares for its 2026 IPO, Korean stock exchange listing standards, IFRS reporting requirements, and investor pressure to improve the debt-to-equity ratio may force SK to further monetize the Bloom stake. The Bloom stake is carried at approximately book value; selling additional shares at current market prices could generate substantial accounting gains that improve SK ecoplant's IPO valuation metrics.

**If SK ecoplant sells enough shares to drop below 5%, the standstill expires and the board seat is lost** — potentially freeing SK to either (a) approach Bloom with a buyout proposal unburdened by the standstill's restrictions, or (b) exit entirely and operate purely as a commercial partner.
