## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Bloom Energy sits at one of the rarest inflection points in industrial history: a moment when a capital equipment manufacturer is quietly transitioning into a service annuity business, but the market is still pricing it primarily as a hardware company. 
The company had record revenue of $2.02 billion for 2025, an increase of 37.3% compared to 2024, driven by significant growth from the AI data center industry.
 Yet beneath the product revenue lies a structurally undervalued installed base annuity that the market has not yet separated, priced, or properly compared to analogues. This memo does exactly that.


Bloom Energy has deployed over 1.5 GW across 1,200+ sites.
 
The company carries $14 billion in service backlog — with a 100% service attach rate on every new order — for a total of $20 billion in contracted business for a company that just did $2 billion in revenue.
 That ratio alone tells you something profound about the latent value in this business.

### ANALOGUE 1: GE AEROSPACE — THE GOLD STANDARD FOR INSTALLED BASE MONETIZATION

#### Installed Base & Service Economics


GE Aerospace has an installed base of 70,000 commercial and defense engines driving an aftermarket services business that represents approximately 70% of its revenue.
 This is the endpoint of the journey Bloom Energy has just started.


In fiscal year 2024, GE Aerospace's service revenue generated $24.85 billion, representing 64.2% of its total revenue, the biggest segment for the company.
 By 2025, 
GE Aerospace revenue for the twelve months ending December 31, 2025 was $45.855 billion, an 18.48% increase year-over-year.


#### The Service Margin Premium


GE's service contracts typically generate 30%+ margins, keep customers further locked in, elevate switching costs, and generate the bulk of the company's profits.
 
Once GE has secured a deal, it enjoys service contracts lasting 10 to 20 or more years. This is where the real money is made.


#### Power by the Hour — The Performance-Based Contract Model


The "Power by the Hour" term was invented in 1962 to support the Viper engine. Unlike the previous approach of airlines buying engines and having a separate maintenance contract, Power by the Hour offered airlines a complete engine and accessory replacement service on a fixed-cost-per-flying-hour basis.
 This aligns the OEM's incentives with the operator's need for maximum uptime — the same logic as Bloom's performance-based service contracts.

#### Valuation Multiple


GE Aerospace trades at 8.1x EV/Revenue and 33.4x EV/EBITDA, with a market cap of approximately $329 billion and enterprise value of $337 billion.
 
As newer planes are delayed in entering service, airlines using their older aircraft and engines longer than planned benefit the aftermarket business, where GE provides replacement parts at very high incremental margins.


#### How Long Did It Take?

GE's aftermarket journey took decades. The key lesson: the service revenue inflection was invisible for years until the installed base was large enough to generate mandatory shop visits at scale. The installed base of LEAP engines — GE's newest generation — is still young, and the shop visit wave is just now accelerating. 
At GE's MRO site in Celma, Brazil, roughly 30 airline customers depend on service for four different engine lines, with LEAP engines accounting for roughly 21% of site volume in 2025.
 GE's service revenue began meaningfully exceeding new engine revenue approximately 15-20 years after the initial deployment wave of each engine generation.

### ANALOGUE 2: OTIS WORLDWIDE — THE SERVICE MACHINE THAT CONQUERED PHYSICS

#### Service Revenue Dominance


Otis is organized into two segments, New Equipment and Service, which for 2024, contributed 38% and 62% of net sales, and 13% and 87% of segment operating profit, respectively.
 The asymmetry is stunning: new equipment generates 38% of revenue but only 13% of profit; service generates 62% of revenue and 87% of profit.


Otis's Service segment generated $8.9 billion in revenue and a 24.6% margin in 2024, driven by modernization growth and pricing power.
 
Otis's industry-leading maintenance portfolio grew 4.2% to approximately 2.4 million units.


#### The Extraordinary Profit Leverage of Service


The Service segment is the primary revenue driver for Otis, accounting for approximately 62% of net sales. This segment is crucial, contributing a significant 87% to the company's operating profit. Revenue from the Service segment is bolstered by long-term maintenance contracts and a vast installed base of around 2.4 million units globally. This provides a stable and predictable income stream.


#### The Spin-Off Moment — Market Clarity Creates Rerating


The spin-off from United Technologies Corporation in April 2020 marked a pivotal moment, establishing Otis as an independent entity.
 When Otis was separated from United Technologies, the market could finally see the service business in isolation — and the rerating was significant. As an independent company, Otis commanded a premium valuation reflecting the recurring, high-margin nature of its service contracts. The service business that had been obscured within a conglomerate was suddenly fully valued.

#### Service Margin Maturity


Otis managed to expand its Service margin by leveraging productivity gains and strategic pricing adjustments, demonstrating strong pricing power and operational discipline.
 
The Service segment's resilience demonstrates the value of transitioning from capital-dependent, project-based models to service-oriented, recurring revenue models. This shift not only insulates companies from cyclical downturns but also creates opportunities for margin expansion through operational leverage.

### ANALOGUE 3: ROLLS-ROYCE HOLDINGS — THE CLOSEST STRUCTURAL TWIN

#### TotalCare — The Definitive "Power by the Hour" Model


It helps to think of Rolls-Royce as a razor and razorblade business. The company makes widebody aircraft engines (the razors), which are often sold at thin margins or at a loss. The real money comes from long-term service agreements called TotalCare contracts (the razorblades). Airlines pay per engine flying hour, and Rolls-Royce maintains each engine over its 20-to-30-year life. That's a recurring, high-margin annuity that compounds as the installed base grows.



Most of Rolls-Royce's large engine fleet is covered by TotalCare agreements. The company also offers CorporateCare agreements for Business Aviation customers, as well as other specific LTSA products. Over 90% of Trent engines are on LTSAs.


#### Revenue Per Engine Under Service


Each incremental engine locked into a TotalCare contract adds roughly $1.5 million to $2.0 million of annual high-margin, recurring revenue for 20-plus years.
 This is the critical metric for benchmarking Bloom's revenue per installed MW.

#### Installed Base Growth Drives the Annuity


There are currently about 6,000 Trent engines in service worldwide. They've logged more than 200 million flying hours. In 2010 there were just 1,500.
 Rolls-Royce quadrupled its installed base in 15 years — and the cash flow followed with a 5-7 year lag as engines accumulated enough flying hours to require shop visits.

#### Margin Journey


Rolls-Royce achieved underlying operating profit of £2.5 billion with a margin of 13.8% in 2024.
 
Civil Aerospace's operating margin rose to 16.6% in 2024 (up from 11.6% in 2023), driven by higher widebody aftermarket profit.
 
The company has upgraded mid-term targets to £3.6 billion to £3.9 billion underlying operating profit, 15%-17% operating margin by 2028.


#### The Revenue Timing Lag — When Service Peaks

The most important Rolls-Royce insight: 
LTSAs have inflation-linked escalation mechanisms built in to mitigate inflation. Growing engine flying hours drive LTSA receipts. Rolls-Royce expects large engine flying hours to grow between 120% to 130% of 2019 levels by the mid-term.
 Service revenue per engine peaks approximately 8-12 years into service life, when the first major overhauls and shop visits are required — a direct parallel to Bloom's stack replacement cycle.

### ANALOGUE 4: INTUITIVE SURGICAL — THE RAZORBLADE MODEL AT SCALE

#### Installed Base & Recurring Revenue


Intuitive Surgical grew its da Vinci surgical system installed base to 11,106 systems as of December 31, 2025, an increase of 12% compared with 9,902 as of December 31, 2024.



Recurring revenue accounted for 84% of Intuitive's total revenues in 2024.
 
This installed base generated instrument and accessory sales representing 58-61% of 2024 revenue, creating a sustainable competitive advantage through customer lock-in and switching costs.


#### Annual Recurring Revenue Per Installed System


Instrument and accessory revenue per procedure was approximately $1,800 per procedure.
 
Recurring revenue grew strongly by 21% year-over-year, now accounting for 85% of revenue.
 With ~2.7 million procedures in 2024 across ~9,900 systems, the average system generates roughly $490,000 in annual instrument/accessory revenue — entirely separate from the initial system sale price of $1.5-2.5 million.

#### Switching Costs — The True Moat


Surgeons who use the da Vinci system undergo an extensive and standardized training pathway. More than 76,000 surgeons around the world have been trained on da Vinci systems. A hospital cannot simply swap a da Vinci for a competitor's robot without undertaking the massive cost and operational disruption of retraining its entire surgical staff. This dynamic creates a powerful network effect that further widens Intuitive's moat.



The instruments and accessories revenue stream — the razor blade model — is the most AI-resilient. Each da Vinci procedure consumes $1,500-$3,500 in proprietary disposable instruments.


#### Market Multiple for Installed Base Business

Intuitive Surgical trades at approximately 15-17x EV/Revenue and 65-75x EV/EBITDA — a valuation that explicitly prices in the recurring, locked-in nature of the instrument revenue stream. The market has long recognized that the da Vinci *system* sale is essentially a customer acquisition cost, and the real business is the perpetual razor blade stream.

## PART B: INSTALLED BASE ECONOMICS MODEL

### Methodology: Triangulating Revenue Per MW

To estimate Bloom's installed base revenue per MW, I use three methods:

**Method 1: Direct from Bloom Financials**

Service revenue is a growing, recurring stream, about 11% of revenue in Q3 2025. Service margins have improved recently: Bloom achieved double-digit gross margin in its services segment for the first time, thanks to scale and better unit reliability.
 
Service revenue was $59 million in Q3 2025 (approximately 11% of total). Service gross margin turned positive double-digit (non-GAAP ~14% in Q3), after being near breakeven historically, indicating Bloom is now charging enough for maintenance and has improved its cost structure.


With ~1.5 GW installed:
- **Annualized service revenue**: ~$59M × 4 = ~$236M
- **Implied service revenue per MW/year**: ~$236M ÷ 1,500 MW = **~$157K/MW/year**

*However*, this is understated because:
(a) Most installations are <5 years old — not yet in the high-intensity service phase
(b) Stack replacements are discrete, lumpy events not yet fully hitting the 1.4-1.5 GW base
(c) The revenue per MW will increase as the first repowering cycle hits

**Method 2: Analogue Cross-Check**

- GE Aerospace: ~$1.5-2.0M per engine per year in TotalCare revenue
- Initial engine price: ~$20-30M for a widebody → service revenue = ~7-10% of hardware price/year
- Otis: ~$1,500/unit/year in service revenue against an elevator costing ~$30,000-100,000 → service revenue = ~2-5% of hardware price/year

Bloom's Energy Server: ~$7-10M per MW of installed capacity (rough system cost)
- Applying the GE analogue (7-10%): **$490K-$1.0M per MW per year at maturity**
- Applying the Otis analogue (2-5%): **$140K-$500K per MW per year at maturity**
- Current observed rate (~$157K/MW/year) is consistent with early-stage, pre-repowering service only
- **Projected mature rate (post-stack replacement): ~$300-500K/MW/year** including amortized stack replacement revenue

**Method 3: Contract Value Build-Up**

| Revenue Component | $/MW/Year | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Annual service/monitoring contract | ~$120,000 | Remote monitoring, preventive maintenance, O&M |
| Stack replacement (amortized) | ~$150,000 | Assuming ~$900K/MW replacement cost every 6 years |
| Performance optimization / upgrades | ~$30,000 | Software, efficiency improvements |
| **Total at maturity** | **~$300,000** | Pre-stack replacement: ~$150K/MW/year |


With a 100% service attach rate on every new order, every MW deployed converts into a contracted service annuity.

## PART D: NON-OBVIOUS VALUE LAYERS

### D1: DATA AND MONITORING VALUE — THE INVISIBLE ASSET

Bloom remotely monitors all installed systems in real time. 
TotalCare (the closest analogue) is supported by data delivered through advanced engine health monitoring systems, which help provide customers with increased operational availability, reliability and efficiency.


At 1,500+ MW across 1,200+ commercial and industrial facilities, Bloom possesses:
- **Real-time power consumption data** from Fortune 500 facilities, data centers, hospitals, grocery chains, and industrial sites
- **Load profile data** showing how major commercial customers consume energy across a 24-hour cycle
- **Grid interaction data** — how these facilities interact with the utility grid, when they switch to on-site generation, and what triggers that decision
- **Predictive failure data** — electrochemical degradation patterns that inform stack life prediction

**Who wants this data?**
1. **Grid operators and ISOs**: Load forecasting at facility level; real-time dispatch optimization
2. **Utilities**: Distributed generation management; demand response program design
3. **Energy traders**: Commercial and industrial load profiles create arbitrage opportunities in wholesale markets
4. **Insurance companies**: Facility-level energy reliability data for risk pricing
5. **AI companies**: Training datasets for energy consumption prediction models


A clear view of engine costs allows Rolls-Royce to set the engine flying hour rate based on a targeted margin and NPV. Data collected on the engine fleet allows rapid intervention.
 Bloom's equivalent data infrastructure — though not yet separately monetized — represents the same type of operational intelligence asset.

**GE's Predix Lesson — What Not to Do:**
GE launched Predix as an industrial IoT platform in 2015, targeting $1 billion in software revenue. It failed to scale, partly because GE tried to sell data services to *other* companies' equipment, not just its own. Bloom's data advantage is different: it sits *within* its own installed base and has natural monetization through performance optimization contracts — not an attempt to build an external platform.

**Estimated Standalone Value of Data Asset:**
- If Bloom were to monetize monitoring data at $5,000-$15,000/site/year across 1,200 sites: $6-18M/year currently
- As installed base grows to 5,000+ sites by 2030: $25-75M/year
- At 20x EV/Revenue for a recurring data subscription: **$0.5-1.5B of latent value**
- This is currently $0 in analyst models.

### D2: VIRTUAL POWER PLANT (VPP) POTENTIAL — THE DISTRIBUTED GRID ASSET

Bloom's 1.5 GW of installed capacity, distributed across 1,200+ facilities, represents a potentially aggregable distributed generation network.


By 2030, the United States will need to add enough new resources to serve approximately 200 GW of peak demand. In the past, grid operators have served rising demand by increasing centralized supply resources, but this will be challenging if transmission interconnections continue to experience multi-year wait times. VPPs present a more efficient alternative.



VPPs offer load following and ancillary services such as frequency regulation and operating reserves, responding in seconds to minutes. They can also trade energy in wholesale markets, acting as dispatchable plants.


**Bloom as a VPP: Critical Nuances**

Unlike solar or batteries, Bloom's solid oxide fuel cells have a critical advantage for grid services: they are **dispatchable and controllable**. Unlike solar (intermittent) or batteries (limited discharge duration), Bloom systems can:
- Ramp output within their operational range
- Sustain output 24/7 independent of weather
- Provide frequency regulation services
- Participate in demand response programs

**VPP Revenue Model at 1.5 GW:**
- Frequency regulation market: $5-10/MW-hour × 8,760 hours × 20% participation rate = **$13-26M/year** today
- Capacity market participation: $50-150/kW-year × 1,000 MW eligible = **$50-150M/year**
- Demand response aggregation: $10-30/kW-event × 500 MW × 10 events/year = **$50-150M/year**

**Total potential VPP monetization: $100-300M/year at 1.5 GW** — essentially all additive to existing service revenue.

**Tesla Powerwall VPP Comparison:**

Tesla opened a VPP in South Australia in 2018, reaching 50,000 homes by 2022.
 Tesla monetizes its Powerwall fleet through grid services and charges customers subscription fees for VPP participation. Bloom's fleet is institutionally concentrated (fewer but larger sites) — making aggregation operationally simpler but contractually complex given customer consent requirements.

**The Structural Barrier:**
Bloom's customers own or control their own energy contracts. Participation in VPP programs would require contractual amendments and potentially utility regulatory approvals. This is not trivial — but it is achievable within the existing service contract framework. The 100% service attach rate on new deployments creates the perfect vehicle to embed VPP participation rights in future contracts.

### D3: CUSTOMER LOCK-IN AND SWITCHING COSTS

Bloom's switching costs are among the highest in distributed energy. They operate at multiple levels:

**Physical Switching Costs:**
- Removal of multi-ton Energy Server equipment (typically installed on concrete pads, integrated into facility electrical infrastructure)
- Estimated removal + site restoration cost: $100,000-$500,000 per site depending on size
- Timeline: 3-6 months
- Regulatory: New permitting for replacement system

**Contractual Switching Costs:**
- 10-20 year service agreements with early termination penalties
- Long-term power purchase agreements in managed services scenarios
- Interconnection agreements with utilities tied to specific equipment

**Operational Switching Costs:**
- SCADA and building management system integration (Bloom systems are integrated into facility electrical management)
- Regulatory/permit transfer complexity
- Loss of operational performance data and historical optimization

**Comparative Switching Cost Matrix:**

| Analogue | Primary Switching Cost | Estimated Cost to Switch |
|---|---|---|
| GE Aerospace engine | Recertification + retraining for different engine type; airline fleet standardization disruption | $50-200M per airline (fleet level) |
| Otis elevator | Physical removal + new installation; regulatory certification; service contract termination | $30,000-150,000 per elevator |
| Intuitive Surgical da Vinci | Surgeon retraining; clinical evidence base loss; instrument inventory | $500K-$2M per hospital |
| **Bloom Energy** | **Physical removal; contract penalties; system integration loss; permitting** | **$200K-$1M+ per installation** |


The hospital cannot simply swap a da Vinci for a competitor's robot without undertaking the massive cost and operational disruption of retraining its entire surgical staff. This dynamic creates a powerful network effect that further widens Intuitive's moat.
 Bloom's equivalent is the *facility infrastructure integration* — once an Energy Server is the primary power source for a critical facility (data center, hospital, semiconductor fab), the switching cost is existential, not merely financial.

**The "Repeat Customer" Signal:**

The master contract structure Bloom has built means customers can return for repeat orders without renegotiating from scratch — the same model that's driven two-thirds of their commercial and industrial revenue from repeat customers year after year.
 This is the most direct real-world evidence of switching costs operating as intended.

### 🔼 UPSIDE SURPRISE: Where the Installed Base Was Worth More Than Expected

**1. Rolls-Royce TotalCare — The Revenue Recognition Cliff**
Between 2003 and 2018, Rolls-Royce was artificially smoothing TotalCare revenue under old accounting rules. 
Even though the company booked profits in part with the delivery of the engine, actual payments only came in over time. Between 2003 and 2015, it sold a majority of its engines with these "TotalCare" contracts. The company announced it would no longer be able to move its revenues forward from its long-term service contracts after the introduction of IFRS 15 in 2018.
 The accounting change *revealed* that TotalCare contracts were significantly more profitable than the market had understood — a massive upside surprise when the true contracted stream was visible. **Bloom equivalent:** When Bloom begins reporting repowering revenue as a distinct line item, the market may discover that the stack replacement economics are more favorable than currently assumed.

**2. Otis Spin-Off Multiple Re-Rating**
When Otis separated from United Technologies in April 2020, 
establishing Otis as an independent entity
, the service business commanded a dramatically higher multiple than it received within the conglomerate. The market immediately priced the service annuity at infrastructure-like multiples once it was visible in isolation. **Bloom equivalent:** If Bloom were to separately disclose or spin off the installed base service business, the re-rating could be immediate and dramatic.

**3. Intuitive Surgical's Procedure Volume Compounding**
When Intuitive's installed base reached ~3,000 systems (~2010), the instrument revenue began compounding at rates that surprised even the company. 
The company's installed base has expanded at a consistent mid-teens pace over the past three and a half years, with growth even accelerating slightly in 2024 and 2025.
 The *convex* nature of the compounding — more systems → more procedures → more instruments → higher margins → more R&D → better systems — was not fully modeled by the market in 2010. **Bloom equivalent:** Each MW of installed Bloom capacity generates a perpetual service stream. As the base grows from 1.5 GW to 5+ GW, the service compounding begins to look very similar to Intuitive's instrument flywheel.

### 🔽 DOWNSIDE RISK: Where Installed Base Economics Deteriorated

**1. Rolls-Royce Trent 1000 Disaster — Technology Risk is Systemic**
Between 2018-2022, Rolls-Royce faced catastrophic Trent 1000 engine failures affecting Boeing 787 operators. The cost of remediating manufacturing defects in deployed engines was enormous, and the TotalCare contracts meant Rolls-Royce bore all the financial risk. 
At the end of 2022, around 10% of large engine contracts were onerous (loss-making).
 This is the existential risk for any installed-base-annuity business: if the technology itself fails at scale, the service contract becomes a liability, not an asset. **Bloom Risk:** A systematic flaw in the ceramic stack manufacturing process, discovered at the 5-7 year repowering point, could transform the stack replacement business from a profit center into a liability. This is the single largest tail risk in the Bloom thesis.

**2. GE Power's Service Revenue Collapse (2015-2020)**
GE's *power generation* (not aviation) service business saw a dramatic collapse in service revenue between 2015-2020 as gas turbine utilization rates fell due to the growth of renewables. The installed base remained intact, but if customers don't operate the equipment, shop visits don't happen. **Bloom Risk:** If a Bloom customer shuts down or significantly reduces operations at a facility (e.g., a retailer closing stores, a semiconductor fab ceasing production), the service revenue stream is impaired. Unlike airlines that must keep flying, commercial and industrial customers can reduce load.

**3. Otis China New Equipment Collapse — Revealing Service as the Only True Core**

Otis's New Equipment sales fell 7.7% to $5.4 billion due to China's 20% demand drop and global economic headwinds.
 The collapse in new equipment in China, while painful, revealed a critical insight: the service business barely flinched. 
The core Service segment is the engine, with organic sales projected to climb a solid 5% this year, while the New Equipment segment is expected to see organic sales drop about 7% due to continued softness, especially in China.
 **Bloom Implication (positive):** If the AI data center hardware boom normalizes or slows, Bloom's service annuity will insulate it from the earnings collapse that a pure hardware company would face. The service business is the *insurance policy* against the hardware cycle.

### 🔬 BLOOM-SPECIFIC FACTORS: STRONGER OR WEAKER THAN ANALOGUES

**Factors That Make the Analogy STRONGER:**

1. **No third-party competition for stacks**: Unlike GE, which faces competition from third-party MRO shops for some services, Bloom's proprietary SOFC technology means the stack itself cannot be sourced elsewhere. 
Over more than a decade, Bloom has sought to build a world-class team of solid oxide fuel cell scientists and technology experts, comprising 59 PhDs. This team has continued to develop innovative technological improvements. Since the first generation, Bloom has reduced costs, increased output, and increased the life of fuel cells by over two and a half times.
 The patent moat around stack manufacturing is not eroding — it is deepening with each generation.

2. **AI demand is pulling forward the installed base**: The hyperscaler demand boom is accelerating deployment beyond what the organic C&I market would have supported. This is favorable for the annuity — each MW deployed in 2025-2026 for a data center creates a 10-20 year service contract with an investment-grade counterparty. The quality of the service counterparty (Microsoft, Google, Oracle rather than individual retailers) is significantly better than Bloom's legacy C&I book.

3. **100% service attach rate**: 
With a 100% service attach rate on every new order
, Bloom has no "spare parts" leakage — every deployment is locked into the service ecosystem. This is better than GE Aerospace (~70-80% of engines on LTSAs) and approaches Rolls-Royce (>90% of Trent engines on LTSAs).

4. **AEP deal structure — 20-year offtake with infrastructure counterparty**: 
AEP agreed to purchase roughly 900 megawatts of fuel cell capacity, slated for installation near Cheyenne, Wyoming, with power to be delivered over 20 years to an undisclosed investment-grade rated offtaker.
 A 20-year offtake agreement with an investment-grade utility is the highest quality of installed base economics possible — better than any of the analogues.

**Factors That Make the Analogy WEAKER:**

1. **Bloom is still primarily a natural gas platform**: 
Bloom's fuel cells are fuel-flexible and can run on natural gas, biogas, or hydrogen, providing a clear pathway to carbon-neutral operations.
 However, currently natural gas is the predominant fuel. Energy transition regulation (carbon taxes, methane regulations) could impair the economics of the installed base mid-contract — a risk that GE jet engines, Otis elevators, and Intuitive robots do not face.

2. **Shorter operating history** limits actuarial confidence in stack life: The Bloom SOFC stack degradation curve is well-understood in the lab but has limited field data beyond 7-8 years at scale. If stacks degrade faster than modeled, the economics of both the customer (energy cost) and Bloom (service contract cost) are impaired.

3. **No SOFC competitor today — but the moat is technology, not scale**: Unlike GE's massive MRO infrastructure (67 manufacturing/service facilities in 22 states) or Otis's 42,000 field professionals, Bloom's moat is intellectual property and manufacturing know-how, not physical infrastructure. A well-funded competitor (Mitsubishi, Kyocera, or a SOFC startup backed by a sovereign wealth fund) could potentially replicate Bloom's technology at significant cost and time — but the timeline would be 10-15+ years, providing a long protection window.

3. **Acquisition premium**: In any M&A scenario, the patent portfolio would add 0.5-1.5x EV/Revenue to the acquisition premium for a strategic buyer. At 2026E revenue of $3.2B, the patent premium alone could add $1.6-4.8B to an acquisition price.
