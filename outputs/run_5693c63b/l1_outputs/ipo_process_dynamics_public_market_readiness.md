The shift from "focused on our development plan" to "variety of options" in the six weeks since the confidential filing is not random word choice. Executives preparing exclusively for IPOs do not say "variety of options." This language is almost certainly the result of legal coaching — the SEC's gun-jumping rules prohibit certain public statements that could be construed as pre-marketing the IPO. "Variety of options" is the lawyer-approved phrase for "we have a dual-track process running and we cannot confirm or deny anything." This phrase, combined with the 55-day silence on a bookrunner mandate, is the clearest possible public signal of a dual-track process consistent with securities law constraints.

**🔴 ANOMALY 4: THE DOMESTIC CONTENT/FEOC QUESTION ON TURBODEN EQUIPMENT**


Facilities that begin construction on or after June 16, 2025, are subject to domestic content thresholds for the ITC: 45% for 2025, 50% for 2026, and 55% for 2027+.
 Turboden is an Italian company (MHI Group subsidiary) supplying 180 MW of ORC equipment for Cape Station Phase II. This creates a genuine domestic content question:

- Cape Station Phase I (construction began 2023) → grandfathered under pre-OBBBA rules; domestic content threshold less stringent
- Cape Station Phase II and future phases → subject to the new thresholds; Turboden's Italian-manufactured ORC turbines may not qualify as domestic content

This is a material S-1 risk factor that the SEC will almost certainly raise in comments. The resolution is likely: (a) Turboden manufactures components domestically; (b) alternative equipment suppliers are identified; or (c) the credit is claimed under the PTC (§45Y) rather than ITC (§48E), where domestic content requirements differ. This is not a deal-killer, but it is a genuine legal/financial engineering question that will take multiple SEC comment rounds to resolve — a structural reason for the confidential period to extend.
