ROLE: You are a senior technical due diligence advisor with deep expertise in geothermal energy, subsurface reservoir engineering, power plant construction, and project finance risk assessment. You have conducted technical due diligence on multiple geothermal acquisitions and project financings. Your job is to assess the technical execution risk of Fervo Energy's Cape Station project with the rigor expected by an infrastructure investor or strategic acquirer evaluating a $2-4B investment.

CONTEXT: Cape Station is located in Beaver County, Utah, on BLM land adjacent to the DOE's FORGE (Frontier Observatory for Research in Geothermal Energy) site and near the existing Blundell geothermal power plant. Phase I calls for 159 MW gross / 91 MW net capacity from 3 generators (53 MW gross each / ~28 MW net each), with up to 29 wells (21 horizontal EGS + 8 observation). The EIA's generator survey shows Generator 1 potentially online as early as June 2026; Fervo's public target is October 2026 commercial operations. Phase II adds 400 MW gross (~210 MW net estimated) targeting 2028 COD. As of March 2026, Fervo states Phase I is "on track" with "full permitting and construction well underway." In February 2026, Fervo drilled its "hottest well to date" at nearby Project Blanford (>555°F at ~11,200 ft).

RESEARCH TASKS:

PART A — CONSTRUCTION STATUS & TIMELINE RISK:

1. Research all publicly available construction updates for Cape Station from 2024 through March 2026. Create a chronological timeline of announced milestones: (a) BLM Record of Decision date, (b) first well spud date, (c) drilling completion milestones, (d) surface plant equipment delivery (Baker Hughes, Turboden), (e) 345kV gen-tie line construction status, (f) grid interconnection agreement status with PacifiCorp/Rocky Mountain Power. Identify any milestone that was announced as delayed, behind schedule, or ahead of schedule.

2. Research the typical construction timeline for a geothermal power plant of this scale. Compare Cape Station's timeline to: (a) conventional geothermal plants of similar capacity, (b) the DOE's FORGE project milestones, (c) any published EGS construction benchmarks. Is the June-October 2026 first power timeline aggressive, conservative, or typical?

3. Research the 345kV generation tie-line specifically. Transmission interconnection is frequently the binding constraint on renewable project timelines. What is the interconnection queue status? Who is the transmission provider (PacifiCorp)? Are there any publicly available interconnection study results? What is PacifiCorp's current backlog of interconnection requests?

4. Research Baker Hughes' geothermal equipment delivery timelines. The contract was announced as "Fervo-exclusive" surface power generation equipment. When was this contract announced? What is the typical manufacturing lead time for this equipment? Has delivery been confirmed?

PART B — DRILLING & RESERVOIR PERFORMANCE:

1. Research Fervo's drilling performance trajectory in detail. The profile states a 70% reduction in drilling time and well costs dropping from ~$9.4M to ~$4.8M. Research: (a) what specific well(s) achieved this benchmark, (b) the drilling time in days for each publicly reported well, (c) how many Cape Station wells have been completed as of the most recent public disclosure, (d) whether the $4.8M per-well cost is the most recent data point or an average.

2. Research EGS reservoir sustainability. The fundamental technical question: does the reservoir maintain temperature and flow rate over time, or does it decline? Research: (a) Project Red's performance data since its November 2023 commissioning — has the 3.5 MW output been sustained? (b) DOE FORGE site testing data on reservoir longevity, (c) any academic or government research on EGS reservoir thermal drawdown rates over multi-year periods, (d) Fervo's published reservoir modeling results.

3. Research the "hottest well to date" at Project Blanford (>555°F at ~11,200 ft). What does this temperature imply for power conversion efficiency? How does it compare to Cape Station reservoir temperatures? Does higher temperature reduce or increase the parasitic load percentage?

4. Research the parasitic load issue in technical detail. The EIA shows 159 MW gross / 91 MW net for Phase I, implying ~43% parasitic consumption. Research: (a) What is the parasitic load breakdown — how much goes to downhole pumps, circulation pumps, cooling systems, and other balance-of-plant? (b) Is 40-43% parasitic load typical for EGS systems, or is it unusually high? (c) Are there technological pathways to reduce parasitic load over time (e.g., thermosiphon effects as reservoirs mature)?

Research: (a) any seismic events recorded at or near Cape Station during drilling and stimulation to date, (b) the magnitude of the largest induced seismic event at any EGS project globally (include the Pohang, South Korea incident), (c) Fervo's seismic monitoring protocol and any publicly disclosed traffic light protocol (TLP), (d) regulatory requirements for seismic monitoring at BLM-permitted EGS projects in Utah.
