## I. THE OPPORTUNITY

CPUC regulatory filings show Fervo Energy has 398 MW contracted with Southern California Edison — 24% more than the 320 MW the company has publicly disclosed — representing approximately $40-50M in incremental annual revenue that appears in no published model.^1 ^2 ^3 Combined with a confirmed $107/MWh price on its NV Energy/Google contract and >82% capacity factors validated by CPUC documents, Fervo's contracted revenue base supports a stabilized EBITDA of $150-177M at Cape Station alone, implying $2.1-2.8B in enterprise value at peer geothermal multiples — before assigning any value to a 16 GW permitted pipeline or the first-mover regulatory franchise Fervo co-architected with Google.^4 ^5 ^6 The $2-3B IPO target therefore prices the operating asset at approximately fair value and the platform at zero.^7 The comparison that makes this specific: Google paid $4.75B for Intersect Power's *intermittent* solar development pipeline on March 11, 2026, spinning off the operating assets.^8 Fervo offers firm, 24/7 power — the asset class for which hyperscalers are paying nuclear-equivalent pricing — and is valued at roughly half. What most market participants have not yet processed: EIA data shows Cape Station's first generating unit is registered for June 2026 grid interconnection, contemporaneous with the IPO — meaning public market investors will be pricing an operating asset, not a construction story.^9 The gap between the $2-3B IPO range and the $4.75B Intersect precedent represents $1.75-2.75B in potential value arbitrage, available only to a buyer who acts before the public S-1 filing, expected within 30-60 days.

## II. COMPANY SITUATION

Fervo Energy adapts horizontal drilling and hydraulic fracturing — the core technologies of the U.S. shale revolution — to create engineered geothermal reservoirs in hot dry rock at 8,000-15,000 ft depth. Its physical footprint spans three assets: **Cape Station** (Beaver County, Utah), a 500 MW gross / ~400 MW net project under active construction with Phase I (100 MW) targeting late 2026 and Phase II (400 MW) targeting 2028; **Corsac Station** (Nevada), a 115 MW development for Google data centers, online "later this decade"; and **Project Red** (Nevada), a 3.5 MW pilot operating since November 2023.^10 ^11 ^12 Cape Station is 100% contracted across four investment-grade counterparties under 15-year PPAs: SCE (398 MW per CPUC filings), Shell Energy (31 MW), Clean Power Alliance (~53 MW), and others.^13 The NV Energy/Google Corsac contract carries a confirmed $107/MWh price; SCE pricing remains redacted, but CPUC Staff found it was "the lowest geothermal price offered at the time of the solicitation" after overruling a Cal Advocates protest.^14 ^15 At stabilization, estimated annual revenue is $270-310M, with EBITDA margins of 55-65% — comparable to Ormat Technologies, the only public geothermal pure-play, which trades at ~16x EV/EBITDA.^16 ^17

Revenue today is effectively zero. Fervo has raised $1.69B through an unusually complex capital structure: at least 8-9 preferred stock series with cascading liquidation preferences, a $100M Mercuria corporate term loan (where Mercuria simultaneously holds equity — an unprecedented dual position), a $145.6M XRA bridge loan, and $175M in project-level preferred from Breakthrough Energy Catalyst and Centaurus Capital.^18 ^19 ^20

Ownership creates specific deal dynamics. Devon Energy confirmed a 15% equity stake in its Q4 2025 earnings — the largest disclosed strategic position — with subject matter experts physically embedded at Cape Station.^21 ^22 The Devon-Coterra $58B all-stock merger has closed, creating a combined shale operator with no strategic rationale for a minority geothermal holding.^23 B Capital led the $462M Series E at an implied ~$2.86B post-money; the IPO targets $2-3B, meaning Series E investors face break-even-to-loss outcomes at the range floor — almost certainly activating a QIPO price threshold that B Capital controls.^24 ^25 MHI (via wholly owned Turboden) supplies 300 MWe of ORC equipment across both Cape Station phases — 60% of total generating capacity — while holding Series D and E equity.^26 Google joined as a first-time equity investor in the same Series E round, alongside Mitsui & Co. with stated Japan EGS deployment intent.^27 ^28

The confidential S-1 was filed January 22, 2026 — nearly two months ago with no public version yet.^29 The company has held formal meetings with six banks — BofA, Barclays, Goldman Sachs, HSBC, RBC, and SMBC — though Goldman was reportedly mandated as early as July 2023 for a 2024 IPO that never materialized, which calibrates timeline confidence downward.^30 ^31 Critically, the OBBBA (enacted July 2025) preserved geothermal's IRA tax credits through 2033 while slashing wind and solar credits — a legislative moat that is structurally underappreciated and that insulates Fervo from the policy risk discount compressing other clean energy valuations.^32 Drilling costs have fallen from ~$9.4M per well at Project Red to under $4M in under 20 days — a ~57% cost reduction that Fervo's SVP confirmed is now averaging "mid-teens" days — transforming the economics of the entire 16 GW pipeline.^33

Where the narrative diverges from data: Fervo consistently describes Cape Station as "500 MW" without distinguishing gross from net. EIA data shows Phase I's three generators at 159 MW gross / ~91 MW net — a ~43% parasitic load from injection pumping and air-cooled condensation.^34 Phase II achieves substantially lower parasitic (~10-15%), yielding a blended ~20% across the full project and ~400 MW net delivered to the grid. Any model using 500 MW net overstates revenue by approximately 25%. Separately, the IPO valuation range has compressed from $2-4B (March 2025 discussions) to $2-3B (post-filing), a meaningful downward revision that has received almost no public attention and that tightens the governance friction around B Capital's conversion threshold.^35 ^36

## III. KEY FINDINGS

### CPUC Records Show 398 MW Contracted to SCE — 24% Above Fervo's Public Disclosure

**The Observation.** Fervo's June 2024 press release announced "the execution of two power purchase agreements totaling 320 MW with Southern California Edison."^37 This remains the company's last public statement of the SCE contract size. CPUC Resolution E-5404, approved July 2025, tells a different story: the contracts were amended to 62 MW (Cape Station 3, reduced from 70 MW) and 336 MW (Cape Station 4, increased from 250 MW), for a total of 398 MW.^38 The same resolution approved a price increase on the amended contracts — an extraordinarily rare upward renegotiation in utility regulation, signaling the original solicitation price was below actual EGS delivery cost.^39 SCE's proportional share of Cape Station capacity (~80%) was maintained through the 400→500 MW project upsize, consistent with contractual expansion rights.

**The Deal Implication.** At estimated PPA pricing of $90-100/MWh and 82% capacity factor, the undisclosed 78 MW increment generates approximately $40-50M in additional annual revenue — or $340-430M in 15-year NPV at an 8% discount rate.^40 When the S-1 reveals the 398 MW figure and actual SCE pricing, it will force a mechanical upward revision to every consensus revenue estimate. For a buyer conducting diligence now, this is a quantifiable information edge: the contracted revenue base is materially larger than the market believes.

**Key Uncertainty.** The price increase direction is confirmed but the magnitude remains redacted. Cal Advocates protested the original pricing as above-market; CPUC Staff approved it as the "lowest geothermal price offered."^41 The S-1 will contain definitive PPA pricing.

---

### Devon Holds 15% of Fervo — The Coterra Merger Creates a $58B Entity With No Strategic Interest in a Minority Geothermal Stake

**The Observation.** Devon Energy's Q4 2025 earnings disclosure confirms a 15% equity stake in Fervo, described as "a strategic hedge and technology play leveraging Devon's horizontal drilling and subsurface expertise."^42 Devon led the $244M Series D, has subject matter experts embedded with Fervo's teams to design and drill wells, and evidence suggests was advised by JPMorgan on the original investment.^43 ^44 JPMorgan is absent from the six-bank IPO roster — a pattern consistent with a strategic alternatives mandate rather than an ECM role.^45 The Devon-Coterra all-stock merger (~$58B) has closed; the combined entity targets $1B in annual synergies from Delaware Basin and Marcellus operations.^46 ^47

**The Deal Implication.** A ~$430M illiquid geothermal stake is a rounding error for a $58B shale company — and a distraction for investor relations explaining shale synergies to O&G institutions. Devon is simultaneously the most informationally advantaged buyer (embedded staff, deep financial diligence) and the most motivated potential seller. If Devon acquires the remainder: the incremental cost for 85% at a $3.5B total valuation is ~$2.98B, approximately 15% of combined market cap — comparable in scale and strategic logic to Devon's 2021 WPX Energy acquisition.^48 If Devon sells its 15% block pre-IPO: it creates massive secondary supply pressure and potentially triggers ROFR cascades. Either path produces a transaction catalyst within 12-18 months.

**Key Uncertainty.** Devon's shareholder agreement — specifically ROFO/ROFR provisions and the QIPO consent structure — is not publicly disclosed and will be revealed in the S-1. Whether New Devon's board has communicated a hold-or-sell position to Fervo management is unknown.

### EIA Data Confirms First Power in June 2026 — Five Milestones Converge in a Single Six-Week Window

**The Observation.** The EIA's formal generator survey registers Cape Station's first unit — 53 MW gross, 28 MW net summer — for grid interconnection in June 2026, with two additional units expected January 2027.^49 This is a self-certification to the federal government, not Fervo marketing. The Shell Energy PPA (31 MW, commencing "in 2026") aligns precisely with Unit 1's 28 MW net summer capacity, confirming Shell as the first offtaker.^50 Mapping five independent clocks — public S-1 filing (April-May), IPO roadshow (May-June), first power (June), XRA bridge retirement (June), and Shell offtake delivery (mid-2026) — reveals convergence in a single 6-8 week window.^51 The three-unit staggered commissioning structure also means Phase I is not a single 100 MW switch-on event — it is a sequential ramp, with "full" 100 MW commercial operation arriving in January 2027, not October 2026.

**The Deal Implication.** This convergence fundamentally changes the IPO narrative. Investors will be pricing a company with its first generating unit on-grid and first PPA revenue flowing — not a pure construction story. For a strategic acquirer, the window to pre-empt this de-risking event is approximately 30-60 days. Post-first-power, Fervo's negotiating leverage increases substantially and the achievable IPO price likely moves toward the upper end of the $2-3B range, potentially clearing the ~$2.86B QIPO threshold. A four-week construction slip, however, means the roadshow begins before first power — converting a "proven" narrative into a "pending" one with material disclosure consequences.

**Key Uncertainty.** Construction language has softened incrementally — from "October 2026" (January sources) to "by end of 2026" (company) to "late 2026 or early 2027" (one March 2026 source).^52 No formal delay has been announced, but the 6-8 week convergence window has essentially zero buffer.
