CONTEXT:
Fervo Energy's Cape Station project in Beaver County, Utah uses an enhanced geothermal system (EGS) approach adapted from horizontal drilling and hydraulic fracturing techniques used in oil and gas. The project's Phase I is approximately 116 MW gross capacity. However, EGS systems require significant parasitic electrical loads — electricity consumed by downhole pumps (ESPs), surface circulation pumps, cooling systems, and well stimulation equipment. Industry sources and Fervo's own published data from its Project Red pilot (Milford, Utah) suggest parasitic loads of 30-40% for initial wells, potentially declining to ~20% at scale as the system optimizes. This means the "400 MW" or "500 MW" headline capacity figures may overstate net saleable power by 20-25%.

Simultaneously, Fervo has reported dramatic drilling cost improvements. CEO Tim Latimer has publicly stated that drilling times have fallen from ~40 days per well to under 20 days, with well costs reportedly dropping below $4M per well (compared to early costs of $7-10M+ per well). If confirmed, this represents a 50-60% reduction in the single largest capital cost component. However, no independent financial model or analyst has incorporated these improved drilling metrics into a project-level economic analysis.

These two dynamics — one negative (parasitic load reducing revenue) and one positive (drilling cost reduction improving returns) — partially offset each other. The net effect determines whether Fervo's true LCOE is competitive with solar+storage, wind+storage, and natural gas.

HYPOTHESIS TO INVESTIGATE:

If the drilling cost reduction is real and sustainable, Fervo's all-in LCOE could be $45-55/MWh (competitive with solar+4hr storage), which would support a significantly higher valuation multiple than the market currently assigns to geothermal assets.
