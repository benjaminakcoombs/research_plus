## PART II: TITANIUM IN HCl LEACHATE — THE DEFINITIVE FINDING

### 2.1 Ilmenite Dissolution Kinetics in HCl: Quantified

Round 3 searches retrieved the comprehensive ilmenite-HCl leaching literature with specific dissolution rates. The findings are unambiguous and severely adverse to Mechanism A:


The maximum dissolution of ilmenite was obtained on agitating ilmenite with HCl at a speed of 400 rpm for 300 min, resulting in **96.37% and 92.87% leaching of iron and titanium**, respectively.



A 6 M HCl concentration at 90°C facilitates **nearly 100% extraction of iron and titanium**.



An agitation speed of 450 rpm yielded a superior Fe extraction of about **92.32% and a 2.40% titanium loss** [into residue].


The third data point — only 2.40% Ti reporting to residue — means that under aggressive HCl leaching conditions (7.5 M HCl, 90°C, standard agitation), **97.6% of titanium dissolves into the leachate.** This is the dominant kinetic behavior of ilmenite in HCl.


It has been found that the leachability of iron increased linearly to 67% until the concentration of HCl was 20% and **further increased non-linearly** till the HCl concentration increased to 25%. On the other hand, **leachability of Ti increased with increasing HCl concentration to 20% and decreased with further increments.**


This last observation reveals the one controllable parameter: very high HCl concentrations (>20%) begin to suppress Ti dissolution while maintaining Fe dissolution. This is consistent with Ti hydrolysis forming insoluble TiO(OH)₂ or TiO₂·nH₂O at very high acid concentrations — but at the cost of massive acid consumption and TiO₂ fouling of the leach vessel. It is not a commercially viable approach to Ti removal at scale.

### 2.2 The Plagioclase-Ilmenite Co-Leach: A Partial but Unreliable Ti Suppression

The single most important nuance in the titanium leaching literature — and the one piece of evidence that could partially support Brimstone's process as designed — is the silica gel interference effect:


Mechanical activation enhanced the leaching reactivity of plagioclase and ilmenite with 20% HCl at 105°C, consistent with the lattice disorder and decreased particle size. The leaching of plagioclase was hardly affected by the existence of ilmenite, but **the leaching of ilmenite was inhibited when plagioclase co-existed in the sample**, which is attributed to silica gel produced from leached plagioclase blocking the surface of unleached ilmenite.



The leaching of plagioclase was hardly affected by the existence of ilmenite, but the leaching of ilmenite was inhibited when plagioclase co-existed in the sample, which is attributed to silica gel produced from leached plagioclase blocking the surface of unleached ilmenite. Furthermore, **a compact layer was formed on the surface of unreacted ilmenite particles**, thus retarding the ilmenite leaching.


This is a real physical mechanism — and it operates in precisely the mineral system Brimstone would be leaching (calcic plagioclase + ilmenite). If the silica gel film that forms as plagioclase dissolves is thick enough and forms fast enough relative to ilmenite dissolution kinetics, it could physically coat the ilmenite surface, blocking HCl access and keeping Ti in the solid.

**However, this mechanism has three critical limitations:**

1. It was studied specifically in the context of the Panzhihua titanium ore industry, where the **goal was to increase ilmenite leaching** (i.e., researchers were trying to overcome the inhibition). The degree of inhibition achieved was not characterized as eliminating Ti dissolution — only reducing it relative to a pure-ilmenite leach.

2. 
Extensive SiO₂ gel formation can make **the separation of pregnant leach solution from the leached residue impossible**. Consequently, any acid leaching of aluminosilicate materials must be optimized for avoidance of these gelation phenomena.
 The same silica gel that inhibits ilmenite leaching also creates severe solid-liquid separation problems for the aluminum-bearing leachate — a double-edged sword.

3. Mechanical activation (grinding) is simultaneously required by Brimstone's process for other reasons. 
Mechanical activation enhanced the leaching reactivity of both plagioclase and ilmenite.
 Grinding to improve alumina extraction will also accelerate Ti dissolution, working against the passivation effect.

### 2.3 The Back-Calculated Pre-Calcination Ti Specification: The Hardest Constraint

The Bureau of Mines concentration factor from Round 2 (4.73× Ti concentration upon ACH calcination) generates a pre-calcination target that can now be expressed quantitatively:

**Working backwards from SGA spec (TiO₂ ≤ 0.003% = 30 ppm) through the 4.73× concentration factor:**
- Maximum TiO₂ in ACH crystals before calcination: **30 ppm ÷ 4.73 = 6.3 ppm Ti** in the ACH

**Working backwards from ACH crystallization through Ti dissolution:**
- If the leachate contains, say, 1,000 ppm Ti (from 2% TiO₂ feedrock, 60% Ti extraction), the ACH crystallization step must achieve a Ti DF of: **1,000 ppm ÷ 6.3 ppm = 159×** in a single crystallization pass

No published ACH crystallization study demonstrates a 159× Ti decontamination factor in a single pass. The achievable DF for Ti in ACH crystallization from the HCl-clay Bureau of Mines literature is not well-characterized, but given that iron — a far more studied contaminant in ACH — requires dedicated SX pre-treatment before crystallization to reach acceptable levels, achieving 159× Ti DF from ACH crystallization alone is implausible.

This calculation establishes that **either (a) Ti must be removed from the leachate by SX or IX before ACH crystallization to below ~6 ppm, or (b) multiple sequential crystallization stages must each achieve partial Ti separation, or (c) Brimstone has pilot plant data showing Ti does not dissolve substantially from GMLC ilmenite under their specific leach conditions — data that has not been published.**

## PART IV: THE ACH ROUTE — COMPLETE ASSESSMENT

### 4.1 What the Patent Says (Confirmed from Round 2)

The patent (US20210070656A1) explicitly describes the ACH crystallization route as a core embodiment: the method comprises precipitating the aluminum chloride in the presence of hydrochloric acid; and optionally reacting the precipitated aluminum chloride with sulfuric acid to form solid aluminum sulfate; heating the aluminum sulfate and/or aluminum chloride to form alumina. This means Brimstone has explicitly disclosed the ACH pathway. The question is not whether they know about it — it is whether they have demonstrated that it achieves SGA-specification purity with GMLC feedstock.

### 4.2 ACH Crystallization: What It Achieves and What It Does Not

**What ACH crystallization effectively separates from aluminum:** Ca²⁺, K⁺, Na⁺, most residual Si⁴⁺ (reports to gel). These elements do not co-crystallize with AlCl₃·6H₂O and are left behind in the mother liquor.

**What requires SX pre-treatment before ACH crystallization:** Fe³⁺. As documented from the Bureau of Mines program in Round 2, the main impurity in ACH crystals is iron, concentrated on crystal surfaces. Iron must be pre-removed by tertiary amine solvent extraction before ACH crystallization to achieve SGA-grade Fe₂O₃.

**What requires dedicated separation that is not described in the patent:** Ti⁴⁺. There is no characterization of Ti behavior in ACH crystallization from ilmenite-bearing HCl leachates. Titanium in HCl solution exists as TiCl₄, TiOCl₂, or TiOCl⁺ species. These are hydrolytically unstable and can precipitate as TiO₂·nH₂O under the right pH conditions — which could mean Ti partially reports to gel during crystallization, or it could mean Ti co-precipitates with the AlCl₃·6H₂O crystals. This remains experimentally uncharacterized for the specific leachate chemistry Brimstone would produce.

**The P and Mg complication (confirmed in Round 2, now reinforced):** Brimstone's feedstock contains significant CaO (for cement production — this is the design intent) and MgO (from olivine and pyroxene in the anorthositic gabbro). Under HCl leaching, both Mg²⁺ and Ca²⁺ dissolve freely. Mg²⁺ partially co-crystallizes with ACH and requires double crystallization to remove. Ca²⁺ is largely excluded from ACH but must be controlled in the mother liquor to prevent scaling. P⁵⁺ from apatite (which the GMLC contains as a documented accessory mineral in the Fe-Ti gabbro zones) co-crystallizes with ACH and requires additional recrystallization.

### 4.3 Calcination: The Narrow Phase Window

The ACH calcination literature is now fully characterized:
- **Below 800°C:** Incomplete decomposition, residual Cl, poor crystallinity
- **800–900°C:** γ-Al₂O₃ production, BET ~100–134 m²/g, acceptable Cl removal — **this is the SGA window**
- **900–1000°C:** γ→δ→θ transition begins, BET declining
- **>1000°C:** Rapid phase transformation to α-Al₂O₃, BET collapses to <20 m²/g — **unacceptable for SGA**

The window is tight but achievable with modern fluid-bed calciners. This risk is manageable — but Orbite's experience shows that achieving precise temperature control at commercial throughput is exactly where non-Bayer calciners have historically failed.

## PART VI: PRECEDENT SYNTHESIS

### 6.1 Orbite Technologies — The Closest Analogue

The Orbite failure is now even more precisely understood in light of Round 3's ilmenite leaching data:

Orbite leached aluminous clay with HCl. Clay-based feedstocks contain titanium primarily as anatase (TiO₂) and rutile (TiO₂) — mineral forms that are **substantially less reactive** toward HCl than ilmenite (FeTiO₃). Yet even with this more favorable Ti mineralogy, Orbite's process documentation explicitly states that titanium was not cleanly managed in the chloride leachate, which is why their patent describes titanium as a persistent contaminant requiring downstream management.

Brimstone's GMLC ilmenite is significantly more HCl-reactive than Orbite's clay anatase, meaning Brimstone faces a harder Ti separation problem than Orbite did — from feedstock that is also higher in absolute TiO₂ content.

### 6.2 The Bureau of Mines Clay Alumina Program

The Bureau of Mines HCl-clay program produced SGA-grade alumina from clay in bench-scale tests after implementing: (a) iron SX with tertiary amine, (b) double ACH crystallization, (c) recrystallization for Mg and P, and (d) tight temperature-controlled ACH calcination. This is the closest precedent to Brimstone's intended process. Its key lessons:

- The process works — but it requires **five distinct purification unit operations**, not the single-step elegance that Brimstone's public communications imply
- Ti behavior in the clay alumina process was not characterized because clay feedstocks contain Ti as resistate anatase/rutile that partially remains in the leach residue — fundamentally different from GMLC ilmenite
- The Bureau of Mines program was shut down in the 1980s because it could not compete economically with the Bayer process even at government-subsidized pilot scale

## CONCLUSION: FINAL INTEGRATED VERDICT

### Risk Assessment: Probability Distribution Over Outcomes

After three rounds of research integrating geology, hydrometallurgy, phase chemistry, precedent analysis, and financial modeling, the probability distribution over the SGA feasibility question is:

**Scenario A — SGA as claimed, no undisclosed unit operations needed (Probability: 5–10%):**
This requires that GMLC ilmenite, under Brimstone's specific leach conditions, remains largely undissolved (contra the 93–97% dissolution rates in the published literature). This would require unusual grain coarsening, passivation conditions, or deliberate leach engineering not described anywhere in the patent or public communications. Possible but requires extraordinary evidence.

**Scenario B — SGA achievable, but requiring additional undisclosed unit operations (Probability: 30–40%):**
This is the most technically plausible recovery scenario. Brimstone has: (a) an undisclosed Ti SX step or pH-controlled Ti hydrolysis step; (b) an Fe SX step (consistent with the Bureau of Mines precedent); (c) double ACH crystallization; and (d) tight temperature-controlled ACH calcination. Under this scenario, SGA is physically achievable — but the capital cost is materially higher than publicly stated, the opex is higher, and the economics are worse than marketed. Disclosure of these unit operations would validate the SGA claim while raising questions about project economics.

**Scenario C — SGA not achievable from GMLC feedstock with current design; product is chemical-grade (Probability: 45–55%):**
This is the central scenario given the evidence. The combination of high Ti dissolution from ilmenite under HCl, absence of a Ti removal step in the patent, and zero published specification data aligns with an undisclosed technical barrier. The product is likely of high chemical purity (95–98.5% Al₂O₃) but fails on TiO₂, possibly Fe₂O₃, and possibly BET surface area. This is a valuable product — chemical-grade alumina at $200–250/t is commercially viable — but it does not support the "critical minerals platform" narrative or the associated valuation multiples.

**Scenario D — SGA not achievable; product quality unreported because pilot scale is insufficient (Probability: 10–15%):**
Brimstone may not yet have produced enough alumina at sufficient quality to characterize. The Oakland pilot plant is a cement demonstration; alumina production at pilot scale may not have been optimized. Under this scenario, the SGA claim is aspirational rather than demonstrated — a target, not an achievement. This is technically honest but commercially misleading in the current fundraising context.

### The Single Most Important Pending Data Point

The entire analysis — all three rounds, all 13 SGA specification parameters, all the financial modeling — collapses to one measurement:

**What is the dissolved titanium concentration in Brimstone's HCl leachate when GMLC anorthositic gabbro is leached at their process conditions?**

If the answer is <100 ppb Ti (extremely low, suggesting Ti remains in residue), the SGA pathway opens dramatically.
If the answer is >1 ppm Ti (consistent with the published ilmenite dissolution literature), the SGA pathway is closed without a dedicated Ti removal step that does not currently appear in any public document.

This single ICP measurement — worth perhaps $50 in laboratory analysis fees — is the most valuable undisclosed piece of data in Brimstone's possession. The fact that it has not been disclosed in six years of development is itself informative.
