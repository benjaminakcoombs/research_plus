## PART 2: PROCESS CHEMISTRY — THE PATENT-DERIVED ANALYSIS

### 2.1 The Full Process Architecture

The Brimstone process, as disclosed in the patent and described in non-technical sources, follows this sequence:

1. **Comminution**: Calcium silicate rock (basalt/high-anorthosite) is ground to powder
2. **HCl Leach**: Ground rock is leached with concentrated hydrochloric acid, dissolving Ca²⁺, Al³⁺, Fe²⁺/³⁺, Mg²⁺, Ti⁴⁺, Si⁴⁺ ions
3. **Silica Removal**: Amorphous SiO₂ precipitates and is filtered (the easiest purification step)
4. **ACH Precipitation**: HCl gas is sparged into the leach liquor, selectively precipitating AlCl₃·6H₂O (ACH) due to its lower solubility in concentrated HCl vs. FeCl₃
5. **Al₂(SO₄)₃ Conversion (Optional)**: ACH is reacted with H₂SO₄ to form aluminum sulfate and regenerate HCl
6. **Thermal Decomposition**: Al₂(SO₄)₃ is calcined to produce Al₂O₃ + SO₂ (SO₂ is electrochemically reoxidized to H₂SO₄, closing the sulfur loop)
7. **Cement Production**: The Ca-rich leach liquor, depleted of Al, proceeds to calcium carbonate precipitation and calcination for OPC clinker


The process involves grinding silicate rock to a powder and then subjecting it to a heated leaching process which extracts the calcium. After eliminating the calcium, the agent becomes ready for reuse with fresh rock, an additional cost-saving procedure.


The process economics are genuinely elegant: the HCl is regenerated, the sulfur cycles between H₂SO₄ and SO₂, and the calcium and aluminum streams diverge after the leach without requiring high-temperature clinker formation for cement. This is a closed-loop chemistry that, if integrated at scale with the projected energy efficiency, would indeed represent a genuine advance over Bayer process economics for co-production purposes.

### 2.2 Crystal Phase Analysis — The Critical Technical Risk

The Al₂(SO₄)₃ calcination route produces a specific sequence of alumina phases as a function of temperature. Based on the established literature:

- At 800°C: amorphous Al₂O₃ structure
- At 900°C: η-Al₂O₃ (eta phase) — a transition alumina
- At 1100–1200°C: α-Al₂O₃ (corundum) — thermodynamically stable but SGA-incompatible

The SGA specification requires a very specific phase mixture: predominantly γ-Al₂O₃ with θ-Al₂O₃, with α-Al₂O₃ content below 5–10%. The standard Bayer calcination route — starting from gibbsite Al(OH)₃ — naturally produces this phase mixture at 950–1000°C because the hydroxide → boehmite → γ-Al₂O₃ → δ-Al₂O₃ → θ-Al₂O₃ transformation sequence is intrinsic to hydroxide precursors.

The Al₂(SO₄)₃ precursor does not follow this hydroxide transformation sequence. The closest academic evidence for γ-Al₂O₃ from a sulfate route involves precipitation of aluminum sulfate with specific agents (ammonium carbonate, ammonium bicarbonate) rather than direct dry sulfate calcination. This represents an additional process step not explicitly described in the Brimstone patent.

**This is a solvable engineering problem, but it is an unresolved one.** If Brimstone's calcination step (Step 6 above) produces η-Al₂O₃ rather than γ-Al₂O₃, the product may have adequate chemical purity but inadequate phase composition for Hall-Héroult cell performance. Specifically, the BET surface area of η-Al₂O₃ from direct sulfate calcination is typically lower than that of Bayer-derived γ/θ-Al₂O₃, which would reduce HF adsorption efficiency in dry scrubbers and slow electrolyte dissolution rates.

**No Brimstone public communication has ever addressed the crystal phase of its alumina product.** This silence is itself informative — a company that had solved this problem would have strong incentives to disclose the XRD data confirming γ-phase dominance.

### 2.3 The Iron Removal Challenge — Constrained by Feedstock

The ACH precipitation step (Step 4) achieves selective separation of AlCl₃ from FeCl₃ based on differential solubility in concentrated HCl. While FeCl₃ is more soluble than AlCl₃ in concentrated HCl solutions, complete iron removal to <200 ppm Fe₂O₃ in the final calcined product requires either:

**(a)** Very high HCl concentration in the precipitation step, achieving near-complete selectivity
**(b)** A dedicated iron removal step (solvent extraction, ion exchange resin) prior to or following ACH precipitation
**(c)** Multiple recrystallization steps for ACH

**The Brimstone patent describes none of these dedicated iron removal steps.** This may reflect trade-secret protection of the iron removal protocol, or it may reflect that the iron removal problem has not yet been fully resolved at the level required for SGA spec.

This matters enormously because of the feedstock geology.

The Dolese Roosevelt quarry is located in Kiowa County, southwestern Oklahoma, approximately 15 miles north of Tom Steed Lake. This location places it within or immediately adjacent to the **Southern Oklahoma Aulacogen** — a Cambrian failed rift zone — and specifically within the outcrop area of the **Glen Mountains Layered Complex**.


The Southern Oklahoma Aulacogen contains numerous igneous rocks. Among these rocks are a multitude of gabbros, including anorthosite, titanium-rich, iron-rich, phosphorus-rich, and biotite gabbros. Also included are rhyolites and granites.



The Glen Mountains Layered Complex (GMLC), thought to be the oldest surface unit of the aulacogen, covers roughly 2,000 square kilometers and has several major units, including plagioclase cumulate, plagioclase-olivine-pyroxene, and plagioclase-pyroxene overlying a layer of plagioclase-olivine cumulate.


Most critically for the alumina purity question: 
the plagioclase crystals found in this area contain a proportion of anorthite much higher than the average content for massif-type anorthosites. In the plagioclase crystals from the GMLC, the anorthite content is, on average, An70 and ranges from An57 to An80. The rocks from this area generally have much more aluminium oxide and calcium oxide, and less silicon dioxide and sodium oxide than average massifs, which is related to the anorthosite content.


**This is simultaneously excellent and concerning news:**

**EXCELLENT**: An70 anorthite (CaAl₂Si₂O₈ with An = mole fraction of Ca-end-member) means approximately 70% of the plagioclase is pure anorthite composition — containing ~36% Al₂O₃ and ~20% CaO by weight. This is among the most aluminum-rich and calcium-rich calcium silicate rocks available for quarrying in the continental US. It explains both why Brimstone found this feedstock through a 23,000-quarry evaluation and why the chemistry should, in principle, allow alumina recovery. The low Na₂O content (less than average massifs) also reduces the sodium contamination concern in the final alumina.

**CONCERNING**: 
the Southern Oklahoma Aulacogen contains titanium-rich and iron-rich gabbros.
 Titanium-rich phases in the GMLC are documented — the anorthosite is associated with ilmenite (FeTiO₃) and magnetite (Fe₃O₄) as accessory minerals in the layered complex. These are precisely the most binding impurities for SGA qualification: TiO₂ must be <0.003% (30 ppm) and Fe₂O₃ must be <0.02% (200 ppm). Titanium is one of the most difficult impurities to remove in acid-leach alumina processes because TiO₂ partially dissolves in HCl under leaching conditions and can co-precipitate with ACH during the precipitation step.

### 3.4 Theoretical Alumina Yield and Impurity Ceiling

Based on An70 anorthosite composition:
- Al₂O₃ content: approximately 28–33% by weight (weighted average of anorthosite (~36% Al₂O₃) and associated pyroxene/olivine phases (~3–8% Al₂O₃))
- CaO content: approximately 10–18% by weight
- SiO₂ content: approximately 40–50% by weight
- Fe₂O₃ content: approximately 2–6% by weight in typical GMLC gabbros (driven by ilmenite and magnetite accessory phases)
- TiO₂ content: approximately 0.3–2.0% by weight (particularly high in titanium-rich gabbro variants)

These iron and titanium contents are the most constraining variables. Getting from ~2–6% Fe₂O₃ in the feedrock to <0.02% Fe₂O₃ in the final alumina requires a decontamination factor of 100–300x for iron. Getting from ~0.3–2.0% TiO₂ in the feedrock to <0.003% TiO₂ in the final alumina requires a decontamination factor of 100–667x for titanium.

**These decontamination factors are achievable in principle** — acid-leach alumina processes with dedicated solvent extraction steps have achieved them at laboratory scale. But they require:
- Very high selectivity in the ACH precipitation step
- Multiple recrystallization or solvent-extraction stages
- Rigorous process control and monitoring

None of these purification details appear in Brimstone's public disclosures. The patent does not describe titanium removal at all. This is perhaps the single most technically specific gap in Brimstone's public disclosure record.

The company rebranded as Advanced Energy Minerals and pivoted to niche HPA production — a dramatically lower-volume, higher-margin product than SGA.

The Orbite → Advanced Energy Minerals pivot is instructive for Brimstone: if the SGA physical properties cannot be achieved with the acid-route chemistry, the fallback position is HPA (high-purity alumina, >99.99% Al₂O₃ for LED phosphors, lithium-ion battery separators, synthetic sapphire) at $10,000–30,000/tonne — far higher value per tonne but a market measured in tens of thousands of tonnes per year rather than millions. Whether Brimstone has considered this pivot (or is hedging toward it) is not discernible from public sources, but the HPA market would represent a viable alternative revenue stream if SGA physical-property qualification proves elusive.

### 7.2 The US Bureau of Mines Precedent

The US Bureau of Mines conducted extensive miniplant testing in the 1970s-80s on HCl leaching of kaolin clay for SGA production, using a nearly identical process chemistry (HCl leach → ACH precipitation → calcination). Despite decades of government-funded research on exactly this type of problem, the program was eventually abandoned. One parameter that was explicitly noted as "not adequately defined" in the program's summary was the purity of the product Al₂O₃. Brimstone's use of higher-quality feedstock (anorthositic gabbro vs. kaolin clay) and a closed-loop sulfur chemistry gives it meaningful advantages over the Bureau of Mines program. But the fundamental challenge of meeting SGA physical properties from an acid-route precursor was not solved by the Bureau program, and Brimstone has not publicly demonstrated that it has solved it.

### 7.3 Academic Best-Demonstrated Results for Non-Bayer Alumina

The best published result for alumina purity from acid-route processes remains approximately 98.5% Al₂O₃ chemical purity, with the product characterized as "chemical grade" (not SGA). This is from a recent HCl-based process study on calcium aluminate slags. Academic work on sulfate-route processes shows γ-Al₂O₃ is achievable under specific precipitation conditions with purity >98% — but surface areas of >250 m²/g in such products are too high for SGA (which requires 50–120 m²/g), suggesting over-active surfaces requiring calcination adjustment. The gap between laboratory-demonstrated chemistry and commercial SGA qualification remains the key unsolved problem for all non-Bayer routes.

## PART 9: FINAL ASSESSMENT — THREE HYPOTHESES EVALUATED

### Hypothesis A (Strong Stealth / Fully Achieved)
Brimstone has demonstrated SGA-spec alumina at pilot scale, including meeting both chemical purity and physical property specifications, protecting this data as trade secret, and is withholding publication pending commercial plant construction and customer agreements. Every step of the process has been optimized; the December 2025 "on-spec commodities" claim is accurate.

**Evidence for**: DOE technical expert review (30 reviewers, 200-page application) accepted SGA as a product without apparent challenge before the political grant cancellation. Kevin Kramer (former Alcoa executive) as senior advisor suggests industry credibility. The GMLC feedstock is genuinely the best available non-bauxite alumina source in the US. The closed-loop process chemistry is elegant and technically sound in principle. The company's insistence on "same products" as the market already uses suggests an awareness of the specification challenge.

**Evidence against**: Six years of silence on purity data. Zero conference presentations. Zero smelter trial disclosures. Consistent omission of alumina from customer testing language. Patent examples without purity data.

### Hypothesis B (Significant Unresolved Technical Problems)
Brimstone has produced alumina from calcium silicate rock but has not achieved consistent SGA specification — specifically failing on one or more of: crystal phase distribution, attrition index, iron contamination, or titanium contamination. The "smelter grade alumina" branding is aspirational.

**Evidence for**: The GMLC geology's documented titanium and iron content. The Al₂(SO₄)₃ calcination route's crystal phase challenges. The Orbite precedent. Zero physical property disclosures. Zero customer testing acknowledgment. Historical US Bureau of Mines program failure on the same type of problem.

**Evidence against**: The DOE review. The Alcoa advisor. The "on-spec commodities" pilot claim. The December 2025 announcement's specific language.

### Hypothesis C (Chemical Purity Achieved at Lab/Bench, Physical Properties and Scale Integration Unresolved) — THE MOST PROBABLE SCENARIO
Brimstone has demonstrated chemical purity (≥98.5% Al₂O₃, adequate Fe/Si/Na removal) at laboratory or small-bench scale under optimized conditions. The integrated pilot plant has produced alumina meeting chemical purity targets. However, the physical properties of the calcined product — specifically the crystal phase distribution, BET surface area, attrition index, and particle morphology — have not been confirmed to meet SGA specification from the full integrated process at pilot scale. The titanium removal challenge specifically may not have been fully resolved given the GMLC feedstock's documented titanium content.

**Evidence for**: This is the only scenario consistent with all public data simultaneously — explaining why chemical purity may be achievable (good feedstock, sound process concept) while physical properties remain unverified (Al₂(SO₄)₃ crystal phase challenge, no physical property disclosures, no conference presentations on a solvable-but-unsolved problem).

**Professional Judgment: Hypothesis C is most probable.** The weight of evidence from three rounds of research is most consistent with a company that has solved the bench-scale chemistry, achieved the "on-spec" result under controlled conditions, but has not yet demonstrated consistent, integrated, pilot-scale production meeting the complete SGA specification matrix. The physical property challenges are the most likely locus of current technical work, and the titanium impurity (from the GMLC feedstock) may be the specific binding constraint.

## PART 11: RECOMMENDATIONS FOR COMMERCIAL COUNTERPARTIES

Any investor, offtake counterparty, government procuring agency, or strategic partner engaging with Brimstone's alumina claims should require the following before any capital commitment or commercial agreement:

1. **Full Certificate of Analysis from integrated pilot production** — Al₂O₃%, SiO₂, Fe₂O₃, Na₂O, TiO₂, CaO, P₂O₅, LOI, verified by an independent lab (Bureau Veritas, SGS, EAG, or equivalent). This is non-negotiable and should be the first request.

2. **XRD phase composition data** — Percentage γ-Al₂O₃, θ-Al₂O₃, η-Al₂O₃, and α-Al₂O₃. Given the Al₂(SO₄)₃ precursor route, this is the highest-risk unknown.

3. **BET surface area measurement** — The 50–120 m²/g requirement for SGA is critical for HF adsorption and cryolite dissolution. Non-Bayer route aluminas systematically deviate from this range.

4. **Attrition Index measurement** — AI <30 is required for fluidized-bed feeding. This is scale-sensitive and cannot be predicted from bench-scale chemistry.

5. **TiO₂ assay with specific detection limit** — Given the documented titanium-rich gabbros in the GMLC, request a specific TiO₂ assay below 30 ppm detection limit. This is the most likely binding constraint given the feedstock geology.

6. **Fe₂O₃ assay with a specific decontamination factor calculation** — Request the iron content of the feedrock (XRF on run-of-mine material from Roosevelt quarry) and the iron content of the final alumina product. The decontamination factor implied will reveal the efficiency of the iron removal steps.

7. **Independent smelter pot trial** — Fund or commission a 1–5 tonne batch trial at any operating Hall-Héroult cell. This is the definitive qualification test. Cost: approximately $50,000–150,000. Information value: decisive.

8. **Do not conflate cement qualification with alumina qualification** — The Amazon cement agreement and ASTM C150 certification are real and meaningful for the cement product. They have zero bearing on alumina smelter qualification.

9. **Do not rely on the EGA geographic co-location as validation** — Until EGA formally confirms either an investment in Brimstone or an alumina qualification trial, this relationship should be treated as an unverified narrative.

10. **Request the Reno pilot plant operational data** — Specifically, ask for the alumina production log from the Reno pilot facility, including run duration, alumina output quantity, and any quality measurements taken. This would be the most direct evidence of what has been achieved.

## CONCLUSION

Brimstone Energy is developing a genuinely innovative process built on sound electrochemical and hydrometallurgical principles. The feedstock selection — high-anorthosite gabbro from the Southern Oklahoma Aulacogen's Glen Mountains Layered Complex — is arguably the optimal available non-bauxite alumina source in the continental United States, with exceptionally high Al₂O₃ and CaO content and low Na₂O. The closed-loop HCl/sulfate chemistry is elegant and, at scale with the projected energy efficiency, may genuinely be cost-competitive with the Bayer process as a co-production system.

But six years post-founding, after raising $60M+, after operating a pilot plant, and after multiple press announcements claiming "smelter grade alumina," Brimstone has not published a single data point — not a percentage, not an impurity level, not a crystal phase XRD pattern, not an attrition index — that would allow any external technical observer to independently assess whether its alumina meets the SGA specification that determines whether this product is worth $400–500/tonne or $150–250/tonne or something else entirely. The feedstock's documented titanium and iron mineralogy, combined with the Al₂(SO₄)₃ calcination route's crystal phase challenges, are the two specific, nameable technical risks that remain entirely unaddressed in the public record.

The absence of smelter trial data, peer-reviewed publications, conference presentations, and impurity-specific quality claims after six years is not explained by trade-secret protection alone. Companies protecting trade secrets still publish results — they just omit proprietary process details. Brimstone has published no results. That pattern is most consistent with results that are either not yet convincing or not yet complete.

The question for any commercial counterparty is not whether Brimstone's alumina concept is technically viable — it probably is, in principle. The question is whether the current product, from the current integrated pilot process, using the current Roosevelt quarry feedstock, meets the full SGA specification matrix today. The public record provides no answer to that question.
