## SECTION 2: U.S. ALUMINA SUPPLY GAP — QUANTITATIVE ANALYSIS

### 2.1 Current U.S. Consumption and Import Dependency


About 85 percent of the aluminum needs of American industries are currently met by imports.
 
The United States imports nearly all of the alumina it needs to feed its giant, energy-hungry smelters. Over half that supply comes from Brazil, with Australia, Jamaica, and Canada providing most of the rest.


Annual U.S. alumina production has fallen from approximately 5 million tonnes in 2000 to roughly 810,000 tonnes in 2024 — a structural collapse driven by energy cost disadvantages and the offshoring of bauxite refining to tropical source countries. 
China currently dominates both the alumina and aluminum markets, while the U.S. is home to just one operational alumina refinery today.


### 2.2 Gramercy Refinery — Status, Expanded Capacity, and Risks

The Atalco (Atlantic Alumina) refinery in Gramercy, Louisiana is the sole operating U.S. alumina refinery. A $450M DOD/Department of War investment, including $150M in preferred equity from the Industrial Base Analysis and Sustainment program, is expanding it toward 1 million tonnes per year while adding a gallium co-production circuit.

The Trump administration's bauxite tariff has created a paradox that directly harms Gramercy while ostensibly supporting domestic aluminum. Tariffs on bauxite imports were set at 25% in March 2025 and doubled to 50% by June 2025, adding $30–50/tonne to Gramercy's raw material costs. Gramercy sources most of its bauxite from Jamaica — the same tariff exposure that affects Century's Jamalco supply chain. This structural contradiction is Brimstone's greatest political opportunity: it is the only domestic alumina production approach entirely immune to the bauxite tariff, and it therefore aligns with Trump's trade policy rather than suffering from it.


Unlike conventional alumina production, which relies on bauxite — an ore found in certain tropical and subtropical regions overseas — Brimstone produces alumina from carbon-free feedrocks that are abundant in the U.S. The widespread availability of Brimstone's rocks reduces the U.S. alumina/aluminum industry's dependence on imported bauxite, unlocking the ability to produce alumina efficiently and sustainably virtually anywhere.

For Brimstone's fully electrifiable process, Oklahoma's wind-heavy grid provides both cost advantage (~60–100 MW estimated demand for Plant 1) and a strong carbon-intensity argument for "zero-carbon alumina" positioning — relevant for both Amazon's sustainability commitments and for potential EU carbon border adjustment mechanism (CBAM) compliance in future export scenarios.


PSO is focused on providing cost-effective, reliable energy to all of its customers. "Wind power accounts for more than 40 percent of electricity generation in Oklahoma already, making the state one of the wind leaders nationwide," said Simon Buerk of Emirates Global Aluminum.


### 4.5 Labor Market Assessment


The Inola plant will create 1,000 permanent direct jobs at the facility and 4,000 jobs during construction.
 Brimstone's Plant 1 would create approximately 100–150 permanent industrial jobs. The geographic separation between Inola (northeastern Oklahoma, Tulsa metro) and Roosevelt/Lawton/Duncan (southwestern Oklahoma) reduces direct day-to-day labor competition. However, both projects will compete for specialized industrial commissioning engineers and process operators at the state level during overlapping construction windows (~2027–2030). The addition of Kajima Ventures as a Brimstone investor suggests the company may have access to Kajima's industrial construction workforce pipeline — a meaningful practical advantage.

EGA/Century's window to enter as the primary industrial strategic is **open but narrowing**.

### 5.2 Strategic Rationale — Mapped Explicitly

**A. Domestic Alumina Supply Hedge (Primary Driver)**
The Inola smelter requires 1.5 million tpy SGA with zero supply agreements announced. A strategic equity stake combined with a long-term offtake:
- Signals domestic supply chain integration to DOE and Congress — critical for maintaining the $500M DOE grant
- Locks in preferential pricing/access to Brimstone's domestic output and all future Oklahoma plants
- Provides ROFO on future plants nationally
- Satisfies Congressional oversight requirements for the DOE grant (which implicitly expects U.S. industrial integration given the project's U.S.-UAE bilateral political status)

**B. Critical Minerals Onshoring Narrative**
The Inola project was secured by President Trump as part of a U.S.-UAE bilateral commitment. 
Brimstone's Rock Refinery would represent the first new, domestic source of alumina in a generation — a critical step toward onshoring U.S. manufacturing, a Trump Administration priority.
 An EGA equity stake in Brimstone is the ideal supply-chain integration story for Washington: American rocks, American quarry, zero bauxite imports, zero tariff exposure.

**C. Co-Location Logistics Efficiency**
The Roosevelt quarry's on-site rail makes SGA shipment to Inola (150 miles) operationally straightforward. Future Rock Refinery siting nearer to Inola is possible — the Dolese LOI covers one quarry; future plants could site at other Oklahoma quarries.

**D. IRA Section 45X Tax Credit Capture**
If EGA/Century holds equity in Brimstone, Section 45X credits on Brimstone's alumina production could potentially be monetized through a related-party election or transferred for cash. At 10% of a $200/t production cost = ~$25/t — $500,000/year from Plant 1, scaling to $5M/year at 10 plants.

**E. "Fourth Great Refinery" Platform Option Value**

Brimstone has announced plans to co-produce steel, aluminum, magnesium, titanium, and other critical minerals, in addition to its previously announced products.
 
As part of the company's future product roadmap, Brimstone will also produce magnesium, titanium, and other critical minerals, which are essential inputs for technologies such as electric vehicles, airplanes, defense equipment, batteries, and advanced manufacturing.
 For Mubadala, whose industrial portfolio spans EGA (aluminum), GlobalFoundries (semiconductors), and M42 (healthcare), a Brimstone stake fits naturally into a broader critical materials strategy with aerospace and defense adjacency.

### 5.3 Historical Precedents for Upstream Integration

The aluminum industry's entire history validates this investment pattern:
- **EGA's own Al Taweelah:** Built at $3.3B specifically to secure competitive alumina supply, "a strategic expansion upstream in the aluminium value chain"

Century Aluminum is an integrated producer of bauxite, alumina, and primary aluminum products

### 5.4 Conflict of Interest Analysis — Complementary, Not Competing

An EGA/Century investment would be in the alumina stream only. 
Large customers already want to buy Brimstone's cement. Amazon recently announced plans to reserve volumes of the cement and SCM from the upcoming plant. EGA/Century have no cement business interests. Each track has independent commercial logic with non-overlapping market exposure.

This argues for a milestone-based equity investment structure with EGA/Century taking governance rights over Plant 1 milestones before committing full offtake volumes.

Raw material sourcing strategies for the facility emphasize diversified supply chains combining Australian bauxite imports with potential future domestic alumina production.

The absence of competing bidders for a Brimstone strategic stake is real but could change rapidly once Brimstone closes its next fundraising round and the project becomes more publicly visible. The advisory team should treat the current absence of competing interest as an opportunity window, not a permanent condition.
