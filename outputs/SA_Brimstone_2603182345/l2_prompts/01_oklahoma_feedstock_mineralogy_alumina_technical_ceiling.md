You are a senior hydrometallurgical process engineer with deep experience in non-bauxite alumina production, acid-leach purification systems, and transition alumina phase chemistry. You are investigating a compound technical risk that could determine whether Brimstone Energy's alumina product is worth $400–500/t (smelter-grade) or $150–250/t (chemical-grade) — a binary gate with $5–10M/year revenue impact at first-plant scale and transformational implications for the company's valuation as a "critical minerals platform."

CONTEXT:
Brimstone Energy is a venture-backed startup (~$80M+ raised) developing a process to co-produce Portland cement and alumina from calcium silicate rock instead of limestone/bauxite. Since January 2025, every Brimstone press release has claimed the process produces "smelter-grade alumina" (SGA), yet after six years of development the company has published zero of the thirteen standard SGA specification parameters — no Al₂O₃ percentage, no Fe₂O₃ assay, no TiO₂ level, no crystal phase XRD data, no BET surface area, no attrition index. By contrast, its cement product has been independently certified to ASTM C150 by Twining Consulting and tested by Amazon.

Brimstone's planned Oklahoma commercial plant will source feedstock from or near the Glen Mountains Layered Complex (GMLC) in Kiowa County, Oklahoma — specifically from or near Dolese Bros.' Roosevelt quarry. The GMLC is a well-studied Cambrian-age layered mafic intrusion in the Southern Oklahoma Aulacogen, documented by the Oklahoma Geological Survey as containing anorthosite with An70 plagioclase (exceptionally alumina-rich and calcium-rich — ideal for the process) but with associated ilmenite (FeTiO₃) and magnetite (Fe₃O₄) as accessory minerals. The Oklahoma Geological Survey describes the region as containing "titanium-rich and iron-rich gabbros." SGA specification requires TiO₂ <0.003% (30 ppm) and Fe₂O₃ <0.02% (200 ppm). If feedrock contains ~2–6% Fe₂O₃ and ~0.5–3% TiO₂, the decontamination factors required are 100–300x for iron and 100–667x for titanium.

A separate but compounding risk: Brimstone's patent (US20210070656A1) describes calcining aluminum sulfate (Al₂(SO₄)₃) to produce alumina. This is fundamentally different from the Bayer process, which calcines aluminum hydroxide (Al(OH)₃). The sulfate calcination route at standard temperatures (~900°C) typically produces η-Al₂O₃ (eta phase), not the γ/θ-Al₂O₃ mixture that smelters require. Crystal phase determines BET surface area, HF adsorption, and dissolution rate in cryolite — all critical for Hall-Héroult cell performance. No Brimstone communication has ever addressed crystal phase.

HYPOTHESIS TO INVESTIGATE:
The compound technical risk — (a) high iron and titanium in GMLC feedstock requiring unprecedented decontamination factors from acid-leach, (b) no titanium removal step described in the patent, and (c) sulfate calcination producing the wrong crystal phase — may make true SGA production infeasible without capital-intensive additional purification and calcination control steps not in the current plant design. If confirmed, this shifts the alumina from SGA ($400–500/t) to chemical-grade alumina ($150–250/t), destroying $5–10M/year in revenue at Plant 1 and invalidating the "domestic aluminum supply chain" narrative used to justify critical minerals valuation multiples.
