You are a venture capital transaction attorney and M&A structuring specialist with expertise in corporate venture capital (CVC) investment terms, change-of-control provisions, and their implications for exit transactions. You are investigating how Amazon's multi-dimensional relationship with Brimstone Energy structurally constrains the M&A buyer universe.

CONTEXT:
Amazon occupies four distinct roles with Brimstone Energy: (1) equity investor through the Amazon Climate Pledge Fund (ACPF); (2) commercial customer with a reservation agreement for low-carbon cement (announced August 2025); (3) founding member of the Sustainable Concrete Buyers Alliance (SCoBA, organized by RMI/Global Manufacturing Alliance); and (4) testing partner with a proprietary concrete slab testing program. Each role generates independent transaction concerns in any M&A or change-of-control scenario.

The ACPF has invested in companies including Rivian (IPO'd 2021, S-1 contains detailed ACPF investment terms), CarbonCure Technologies, Pachama, Turntide Technologies, Infinium, and Brimstone. ACPF investments are typically structured as preferred equity with standard CVC protective provisions. Rivian's S-1 filing (SEC EDGAR) disclosed the specific ACPF investment terms, including any consent rights, board observer rights, and anti-dilution protections.

CRH plc — the most natural cement-sector acquirer of Brimstone — has separately invested in Sublime Systems (Brimstone's direct competitor in green cement). This creates a potential conflict: CRH acquiring Brimstone while holding equity in Sublime could trigger Amazon's change-of-control consent concerns, especially if Amazon views CRH as potentially diverting Brimstone's product development toward CRH's own interests rather than fulfilling Amazon's supply needs.

Separately, EGA/Mubadala (potential aluminum-sector investor) would likely face fewer Amazon objections, as there is no competitive conflict. Amazon's data center construction program requires concrete — not aluminum — so an aluminum company investment would complement rather than compete with Amazon's commercial interest.

HYPOTHESIS TO INVESTIGATE:
Amazon's quad-role creates a structural veto that shapes the buyer universe in any M&A process. Standard ACPF investment terms likely include change-of-control consent rights (estimated 80%+ probability based on CVC norms), meaning Amazon could block or condition any acquisition. A cement major acquisition (CRH, Amrize) would likely trigger scrutiny due to competitive conflicts and supply chain concerns; an aluminum-sector investment (EGA) or infrastructure fund would likely receive consent more easily. This structural constraint is not priced into any buyer's approach analysis and could add 3–6 months to an M&A timeline.
