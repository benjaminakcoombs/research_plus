You are a defense industrial policy analyst specializing in Department of Defense critical minerals financing programs, including the Office of Strategic Capital (OSC), Defense Industrial Base Council (DIBC), and Defense Production Act (DPA) Title III investments. You are investigating whether Brimstone Energy has a viable pathway to non-dilutive government financing through DOD channels, despite having lost its DOE OCED grant.

CONTEXT:
Brimstone Energy is developing a process to co-produce Portland cement and alumina from calcium silicate rock in Oklahoma. In March 2024, the DOE's Office of Clean Energy Demonstrations (OCED) selected Brimstone for up to $189M in federal cost-share for its first commercial plant. In May 2025, the Trump administration terminated this grant along with other OCED awards.

Meanwhile, a striking policy contradiction exists: The same administration is actively maintaining a $500M OCED cooperative agreement for Century Aluminum's smelter in Inola, Oklahoma (confirmed in Century's February 2026 10-K filings). The Inola smelter will consume ~1.5 million tonnes/year of alumina, and the U.S. has only one operating alumina refinery. Brimstone is the only announced domestic non-bauxite alumina production project. The December 2025 White House aluminum strategy brief explicitly calls for developing the domestic aluminum supply chain.

The DOD Office of Strategic Capital (OSC) offers loans at U.S. Treasury rates, up to 50-year tenor, covering up to 80% of total project costs — the most favorable terms available from any government or private lender. The DOD has deployed $777M through the DIBC mechanism in FY2025 alone, with awards from $7M to $400M. The MP Materials precedent is directly relevant: MP received a $400M DOD equity investment plus $150M OSC loan for rare earth processing — another critical minerals application. However, OSC requires a "repayment guarantee from a creditworthy sponsor" for pre-revenue companies.

Brimstone's Oklahoma site is in Tom Cole's Congressional district. Cole chairs the House Appropriations Committee. Alumina was included in President Trump's March 2025 critical minerals executive order. CEO Cody Finke stated: "If the Trump administration put out a grant for critical materials, we would have applied to that grant — basically the exact same project."

HYPOTHESIS TO INVESTIGATE:
The documented policy incoherence (funding Century's alumina-consuming smelter while cancelling Brimstone's alumina-producing plant) is Brimstone's strongest political asset. It creates leverage for DOE grant reinstatement through Congressional appropriations, DOD OSC/DIBC financing through the critical minerals door, or enhanced strategic investor leverage. The OSC pathway (Treasury rates, 50-year tenor, 80% LTV) would make Plant 1 economics unambiguously bankable, but requires a creditworthy sponsor guarantee first — creating interdependency with the strategic equity round.
