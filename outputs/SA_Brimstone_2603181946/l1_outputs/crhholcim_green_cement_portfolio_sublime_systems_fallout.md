## SECTION 2: THE AMRIZE INVESTMENT STRUCTURE — DEFINITIVELY REVISED

### 2.1 Convertible Note + Advance Payments: What This Actually Means

This is the most analytically important structural finding of all three research rounds. Amrize's 10-Q SEC disclosure is explicit: 
Amrize has investments in Sublime Systems, a disruptive cement technology startup which plans to use renewable electricity and carbon-free raw materials for cement production, in the form of a convertible note and advance payments for future supplies and may participate in the startup's future potential rounds of capital raising to finance its manufacturing facility.


**Legal implications of a convertible note (vs. equity):**

1. **Amrize is a creditor, not yet an equity holder.** The note converts to equity only upon a qualified financing event (typically a priced equity round above a defined threshold). If Sublime never raises another qualified round, the note may remain as debt — or convert at a distressed valuation.

2. **In a bankruptcy or wind-down, convertible note holders have priority over equity.** Amrize's principal and accrued interest would be recovered before any equity holders receive distributions. This significantly reduces Amrize's economic downside exposure.

3. **Amrize has no obligation to "participate in future rounds"** — the 10-Q says it "may participate." This is optional, not committed.

4. **Advance payments for future supplies are a supply contract claim.** If Sublime cannot deliver the cement, Amrize has a contract claim for either (a) refund of advance payments or (b) damages. These are not equity losses — they are commercial contract exposures.

**ANALYST JUDGMENT — MAJOR REVISION:** The prior analysis overstated Amrize's entanglement with Sublime. The "fiduciary floor" argument — that Amrize cannot quietly walk away because the investment is SEC-disclosed — still applies in terms of *disclosure* (Amrize must report the investment's status in its 10-Ks and 10-Qs), but does not create an *obligation to rescue* because Amrize holds debt, not equity. A convertible note that is never converted is simply a loan — if Sublime repays it (or is acquired at a price that repays it), Amrize recovers its principal plus interest and walks away with no strategic asset and no ongoing obligation. The advance payments may or may not be recoverable depending on contract terms. This is a significantly more arms-length relationship than "strategic equity co-investor" as described in all prior public coverage.

**A parallel note:** CRH Ventures' Sublime investment is similarly described as an equity investment with offtake and co-development rights — but if CRH's structure also involves a convertible note (which is common in strategic cement-sector innovation investments of this type), CRH's exposure may be similarly structured. No CRH SEC disclosure (CRH is listed on NYSE but its ventures investments are not individually disclosed at the same level of granularity as Amrize's 10-Q) has surfaced the precise instrument type. **This is the one remaining material unknown that a banker should request directly from CRH Investor Relations before finalizing a pitch.**

### ANOMALY 4: THE SIAM CEMENT GROUP WILDCARD


Sublime raised over US$50M from a consortium of climate technology investors, ARPA-E funding, and strategic investor Siam Cement Group.


Siam Cement Group (SCG) is one of Asia's largest industrial conglomerates and Southeast Asia's dominant cement producer, operating across Thailand, Vietnam, Indonesia, Philippines, and Myanmar. SCG has a pre-existing, multi-year relationship with Sublime and a strategic need for clean cement technology in markets where calcined clay and CCUS options are less developed. If CRH and Amrize decline to rescue Sublime, SCG is the most logical acquirer of the Sublime technology for Asian deployment. An SCG acquisition or licensing deal would: (a) remove Sublime from CRH/Amrize's control, (b) deprive them of the "first mover on next-gen cement technology" narrative, and (c) create a potential competitive threat in Southeast Asian markets where CRH and Holcim have operations. ### ANOMALY 5: BRIMSTONE IS SIMULTANEOUSLY GOING PUBLIC AND BUILDING A PLANT — AN UNUSUAL CAPITAL STRUCTURE SIGNAL


Brimstone has successfully gone public, marking a significant milestone in its development as a major player in the alternative resource sector.

This is not a traditional IPO. However, combined with the

The timing alignment with Sublime's collapse is unlikely to be coincidental. ### ANOMALY 6: THE DOE CANCELLATION DID MORE DAMAGE TO SUBLIME THAN TO BRIMSTONE — AND THE REASON IS STRUCTURAL

Both companies lost DOE grants. Brimstone lost $189M — a larger absolute amount than Sublime's $87M. Yet Brimstone has not paused operations, while Sublime has gone from 128 to ~28 employees. Why?

The answer is structural, not financial: Sublime's entire first commercial plant was designed around the DOE grant as a 50% funding contribution. The Holyoke construction program had no alternative funding plan for the $87M gap. Brimstone's CEO explicitly stated that the company "never counted on the inevitability of government grants" and that its fundraising strategy does not depend on the DOE. The difference reflects a deeper difference in business model maturity: Brimstone has an Amazon anchor customer and investor, a co-product (alumina) that generates independent revenue, and a site selection process funded by private capital. Sublime had a Microsoft offtake commitment but no comparable private investor willing to fund 100% of the plant without federal co-investment.

This structural difference is likely to persist into future capital-raising cycles. For strategic investors, Brimstone's capital structure independence from federal grants is a significant de-risking factor relative to the grant-dependency that contributed to Sublime's crisis.

### ANOMALY 7: CRH IS THE ONLY PARTY WITH THE RIGHT PROFILE TO MAKE TWO MOVES SIMULTANEOUSLY

The following strategic moves are simultaneously available to CRH, and to no other party:

1. **Acquire Sublime's IP/pilot plant** at a distressed valuation (far below the ~$150M+ implied by the original investment round), securing the electrochemical technology as a long-term option on C1157 green cement for private/data-center markets
2. **Invest in Brimstone** as a first cement-sector anchor at the Series B level, securing C150 OPC supply for CRH's entire North American market — including the DOT/infrastructure segment that Sublime could never serve

Amrize cannot do both — its Dallas-Fort Worth cement expansion creates a competitive conflict with Brimstone, and its convertible note structure with Sublime creates a creditor (not equity) relationship. Holcim parent has no North American operating footprint. Heidelberg and Buzzi have not moved. **CRH Ventures, with a $250M fund and $5B annual FCF, is the only party with the right geography, the right financial capacity, the right relationship with both companies, and the absence of internal competitive conflicts to execute both transactions.**
