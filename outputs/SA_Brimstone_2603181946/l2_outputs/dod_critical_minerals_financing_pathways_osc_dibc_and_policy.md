## PART II: THE GOVERNMENT FINANCING LANDSCAPE — COMPLETE PICTURE

### 2.1 OSC: The OBBBA Transformation

**Scale:** 
$5 billion of the $8.2 billion appropriated to IBF are specifically for investments in critical mineral supply chains. This $5 billion investment is paired with an additional $2 billion investment for the National Defense Stockpile Transaction Fund to improve the U.S. stockpile of critical minerals. Additionally, $500 million of OSC's appropriated funds are specifically designated for critical mineral investments.



Together, this $7.5 billion investment in critical minerals financing builds on an earlier Executive Order that sought to leverage DOD and other agency authorities to catalyze investments in domestic critical mineral production.


**OSC loan mechanics:** OSC loans can be made for a tenor of up to 50 years at Treasury-equivalent interest rates, with the Secretary of Defense retaining authority to waive the rate floor if deemed vital to national security. 
With billions in new appropriations, ability to make multi-year arrangements, and stronger appetite for investment in new technologies, DLA is poised to continue growing into a receptive customer and funding partner.


**NOFA 2 status:** The original NOFA 1 window closed February 3, 2025. OSC has confirmed it is developing additional financial instruments and will announce them via the Federal Register. Post-OBBBA, OSC has already committed $850M across three deals (MP Materials: $150M; Vulcan Elements: $620M; ReElement: $80M) — all outside the formal NOFA 1 process, demonstrating bilateral engagement is viable without waiting for a published solicitation.

**OSC creditworthy-sponsor requirement — confirmed:** OSC explicitly excludes "companies who are early-stage, pre-revenue or have insufficient operating history **without a repayment guarantee from a creditworthy sponsor**." The phrase "without a repayment guarantee" makes the exclusion conditional and curable. The statute allows "the applicant, or its sponsors" to demonstrate operating history — meaning an industrial partner with a creditworthy balance sheet can satisfy the test on Brimstone's behalf.

**Critical minerals eligibility — confirmed unambiguous:**
Alumina/aluminum is explicitly the first-listed critical mineral in the USGS's final 2025 list, and alumina is specifically assessed as a distinct supply chain stage within the aluminum critical minerals designation. There is no legal ambiguity about OSC's OBBBA authority covering Brimstone's plant.

### 2.3 DLA National Defense Stockpile: Alumina Is NOT a Current Active Target

This is the most important adverse finding from Round 3. 
The minerals covered in DLA's 2025 RFIs include minerals for which the United States has known deposits but has had insufficient commercial production capacity, such as scandium, tungsten, and graphite, as well as rare earth elements facing particularly acute shortages, such as samarium, dysprosium, and terbium.
 
Several RFIs, including those for scandium and indium, advanced to further evaluation of proposals or procurement in the form of sole-source IDIQ contracts.



The statute defines "strategic and critical materials" as materials needed for military, industrial, and essential civilian uses during a national emergency that are not domestically produced in sufficient quantities. The NDS list of materials of interest largely overlaps with the Department of Energy's list of "critical materials," which in turn incorporates the Department of the Interior's "critical minerals" list. The NDS list includes copper, nickel, lithium, antimony, as well as 16 rare earth elements.


Alumina is not on this list of currently procured NDS materials. The statutory definition — "not domestically produced in sufficient quantities" — does apply to alumina (the U.S. imports 1.1M tonnes/year against Atalco's ~400K tonne actual output), but DLA has not yet issued an RFI or RFP for alumina. Activating this pathway would require: (1) a formal shortfall finding in the next biennial requirements report (FY2025 report, due to Congress in late 2025/early 2026), (2) DLA issuance of an alumina RFI, and (3) a multi-year procurement process. This is a 3–5 year pathway, not a near-term financing solution.


Prior to the passage of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, the NDS Transaction Fund had $958M+ in assets.
 The OBBBA's $2B additional NDS appropriation supplements this revolving fund but does not direct specific acquisitions — DLA retains discretion over what to buy. 
In Congress, proposals for creating a Strategic Resilience Reserve for critical minerals have been introduced in both houses with bipartisan support. The proposed mechanism involves an independent government corporation managing the purchase and storage of the minerals reserve. The Trump Administration also announced a new initiative for a $12 billion critical minerals stockpile to be made accessible to private industry.
 This $12B initiative, financed by private institutional investors and the U.S. Export-Import Bank, could ultimately include alumina — but is even further from activation.

**Strategic implication:** The DLA pathway is real but long-dated. Brimstone's near-term strategy should not depend on it. However, engaging DLA proactively to request an alumina shortfall study — using the USGS critical minerals designation and the Oklahoma aluminum hub supply gap as evidence — could accelerate DLA's formal shortfall finding, which is the prerequisite for a future procurement action.

### 2.4 Tom Cole and the Congressional Pathway

Cole is directly engaged with Brimstone's grant appeal, confirmed by local Oklahoma reporting. His appropriations bill eliminates OCED, constraining the reinstatement framing. However, the correct reframe is a **DOD appropriations rider directing IBF to prioritize domestic alumina production** — entirely consistent with Cole's vote and his stated critical minerals priorities. 
The National Defense Stockpile is a mechanism for the federal government to acquire and maintain stocks of certain "strategic and critical materials." The reserve aims to foster the development of domestic sources of these materials and prevent dependence on foreign sources in times of national emergency.
 This statutory language — "foster the development of domestic sources" — provides the direct hook for a Congressional directive to DLA to conduct an alumina shortfall study, which Cole could attach to the FY27 NDAA or Defense Appropriations bill.

## CONCLUSION: THE DEFINITIVE ASSESSMENT

Across three rounds of research, the following picture has crystallized with high confidence:

**The opportunity is real and unambiguous.** Alumina is the first-listed critical mineral in the USGS's final 2025 list. The OSC has $100B in new lending authority specifically for critical mineral industries. IBF has $5B for critical mineral supply chain investments. The DOD has explicitly signaled it will replicate the MP Materials structure. Brimstone is the only announced domestic non-bauxite alumina project in the country.

**The obstacle is singular and specific.** Brimstone has not raised a strategic equity round since March 2022. It has no revenue, no operating history, and no creditworthy industrial partner with a balance sheet capable of providing OSC's required guarantee. Every government financing pathway — OSC direct loan, OSC loan guarantee, IBF equity enhancement — is facilitated by resolving this single constraint. The new CFO hire in October 2025 is the clearest signal that Brimstone's leadership knows this and is preparing to act.

**The critical path is clear.** The sequence is: **(1) Alcoa or Century/EGA strategic partnership (Kevin Kramer channel)** → **(2) OSC pre-application bilateral consultation** → **(3) IBF equity term sheet** → **(4) OSC loan or guarantee** → **(5) Private project finance syndication.** Steps 2 and 3 can run in parallel with step 1; steps 4 and 5 require step 1.

**The political window is open but finite.** The administration's enthusiasm for critical minerals deals — seven major investments in approximately 12 months, $550M to MP Materials alone — will not be unlimited. IBF has a $5B bucket specifically for critical mineral supply chains and a mandate to deploy it before 2029. Brimstone is uniquely positioned as the only domestic alumina project in a policy environment that has declared alumina a first-priority critical mineral. 
Finke acknowledged that the fundraising process is slow right now. But the company's not in a cash crunch, and he thinks Brimstone will be able to attract the funding it needs to move forward.
 The question is not whether Brimstone can attract financing — it is whether it can close a creditworthy strategic partnership fast enough to convert the current extraordinary policy environment into committed capital before the window closes.


CEO Finke has said: "Smelter-grade alumina is a critical material that the United States cannot make. The Trump administration is very interested in critical materials. And what we like to say is that if the Trump administration put out a grant for critical materials, we would have applied to that grant — basically the exact same project."
 He is right on the substance. The policy architecture now exists to fund that project through DOD channels rather than DOE. The missing piece is not a government grant — it is a strategic partner with a creditworthy guarantee. That is a commercial negotiation, not a policy battle. And it is entirely within Brimstone's control.
