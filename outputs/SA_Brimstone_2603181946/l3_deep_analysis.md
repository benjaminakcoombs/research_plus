### I. THE OPPORTUNITY

CRH and Holcim committed $75M in combined equity and binding offtake to Sublime Systems in September 2024 — the largest strategic bet by incumbent cement producers on next-generation green cement technology.^1 Six months later, Sublime paused its Holyoke, Massachusetts plant, replaced its founder-CEO, and cut its workforce from 128 to approximately 28 employees. It is now operationally suspended with no announced path to restart.^2 Both cement majors' primary green OPC technology investments are effectively stranded. Meanwhile, in Oklahoma, EGA and Century Aluminum have signed a joint development agreement to build a 750,000-tonne-per-year primary aluminum smelter at Inola — the first new U.S. smelter since 1980 — requiring approximately 1.5 million tonnes per year of alumina feedstock.^3 ^4 The United States currently imports 1.1 million tonnes of alumina annually against a single domestic refinery operating at one-third capacity.^5

Brimstone Energy is the only company in the world that has achieved ASTM C150 certification for portland cement produced from non-limestone feedstock — and simultaneously co-produces domestic alumina from the same rock.^21 Its planned Oklahoma plant sits in the same state as the Inola smelter, with quarry feedstock already secured. The non-obvious finding: the cement majors' Sublime failure and EGA's alumina deficit converge on one company, creating two independent buyer universes competing for the same asset. A strategic investment of $100–200M that captures both theses — before CRH resolves its Sublime contractual entanglements and before Brimstone closes a Series B that the CFO hire, narrative repositioning, and database analytics all suggest is imminent^6 ^15 — represents a narrow window. By Q3 2026, the pre-competitive pricing advantage will likely be gone.

### II. COMPANY SITUATION

Brimstone Energy, founded in 2019 at Caltech, has developed a patented process — the Rock Refinery — that co-produces ASTM C150 portland cement, supplementary cementitious materials, and smelter-grade alumina from calcium silicate rocks rather than limestone. The plant design: mine one rock, grind it once, extract multiple products through acid leaching, crystallization, and calcination using commercially available (TRL 9) equipment.^7 The first commercial plant, sited at Dolese Bros.' Roosevelt quarry near Lawton, Oklahoma, targets 80,000 tonnes per year of OPC and 20,000 tonnes per year of alumina at a total capital cost of approximately $400M.^8 ^9

Revenue is zero. At Plant 1 nameplate capacity, estimated annual revenue is approximately $22M: roughly $11M from OPC, $9.6M from alumina (reflecting a domestic premium anchored by Inola smelter demand), and $1M from SCM.^10 The critical economic insight is that cement — the primary product by volume — operates near breakeven, while alumina — the co-product extracted at marginal cost from the same rock — generates the majority of economic surplus at an estimated unit margin of $320–390/t.^11 This is structurally identical to the Freeport-McMoRan model, where gold and molybdenum byproducts from copper mining generate most of the free cash flow.

Brimstone is private, VC-backed, and founder-led. CEO Cody Finke and co-founder Hugo Leandri (now CSO) control a two-person board — an unusually concentrated governance structure for a company with over $80M raised from institutional investors including Breakthrough Energy Ventures, DCVC, Amazon Climate Pledge Fund, and Kajima Ventures.^12 ^13 BEV holds a confirmed board seat; Amazon almost certainly holds change-of-control consent rights per standard CVC architecture. The company maintains a bifurcated entity structure — Brimstone Energy, Inc. (parent) and Brimstone Commercial, LLC (DOE grant recipient and likely project vehicle) — which is transaction-ready and would allow a strategic partner to invest at the project level without diluting the parent cap table. The last priced equity round was a $55M Series A in March 2022.^14 No Series B has been announced in the four years since. The October 2025 hire of CFO Tim Rens — who has raised approximately $20B across his career, including $750M for Monolith Materials' first-of-kind industrial plant with J.P. Morgan and Goldman Sachs as placement agents — is the clearest pre-transaction signal in the profile.^15 The company has 82 employees.^16

Three events in the last twelve months have compressed the deal timeline. First, the DOE cancelled Brimstone's $189M OCED grant in May 2025, disbursing only $8.7M of the total.^17 ^18 Brimstone is appealing but proceeding with private financing regardless. Second, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (July 2025) repealed the IRA's loan authority for the DOE Loan Programs Office, closing the most viable alternative government financing path.^19 The entire ~$400M plant must now be financed privately — or through DOD channels, where the Office of Strategic Capital has expanded with $500M in OBBBA appropriations specifically for critical mineral investments and has already deployed $550M to MP Materials under the same authority.^33 ^34 Third, Sublime's collapse has created an acute strategic gap at both CRH and Holcim — with Brimstone as the only operational alternative producing ASTM C150-certified product.

In December 2025, management rebranded the company as the "Fourth Great Refinery," expanding the product roadmap to include steel, aluminum, magnesium, and titanium — framing the addressable market at $2.4 trillion.^7 This is pre-roadshow narrative positioning. Steel, magnesium, and titanium co-production have not been validated at any scale; the first plant produces only OPC, alumina, and SCM.^20

The most important gap between narrative and reality concerns the alumina. Brimstone's cement has been independently certified to ASTM C150 by Twining Consulting and tested by Amazon's construction team.^21 ^22 The alumina, by contrast, has zero published specification data across any of the thirteen standard smelter-grade parameters — no Al₂O₃ purity, no Fe₂O₃ assay, no TiO₂ level, no crystal phase analysis — after six years of development.^23 Customer engagement language references "cement and SCM" when describing testing; no aluminum producer has signed any agreement for alumina volumes. The company's most strategically valuable narrative — "the only domestic non-bauxite alumina source" — rests on an unverified product claim. This is the central diligence question for any transaction.

### III. KEY FINDINGS

**The Alumina That Hasn't Been Tested: A $49M-Per-Plant Valuation Gap Hiding in the Geology**

**The Observation.** Brimstone claims "smelter-grade alumina" in every communication since January 2025, yet has published none of the thirteen standard SGA specification parameters after six years of development and $80M+ raised.^23 The cement product, by contrast, carries independent ASTM C150 certification.^21 Geological characterization of the Glen Mountains Layered Complex — the formation underlying the Roosevelt quarry — reveals the feedstock is not pure anorthosite but "Fe-rich, Ti-rich, and P-rich biotite-bearing gabbro."^24 SGA specification requires TiO₂ below 30 ppm; published hydrometallurgical research demonstrates that ilmenite (the dominant titanium mineral in this formation) dissolves 93–97% of its titanium under standard HCl leaching conditions.^25 ^26 Brimstone's patent describes no dedicated titanium removal step.^27 The decontamination factor required to reach SGA spec from this feedstock exceeds 700x for titanium alone — a level not demonstrated commercially from any acid-leach process.

**The Deal Implication.** If the alumina meets only chemical-grade specification ($150–250/t rather than $400–500/t), Plant 1 alumina revenue falls by approximately $5.6M annually, with an NPV impact of roughly $49M per plant at 8% over 20 years.^28 More consequentially, the entire "critical minerals platform" identity that justifies a 12–15x EBITDA multiple (vs. 7x for a green cement startup) depends on this claim — and so does the DOD financing architecture described in Finding 2: if the alumina is chemical-grade, the "critical minerals" eligibility that supports OSC loan access weakens materially. An independent SGA certification — equivalent to Twining's cement work — would cost approximately $150K and would be the single most value-accretive data point Brimstone could produce.

**Open Questions.** Has Brimstone's R&D team characterized titanium deportment through its process — specifically, does dissolved Ti co-precipitate with aluminum chloride hexahydrate crystals or report to the mother liquor? The answer, determinable via one ICP-MS measurement on pilot leachate, resolves the feasibility of the SGA pathway. It is worth asking why, after six years, this data has not been published — deliberate information management pending commercialization, or a genuine gap in what the pilot has demonstrated? And if the product is chemical-grade rather than smelter-grade, does the co-production cost advantage ($125–225/t estimated production cost vs. $560/t landed import cost^11) still create a commercially viable — if lower-valued — alumina business?

**Kajima's Quiet Investment Signals an EPC Architecture That Could Collapse the Financing Timeline by Two Years**

**The Observation.** Kajima Ventures — the CVC arm of Japan's largest construction conglomerate — appeared in Brimstone's investor list for the first time in October 2025, absent from the January 2025 roster, and disclosed only inside another press release.^13 Kajima Corporation holds an A+ credit rating, has seven decades of building chemical plants, steelworks, and nuclear reactors globally, and maintains a trilateral strategic alliance with Bechtel.^29 ^30 The DOD Office of Strategic Capital requires a "repayment guarantee from a creditworthy sponsor" for loans to pre-revenue companies.^31 No other entity on Brimstone's cap table satisfies this requirement. Dolese Bros., whose university foundation majority shareholders carry fiduciary duties to engineering scholarship programs, cannot serve as an equity co-sponsor.^32 The OSC has expanded with $500M in OBBBA appropriations specifically for critical mineral investments, and has already deployed $550M to MP Materials (domestic rare earths) under the same authority.^33 ^34

**The Deal Implication.** A Kajima-provided fixed-price EPC contract with performance bonds would simultaneously satisfy the OSC creditworthy sponsor requirement, transfer the pilot-to-commercial scale-up risk to an A+-rated counterparty, and do so at an estimated cost of $5–6M in bond premiums — less than 2% of the EPC contract value.^35 This single instrument could unlock $100–150M in OSC debt at Treasury-equivalent rates, compressing the financing timeline from approximately five years (multiple equity rounds) to approximately two and a half years (EPC guarantee → OSC loan → project finance close). This timeline compression is particularly consequential in light of CRH's Sublime constraints (Finding 3): the buyer who secures Brimstone before CRH's contractual entanglements are resolved captures the replacement asset at pre-competitive pricing. Evidence for the EPC linkage is circumstantial — consistent with Kajima's corporate strategy of full project lifecycle support from CVC investment through construction — but no EPC contract has been publicly disclosed.

**Open Questions.** Has Brimstone engaged Kajima Corporation (the parent, not just Kajima Ventures) for preliminary plant engineering? Is the Bechtel trilateral alliance being activated — and if so, does Bechtel's simultaneous FEED engagement on the EGA Inola smelter create a linked Oklahoma construction ecosystem? The most telling signal to monitor: if Kajima Corporation's FY2025 annual report (due June–July 2026) references a North American industrial minerals project, the hypothesis is confirmed. Separately, could an EPC guarantee be structured to satisfy both OSC and the new Energy Dominance Financing Program's critical minerals provisions — effectively accessing two government debt pools from one contractual instrument?

**CRH's Sublime Contracts Haven't Expired — Creating a Structural First-Mover Window That Closes With the Next Filing**

**The Observation.** CRH invested approximately $37.5M in Sublime Systems for equity, binding offtake, and "priority site selection rights for full-scale plant development in key markets."^36 Sublime has not filed for bankruptcy or initiated formal dissolution as of March 2026 — it remains in operational suspension with a new CEO (Rob Davies, former COO, appointed November 2025) and approximately 28 employees.^37 Because Sublime has not formally wound down, CRH's contractual provisions — including any non-compete covering competing green cement technology — remain legally in force. Separately, Amrize's Sublime position is structured as a convertible note and advance payments, not equity — a senior claim recoverable ahead of all equity in any wind-down.^38 Amrize can walk away from Sublime cleanly. CRH likely cannot invest in Brimstone without first resolving its Sublime obligations.

**The Deal Implication.** The buyer who moves on Brimstone before CRH's Sublime constraints are released captures the replacement asset at pre-competitive pricing. CRH completed 38 acquisitions totaling $4.1B in 2025, including the $2.1B Eco Material Technologies deal.^39 When CRH is free to act (upon Sublime's formal dissolution), it will move aggressively. For a competing buyer — EGA, Century Aluminum, or an infrastructure fund — the window between today and that event is the highest-leverage entry period. Brimstone's Series B provides the closing bracket.^6 ^15

**Open Questions.** Does CRH's Sublime agreement contain an explicit non-compete covering C150-certified green cement technology — or only the more limited "priority site selection" language that has been publicly disclosed? Could CRH structure an initial Brimstone position as a passive financial investment through CRH Ventures without triggering operational partnership restrictions? What does the Amrize AGM (April 21, 2026) reveal about the Sublime convertible note — impairment, bridge, or write-off? And is Siam Cement Group, an early Sublime investor, positioning to acquire Sublime's IP for Southeast Asian markets — a move that would permanently strand CRH's green cement technology bet and intensify CRH's urgency to secure Brimstone? For any non-cement buyer evaluating this window, the alumina specification question (Finding 1) becomes the critical differentiator — independent SGA certification would create a strategic rationale that CRH's cement-only Sublime thesis cannot match.
