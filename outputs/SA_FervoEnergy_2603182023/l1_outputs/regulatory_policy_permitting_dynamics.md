### A.2 | IRA Tax Credit Structure — Current Law Under OBBBA (July 4, 2025)

#### The Controlling Statutory Framework

The OBBBA is the controlling legal framework. It simultaneously terminated wind/solar tax credits on an accelerated timeline and preserved geothermal credits on the original IRA timeline with explicit statutory codification.

**Phase-down schedule for geothermal (§45Y/§48E) — current law:**

Other technologies like geothermal, nuclear, and hydrogen retain longer timelines, with a gradual phaseout starting in 2034. Wind and solar projects must meet the July 4, 2026 "beginning of construction" date or the December 31, 2027 placed-in-service deadline. Other technologies such as geothermal, hydrogen, and nuclear are subject to the same phaseout schedule as the §48E ITCs,
 meaning 100% in 2033, 75% in 2034, 50% in 2035, 0% in 2036.

**Wind/solar contrast — the codified statutory moat:**

Wind and solar face early termination deadlines — in order to be eligible for this credit, construction must begin on or before July 4, 2026, or, for projects beginning construction after July 4, 2026, such projects must be placed in service by the end of 2027.
 No such hard placed-in-service deadline applies to geothermal.

**Base credit rate / PWA uplift:** The base §48E ITC is 6%, rising to 30% for projects meeting prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements. Cape Station's union-labor construction profile is consistent with PWA compliance.

**ITC vs. PTC Selection:** For Cape Station Phase I — with hundreds of millions in upfront drilling capex — the §48E ITC at 30% is structurally superior to the §45Y PTC. A 30% ITC on eligible project costs generates a direct tax credit in the year of COD, dramatically improving project-level IRR and enabling tax equity financing. For later-stage pipeline projects where drilling costs have declined toward the Enhanced Geothermal Shot target, the PTC calculus becomes more favorable. Fervo's ability to elect either credit on a project-by-project basis is a key structural flexibility.

**Credit transferability (§6418) — retained for geothermal:** The OBBBA preserves the ability to transfer credits under §§45Y and 48E. Tax equity market access is intact.

**Five Percent Safe Harbor — retained for geothermal, eliminated for wind/solar:**

The familiar "5% safe harbor" introduced under Notice 2013-29 is eliminated for most wind and solar projects. Instead, taxpayers must now satisfy the "physical work test," which requires that physical work of a significant nature begins before July 5, 2026.
 
The interim guidance reaffirms that IRS Notice 2025-42 guidance on the beginning-of-construction (BOC) standards for solar and wind developers is not applicable to geothermal. The statutory deference to existing BOC standards established by Notices 2013-29 and 2018-59, and any subsequent guidance in effect on or prior to January
 [the OBBBA enactment date] governs for geothermal. Fervo therefore retains the Five Percent Safe Harbor for its pipeline projects — a material competitive advantage for locking in credit eligibility at a fraction of total project cost.

### A.4 | OBBBA FEOC/PFE Rules — IRS Notice 2026-15 (February 12, 2026): Materially New Guidance

#### Notice 2026-15: The First Actionable Compliance Framework


On February 12, 2026, the Treasury Department and IRS released Notice 2026-15, providing long-awaited interim guidance on the prohibited foreign entity (PFE) restrictions enacted by the OBBBA.



MACR is the quantitative test determining whether a qualified facility, energy storage technology, or eligible component includes too much PFE-sourced content to qualify for energy tax credits under §§45Y, 48E, and 45X. Since the OBBBA's passage, the absence of implementing guidance has been a significant source of uncertainty in the clean energy tax credit and associated transfer markets, constraining capital deployment and complicating deal execution. Notice 2026-15 begins to resolve that uncertainty by offering defined safe harbor pathways, reliance standards, and benchmarking for complex questions regarding "effective control."


#### Who Is a PFE? — Japan Is Not Covered


A specified foreign entity is the government of, an agency of, an individual of, or an entity organized under the laws of or having its principal place of business in Russia, China, Iran or North Korea, or a foreign-influenced entity, which is an entity controlled by a specified foreign entity.
 Japan is explicitly not a covered nation. Mitsui, MHI, and Turboden are Japanese entities — **not SFEs under current law.**

#### The Turboden/MHI MACR Compliance Pathway — Favorable Structure


Notice 2026-15 confirms that for §§48E and 45Y, MACR is calculated using the taxpayer's acquisition cost of manufactured products incorporated into the facility. Project owners are not required to reconstruct the manufacturer's internal cost structure. Instead, manufacturers certify the portion of product costs attributable to PFE-produced components, and the remaining portion of the acquisition cost is treated as non-PFE cost.


This is highly favorable for Fervo: the compliance burden for the Turboden ORC equipment supply falls on Turboden (as manufacturer), not Fervo. Turboden must certify what percentage of its ORC unit costs are attributable to PFE-sourced components. Since Turboden is Italian-headquartered (a Mitsubishi subsidiary but organized under Italian law, operating primarily in EU supply chains), the PFE attribution within Turboden's own supply chain is likely to be low — but must be formally certified.

**Critical limitation — geothermal not in safe harbor tables:** 
The safe harbors in Notice 2026-15 rely heavily on domestic content safe harbor guidance published in several notices between 2023 and 2025 listing manufactured products and components for certain technologies, such as ground mount and rooftop solar, battery energy storage technology and land-based wind facilities. Use of these safe harbors makes these rules largely workable and favorable for taxpayers, especially for those that have components listed in those notices.
 Geothermal ORC equipment is **not** in the 2023-2025 safe harbor tables. 
The Identification Safe Harbor is available only for projects listed as an 'Applicable Project' in the designated Safe Harbor Tables.
 This means Fervo's pipeline projects (BOC after December 31, 2025) must use the general MACR tracing methodology under Section 3 of Notice 2026-15, or obtain direct Turboden certifications under the Certification Safe Harbor — a workable but more burdensome path than the solar/wind/storage Identification + Cost Percentage safe harbor combination.

#### The Effective Control / Equity Ownership Gap — MHI Direct Equity Is Unresolved


The Notice is limited in scope. It does not address many "foreign entity of concern"-related debt and equity ownership, effective control, and material assistance rules enacted by the OBBBA that the industry has been awaiting. Future guidance on these issues is expected throughout 2026 and beyond.


**Critical new finding from Round 3 research:** 
Returning Series E investors include Mitsubishi Heavy Industries.
 MHI is therefore **both** Fervo's ORC equipment supplier (through Turboden) **and** a direct equity investor in Fervo. This dual relationship — equipment supplier + equity owner — is precisely the pattern that the OBBBA's "effective control" provisions are designed to scrutinize. 
Effective control exists when a PFE holds enforceable contractual rights that allow it to influence key aspects of a
 facility's development or operation. The pending guidance on effective control rules (expected throughout 2026) will determine whether MHI's combined equity + supply contract position constitutes "effective control" for OBBBA purposes. Since MHI is Japanese (not an SFE), this would only be a credit-disqualification issue if MHI were somehow deemed a "foreign-influenced entity" by being controlled by a covered nation entity — which is not the case for a Tokyo-listed Japanese industrial company. However, this needs formal legal analysis once IRS guidance on effective control is published.

**IP licensing alert:** 
IP licensing agreements entered into or modified after July 4, 2025, with a specified foreign entity are confirmed to disqualify the project.
 Any technology licensing between Fervo and a Chinese entity (e.g., any Chinese-owned equipment supplier or technology licensor) post-July 4, 2025 would be immediately disqualifying. This should be a specific diligence item: verify that no IP licensing agreements have been entered into with Chinese-connected entities since OBBBA enactment.

**Credit recapture (10-year rule):** For §48E projects, if any prohibited effective-control payments are made within 10 years after the facility is placed in service, the taxpayer is required to repay the entire value of the previously claimed tax credit. Given Cape Station Phase I's target COD of October 2026, this 10-year recapture window runs to approximately October 2036 — a long tail risk for any transaction involving post-closing changes in ownership or supply arrangements.

**Comment deadline — March 30, 2026:** 
The IRS has requested general comments with respect to PFE and material assistance issues, as well as specific comments with respect to calculating Total Direct Costs, anti-circumvention rules, and substantiation and documentation requirements. Comments are generally required to be submitted by March 30, 2026.
 Fervo (and any transaction advisor) should monitor the comment submissions and subsequent IRS guidance, which is expected to include new geothermal-specific safe harbor tables before December 31, 2026.

### D.3 | CFIUS Review — Foreign Acquisition Risk

**Framework:** Fervo triggers CFIUS mandatory jurisdiction on multiple prongs: critical infrastructure operator (power generation on federal lands), technology company with DOE-funded programs, and owner of real property interests on BLM leases.

**MHI/Mitsui scenario:** Japan is not a covered nation; CFIUS clearance for Japanese acquirers is historically routine with mitigation conditions. However, conversion of Mitsui's (or MHI's) existing minority position to a control transaction mandates a full CFIUS filing under FIRRMA. A 45-90 day review, clearance with mitigation conditions (security agreements, U.S. citizen board requirements, restrictions on foreign access to proprietary subsurface data), and DOE coordination for grant amendments would be required. Timeline addition: 3-6 months — in direct competition with the IPO window. The February 2026 DOJ lawsuit to enforce a CFIUS ruling (the first ever) signals heightened enforcement posture; voluntary pre-filing is strongly indicated.

**Shell (UK/Netherlands) scenario:** Shell Energy is already a Fervo PPA offtaker (31 MW; April 2025). Shell plc is foreign — CFIUS mandatory filing. UK entities are viewed favorably; clearance is probable with standard mitigation. Timeline similar to MHI/Mitsui scenario.

**Devon (U.S. domestic) scenario:** No CFIUS required. Devon is the cleanest M&A path from a regulatory standpoint. However, CPUC and PUCN PPA change-of-control provisions would still apply (requiring utility consent and potential commission notification). A fossil fuel parent would likely require enhanced financial assurances (performance bonds, parental guarantees) from CPUC, but there is no ownership restriction on fossil fuel companies owning clean energy assets under California or Nevada law.

### D.4 | PPA Assignment and Change-of-Control Provisions

Standard utility PPA change-of-control provisions require written notice to SCE and NV Energy, utility consent for assignment of PPA to new counterparty entity, and CPUC/PUCN approval for changes in seller entity. NV Energy's CTT structure — where NV Energy holds the Energy Supply Agreement with Fervo — means a Fervo change of control requires PUCN notification and potentially new ESA approval with the successor entity. These are standard, workable requirements — they create process and timeline risk, not substantive barriers to deal completion.

### ANOMALY 2 — MHI Is Both Equipment Supplier AND Direct Equity Investor: The FEOC Effective Control Analysis Is Unresolved

Returning Series E investors include Mitsubishi Heavy Industries.
 Combined with Turboden (MHI subsidiary) as the ORC equipment supplier for both Phase I and Phase II of Cape Station, MHI occupies a dual role — equipment supplier + equity investor — that is precisely what the OBBBA's effective control provisions are designed to scrutinize. The critical mitigant is that Japan is not a covered nation (SFE), so MHI is not a PFE unless it is "controlled by" a China/Russia/Iran/DPRK entity (which it is not). 
The Notice does not address foreign entity of concern-related debt and equity ownership and effective control rules enacted by the OBBBA that the industry has been awaiting. Future guidance on these issues is expected throughout 2026 and beyond.
 Until IRS issues effective control guidance, the MHI dual-role position creates a material regulatory uncertainty that cannot be fully resolved in diligence. Any transaction involving MHI as acquirer or post-closing operator should condition closing on IRS guidance confirming MHI's non-PFE status in the combined equipment supply + equity ownership context.

The bipartisan shelter thesis has graduated from political posture to federal statute. 
The Trump 2.0 budget bill took a hacksaw to most clean energy credits but largely spared geothermal.
 Geothermal credits phase down starting in 2034 (not immediately terminated like wind/solar). Any analyst treating Fervo's IRA/OBBBA exposure as equivalent to a wind or solar developer is analytically wrong as a matter of current enacted federal law.

### ANOMALY 4 — Corsac Station Is Mid-Permitting: The CTT Revenue Stream Has a 12-24 Month Minimum Permitting Lag
The most important unresolved commercial risk in Fervo's profile has received essentially no coverage in media reporting. 
According to GlobalData, Corsac Station is currently at the permitting stage, with construction likely to commence in 2026 and commercial operation expected in 2027.
 The 115 MW Nevada project — which underpins the entire CTT/NV Energy/Google revenue stream — has not received BLM permits. A 2027 COD implies the CTT contracted revenue begins at the earliest approximately 24 months after the current analysis date. At $80-100/MWh × 115 MW × 8,760 hours, the annual contracted revenue is approximately $80-100 million. A 2-year delay in this revenue stream — at a 10% discount rate — implies a present-value impact of $145-180 million on deal pricing. This is a material, unpriced risk in most public analyses of Fervo's value.

### ANOMALY 5 — Cape Station's Phase I Net Capacity Is ~60-65 MW, Not 100 MW: The 40% Parasitic Load Issue

The EIA shows the first three generators at Cape Station have a combined nameplate capacity of 159 megawatts, but only 91 megawatts of net capacity. In plain English, that means the system is expected to use 40% of the electricity it generates just to operate.
 The "100 MW Phase I" headline figure is gross generating capacity. Net capacity — what is actually delivered to the grid and sold under PPAs — is approximately 60-65 MW for Phase I at the facility's configuration. This has direct implications for: (a) revenue modeling (net MWh sold ≠ gross MWh generated × $X/MWh); (b) the §48E ITC eligible cost basis (is the full capex attributable to 100 MW gross or 60 MW net?); (c) PPA delivery obligation compliance (are the SCE contracts written on net or gross capacity basis?). This anomaly has been almost entirely absent from media coverage and investor materials.

### ANOMALY 6 — The CPUC Docket Contains a Formal Pricing Protest That Was Overruled: EGS Is Premium-Priced but Defensible
The Cal Advocates pricing protest of the Cape Station 3 and 4 contracts is buried in AL 5207-E and has received essentially no media coverage. Cal Advocates requested that CPUC reject and require renegotiation, characterizing the price as above "the range of levelized costs for incremental binary geothermal." CPUC Staff found the contracts were "the lowest geothermal prices offered at the time of the solicitation." This protest establishes: (a) EGS is priced at a premium to conventional geothermal ($60-70/MWh); (b) the premium is defensible on the grounds that EGS is a distinct technology from binary geothermal; (c) CPUC-approved EGS pricing can survive Cal Advocates opposition — an important precedent for all future California geothermal PPA approvals.

### ANOMALY 7 — IRS Notice 2026-15 Establishes MACR Compliance but Geothermal Is Not in the Safe Harbor Tables

The safe harbors in Notice 2026-15 rely heavily on domestic content safe harbor guidance listing manufactured products and components for certain technologies, such as ground mount and rooftop solar, battery energy storage technology, and land-based wind facilities.
 Geothermal ORC equipment is absent. This means Fervo's pipeline projects (BOC after December 31, 2025) cannot use the streamlined Identification + Cost Percentage safe harbor methodology — they must use the general MACR tracing methodology or Turboden certifications. 
Taxpayers may rely on supplier certifications and the assigned cost percentages provided in Notice 2025-08 until Treasury issues updated safe harbor tables, expected sometime before December 31, 2026.
 The practical implication: Fervo's Corsac Station project (BOC after December 31, 2025 if construction commences in 2026) must obtain Turboden MACR certification before claiming any §48E credit — a compliance step with no precedent in the geothermal industry. Fervo should proactively engage Turboden/MHI on certification documentation now, and comment on the Notice 2026-15 comment process (deadline: March 30, 2026) to advocate for geothermal-specific safe harbor table inclusion.

### ANOMALY 8 — The Five Percent BOC Safe Harbor Is Fully Retained for Geothermal: A ~$800M+ Pipeline Insurance Policy at Minimal Cost

The interim guidance reaffirms that IRS Notice 2025-42 guidance on the beginning-of-construction standards for solar and wind developers is not applicable to geothermal. The statutory deference to existing BOC standards established by Notices 2013-29 and 2018-59
 applies to geothermal. This means Fervo can spend 5% of projected project cost on any pipeline project to "begin construction" and lock in full ITC/PTC eligibility through the 2033 BOC deadline. On a 16 GW pipeline at $1-2M/MW, a 5% safe harbor expenditure on a representative 500 MW project costs approximately $25-50 million but locks in credit eligibility on $2.5-5B in future project capex generating $750M-$1.5B in future tax credits. This safe harbor retention is worth far more than its option cost — and is entirely absent from public discourse about Fervo's competitive advantages.
