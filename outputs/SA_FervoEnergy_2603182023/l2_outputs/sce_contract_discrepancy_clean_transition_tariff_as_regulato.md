### 4.1 What It Is, What It Isn't (Corrected Across All Rounds)

The CTT is a Nevada PUCN-approved mechanism, not a CPUC/California instrument. The distinction matters structurally: California's contracts are utility-obligation PPAs driven by the MTR reliability mandate. Nevada's CTT is a voluntary corporate-subsidized pathway that bypasses least-cost utility procurement constraints. Fervo benefits from *both*, through *separate* regulatory mechanisms in *separate* jurisdictions.

The CTT operates as a differential cost-plus tariff: if NV Energy would have deployed solar at $45/MWh in a least-cost scenario, and geothermal costs $95/MWh, Google pays the $50/MWh differential. NV Energy is economically whole; ratepayers pay zero premium; Google receives the environmental attributes and supply certainty. 
There are no commercial-scale dedicated EGS plants in operation in the United States
 — meaning the CTT is the mechanism that fills the gap between EGS LCOE and utility least-cost benchmarks during the commercialization phase.

## SECTION 5: PURPA PRECEDENT AND THE ØRSTED/CfD ANALOGY — FINAL SYNTHESIS

### 5.1 PURPA's Structural Parallel

The MTR mandate is the modern institutional equivalent of PURPA's avoided-cost must-buy obligation. In both cases:
- A regulatory mandate created a *non-market* procurement channel for specific technology categories
- First movers who secured long-duration contracts at favorable terms captured disproportionate value
- The regulatory structure persisted for 15-20 years, protecting contracted cash flows even as the broader energy market repriced

The key difference: PURPA's avoided cost pricing was based on forecast fuel prices that proved too high, creating a windfall for QF developers. The MTR mandate's pricing is contested (Cal Advocates protest), approved as "lowest geothermal price available," and subsequently increased — suggesting the pricing is calibrated to actual EGS LCOE rather than a regulatory windfall.

### 5.2 The Ørsted/CfD Analogy — Final Corrected Version

Ørsted built its valuation on sequentially locked-in CfD contracts (UK government-backstopped, inflation-indexed, 15-year duration) for offshore wind — an immature technology at the time of early CfD rounds. The structural parallel to Fervo's contracted position is close:

| Feature | Ørsted (CfD) | Fervo (MTR PPA + CTT) |

## SECTION 9: WHAT WOULD DISPROVE THIS — FINAL RISK REGISTER

Ranked by probability × materiality:

1. **Phase 1 operational underperformance below 70% capacity factor (HIGH materiality, LOW-MEDIUM probability after construction confirmation).** If the 100-well Phase 1 field produces below 70 MW sustained output, the entire revenue thesis restructures. This is the single remaining binary risk. The DeGolyer & MacNaughton third-party reserves report and the 107 kg/s well test substantially mitigate this risk, but reservoir interference at fleet scale has not yet been demonstrated.

2. **Phase 2 (400 MW) cost escalation beyond the $2B total Cape budget (HIGH materiality, MEDIUM probability).** Phase 2 is 4x the scale of Phase 1. If per-well costs escalate from drilling complications at deeper intervals (Phase 2 likely uses Sugarloaf-depth wells at 13,000-15,000+ ft), the $4,000/kW capex assumption could prove conservative. The E-5404 price increase provides a partial hedge, but the magnitude is unknown.

3. **CPUC MTR mandate reform or repeal (MEDIUM materiality, LOW probability).** 
In 2023, the CPUC issued a supplemental MTR procurement order, based on the 2022 IEPR load forecast, requiring jurisdictional LSEs to procure an additional 4 GW NQC (15.5 GW NQC in total) of procurement for 2027-28.
 The MTR mandate is being *expanded*, not contracted. The 15.5 GW total requirement represents an enormous unfilled demand pipeline that insulates Fervo's approved contracts from policy reversal.

4. **The 398 MW reflects CPUC capacity credit conversion rather than nameplate generation (LOW probability, confirmed against primary source language).** E-5404 uses consistent "MW contract" language across the filing. The 62+336=398 MW is nameplate, not NQC-adjusted.

5. **CTT framework abandoned by Google or corporate CFE commitments retreat (LOW near-term probability, MEDIUM long-term).** The Corsac Station contract's existence depends on Google's sustained willingness to pay a premium. Given Google's multi-year, publicly disclosed CFE commitments and its own Series E investment in Fervo, this risk is low in the contractual timeframe.
