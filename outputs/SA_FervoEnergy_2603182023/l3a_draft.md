## I. The Opportunity

On March 11, Google closed its $4.75B acquisition of Intersect Power — then spun off the operating assets, paying $4.75B effectively for an intermittent solar development pipeline.^1 One week later, Fervo Energy's CEO shifted to textbook dual-track language ("looking at a variety of options") six weeks after filing a confidential S-1.^2 Fervo offers what Intersect structurally cannot: 500 MW of firm, 24/7 carbon-free electricity under 15-year PPAs with investment-grade counterparties, approaching first power in June 2026.^3 The IPO targets $2–3B.^4 Google's own environmental report shows it reached only 66% hourly clean energy matching in 2024 — a 34-percentage-point gap to its 2030 target that cannot be closed by adding more solar.^5 Fervo is the only commercial-scale enhanced geothermal developer in the United States with contracted offtake at this scale, holding roughly 63% of all next-generation geothermal PPAs ever signed.^6 The observation most likely missing from the reader's coverage: CPUC regulatory records show Fervo's Southern California Edison contract totals 398 MW — not the 320 MW in every Fervo press release — with an approved price increase embedded in the amendment.^7 That 78 MW delta alone is worth approximately $450–480M in 15-year NPV.^8 The pre-IPO acquisition window — between today and the expected public S-1 filing in April–May 2026 — is measured in weeks.

## II. Company Situation

Fervo Energy is a Houston-based enhanced geothermal systems developer that adapts horizontal drilling and hydraulic fracturing from shale oil and gas to manufacture geothermal reservoirs in hot dry rock. Cold water is pumped underground, circulated through fractured granite, and returned to the surface where Organic Rankine Cycle turbines convert the thermal energy to electricity. The company's sole revenue model at maturity is contracted, fixed-price $/MWh electricity sales under long-term PPAs — a high-quality infrastructure revenue stream with no fuel cost and no commodity exposure. Today, Fervo is effectively pre-revenue: its only operating asset is a 3.5 MW pilot (Project Red, Nevada, operational since November 2023).^9 All material revenue depends on Cape Station Phase I achieving commercial operation in late 2026.

Cape Station, sited in Beaver County, Utah, is a 500 MW gross project (~400 MW net after blended parasitic load) with BLM permits for up to 2 GW and an independently certified 5+ GW geological resource base from DeGolyer & MacNaughton.^10 Phase I (100 MW, three generating units) is approaching commissioning, with EIA records showing the first 53 MW unit registered for June 2026 grid interconnection.^11 Phase II (400 MW) has ORC equipment contracted from both Turboden (MHI subsidiary, 180 MWe) and Baker Hughes (300 MWe), with a 2028 COD target.^12 The full 500 MW is contracted across four offtakers: SCE (398 MW per CPUC records), Shell Energy (31 MW), Clean Power Alliance (53+ MW), and NV Energy/Google at Corsac Station (115 MW, $107/MWh confirmed, online "later this decade").^13 At stabilization, estimated annual revenue is $260–350M with EBITDA margins of 55–65%, consistent with mature geothermal operators.^14

Fervo has raised $1.69B across equity, project-level preferred, debt, and grants.^15 The Series E ($462M, December 2025, led by B Capital) closed at an implied post-money of approximately $2.86B — above the low end of the current $2–3B IPO target range, creating a structural tension with B Capital's likely QIPO conversion threshold.^16 The capital structure is layered and complex: a Mercuria corporate term loan ($100M, with change-of-control covenants), an XRA bridge loan ($145.6M, construction-phase), Breakthrough Energy Catalyst project preferred ($100M), and Centaurus Capital project preferred ($75M).^17 Any acquirer navigating this cap table faces at least three lender consent requirements before closing.

The ownership map is the most deal-relevant feature of the company. Devon Energy disclosed a 15% equity stake in its Q4 2025 earnings — making it the largest identified strategic shareholder — with engineers embedded at Cape Station.^18 The Devon-Coterra $58B all-stock merger has now closed, creating a combined shale operator for whom a pre-revenue geothermal stake is a strategic orphan.^19 Google entered the cap table in the Series E alongside Mitsui & Co., joining MHI (returning investor and parent of Turboden, the primary ORC supplier).^20 CalSTRS and CPP Investments, both over their PE allocation targets, want liquidity. B Capital, controlling the Series E class vote, is the gatekeeper of any sub-$2.86B IPO pricing. These preferences do not align on a single optimal transaction structure.

The most consequential recent signal: six banks — BofA, Barclays, Goldman Sachs, HSBC, RBC, and SMBC — are in active IPO discussions, though no formal mandate has been announced.^21 Goldman was reportedly mandated as lead underwriter in July 2023 and the IPO was delayed over two years — a precedent that tempers timeline confidence.^22 The CEO's March 12 "variety of options" language, combined with the 55-day absence of a bookrunner announcement after the confidential S-1 filing, is consistent with a dual-track process.^23 Critically, JPMorgan — which advised Devon on the Fervo Series D — is notably absent from the IPO banker roster, suggesting JPM may be advising on strategic alternatives rather than the equity offering.^24

Where the narrative diverges from reality: Fervo describes Cape Station as a "500 MW" project without distinguishing gross from net. EIA data shows Phase I's three generators have 159 MW of nameplate capacity but only 91 MW of net summer capacity — a 43% parasitic load consumed by injection pumps and cooling systems.^25 Phase II equipment claims suggest dramatically lower parasitic rates, but this is unconfirmed at scale. Anyone modeling 500 MW net overstates revenue by approximately 20–25%.^26 The 28-month silence on Project Red operational data — no updated production figures since February 2025 — is explainable by pre-IPO information staging, but an acquirer committing $3B+ of capital should treat an independent reservoir engineering review as non-negotiable diligence.

## III. Key Findings

**Devon Energy's 15% Fervo Stake Sits Inside a $58B Shale Merger — Creating the Sector's Most Motivated Block Seller or Most Informed Acquirer**

**The Observation.** Devon Energy disclosed a 15% ownership stake in Fervo in its Q4 2025 earnings, describing it as "a strategic hedge and technology play leveraging Devon's horizontal drilling and subsurface expertise."^18 The Devon-Coterra $58B all-stock merger closed in approximately March 2026.^19 The combined entity — led by Clay Gaspar and Tom Jorden — targets $1B in annual pre-tax synergies focused on Delaware Basin oil and Marcellus gas.^27 A ~$430–450M illiquid geothermal stake is a rounding error for a $58B shale operator and a distraction for an investor relations team pitching shale synergies to O&G-focused institutions. Devon's technology transfer to Fervo is "no longer experimental — it's an institutionalized process with Devon's subject matter experts embedded with Fervo's teams."^28

**The Deal Implication.** Devon is simultaneously the most motivated potential seller and the most informationally advantaged potential acquirer in Fervo's cap table. At a $3B total valuation, Devon's existing 15% is worth $450M; the incremental cost for the remaining 85% is ~$2.55B — well within range for the combined Devon-Coterra entity. Alternatively, Devon could sell its 15% block to a strategic buyer (MHI, Mitsui, Google) in a secondary transaction that would reshape the cap table dynamics ahead of the IPO. JPMorgan's absence from the IPO banker roster, despite having advised Devon on the Fervo Series D, is consistent with JPM having been retained for a Devon-side strategic advisory role.^24 A secondary block trade or pre-IPO strategic advisory mandate sits directly at this intersection.

**Open Questions.** Has New Devon's corporate development team communicated a position on the Fervo stake to Fervo's board — and if so, did that communication trigger the CEO's shift to "variety of options" language six days later? Does Devon hold ROFO or ROFR provisions in its shareholder agreement that would give it the right to preempt an IPO by purchasing at the offering price? It is worth asking whether JPMorgan's conspicuous absence from a six-bank IPO bake-off reflects a conflict, or a mandate that runs in the other direction entirely. If Devon's internal strategic review concludes geothermal is non-core, who are the natural secondary block buyers — and would a $300–450M block trade at a discount to IPO pricing create a repricing event that destabilizes the offering?

**CPUC Records Show Fervo's SCE Contract at 398 MW — 78 MW Above Every Public Model, With an Approved Price Increase**

**The Observation.** Fervo's June 2024 press release announced "two power purchase agreements totaling 320 MW with Southern California Edison."^29 This remains the company's last public statement on SCE contract size. However, CPUC Resolution E-5404 (July 2025) approved amendments: Cape Station 3 was adjusted from 70 MW to 62 MW and Cape Station 4 increased from 250 MW to 336 MW, for a net SCE total of 398 MW — a 78 MW (24.4%) increase that has never appeared in a Fervo press release.^7 The resolution also approved an upward price adjustment — a rarity in utility regulation where renegotiations almost universally favor ratepayers.^30 Separately, CPUC staff confirmed that the contracts "were the lowest geothermal prices offered at the time of the solicitation" and that the facilities have "forecasted capacity factors above 80 percent."^31

**The Deal Implication.** At an estimated ~$95/MWh and 82% capacity factor, the 78 MW increment generates approximately $53M/year in revenue — or $450–480M in 15-year NPV at 8%.^8 This is material, underpublicized value that the S-1 will reveal. It also means SCE maintained its proportional share through the 400→500 MW upsize, suggesting contractual expansion rights and deep institutional commitment. Any acquirer or IPO investor modeling from press releases alone understates Fervo's SCE revenue base by approximately 24%. The approved price increase, while the specific amount remains redacted under CPUC confidentiality provisions, signals that the original 2023 pricing was demonstrably below actual EGS delivery costs — and that the post-amendment price sits at or above the top of the $80–100/MWh range sourced from western utility regulatory documents.^32

**Open Questions.** Was the 86 MW Cape Station 4 increment priced at the same $/MWh as the original 250 MW tranche, or at 2025-vintage rates reflecting increased demand and updated cost data? Cal Advocates formally protested the original contracts, requesting CPUC reject them and force renegotiation — the CPUC staff sided with Fervo, but was a final commissioner vote recorded approving the amended contracts?^31 If the protest outcome remains contested at the time of S-1 filing, it would need to be disclosed as a material risk factor — something no public analysis has flagged.

**Mitsubishi Heavy Industries Supplies 60% of Cape Station's Power Equipment and Holds Equity — Making a $4B Acquisition Self-Financing from Supply Economics**

**The Observation.** MHI's wholly owned Italian subsidiary Turboden S.p.A. was selected to supply the entirety of Phase I ORC equipment (120 MWe, now approaching commissioning) and has been awarded a Phase II contract for three additional 60 MWe units (180 MWe), for a total confirmed Turboden scope of 300 MWe — 60% of Cape Station's generating capacity.^33 MHI Group (via MHIA) is simultaneously a Series D and Series E equity investor.^34 Turboden established a dedicated U.S. subsidiary, Turboden America LLC, explicitly "leveraging the support of Mitsubishi Heavy Industries America" — the same entity that holds the equity position.^35 In February 2026, Fervo drilled its hottest well at a new greenfield site (Project Blanford, >555°F at ~11,200 ft), crossing the temperature threshold where Mitsubishi Power's steam turbines — a second MHI business unit — become the thermodynamically preferred equipment.^36

**The Deal Implication.** At $1.5–2.0M per MW of ORC equipment cost, Turboden's confirmed Cape Station revenue is approximately $450–600M.^37 If the Cape Station site develops toward its 2 GW permitted capacity, Turboden's supply pipeline reaches $3–4B. A $3.5–4.5B acquisition of Fervo by MHI Group would be substantially self-financing from supply contract economics alone — before accounting for the technology transfer value of deploying EGS across Japan's largely untapped geothermal resource base (third-largest globally).^38 For any third-party acquirer, MHI's dual position creates a structural challenge: the Turboden supply contracts almost certainly contain change-of-control provisions that could allow MHI to renegotiate terms or exercise preferential rights upon acquisition by a competitor. An acquirer who does not resolve the MHI relationship in advance may find the equipment supply chain is the binding constraint on the deal.

**Open Questions.** Are the Turboden supply contracts priced at arm's-length market rates — or does MHI's equity position create an Item 404 related-party disclosure obligation that will surface in the S-1, revealing exact contract values for the first time? Does MHI hold a right of first offer on Fervo's future project equipment needs — and if so, does this extend to Project Blanford, where Mitsubishi Power's steam turbines would be the relevant technology? CFIUS review of a full MHI acquisition is expected but manageable for a Japanese allied-nation acquirer — the more interesting question is whether MHI's existing position (equity + 60% supply) already constitutes "effective control" under the OBBBA's pending IRS guidance, and whether this could affect Fervo's §48E tax credit eligibility for Phase II.
