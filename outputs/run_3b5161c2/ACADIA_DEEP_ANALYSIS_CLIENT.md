## I. The Opportunity

Acadia Healthcare's 178 methadone clinics — the nation's largest opioid treatment platform — generate an estimated $150–170M in EBITDA at 26–30% margins. At the multiples currently being tested in BayMark Health Services' active sale process (10–12x EBITDA on $75M), that segment alone is worth $1.0–2.0 billion. Acadia's entire enterprise trades at $4.6 billion, roughly 7.6x blended EBITDA, because four simultaneous federal investigations have made the company uninvestable for most institutional capital. Meanwhile, management's headline "$200M+ embedded EBITDA opportunity" from 3,000 new beds overstates shareholder-attributable economics by 27–35% once JV minority interests, Medicaid credentialing lags, and non-recurring state payment catch-ups are stripped out. The realistic figure is $105–135M.^1

The gap is not academic. On March 12, 2026, the board installed Daniel Cancelmi — Tenet Healthcare's former CFO and the architect of Tenet's $8B hospital divestiture program — as a new director "following constructive engagement with Khrom Capital." Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan are retained as financial advisors. Kirkland & Ellis is legal counsel. CEO Osteen's compensation is structured around VWAP hurdles at $25/$35/$45 — transaction triggers, not operating targets. Waud Capital's own website lists Acadia as an "exit."^2

This is a 12–18 month window. The CTC segment is legally separable without triggering DEA or SAMHSA re-registration (a finding most market participants have not identified). Ninety-one percent of non-CTC beds sit on owned real estate worth $1.0–1.5B in sale-leaseback proceeds. And 29–36% short interest creates violent squeeze mechanics on any definitive agreement. The franchise is depreciating under current ownership — every month the DOJ investigation continues, health systems defer new JV partnerships (zero new signings since January 2024) and competitors capture the behavioral health partnerships Acadia should be winning.^3

## II. Company Situation

Acadia Healthcare is the largest pure-play behavioral health company in the United States: 277 facilities, 12,500+ licensed beds, 40 states plus Puerto Rico, 84,000 patients treated daily, $3.31 billion in FY2025 revenue. The business operates across structurally distinct segments that happen to share a corporate parent. Acute inpatient psychiatric hospitals (~52% of revenue, ~$1.73B) are predominantly owned real estate, carry almost all of the company's goodwill and PLGL liability, and are the subject of a DOJ Criminal Division investigation for allegedly holding patients unnecessarily to maximize insurance payments. Comprehensive Treatment Centers (~17%, ~$576M) are 178 outpatient methadone clinics — 86% leased, capital-light, protected by a regulatory moat of DEA + SAMHSA + state licensing + Certificate of Need requirements in 19 states. Specialty and residential treatment (~27%, ~$890M) rounds out the portfolio.^4

Payer mix tells the deeper story: 57.7% Medicaid, 24.6% commercial, 14.3% Medicare. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025, projects $911 billion in Medicaid funding reductions over a decade and loss of eligibility for 10.3+ million people. Medicaid funds more than one-quarter of all behavioral healthcare nationally. Management claims the "medically frail" exemption protects Acadia's acute population, but OBBBA's impacts extend well beyond work requirements. State-directed supplemental payments — which contributed ~$230M to Acadia's 2025 revenue including a $28.5M non-recurring Tennessee catch-up — face a 10% annual phase-down beginning January 2028, with rate caps dropping from average commercial rates to 100–110% of Medicare. Six-month redetermination cycles starting December 2026 will create rolling coverage gaps for Acadia's most vulnerable patient population. For a company where Medicaid is the majority payer, OBBBA is not a background risk — it is a structural margin headwind compressing the very EBITDA base that any transaction must be priced against.^5

**Who owns it and what that means.** This is now a multi-activist situation. Engine Capital (~3% stake) demanded CTC separation and sale-leaseback in September 2025. Khrom Capital (5.5%, 13D filer) called for a formal strategic alternatives review including a full-company sale. Greenlight Capital (David Einhorn) disclosed a position in February 2026 as a value play. Institutional ownership stands at 88.95% with Vanguard, BlackRock, T. Rowe Price, Wellington, and FMR as the top holders. Short interest is 35.9% of float — 32.5 million shares with a 7.3-day cover ratio — among the highest in healthcare services.^6

**What's changed in twelve months.** The board replaced CEO Chris Hunter with returning CEO Debbie Osteen in January 2026, then added Cancelmi in March. A $996.2 million goodwill impairment in Q4 2025 cleaned the balance sheet — write-downs of this magnitude in the quarter before a strategic process are not coincidental. Capital expenditure was slashed from $572M to $255–280M for 2026, with paused projects "mainly non-JV" — revealing that joint venture facilities are protected assets while wholly-owned de novos are discretionary. Osteen publicly stated she would be "open to another way to bring value to shareholders" regarding the CTC segment at the Raymond James Conference on March 2, 2026. Goldman Sachs' equity arm simultaneously accumulated 4.7 million ACHC shares in Q4 2025 while its investment banking arm advises the company.^7

**Where the narrative doesn't match reality.** Management's headline claim — "$200M+ incremental EBITDA opportunity" from 3,000 new beds added 2023–2026 — is overstated by approximately 27–35%. The figure ignores JV minority partner economics (five of six new facilities in 2025 were JVs at 65–87% Acadia ownership, meaning 13–35% of economics flow to partners), six-to-eight month Medicaid credentialing delays extending breakeven to 36–48 months rather than management's implied 18–24, and $28.5M in non-recurring Tennessee state-directed payment catch-ups inflating the 2025 baseline. The realistic shareholder-attributable, timing-adjusted figure is $105–135M. UHS's mature behavioral health platform — the closest operational comparable — shows same-facility volume growth of only 0.7%, confirming that even established operators struggle with behavioral health volume acceleration once initial ramp is complete.^8

## III. Key Findings

**The CTC Segment Is Legally Separable — and the Market Doesn't Know It**

The operating subsidiaries holding Acadia's DEA and SAMHSA registrations — Habit Opco, LLC; CRC Health Corporation; and various state-specific entities — are not Acadia Healthcare Company, Inc. Under 42 CFR Part 8, the "program sponsor" on each SAMHSA OTP certification is the operating subsidiary, not the parent corporation. In an equity spinoff that preserves legal entity continuity, no SAMHSA re-certification or DEA re-registration is triggered. BayMark Health Services has undergone three PE-level ownership transitions since 2015 with zero documented DEA/SAMHSA disruption. Acadia's own 2014 CRC Health acquisition — 140+ OTP programs — closed in four months with operating subsidiary entity continuity. North Carolina eliminated CON requirements for chemical dependency treatment in 2023 (Session Law 2023-7), de-risking one of Acadia's top 10 CTC states.^9

*Deal implication:* A Section 355 tax-free spinoff is feasible. SpinCo would trade at 9–13x standalone EBITDA versus Acadia's blended 6.5x. Net value unlock: $650M base case to $1.23B bull case ($7–14/share on a $16–25 stock). The binding constraint is Acadia's debt architecture — CTC subsidiaries are guarantors of the $550M 7.375% Senior Notes and $650M term loan. A bondholder consent solicitation with a 30–50 bps premium is required.^10

*Key uncertainty:* The DOJ's CTC billing investigation — prompted by the December 2024 NYT exposé documenting record falsification at methadone clinics — could escalate into a formal False Claims Act action against CTC entities before separation closes, contaminating the asset with its own legal cloud.

---

**The Insurance Market Has Already Priced a $400–650M PLGL Reserve Gap**

Acadia's PLGL reserve stands at $153M as of December 31, 2025. The commercial insurance market's verdict is that this number is materially inadequate: self-insured retention increased from $3M in 2022 to $15M currently — a five-fold increase that no actuary imposes on an adequately reserved book. Facility-by-facility litigation inventory identifies 120–200+ named claimants across 10+ facilities. Lakeland Behavioral Health in Missouri has 32 plaintiffs, two employees criminally convicted of sexual assault, and allegations of chemical incapacitation. Detroit Behavioral Institute has 40+ retained claimants. Three consecutive years of adverse reserve development constitutes a statistical pattern with less than 12.5% probability of occurring by chance.^11

*Deal implication:* The probability-weighted reserve gap is $400–650M. Any acquirer must underwrite this liability, reducing bid prices by $4–7/share. Conversely, a board-initiated independent actuarial review and reserve charge would create the clean balance sheet a transaction requires — and capture the re-rating benefit for existing shareholders rather than leaving it on the table for a buyer. UHS's approach (transparent quarterly reserve building, narrowing the DOJ settlement gap incrementally) demonstrates the credit-rational strategy.^12

*Key uncertainty:* Pennsylvania's lookback window bill — which would retroactively open pre-2019 claims — passed the PA House in 2025. If it clears the Senate, Southwood Psychiatric's claims universe expands from dozens to potentially hundreds.

---

**Zero JV Facilities Appear in the PLGL Claims Stream — and the Market Misprices the JV Platform**

Every facility named in DOJ settlements, every facility with active sexual abuse litigation, and every closed facility is wholly-owned. The 17–18 operating JV hospitals — co-governed with Henry Ford, Geisinger, Ascension, Intermountain, Fairview, ECU Health, and other health systems — have generated no documented PLGL exposure. This is structural, not statistical luck: health system co-ownership creates independent medical executive committees, health system compliance officers, and board-level quality reporting that form barriers to the understaffing and oversight failures at the root of the abuse claims. Geisinger has signed two JVs; Ascension has signed two. Repeat partners are the strongest signal that the governance model works. The JV portfolio's VIE assets total $1.046B with only $35.3M in liabilities — virtually unencumbered. And the $100M technology investment Acadia announced in response to its DOJ crisis — standardized EHR, remote patient monitoring, a 50-KPI clinical quality dashboard — is inadvertently building the operational infrastructure that makes the JV platform scalable as a management services organization.^13

*Deal implication:* Encompass Health operates 55–60 structurally identical JV inpatient rehabilitation facilities and trades at 12–14x EBITDA — nearly double Acadia's blended multiple. The same Geisinger health system that operates an Encompass JV also operates an Acadia JV; the legal structures are identical down to the LLC formation documents. The 21 health system JV partners read like the client roster of an established MSO — a buyer who can isolate and scale this platform inherits an Encompass-comparable asset at a 40–50% discount. In a take-private, the wholly-owned portfolio is the liability; the JV portfolio is the prize.^14

*Key uncertainty:* The JV pipeline has frozen — zero new partnerships announced since January 2024. Resolving the DOJ investigation is the prerequisite to restarting JV origination. If resolution takes 3+ years, competitors (UHS, LifePoint/Apollo) may capture the health system relationships permanently.
