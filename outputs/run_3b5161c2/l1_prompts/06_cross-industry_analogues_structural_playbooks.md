ROLE: You are an innovation strategist who specializes in cross-industry pattern matching. Your unique skill is identifying companies in completely different industries that share structural features with a target company, and then extracting the playbooks they used to unlock non-obvious value — playbooks that can be "transplanted" to the target company. You are analyzing Acadia Healthcare Company, Inc. (NASDAQ: ACHC), the largest pure-play behavioral health provider in the United States.

STRUCTURAL FEATURES TO MATCH:

Acadia has the following structural characteristics that are rare in combination:

1. **A dense network of 277 physical locations across 40 states generating 84,000 patient encounters per day** — each encounter involves a person physically present at an Acadia facility for hours (inpatient) or at least 30-60 minutes (CTC visits). This is an installed base of recurring physical traffic with high dwell time.

2. **A JV partnership model with 21 institutional health systems** — Acadia operates facilities branded under partner names (Erlanger Behavioral Health, Henry Ford JV hospital, etc.), essentially providing the operational engine while partners provide the brand, referrals, and local credibility. This is structurally analogous to a franchise or managed-services model.

3. **Government-payer reimbursement providing ~72% of revenue** (57.7% Medicaid + 14.3% Medicare) — highly predictable but rate-constrained revenue. The "customer" (government) is monopsonic but cannot easily switch providers given behavioral health bed shortages.

4. **A daily-visit recurring revenue stream from 178 CTC clinics** — patients visit daily or near-daily for years, creating one of the most predictable recurring revenue streams in all of healthcare. Average patient tenure may be 2-5+ years.

5. **A fragmented local brand architecture** (100+ local facility names, no unified national brand) combined with **national-scale back-office infrastructure** (centralized billing, compliance, HR, purchasing).

6. **25,000 employees deployed at the point of care** — behavioral health clinicians, counselors, nurses, and psychiatric technicians physically embedded in communities.

SPECIFIC ANALOGUES TO INVESTIGATE:

For each of the following companies, research: What structural feature do they share with Acadia? What did they build on top of that feature? What was the revenue model? What was the magnitude? How long did it take? What went wrong?

A) DAVITA INC. (dialysis network → value-based care → PE acquisition)
- DaVita operates 2,600+ dialysis centers with patients visiting 3x/week for years — structurally identical to Acadia's CTC model (daily visits, chronic condition, government payer). DaVita attempted to build a healthcare services conglomerate (DaVita Medical Group, HealthCare Partners) on top of the dialysis installed base before selling the medical group to Optum for $4.9B.
- What worked about DaVita's diversification? What failed? What lessons apply to Acadia?
- DaVita was taken partially private by KKR in 2007, re-IPO'd, and has been the target of activists. How did each ownership structure change the company's strategy?
- DaVita has faced identical regulatory scrutiny (DOJ investigations, PLGL issues, patient safety concerns). How has DaVita managed these while maintaining its stock price?

B) AMERIGROUP → WELLPOINT (ELEVANCE) ACQUISITION (government-payer provider acquired by insurer)
- Amerigroup was a Medicaid-focused managed care company acquired by WellPoint (now Elevance) in 2012 for $4.9B. The thesis: vertical integration of Medicaid managed care with Medicaid provider services.
- Does this precedent inform an Elevance acquisition of Acadia (integrating Carelon Behavioral Health managed care with Acadia's behavioral health delivery)?

C) SURGERY PARTNERS / AMSURG (JV model with health systems → PE take-private → re-IPO)
- AmSurg pioneered the model of JV ambulatory surgery centers with health systems and physicians — structurally identical to Acadia's JV model with health systems for behavioral health hospitals. AmSurg merged with Envision, went private (KKR), encountered financial distress, and restructured.
- Surgery Partners (SGRY) also operates a JV model. How do they handle change-of-control provisions in JV agreements? What is the standard JV consent framework?
- What can Acadia learn from the AmSurg/Envision/Surgery Partners experience about the value and risks of the JV model under ownership transitions?

D) DOLLAR GENERAL / DOLLAR TREE (physical location density in underserved areas)
- Dollar General operates 20,000+ stores in low-income and rural communities — the same communities where behavioral health access is most limited and Medicaid enrollment is highest. Dollar General has explored adding healthcare services (pharmacies, clinics) to its store network.
- Is there an analogue where Acadia's CTCs — small-footprint, community-based, recurring-visit — could function as healthcare access points for more than just methadone? What does the Dollar General healthcare pilot tell us?

E) SERVICEMASTER / TERMINIX / ROLLINS (route-based daily service, regulatory moat, consolidation)
- Pest control and environmental services companies built value through: (1) recurring service contracts, (2) regulatory moats (licensing), (3) dense route networks that create operational leverage, (4) acquisition-driven consolidation of fragmented local operators. 
- Acadia's CTC network is essentially a "route-based" daily service business with regulatory moats (DEA licensing, state OTP licensing). What consolidation and operational playbooks from route-based service companies could Acadia apply?

F) ROLLS-ROYCE "POWER BY THE HOUR" (selling outcomes instead of inputs)
- Rolls-Royce transformed its jet engine business from selling hardware to selling flight hours — "TotalCare" contracts where airlines pay per hour of engine operation. 
- Could Acadia shift from per-diem billing (selling "bed days") to outcomes-based contracting (selling "behavioral health outcomes")? What would it take? What payers would buy this? What is the revenue model?

REQUIRED OUTPUT: 5-7 CONCRETE "TRANSPLANT PLAYS"

For each transplant play, provide:
1. **The analogue**: Which company's playbook are you adapting?
2. **The Acadia application**: What specifically would Acadia (or an acquirer) do?
3. **The structural feature being leveraged**: Which of Acadia's 6 structural features above?
4. **Revenue/value magnitude estimate**: How big could this be ($M)?
5. **Timeline**: How long to build?
6. **Key risks**: What could go wrong?
7. **Precedent evidence**: Has anyone in behavioral health attempted something similar?

The standard for inclusion is: "Would an industry insider say 'I never would have thought of that'?" Do NOT include obvious extensions like "build a telehealth platform" or "expand into new states" — those are incremental improvements, not structural innovations.

End your output with "NOTABLE ANOMALIES" — anything surprising, contradictory, or unusual. Pay particular attention to:
- Analogues where the structural feature turned out to be MORE valuable than the original business (e.g., AWS > Amazon retail, John Deere data > John Deere equipment)
- Cases where government-payer companies successfully transitioned to commercial/value-based models
- Any evidence that behavioral health data (treatment outcomes, population health patterns) has been monetized by anyone, anywhere
- Whether the JV model could be converted into a true franchise/licensing model with dramatically lower capital intensity
