ROLE: You are a senior restructuring and financial engineering analyst at a major investment bank. You specialize in healthcare services companies with complex capital structures, regulatory constraints on asset transfers, and mixed-model businesses where sum-of-the-parts exceeds consolidated value. You are analyzing Acadia Healthcare Company, Inc. (NASDAQ: ACHC) to identify and quantify every financial engineering lever available to the company or a potential acquirer.

RESEARCH OBJECTIVE: Produce a comprehensive financial engineering playbook for Acadia Healthcare, quantifying each lever's potential value creation and identifying execution risks. The output should support both a standalone financial optimization plan and a take-private transaction thesis.

CONTEXT: Acadia has a current enterprise value of ~$4.64B (at ~$25/share), FY2025 Adj. EBITDA of $608.9M, net leverage of ~4.0x, and a capital structure consisting of $550M Senior Notes at 7.375% due 2033, an estimated ~$1.9B term loan, and a $1.0B revolver with ~$595M available. The company spent $572M in CapEx in FY2025 and is guiding to $255-280M in FY2026. Activist investor Engine Capital has explicitly called for sale-leasebacks of owned real estate, divestiture of underperforming assets, and a halt to expensive new facility projects.

SECTION 1: SALE-LEASEBACK OPPORTUNITY

A) REAL ESTATE PORTFOLIO MAPPING
- Research Acadia's property disclosures in its 10-K (the property section of annual filings lists owned vs. leased facilities). How many of the 277 facilities are owned outright? How many are leased? What is the owned real estate footprint in square footage?
- For owned facilities: what types of properties are they? (Purpose-built psychiatric hospitals, converted office buildings, medical office buildings, residential treatment campuses)
- What is the estimated fair market value of Acadia's owned real estate portfolio? Research comparable healthcare facility real estate transactions, cap rates for behavioral health properties, and any available appraisal data
- What cap rate would a healthcare REIT (Medical Properties Trust, Physicians Realty Trust/Healthpeak, Sabra Healthcare REIT, CareTrust REIT) likely apply to behavioral health facilities?

B) SALE-LEASEBACK ECONOMICS
- If Acadia (or an acquirer) sold owned facilities to a healthcare REIT and leased them back, what would be the estimated:
  - Gross proceeds
  - Annual lease payment (rent)
  - Net present value impact (proceeds vs. capitalized lease obligation)
  - EBITDA impact (rent expense vs. current depreciation/ownership costs)
- Research comparable sale-leaseback transactions in healthcare services (e.g., Kindred Healthcare, Encompass Health, Surgery Partners). What were the cap rates, lease terms, and escalators?
- What are the regulatory constraints? Do any state behavioral health licensing laws restrict the separation of real estate from operating licenses?

SECTION 2: CTC SEGMENT SEPARATION

Acadia operates 178 Comprehensive Treatment Centers (methadone/MAT clinics) generating an estimated ~$570M in annual revenue at estimated 25-35% EBITDA margins. The CTC business is structurally different from inpatient psychiatric: outpatient, recurring daily visits, low capital intensity, low labor intensity, government-backed reimbursement.

A) STANDALONE CTC VALUATION
- What is the estimated EBITDA of the CTC segment specifically? (If margins are 25-35%, EBITDA would be $143-200M)
- What comparable transactions exist for OTP/MAT clinic networks? Research:
  - BayMark Health Services acquisitions and valuations
  - Crossroads Treatment Centers (PE-backed by Revelstoke/CDPQ) — valuation multiples
  - Any other OTP network transactions
- At what EV/EBITDA multiple would the CTC network trade as a standalone? (8-12x based on recurring revenue, government payer, moat)
- What is the estimated standalone enterprise value of the CTC network? ($1.1-2.4B range depending on margins and multiple)

B) SEPARATION MECHANICS
- How much corporate overhead is currently allocated to CTCs? What would standalone G&A look like?
- Are there shared service functions (IT, HR, billing, compliance) that would need to be duplicated or transitioned?
- Do CTC facilities have separate DEA licenses and state OTP licenses from inpatient facilities? (This determines regulatory separability)
- Are there any legal or contractual barriers to a CTC spinoff or sale?
- Would a CTC divestiture trigger change-of-control provisions in Acadia's credit agreement?

C) USE OF PROCEEDS
- If Acadia sold CTCs for $1.5-2.0B, how would proceeds be deployed?
- Model the pro forma balance sheet: remaining entity revenue (~$2.7-2.8B), EBITDA (~$410-470M), debt ($2.5B less paydown), leverage ratio
- Would the remaining inpatient/RTC entity be investable at lower leverage? Or would the removal of the highest-quality segment make the remaining entity harder to finance?

SECTION 3: RTC SEGMENT DIVESTITURE OR WIND-DOWN

The youth Residential Treatment Center segment (~$87.5M quarterly, ~$350M annual) carries disproportionate litigation risk, Congressional scrutiny, and reputational damage.

- What is the estimated EBITDA contribution of RTCs? (Likely lower margins due to longer stays, lower daily rates, higher staffing requirements)
- Could the RTC segment be sold? Who would buy it? At what multiple? Or would the litigation overhang make a sale impractical?
- If Acadia wound down RTCs rather than sold them, what would the exit costs be? (Employee severance, lease termination, regulatory wind-down, patient transfer obligations)
- What is the EBITDA margin impact on the remaining company of removing RTCs? (Positive if RTCs are below-average margin; negative if allocated overhead remains)

SECTION 4: CAPITAL STRUCTURE OPTIMIZATION

A) DEBT REFINANCING
- The February 2025 refinancing created a new credit facility and the March 2025 $550M Senior Notes at 7.375%. What are the terms of the credit facility (interest rate, maturity, covenants)?
- What is the weighted average cost of debt?
- Are there prepayment penalties or call provisions on the Senior Notes?
- What covenant headroom exists? If EBITDA declines further (e.g., to $550M in a downside case), when do covenants bite?
- Is there a maintenance leverage covenant? What is the test level?

B) FREE CASH FLOW BRIDGE
- Model the FCF trajectory: FY2025 operating cash flow was $131.9M with $572M CapEx (negative FCF). FY2026 guidance implies $255-280M CapEx. What is the realistic FCF in FY2026 and FY2027?
- At what point does Acadia become self-funding (FCF > debt service + maintenance CapEx)?
- What is the sustainable maintenance CapEx level for the current facility base? (Management implies $200-250M once growth CapEx normalizes)

C) SHARE REPURCHASE ANALYSIS
- Acadia bought back ~$50M in shares in 2025. At current prices (~$25), that's ~2M shares. With 90.4M shares outstanding and 35.9% short interest (32.5M shares short), what is the strategic logic of continued buybacks?
- Could an aggressive buyback ($200M+) create a short squeeze given the 7.3-day short-interest ratio?
- What is the breakeven price at which buybacks at current levels are accretive to a take-private buyer?

SECTION 5: TAKE-PRIVATE FINANCIAL MODEL

For a PE take-private scenario:
- What is the likely acquisition premium needed? (25-50% to current stock = $31-37.50/share)
- What is the total enterprise value at various premium levels?
- How would a PE buyer finance the acquisition? (New debt capacity at ~5-6x EBITDA = $3.0-3.7B; equity check = $1.5-2.5B)
- Day-one execution: sale-leaseback proceeds ($X), RTC divestiture ($Y), CTC retention for ramp — what is the net equity check after financial engineering?
- 5-year model: embedded EBITDA ramp ($200M), PLGL normalization, multiple expansion upon re-IPO. What is the implied equity return (IRR)?
- Who are the lenders? Which banks have relationships with Acadia? Who underwrote the February 2025 refinancing?

OUTPUT FORMAT: For each lever, provide estimated value range (low/base/high), execution timeline, key risks, and precedent transactions.

End your output with "NOTABLE ANOMALIES" — anything surprising, contradictory, or unusual. Pay particular attention to:
- Whether the sale-leaseback opportunity is larger or smaller than Engine Capital's letter implies
- Whether CTC segment separation would actually increase or decrease total enterprise value
- Covenant constraints that could prevent financial engineering moves
- Any creative financial structures (e.g., tracking stocks, segment-level debt, healthcare facility REITs) that could unlock value
- Whether the 35.9% short interest creates a unique opportunity for financial engineering (e.g., tender offers, special dividends, or other mechanisms that force short covering)
