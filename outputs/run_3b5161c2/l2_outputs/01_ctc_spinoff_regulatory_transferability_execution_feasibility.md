## PART I: DEFINITIVE REGULATORY ARCHITECTURE

### I-A. The Program Sponsor Definition — The Pivotal Legal Determination

The regulatory feasibility of the entire hypothesis turns on a single statutory definition. The eCFR states:


**"Program sponsor"** means the person named in the application for certification described in § 8.11(b) as responsible for the operation of the OTP and who assumes responsibility for all its employees, including any practitioners, agents, or other persons providing medical, behavioral health, or social services at the program or any of its medication units. The program sponsor need not be a licensed physician but shall ensure that an actively licensed physician occupies the position of medical director within an OTP.


This definition has a critical qualifier: the person **"named in the application."** In Acadia's case, the named program sponsors in SAMHSA SMA-162 applications are the individual operating subsidiaries — not Acadia Healthcare Company, Inc. This is confirmed by Acadia's Vermont CON application, which explicitly identifies:


Habit Opco, LLC d/b/a Habit Opco-Brattleboro is a wholly owned subsidiary of Acadia and a registered entity in the state of Vermont. As the parent company, Acadia is asking for the JDR on their behalf, noting that "Habit Opco, LLC is a wholly owned [subsidiary]."


The operating subsidiaries confirmed from SEC filings include: **Habit Opco, Inc.; CRC Health Corporation; CRC Health Group, Inc.; CRC Holdings, LLC; Habit Holdings, Inc.;** and state-specific entities (e.g., **"Charleston Treatment Center, LLC; Clarksburg Treatment Center, LLC; Huntington Treatment Center, LLC"** for West Virginia). 
These entities, confirmed in Acadia's 2015 S-4 bond offering documents, are the actual subsidiary guarantors and likely the named program sponsors on SAMHSA certifications.


**Regulatory Conclusion:** In a holding-company equity spinoff where these operating subsidiaries remain intact as the same legal entities with different ultimate parents, no "program sponsor" change has occurred at the facility certification level. SAMHSA's 3-week notification obligation is, at most, triggered at the level of updating the organizational chart showing the "larger organization structure" — a ministerial batch filing of SMA-162 forms, not a substantive re-certification requirement.


Per federal regulations, OTPs must notify SAMHSA of changes to their existing program by logging into the SAMHSA OTP Extranet and submitting the online Form SMA-162.
 This is an online batch process, not an in-person inspection or reaccreditation requirement.

### I-B. DEA NTP Registration — The Subsidiary Continuity Argument


The registration of any person, and any modifications of that registration, shall terminate, without any further action by the Administration, if and when such person dies, ceases legal existence, discontinues business or professional practice, or surrenders a registration.


**In a holding-company equity spinoff:** The operating subsidiaries holding each of the 178 DEA NTP registrations (e.g., "Habit Opco, LLC") do not cease legal existence. They continue as the same entities with a different parent. The DEA's registration trigger is not activated. This conclusion is further supported by the fact that:


If a person owns and operates more than one NTP, each place of business must be separately registered with DEA.
 Each registration is issued to a specific location-entity combination — not to the ultimate corporate parent. When the entity holding the registration remains unchanged (only its owner changes), there is no "transfer" of registration under 21 CFR 1301.52(b).

**An asset sale is categorically different.** 
Pursuant to 21 CFR 1301.52(d), an NTP registrant that desires to transfer its business operations to another NTP registrant must submit in person or by registered or certified mail, return receipt requested, to the Special Agent in Charge in his or her area, at least 14 days in advance.
 An asset sale would trigger this provision for all 178 locations — a 2-3 year administrative process.

Transaction stalls indefinitely pending DEA formal response.

A new state administration interprets the spinoff as creating a "new operator" triggering the moratorium on clinic operations. Acadia's 9 WV clinics (generating estimated $45–60M in revenue based on per-clinic averages) face operational uncertainty. The valuation discount applied by SpinCo's lenders and potential buyers reduces the achievable enterprise value by $300–400M, making the transaction uneconomic.
