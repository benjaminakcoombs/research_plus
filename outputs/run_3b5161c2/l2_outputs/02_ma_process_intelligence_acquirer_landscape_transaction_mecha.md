### 3C. OBBBA Medicaid Impact — New Critical Analysis


On July 4, 2025, President Trump signed the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) into law. This legislation was initially passed by the House on May 22 by a 215-214 vote and passed the Senate with an amendment by a 51-50 vote.



The projected $911 billion in Medicaid cuts — which will reduce federal Medicaid spending by about 15% — will roll out over the next decade via three primary mechanisms. For behavioral health, the stakes are especially high. Medicaid isn't just another payer — it's the single largest funder of behavioral health services in the US, paying for roughly a quarter of all mental health and substance use disorder (SUD) treatment.



Acadia's risk factors specifically cite "potential reductions in payments received by Acadia from government and commercial payors, including because of the significant changes to Medicaid financing mechanisms introduced by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act ('OBBBA') enacted on July 4, 2025."


**OBBBA specific mechanisms affecting Acadia:**
1. **Work requirements for Medicaid expansion population** — effective 2027, states must impose community engagement requirements. Behavioral health patients are partially exempt (behavioral health clinics specifically carved out under the final law), but implementation uncertainty creates volume risk.
2. **State directed payment caps** — OBBBA caps state directed payments (supplemental programs like Tennessee's $28.5M payment Acadia received in 2025) at 100%/110% of Medicare rates, phasing in from 2028. This could eliminate $20-30M+ of annual supplemental payment benefits.
3. **More frequent eligibility redeterminations** — starting 2027, bi-annual Medicaid redeterminations may cause temporary coverage gaps that reduce behavioral health admissions.
4. **Provider tax freeze** — existing state provider tax programs locked; new programs prohibited. This affects states like Tennessee where supplemental payments have been a meaningful EBITDA contributor.


The final legislation adds exemptions to cost-sharing services provided by FQHCs, behavioral health clinicals, and rural health clinics.
 — This carve-out is partially protective for Acadia's inpatient psychiatric hospitals (classified as specialty hospitals, not behavioral health clinics per se), but CTC outpatient locations may benefit from the carve-out.

**Net OBBBA impact on Acadia:** Manageable in 2026 (most changes begin 2027-2028), but creates a 2027-2030 headwind that any LBO acquirer must model. The elimination of supplemental payment growth and state directed payment caps could reduce steady-state EBITDA by $30-60M by 2028 — meaningful but not existential.

### 7C. The Short Squeeze Complication — Expanded Analysis


Acadia Healthcare was the target of a large growth in short interest during February. As of February 13th, there was short interest totaling 32,453,225 shares, a growth of 23.1% from the January 29th total of 26,360,014 shares. Currently 35.9% of the company's shares are short sold. Based on an average daily trading volume of 4,465,676 shares, the short-interest ratio is presently 7.3 days.


The short interest **increased by 23.1%** in the two weeks following the January 20 Osteen appointment — meaning the shorts are NOT covering on the CEO change signal. They are adding. This suggests the shorts believe: (a) the business fundamentals will continue deteriorating, OR (b) a deal is NOT imminent and the stock will revert to $12-15 range.

The short squeeze dynamics are now extreme: 32.5M shares short against 7.3 days to cover on 4.47M average daily volume. A deal announcement before market open would:
- Day 1: Stock gaps up 30-50% at open (shorts forced to cover at open price or above)
- Day 1-7: Squeeze continues as covering demand exceeds liquidity
- Day 7-14: Stock stabilizes near deal price
- Effect: The actual premium paid by the acquirer is 30-50% + the deal price premium to pre-leak levels

**This creates a powerful structural argument for a quietly-negotiated deal announced simultaneously with a definitive agreement**, rather than a public auction. Any leak in a public process would squeeze the stock to $35-40+ before a DA was signed, making the deal economics unworkable.

### 7D. Antitrust Risk — Updated with Geographic Specifics

**LifePoint/Springstone overlap states: AZ, CO, IN, KS, NC, OH, OK, TX, WA (9 states)**
**Acadia inpatient confirmed states: AZ, AR, FL, GA, IN, MI, MO, NM, OH, OK, PA, TN, UT, WI (14 states confirmed)**

**Confirmed overlapping states: AZ, IN, OH, OK (4 states)**

In these overlap states, the combined entity would be the dominant inpatient psychiatric provider. The FTC healthcare merger guidelines (effective 2024 under new enforcement posture) require notification for transactions above $119.5M and scrutiny of any market where the combined share exceeds 30% in a defined geographic market.

For pure inpatient psychiatric (not general acute care), the relevant market definition is typically the hospital service area (HSA) or Health Service Research Area (HSRA), which is more granular than state-level. A post-merger market share analysis would need to be conducted for each MSA in AZ/IN/OH/OK. Preliminary assessment: FTC HSR filing required; likely 2-4 facility divestitures in concentrated markets.

**For a non-LifePoint buyer (KKR, Warburg, TPG, standalone Apollo):** No behavioral health facility overlap exists, meaning antitrust risk is minimal. This is a significant advantage for financial buyers vs. LifePoint as the acquirer — a PE firm can close 3-4 months faster without antitrust remedies.

## SECTION 11 — FINAL SYNTHESIS: THE MOST COHERENT NARRATIVE

The weight of evidence supports the following narrative:

**Acadia's board retained Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan in mid-2025** to evaluate strategic alternatives — triggered by the stock's 70%+ decline, dual activist campaigns, and the DOJ investigation creating existential uncertainty. The retention was publicly disclosed (inadvertently) in September 2025 when Acadia responded to Engine Capital's letter. Kirkland & Ellis was retained simultaneously as M&A legal advisor.

**The process is structured as a quiet, pre-negotiated two-track evaluation:** Track 1 is a whole-company sale to a financial sponsor. Track 2 is a CTC segment sale (178 locations, ~$100M EBITDA, ~$900M-$1.1B value) with the inpatient/specialty platform recapitalized. CEO Osteen's statement at Raymond James — "open to another way to bring value to shareholders" regarding the CTC segment — is the most specific strategic signal she has made publicly.

**The board's governance sequencing is telling:** (November 2025) $179M securities class action settled. (January 2026) Hunter fired; Osteen reinstated with transaction-linked compensation. (February 2026) $996M goodwill impairment (balance sheet reset). (March 12, 2026) Cancelmi appointed (deal execution specialist, Tenet transformation expert).

**The most likely buyer is a financial sponsor WITHOUT the Goldman conflict** — KKR being the most operationally plausible given fund timing, no advisory conflicts, healthcare scale ($20B+ invested), and Kirkland legal relationship. Warburg Pincus is the second most likely. Apollo/LifePoint remains strategically the most logical but faces the most structural barriers.

**The most likely transaction structure:** Pre-negotiated definitive agreement (no formal auction; possible limited outreach to 3-5 pre-qualified sponsors), announced simultaneously with price and financing commitments, pre-market opening to capture the short squeeze in the acquirer's favor rather than against it. Break-up fee of 3-4% (~$150-200M) to deter competing bids while managing DOJ uncertainty.

**The single most important variable is the DOJ investigation.** A corporate DPA or NPA would clear the path for a $30-40/share transaction. A criminal indictment would cause any financial buyer to withdraw and potentially force Acadia into a distressed sale or restructuring process.
