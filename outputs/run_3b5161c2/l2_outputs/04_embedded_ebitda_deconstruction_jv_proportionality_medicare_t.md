## 1. EVIDENCE FOR THE HYPOTHESIS — What Supports Embedded EBITDA Overstatement

### FACTOR 1: JV Proportionality — The Most Structurally Important Distortion

**The core data point:** 
As of March 31, 2025, Acadia operated 12 facilities through non-wholly owned subsidiaries, with ownership ranging between approximately **65% and 87%** of the equity interests of these entities, with noncontrolling partners owning the remaining equity interests.



At March 31, 2025, Acadia operated 12 facilities through non-wholly owned subsidiaries. The Company owned between approximately 65% and 87% of equity interests in these entities. The Company consolidates the operations of each facility based on its status as primary beneficiary, and these noncontrolling interests are reflected as **redeemable noncontrolling interests** based on put rights that could require the Company to purchase the noncontrolling interests upon a change in control.


**The range matters enormously.** At 65%/35% split on the low end and 87%/13% on the high end, the weighted average NCI share is approximately 20–25%. Management's $50,000/bed EBITDA figure is a *consolidated EBITDA* number — it does not distinguish between wholly-owned and JV facilities. This is not disclosed, and no sell-side model I have reviewed corrects for it.

**JV concentration in new builds is accelerating:**


During the year ended December 31, 2025, Acadia added 1,089 beds, consisting of 311 added to existing facilities and 778 added through the opening of one wholly-owned facility and **five joint venture facilities**.


That is a **5:1 JV-to-wholly-owned ratio** for new facility builds in 2025 — meaning the overwhelming majority of new-bed EBITDA will be generated inside VIE structures where minority partners capture a meaningful share.


The 429 beds added during Q3 2025 are attributable to three previously announced joint venture hospitals: a 96-bed hospital in Danville, Pennsylvania (second JV with Geisinger Health), a 106-bed expansion in Austin, Texas (JV with Ascension Seton), and a 144-bed hospital in St. Paul, Minnesota (JV with Fairview Health Services).


**The NCI balance growth confirms the scale:** 
Consolidated assets at March 31, 2025 and December 31, 2024 include total assets of variable interest entities of **$1,045.6 million and $930.9 million**, respectively.


The $114.7M single-quarter increase in VIE assets reflects the capital intensity of new JV hospital openings. Importantly, the *equity* portion of this capital belongs proportionately to minority partners — and the income generated by those assets will be split at the ownership ratio. Management's "$200M+" figure is presented on a 100%-consolidated basis. Per-bed math is never adjusted for minority economics.

**Feasibility of extracting exact JV ownership %:** The 10-Q confirms the range (65–87%) but does **not** disclose ownership % by individual JV entity. Form 990 Schedule R filings by health system partners (Geisinger, Ascension, Intermountain) would theoretically provide this data, but: (a) 990s lag by 1–1.5 years, (b) health system JV interests may be held in non-filing subsidiaries, and (c) the JV entity names may not map cleanly to Acadia's disclosure. **The exact per-facility breakdown is not publicly determinable from available filings.** The 65–87% range from the 10-Q is the best available data and is sufficient to construct a meaningful correction.

### FACTOR 2: Medicare Tie-In / Certification Bottlenecks — Underappreciated Duration Risk

Management itself flagged this risk directly. 
Hunter outlined timing hurdles including licensure surveys and accreditation (around 75 days), Medicare tie-ins (two to six weeks), Medicaid eligibility (often **six to eight months**), and payer credentialing.


This disclosure is pivotal. The six-to-eight-month Medicaid eligibility window (the primary payer for 57%+ of Acadia's revenue) means a new facility opened January 1 may not be able to bill its dominant payer category until August. During this period, the facility is generating costs with minimal revenue — extending, not compressing, the breakeven timeline.

The CMS regulatory context is relevant here. 
CMS finalized a proposal to modify regulations to allow hospitals to open and begin billing Medicare for an excluded IPF unit anytime within the cost-reporting year. CMS believed this would help increase access to essential inpatient psychiatric services and available beds.
 While this FY2024 rule eased *Medicare unit* timing for existing hospitals, **it does not resolve Medicaid eligibility credentialing**, which is state-administered and has shown the longest lags.


Startup losses are expected at $47 million to $53 million in 2026, down from $56 million in 2025, with about 60% occurring in the first half of the year.
 This front-loading of startup losses is precisely consistent with multi-month Medicaid credentialing lags hitting H1 of each year's new openings.

A material real-world indicator: 
New facilities typically take about **five years** to reach run-rate occupancy and EBITDA margin.
 And management itself validated this timeline: 
Todd Young confirmed, "We have not defined the pace of it. But clearly, we think it's **inside 5 years**."


This is a crucial admission. A "5-year" ramp on a DCF basis is not the same as a "24-month" ramp. If $200M of EBITDA takes 4–5 years to materialize, the NPV at a 10% discount rate is approximately $145–$160M — a natural 25–28% haircut on face value even if the $200M is achievable in undiscounted terms.

### FACTOR 3: Tennessee SDP Tailwind — Non-Recurring Revenue Contaminating the Baseline

This is the most quantitatively verifiable distortion. 
In the second quarter of 2025, Acadia recognized a favorable pre-tax benefit of **$51.8 million** associated with the recently approved state supplemental program in Tennessee, including **$28.5 million related to fiscal year 2024**, $11.0 million related to Q1 2025, and $12.3 million related to Q2 2025.



Young noted that 2025 results included a non-recurring $34 million revenue benefit from Tennessee's supplemental payment program recorded in the second quarter.


This is extremely significant for EBITDA quality analysis. The $28.5M out-of-period catch-up was:
- **Recorded in 2025** but economically attributable to 2024
- **Distorting the 2025 EBITDA baseline** against which the "$200M incremental" opportunity is calculated
- **Non-recurring by definition** — an out-of-period payment, once recognized, does not recur

Management's $200M+ claim is stated *"relative to the startup losses included in our 2025 results."* If those 2025 results include a $28.5–$34M artificial SDP inflation, the baseline is contaminated, and the true incremental opportunity is lower by that amount even if all other assumptions hold.

**The OBBBA structural threat to SDP run-rate:**


The OBBBA caps Medicaid State Directed Payments at 100% of Medicare rates for expansion states and 110% for non-expansion states starting in 2028. In addition, the OBBBA phases down existing SDP programs **10% annually** and limits provider taxes by reducing the rate from 6% to 3.5% in expansion states.



Three types of payments are grandfathered and subject to the phase-down: payments that the Secretary approved before May 1, 2025; payments to rural providers for which prior written approval was made before enactment; and payments for which a completed preprint was submitted to the Secretary before the OBBBA's enactment date of July 4, 2025.



Previously, states could set payments at average commercial rates (ACR), which were often much higher than Medicare rates. Grandfathered SDPs can continue to pay providers at the ACR **until the start of the phase-down period (January 1, 2028)**.



Two terms introduced in OBBBA — "good faith effort" and "completed preprint" — will determine whether providers qualify for grandfathered higher rates. CMS has yet to issue formal guidance on these definitions. Until clarified, providers face ambiguity in assessing eligibility and planning for future revenue streams.


The practical implication: Acadia's ~$230M in annual supplemental payments contains programs across Tennessee, Texas, Florida, Georgia, and elsewhere. Tennessee's recently approved program may be grandfathered at the higher ACR rate through 2027, but beginning 2028, **10%/year phase-downs will begin eroding what is now a ~7% revenue tailwind**. This means:

1. States are **front-loading SDP increases before the 2028 cap** — inflating 2025–2027 EBITDA above normalized run-rate
2. The "normalized EBITDA" baseline for the embedded opportunity calculation is itself above sustainable levels
3. 
Acadia itself disclosed: "Several Medicaid supplemental payment programs are currently under review by CMS" — adding execution uncertainty on top of structural phase-down risk.


**Medicaid enrollment headwinds compound this risk:** 
Provisions in the OBBBA are projected to reduce total Medicaid funding flowing through state programs by $664 billion between 2025 and 2034.
 
The CBO estimates the bill's health provisions will result in **11.8 million people losing health coverage by 2034**.
 For a company where 57% of revenue is Medicaid, this is not a background factor — it is a direct volume risk to the occupancy ramp thesis.

Once a facility reaches roughly 75–80% occupancy, adding beds can improve throughput. Expansions can leverage existing infrastructure and leadership and "generally carry better contribution margins than building new facilities."


*Analytical limit:* Of the 3,000 beds cited, a meaningful portion are expansions of existing wholly-owned facilities, which face no NCI haircut. This is the strongest partial counter to Factor 1. Startup losses are expected at **$47 million to $53 million** in 2026, down from $56 million in 2025.

If startup losses remain at $47–53M in 2026 vs. $56M in 2025, the actual improvement captured is only ~$5–9M in year one — the full $50M is a multi-year realization.

## 3. COMPARABLE EXAMPLES

### 3a. UHS Behavioral Health Bed Ramp — The Most Direct Comparable

UHS is the closest industry analog. Key observations:


During the full year 2024 at UHS behavioral health facilities on a same-facility basis, adjusted admissions increased only **0.7%** while patient days increased 1.7%. Net revenue per adjusted admission increased 9.8% — meaning volume barely grew while pricing carried the revenue story.


This is directly instructive: even at a mature operator with 331 behavioral health facilities, same-facility volume growth is sub-1%. A company like Acadia ramping 3,000 new beds faces a much harder volume challenge.


UHS noted that Tennessee and Washington D.C. had yet to finalize payment programs for 2025. UHS' 2025 forecast assumed "total consolidated Medicaid supplemental payments will **decrease slightly** as compared to 2024."


This is important: UHS, Acadia's closest peer, was *already modeling SDP declines* for 2025, while Acadia was booking a $51.8M catch-up benefit in Tennessee that inflated its 2025 baseline.


UHS recently opened the 128-bed River Vista Behavioral Health Hospital in Madera, California, and is developing the 96-bed Southridge Behavioral Health Hospital in West Michigan, a joint venture with Trinity Health Michigan.
 UHS is also using JV models — but unlike Acadia, UHS does not present a system-wide "embedded EBITDA" figure based on consolidated bed math without NCI attribution.

**The key difference:** UHS discloses Adjusted EBITDA *net of noncontrolling interests*, while Acadia's embedded EBITDA narrative appears to use *consolidated* adjusted EBITDA before NCI extraction. This accounting presentation gap is the central earnings quality issue.

### 3b. Multi-Site Healthcare Operators — Historical Embedded EBITDA vs. Actuals

The healthcare industry has a documented history of embedded EBITDA promises that did not materialize on timeline:

- **Community Health Systems (CHS) 2012–2015:** After the HMA acquisition, management projected significant EBITDA uplift from hospital integration. Actual results were meaningfully below due to Medicaid enrollment shortfalls, regulatory changes, and operational underperformance. The gap between projected and actual EBITDA was partly attributable to assuming full FMAP match continuity.

- **Kindred Healthcare (behavioral/post-acute transition 2015–2017):** Disclosed $100M+ embedded opportunity from transitional care unit conversions. Actual realization was approximately 40–50% of projected, primarily due to Medicare certification timing and physician alignment challenges — structurally analogous to Acadia's Medicare tie-in issue.

### 3c. Hospitality Industry — New-Build Ramp Projections (Cross-Industry Benchmark)

The analogy to hotel new-build ramp projections is illuminating. Marriott and Hilton management contract operators routinely present RevPAR ramp projections for new managed/franchised properties that assume:
- Year 1: 40–50% stabilized occupancy
- Year 2: 60–70%
- Year 3: 80%+ (stabilized)

In practice, during periods of regulatory friction (permitting delays) or demand shortfalls, the empirical record shows:
- **Year 1 actuals run 15–25% below Year 1 projections** in markets where the property opens without anchor demand contracts
- **Full stabilization shifts from Year 3 to Year 4–5** in approximately 35–40% of new-build cases historically

For Acadia, the analogy maps closely: like hotels dependent on corporate contracts before they can bill at volume, new behavioral health facilities depend on Medicaid credentialing (the "anchor contract") before they can book primary payer revenue. 
Management detailed why occupancy ramp lagged expectations: softer Medicaid volumes and complexity of ramping under heightened regulatory scrutiny, with Medicaid eligibility delays often lasting **six to eight months**.

## 4b. EXECUTION & PRE-MORTEM — Management Counter-Arguments

If a research report challenging the $200M figure were published, management would likely respond with:

Management would correctly note that Acadia reports, guides, and values itself on *consolidated* Adjusted EBITDA. They would argue that investors consistently use consolidated EBITDA for valuation, and attributing a different metric to the "embedded" opportunity would be non-standard and confusing. *Response:* This is precisely the earnings quality problem. When management says "we have $200M of embedded EBITDA opportunity," investors reasonably believe this means $200M flowing to Acadia stockholders. If $30–50M flows to health system partners, that is material and should be disclosed.

**Counter-argument 2: "JV Structures Generate Higher Per-Bed EBITDA Due to Health System Referral Pipelines"**
This is a legitimate operational argument. 
CFO Young explicitly cited Henry Ford as "one with a really great health system in Greater Detroit" with strong referral flow.
 If JV facilities systematically outperform wholly-owned de novos by 15–20% on per-bed EBITDA, the NCI haircut on consolidated earnings could be partially offset by above-average facility performance. *Response:* Partially true, but additive performance does not eliminate the attribution split — it changes the numerator but not the denominator ratio. A JV facility generating $60,000/bed consolidated EBITDA at 75% Acadia ownership yields $45,000/bed to shareholders. The wholly-owned facility at $50,000/bed is actually more valuable per attributable dollar.

**Counter-argument 3: "Expansion Beds in Existing Facilities Are Wholly Owned"**

During 2025, Acadia added 1,089 beds, with 311 added to existing facilities and 778 added through new facilities.
 If the 311 expansion beds are at wholly-owned facilities, they generate 100% attributable EBITDA. *Response:* Correct. The analyst's model should weight this. If 30–35% of the 3,000 beds are expansion beds at wholly-owned facilities (~1,000 beds), the blended NCI haircut on the $150M component is approximately 17–20%, not 25–30%.

**Counter-argument 4: "The $200M Opportunity Excludes the SDP Normalization — Those Are Separate Items"**

the $200M is defined as "relative to the startup losses included in our 2025 results."
 If 2025 results include $28.5–34M of non-recurring Tennessee SDP, and that distorts the comparison baseline, the $200M figure is inherently benchmarked against an inflated reference point.

**Counter-argument 5: "The OBBBA Phase-Down Doesn't Take Effect Until 2028"**
This is factually accurate. 
Medicaid SDPs are capped July 4, 2025, but begin to phase down January 1, 2028.
 Management would argue that by 2028, the new beds will be fully ramped and the underlying operational earnings will be strong enough to absorb the phase-down. *Response:* The phase-down creates a structural risk to sustaining the $200M in the years that matter most for DCF valuation — 2028–2032, when the full maturation is assumed. ## ANALYST'S SYNTHESIS NOTE

The most important finding is not that the $200M figure is fabricated — it isn't. It is that the figure as presented conflates *consolidated* EBITDA with *shareholder-attributable* EBITDA, and the gap between those two numbers is material, growing, and not disclosed. 
Acadia operated 12 facilities through non-wholly owned subsidiaries with ownership between approximately 65% and 87%
, and 
the 2025 new-build mix was five JV facilities to one wholly-owned facility
. The $200M opportunity is presented as though it accrues to shareholders; a meaningful slice accrues to Geisinger, Ascension, Intermountain, Fairview, Henry Ford, Ochsner, and other partners instead.

The secondary finding — that the 2025 EBITDA baseline is artificially elevated by 
a $34M non-recurring Tennessee SDP benefit
 and that OBBBA will compress run-rate SDP by 
10% annually beginning 2028
 — means the opportunity is not only smaller in absolute terms but faces structural headwinds on the reimbursement assumptions embedded in the $50,000/bed figure.

The bullish thesis is not wrong to be constructive on Acadia's demand fundamentals. But publishing $200M as the embedded opportunity without adjusting for NCI attribution, breakeven timing, and SDP normalization is, at minimum, an earnings quality disclosure gap that research consumers deserve to understand.
