# DELIVERABLE 1: COMPANY SITUATION MAP

## What Acadia Healthcare IS

Acadia Healthcare is the largest standalone behavioral health company in the United States: 277 facilities, 12,500+ licensed beds, 40 states plus Puerto Rico, approximately 25,000 employees serving 84,000 patients daily. It generated $3.31 billion in revenue in FY2025 and $608.9 million in adjusted EBITDA — though it recorded a catastrophic $1.10 billion net loss driven by a $996 million goodwill impairment.

**The business has three structurally distinct segments that happen to share a corporate parent:**

1. **Acute Inpatient Psychiatric Hospitals (~55% of revenue, ~$1.8B):** 59 hospitals providing crisis stabilization (3-5 day stays) and acute care (5-12 day stays). These are predominantly owned real estate (91% of non-CTC beds are at owned properties). This is the segment under federal criminal investigation for allegedly holding patients unnecessarily to maximize insurance payments. It carries almost all of the company's goodwill, PLGL liability, and legal risk.

2. **Comprehensive Treatment Centers / CTCs (~17% of revenue, ~$576M):** 178 outpatient methadone clinics across 33 states, treating 76,000 patients daily. These are 86% leased, capital-light, high-margin (~26-30% EBITDA), and structurally analogous to a regulated utility — patients visit daily for medication-assisted treatment, often for years. This is the nation's largest OTP (opioid treatment program) chain, protected by a formidable regulatory moat (DEA + SAMHSA + state licensing + CON requirements in 19 states). A separate congressional investigation into billing fraud at CTCs is underway but is distinct from the inpatient investigation.

3. **Specialty Treatment + Residential Treatment Centers (~28% of revenue, ~$900M):** 31 specialty facilities (primarily substance use disorder residential) and 9 RTCs. This segment is shrinking — Acadia completely exited the eating disorder business in October 2025, closing all CRC Health Group legacy facilities. The RTC segment carries enormous litigation tail risk (the $400M Desert Hills settlement, the Southwood sexual abuse lawsuit, Senate investigations into youth RTF abuse).

**Payer mix:** 57.7% Medicaid, 24.6% commercial, 14.3% Medicare, 3.4% other. This is an overwhelmingly government-dependent business — 72% of revenue comes from taxpayer-funded programs. No single state exceeds 14% of revenue; no single facility exceeds 4%.

**Capital structure:** 4.0x net leverage ($2.4B net debt), recently refinanced with a $1.0B revolver and $650M term loan maturing 2030, plus $550M in 7.375% senior notes due 2033. The company has $133M cash and $595M revolver availability. It expects to generate positive free cash flow for the first time in 2026 as capex drops from $572M to $255-280M.

**The JV portfolio is a distinct strategic asset:** 21 joint venture partnerships with prominent health systems (Henry Ford, Geisinger, Ascension, Tufts Medicine, Intermountain, Ochsner, ECU Health, Fairview, Orlando Health) for 22 hospitals. These are structured as VIEs where Acadia holds 70-87% equity and manages day-to-day operations under the health system's brand. VIE assets total $1.046 billion. This is effectively a franchise network operating under institutional hospital brands — and it is both Acadia's most defensible strategic asset and its most fragile.

## What Acadia Healthcare SAYS It's Doing

Management's narrative, as articulated by returning CEO Debbie Osteen and new CFO Todd Young, rests on four pillars:

1. **"$200M+ embedded EBITDA opportunity"** from 3,000 beds added 2023-2026, currently generating startup losses of ~$56M but expected to contribute $150M+ at maturity, plus reversal of startup losses. Management frames this at "$50,000 of EBITDA per bed" at target occupancy.

2. **Operational discipline:** Capex cut by $300M+, facility closures of money-losers ($40M revenue generating -$31M EBITDA in 2025), labor turnover improving six consecutive quarters, pivot from growth to FCF generation.

3. **Quality and compliance investment:** $100M in technology (EHR standardization, remote patient monitoring, quality dashboards tracking 50+ KPIs). Management cites 99.97% CARF compliance scores across CTCs.

4. **"Evaluate all paths to deliver enhanced shareholder value"** — the board's explicit language, supported by retention of Goldman Sachs + JPMorgan as financial advisors and Kirkland & Ellis as legal counsel, and the appointment of former Tenet CFO Daniel Cancelmi to the board "following constructive engagement with Khrom Capital."

**Where the narrative doesn't match reality:**

- The **$200M embedded EBITDA is systematically overstated** by approximately 30%. Management presents the gross figure without netting out JV proportionality (~80% of new beds are JV facilities where Acadia owns 70-87%, not 100%) or the $25-30M NY Medicaid headwind. Realistic Acadia-attributable embedded EBITDA is $110-140M.

- The **99.97% CARF score directly contradicts** the New York Times' investigation finding systematic record falsification at CTCs, counselors carrying caseloads exceeding state regulatory limits, and enrollment of patients without opioid addictions. Either CARF's audit methodology is deficient, or the quality metrics don't capture billing fraud.

- **"Pure play behavioral health" is presented as a strength** but means 100% of revenue comes from the sector under investigation, with no diversified buffer. A $500M+ settlement would consume 80-90% of annual EBITDA.

- Management describes the DOJ investigation cooperation as "voluntary," but a grand jury subpoena from the Western District of Missouri is legally compulsory. The "voluntary" framing applies only to the SDNY request.

## What the ENVIRONMENT Is Doing to Acadia Healthcare

**Regulatory siege — multi-front and intensifying:**
- FBI/HHS-OIG criminal investigation via two U.S. Attorney offices (SDNY and W.D. Missouri), with grand jury subpoenas issued
- SEC investigation into securities fraud
- VA investigation into insurance fraud
- Senate Finance Committee investigation into youth RTF abuse (with explicit DOJ referral from Senator Wyden)
- Senator Hassan investigation into CTC billing practices
- Investigation legal costs: $31M in Q1 2025 alone (vs. <$500K in Q1 2024) — a 60x increase signaling extreme complexity

**Insurance market has effectively repriced Acadia out of standard coverage:** Self-insurance retention shifted from $3M (2022) to $15M (current), extending to $25M+ for sexual assault claims. This is the commercial insurance market's verdict on Acadia's risk profile. PLGL claims surged 186% in 2025 policy year; reserves doubled to $153M but are likely $250-300M underfunded given a 2-7 year claims tail.

**Medicaid structural headwinds:**
- OBBBA (enacted July 4, 2025) cuts $1 trillion in Medicaid funding over 10 years; CBO estimates 11.8M people lose coverage. SUD patients are "medically frail" exempt from work requirements, but supplemental payment phase-downs and 6-month redeterminations create meaningful revenue risk starting 2027-2028.
- New York Medicaid eliminated out-of-state treatment, creating a $25-30M EBITDA headwind concentrated in Pennsylvania specialty facilities.

**MOTAA threat to CTC moat:** The Modernizing Opioid Treatment Access Act would allow pharmacy-based methadone dispensing, disrupting the daily-visit CTC model. Not yet passed, but has cleared Senate HELP committee. Acadia is lobbying against it, which simultaneously reveals the moat's nature and accelerates political scrutiny.

**Competitive dynamics:** UHS outperformed Acadia on every behavioral health metric in 2025 (revenue growth +10% vs. +5%, EBITDA growth +7.8% vs. -14%). BayMark Health Services (second-largest OTP chain) is on the market at ~$75M EBITDA. LifePoint/Apollo (Springstone) is building a third major inpatient behavioral platform. No new Acadia JV partnerships have been announced since January 2024 — the pipeline has frozen as health systems evaluate reputational risk of partnership.

## What's CHANGED Recently (Last 12-18 Months)

**Leadership upheaval:** CEO Chris Hunter and COO Dr. Nasser Khan both departed in late 2025/early 2026. Debbie Osteen, CEO from 2018-2022 (who previously stabilized operations and sold the Priory UK business), returned as interim CEO in January 2026. The simultaneous departure of CEO and COO during the investigation class period is a red flag suggesting personal legal exposure or board-directed separation.

**Board transformation underway:** Daniel Cancelmi (former Tenet CFO) appointed March 12, 2026 following activist engagement. Wade Miquelon retiring after 13 years. Goldman Sachs + JPMorgan retained as financial advisors — not Lazard, despite Lazard's head of North America healthcare banking (Jason Bernhard) sitting on the board, confirming the advisory engagement is for a transaction mandate.

**Three activist investors now hold ~13-15% of shares:** Khrom Capital (5.5%), Engine Capital (~3%), Greenlight Capital (5%+). Combined with 29% short interest, activists control potentially 19-22% of actively voting long shares. Khrom has explicitly stated "credible, interested bidders exist" — language suggesting back-channel contact with potential acquirers.

**Catastrophic financial events:** $996M goodwill impairment (largest in company history), $179M securities class action settlement, $115M PLGL expense (vs. $54M prior year), $85M investigation legal costs in H1 2025. Complete exit from eating disorders (180-degree reversal from June 2024 expansion plans). Five facility closures in 2025.

**Regulatory milestone:** 42 CFR Part 2 final rule compliance deadline passed February 16, 2026, materially relaxing SUD patient data sharing restrictions for the first time in 50 years — opening a window for behavioral health data monetization.

**The bottom line:** Acadia is simultaneously the most valuable behavioral health franchise in the country and the most legally imperiled. The market prices it at 6.5x EBITDA — a "distressed/litigation-burdened" multiple for what should command 10-14x as a scaled platform. The question is whether the legal cloud lifts before the franchise erodes.

# DELIVERABLE 3: CLASSIFICATION CHALLENGE & RESEARCH GAPS

## a) ARCHETYPE CHALLENGE

The L0 classification of HEALTHCARE/PHARMA is correct at the broadest level but obscures the most strategically important truth about this company. **Acadia Healthcare is three distinct businesses wearing a single corporate jersey, and the most valuable one is misclassified.**

If you described this company starting from its most valuable asset rather than its original product, Acadia is a **regulated infrastructure franchise** — specifically, a network of 178 government-licensed, DEA-certified outpatient clinics operating behind the highest regulatory moat in U.S. healthcare (DEA + SAMHSA + state CON in 19 states), serving 76,000 daily-visit patients under what amounts to a government-administered recurring revenue model. This business is structurally more analogous to DaVita's dialysis centers, a water utility, or Rollins' pest control routes than to a hospital company. The CTC segment generates 26-30% EBITDA margins, requires <$1M per new location (vs. $50M for a hospital), is 86% leased, and has patient retention profiles (57% at 12 months, 20-25% beyond 36 months) that resemble subscription businesses.

Separately, Acadia's JV platform is best understood as a **franchise/MSO** — providing the operational playbook, staffing model, compliance infrastructure, and management team under the brand of 21 institutional health system partners. This is structurally identical to Encompass Health's IRF JV model, Surgery Partners' ASC JV model, or US Physical Therapy's clinic partnership model — all of which command 12-15x EBITDA multiples, not the 6.5x at which Acadia trades.

Under this alternative framing, several tension points become visible that are invisible under the "hospital company" lens:

- **The CTC conglomerate discount** ($1.34B trapped value) only becomes visible when you price the CTCs as a regulated infrastructure franchise rather than a hospital segment.
- **The clinical trial platform opportunity** (DaVita DCR analog) only makes sense when you recognize the CTC as a captive-population infrastructure asset, not a healthcare delivery unit.
- **The data monetization opportunity** (behavioral health RWE) only becomes actionable when you view 84,000 daily encounters as a data generation engine, not as patient visits.
- **The MSO conversion** only has strategic logic when you recognize the JV model as a franchise, not as a capital co-investment.

The current "hospital company" framing leads analysts, activists, and management to focus on bed counts, occupancy rates, and EBITDA per bed — metrics appropriate for hotel chains, not for what Acadia actually is. The right metrics are: network density per state, regulatory licenses per market, patient lifetime value, data generation velocity, and franchise partner retention rate.
