CONTEXT:
Acadia Healthcare's management presents a "$200M+ incremental EBITDA opportunity" derived from approximately 3,000 beds added during 2023-2026. The math: ~3,000 beds × $50,000 EBITDA per mature bed = $150M, plus ~$50M in startup loss reversal as new facilities reach breakeven. This figure is central to bullish investment theses — Khrom Capital's $132.89M cost basis depends on it, and sell-side analysts (Leerink, Deutsche Bank) have adopted it uncritically.

Three factors suggest the actual Acadia-shareholder-attributable embedded EBITDA is substantially lower:

**Factor 1 — JV Proportionality:** In 2025, 5 of 6 new facility openings were Joint Venture (JV) hospitals where Acadia owns 70-87%, not 100%. Acadia has 21 JV partnerships with health systems (Geisinger, Henry Ford, Ascension, Intermountain, Ochsner, etc.). If ~80% of new beds are JV-sourced at average 75% Acadia ownership, the attributable EBITDA drops from ~$150M to ~$105M. Acadia's noncontrolling interest (NCI) balance grew from $930.9M to $1,045.6M in Q1 2025 alone ($114.7M increase), reflecting significant minority partner economics.

**Factor 2 — Medicare Tie-in Bottleneck:** CFO Young disclosed that "bringing on acute beds often requires Medicare tie-in numbers and other approvals in order to serve involuntary Medicaid patients, and some of those processes have taken longer than historical norms." With 72% of Acadia's revenue from government payers, new beds cannot bill their primary payer source until CMS processing completes — potentially extending breakeven from management's implied 24 months to 36-48 months.

**Factor 3 — Temporary SDP Tailwind:** Tennessee recognized $28.5M in out-of-period supplemental payments in FY2025. Acadia receives ~$230M annually in supplemental payments (~7% of revenue). OBBBA legislation grandfathers existing state-directed payments (SDPs) through 2027 but begins 10%/year phase-downs thereafter. States are front-loading SDP increases before the phase-down, inflating 2025-2027 EBITDA above normalized run-rate.
