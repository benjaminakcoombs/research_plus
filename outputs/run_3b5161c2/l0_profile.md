### 1.2 Plain-Language Description

On the ground, Acadia operates three distinct types of facilities. First, locked or semi-locked psychiatric hospitals where patients — arriving voluntarily or on involuntary holds — are stabilized over 8-14 days for acute mental health crises (suicidal ideation, psychosis, bipolar episodes) and billed on a daily per-diem to Medicaid or commercial insurers. Second, outpatient methadone/buprenorphine clinics (called CTCs) where opioid-addicted patients show up daily or near-daily for medication doses and counseling, generating a bundled federal reimbursement per visit. Third, residential treatment facilities — primarily for children and adolescents with behavioral/emotional disorders — where stays last weeks to months at lower daily rates. The business model is fundamentally a real estate and staffing business: acquire or build licensed beds, staff them with psychiatrists, nurses, and counselors, fill them with patients, bill government programs, repeat. Revenue intensity per bed (not per admission) is the core operating metric. 
Acadia markets directly to individuals with mental health issues, encouraging them to "skip the ER" at their local not-for-profit hospital and come directly to an Acadia facility. It develops relationships with first responders to encourage them to bring people experiencing mental health issues directly to an Acadia facility and with emergency room staff to encourage referrals.

### 2.2 CTC Segment — Deep Dive (The Embedded Gem)

The CTC business deserves separate treatment because it is structurally different from and more defensible than the inpatient business:

- **Scale:** 
In 2014, Acadia entered the methadone-clinic business when it bought CRC Health, a large addiction treatment chain, from the private equity firm Bain Capital. Acadia rapidly expanded this business, and by late 2024, it operated 165 methadone clinics in 33 states, making it the U.S.'s largest methadone-clinic chain.
 Now 178 CTCs as of FY2025 10-K.

- **Revenue:** 
Last year, Acadia Healthcare, the country's largest OTP chain, reported roughly $500 million in revenue from its over 150 methadone clinics nationwide.
 At 178 clinics and ~$570M in estimated FY2025 CTC revenue (derived from Q3 quarterly run rate × 4), revenue per CTC is approximately $3.2-3.5M.

- **CEO Characterization:** 
Osteen characterized CTC as low capital intensity, low labor intensity, and having strong, predictable demand.


- **Political Risk — The MOTAA Threat:** 
Several for-profit clinics are opposed to federal legislation making its way through Congress to liberalize and reform how patients suffering from opioid use disorder can access methadone. Senator Markey's Modernizing Opioid Treatment Access Act (MOTAA) would allow board certified addiction medicine and addiction psychiatry physicians to prescribe methadone that could be picked up at a pharmacy.


- **Acadia's Lobbying Position:** 
Acadia has actively lobbied against the bipartisan proposal in the U.S. Congress to expand access to methadone treatment by allowing opioid use disorder patients to pick up methadone at pharmacies, rather than going to clinics.
 
Acadia is a partner organization in "Program, Not A Pill," a recently formed single-issue group alongside AATOD, BayMark Health Services, Behavioral Health Group, Carolina Treatment Centers, Crossroads Treatment Centers, New Season and Western Pacific Med Corp.


- **Legislative Status:** The MOTAA passed the Senate HELP committee but has not yet been enacted. Jefferies analysts argued the impact would be limited even if passed due to structural barriers to methadone prescribing. **[DEAL RISK: A buyer of the CTC segment must underwrite this regulatory optionality. If MOTAA passes in any form, it is the single largest identifiable threat to the CTC business model. The lobbying effort suggests the industry believes the risk is real and material.]**

- **Congressional Scrutiny of CTCs:** 
In December 2024, The New York Times published an investigative report, based on 60 interviews with current and former employees at Acadia's methadone facilities, that found that Acadia personnel frequently falsified medical records used for billing, and often gave methadone to people who did not have opioid addictions but were dependent on other drugs. The report also found the company's methadone counselors carry large caseloads, sometimes more than twice the limits set by state regulators.


- **Senate Investigation:** 
Sen. Maggie Hassan launched an investigation into whether patients at for-profit methadone clinics encounter unnecessary barriers when seeking lifesaving methadone treatment. She is demanding information about revenue, patient volumes, and employee numbers from Acadia, BayMark, and New Season. The investigation seeks to "better understand the impact of the OTP model on patient access to methadone; patient treatment outcomes; and potential waste, fraud, and abuse in federal health care programs."


- **CTC Audit Defense:** 
A third-party Independent Review Organization reviewed a random sample of Acadia's CTC claims and documentation for services provided from 2019 through 2024. The findings were highly favorable with an overall review score of 99.97% for the initial year reviewed, and a review score of 100% for the following four years, with no errors identified.
 **[This is a significant counter-narrative to the NYT investigation. A buyer should determine whether this audit is genuinely comprehensive or cherry-picked.]**

 The longer-term risk is Medicaid funding levels, not just work requirements.

Ongoing federal enforcement of MHPAEA should gradually improve commercial reimbursement rates — benefiting the 24.6% commercial revenue segment.

## SECTION 5: STRATEGIC OBSERVATIONS — M&A LENS

### 5.1 WHAT WOULD MAKE A BUYER EXCITED

**1. Irreplaceable National Scale in the Largest Untreated Disease Burden in America**
Behavioral health is a $500B+ total addressable market with a structural treatment gap of 40+ million people. 
As the leading pure-play behavioral healthcare provider in the U.S.,
 Acadia cannot be replicated through organic build at anywhere near the acquisition price. The alternative — organic greenfield — would cost $10B+, take 10+ years, and face identical regulatory and staffing challenges.

**2. $200M+ of Embedded EBITDA in Already-Built Assets**

There is a large opportunity to unlock the EBITDA and free cash flow potential within our existing facilities. Relative to the startup losses included in our 2025 results, the incremental EBITDA opportunity represented by the new facilities opened from 2023 through 2026 exceeds $200 million.
 A buyer pays today's trough multiple while collecting a future peak EBITDA stream from assets already built and licensed.

**3. The CTC Network Is a Hidden ~$1.5-2B Asset**
178 opioid treatment clinics across 33 states, producing ~$570M in revenue at high margins (CTC margins are typically 25-35% EBITDA given low capital and labor intensity), with recurring government-backed revenue from patients in multi-year treatment. At 10x EBITDA (conservative for a recurring-revenue, government-backed model), the CTC segment alone is worth $1.5-2B — representing approximately 33-43% of the current enterprise value of the entire company.

**4. JV Pipeline with A-List Health Systems Creates Durable Competitive Moat**

"We have seen tremendous benefits in these joint ventures. The combination of our partners' local market presence and medical capabilities along with our clinical and operating expertise gives these efforts a running start. Our partners' reputations bring instant credibility to these efforts."
 Each JV partner represents a referral pipeline and market access advantage that competitors would spend years attempting to replicate. 
Management noted that in a fragmented industry, it may sometimes be more efficient to acquire beds rather than build.


**5. Valuation Gap Is Extreme and Metrics-Provable**

ACHC trades at 6.5x EV/EBITDA and 1.2x EV/sales, far below its 10-year medians and current peer ratios.
 Against private market comparable transactions at 9-15x, Acadia is priced like a distressed credit, not a scaled platform with $3.3B revenue and positive operating cash flow. This gap is the transaction opportunity.

**6. Sale-Leaseback Upside for PE Buyer**
Engine Capital explicitly identified Acadia's real estate as an asset that "could fetch high multiples" in a sale-leaseback. Given that Acadia owns a portion of its inpatient facilities outright, a PE buyer could execute a sale-leaseback at closing to recapture invested capital — a standard PE deleveraging technique that reduces the net acquisition cost.

### 5.2 WHAT WOULD MAKE A BUYER NERVOUS

**1. The FBI Investigation is an Existential Variable**
The current FBI/HHS-OIG investigation of recent practices — not historical — cannot be diligenced away. A conviction or guilty plea by the company would trigger exclusion from Medicare and Medicaid — essentially ending the business. A strategic buyer with a healthcare license would face its own regulatory jeopardy by association. Representations and warranties insurance will not cover a known federal criminal investigation. This is the single factor most likely to prevent a transaction from happening on a near-term basis.

**2. PLGL Reserve Adequacy Is a Black Box**

Acadia recorded $115 million of medical malpractice expense in 2025 and guided to $100-110 million in 2026. Young said insurance dynamics have also shifted, noting that three years ago the company carried insurance for cases above $3 million, while the threshold has moved to $15 million, changing the risk profile.
 With claims up 186% and a reserve doubled to $155M in one year, any actuarial estimate involves multi-year uncertainty. A buyer's QoE firm would need to rebuild the PLGL reserve from scratch using independent actuarial analysis. A $150M reserve error (completely plausible given claim volume trajectory) would represent 25% of current EBITDA and could flip the deal economics.

**3. 35.9% Short Interest Is a Double-Edged Sword**
At face value, high short interest creates short-squeeze potential if a deal is announced. But sophisticated short sellers have done the diligence too — and their persistent, growing conviction (short interest rose 23.1% in just two weeks in February 2026) suggests they see risks that bulls are discounting. The incremental increase post-Q4 earnings (which beat consensus) is particularly telling.

**4. Medicaid Dependence + OBBBA = Multi-Year Revenue Headwind**
At 57.7% Medicaid and growing, any continued deterioration in Medicaid rates, supplemental payments, or enrollment creates compounding revenue pressure. 
Same-facility volume growth guidance includes an approximate 350 basis point headwind from certain Pennsylvania specialty facilities following changes in New York Medicaid policy.
 This $25-30M annual headwind is just the first visible cut from policy changes.

**5. RTC Segment Should Be Ring-Fenced or Divested**
The youth residential treatment segment — accounting for ~11% of revenue — carries disproportionate headline risk, Congressional scrutiny, and litigation exposure. 
The Senate Committee on Finance and the Senate Committee on Health, Education, Labor, and Pensions found that Acadia and Universal Health Services endangered the welfare of children in their facilities as a result of a business model that optimized revenue instead of optimizing care.
 No strategic or PE buyer should underwrite ownership of the RTC segment without a specific plan to either reform it comprehensively or divest it.

**6. The Full Litigation History Establishes a Pattern**
Total cash litigation settlements from 2019-2024 exceed $617 million — nearly matching the current market capitalization. This is not a one-off legacy issue; it is a repeated pattern across business lines (inpatient, CTC, RTC), time periods (2014-2017 DOJ, 2019 NM Desert Hills, 2024 securities class action), and types of misconduct (billing fraud, patient detention, patient abuse, whistleblower suppression). Any buyer conducting proper diligence will need to assess whether the new CEO and management team represents a genuine cultural inflection point or a continuation of the same model under new names.

**7. JV Change-of-Control Risk**
The 21 JV partnerships involving health systems like Intermountain Health, Henry Ford, Ascension, Geisinger, and Erlanger are the most valuable long-term assets in the portfolio. However, each JV agreement almost certainly contains change-of-control provisions. If a buyer is perceived as culturally incompatible (e.g., a financial buyer focused on cost reduction vs. a health system-affiliated strategic buyer), health system JV partners could invoke termination rights. Loss of even five of the 22 JV hospitals could reduce EBITDA by $50-100M.

### 5.3 TRANSACTION SCENARIOS — PROBABILITY-RANKED

**Scenario 1 — Transformational Strategic Acquisition by Managed Care/Insurer (Probability: 35-40%)**
UnitedHealth/Optum, Elevance Health (Carelon Behavioral), or CVS/Aetna acquires Acadia at 9-11x normalized EBITDA (~$5.5-6.7B EV). Rationale: MHPAEA enforcement forces insurers to provide adequate behavioral health networks; an insurer owning Acadia controls both the coverage obligation and the delivery capacity, creating vertical integration value. These buyers can absorb legal risk (they have legal/compliance infrastructure) and don't need to lever the deal. Precedent: UnitedHealth/Amedisys ($3.3B), Optum/thousands of physician practices. **Key obstacle: Regulatory (FTC/DOJ scrutiny of healthcare vertical integration has been intense; a managed care company acquiring the largest behavioral health provider would face significant antitrust review).**

**Scenario 2 — PE Take-Private, Surgical Risk Management Approach (Probability: 25-30%)**
A large PE sponsor (TPG, Blackstone, Apollo, or CD&R) takes Acadia private at 8-10x trough EBITDA (~$4.9-6.1B EV). The PE thesis: (1) absorb startup losses, (2) execute aggressive PLGL reserve management and litigation settlements, (3) divest RTC segment, (4) execute sale-leaseback on owned real estate, (5) ramp occupancy at 3,000+ new beds, (6) re-IPO in 3-5 years at 12-15x EBITDA once litigation overhang is resolved. **Key obstacle: At 4x net leverage already, a PE take-private requires refinancing the entire capital structure at likely higher rates, plus litigation escrow reserves. Deal mechanics are complex but not insurmountable for a $150B+ fund.**

**Scenario 3 — Partial Asset Sale — CTC Network (Probability: 15-20%)**
Acadia sells the 178-CTC network to a focused OUD operator (Crossroads/Revelstoke, BayMark, or a new PE platform) for $1.5-2.0B, using proceeds to pay down $1.5B+ of debt. This would bring net leverage from 4x to ~2x, restore EBITDA margins by eliminating corporate overhead allocated to CTCs, and allow the remaining inpatient company to trade at a higher multiple. **This is the Engine Capital thesis explicitly stated in their September 2025 letter.** Management has not publicly rejected it but has not endorsed it either.

**Scenario 4 — Operational Turnaround + Re-Rating, No Transaction (Probability: 10-15%)**
Osteen executes the operational playbook: fills new beds to target occupancy, resolves FBI investigation through cooperation (deferred prosecution or NPA), settles open securities litigation, brings PLGL down to $100M run rate, generates $200M+ FCF in 2027 as CapEx normalizes. Stock re-rates to 10-12x EBITDA, shareholders earn 50-100% returns from current levels without a transaction. This is plausible but requires the investigation to resolve favorably — a binary outcome that can't be managed or planned around.

**Scenario 5 — Distressed Scenario / Operational/Financial Deterioration (Probability: 5-10%)**
FBI investigation leads to material restrictions (consent decrees, facility closures, exclusion from government programs in certain states), PLGL reserve proves materially understated, Medicaid cuts are larger than modeled, and free cash flow turns negative. Credit agreement leverage covenants are breached. Lenders exercise remedies or force a restructuring. **Extremely low probability given current covenant compliance and $595M revolver availability, but cannot be dismissed as long as a criminal investigation is live.**
