## 2. RESEARCH WORKSTREAM SUMMARIES

### Workstream 1: CTC Spinoff Regulatory Transferability (L2 Deep Dive 1)

The most critical finding is the "program sponsor" determination under 42 CFR Part 8. The operating subsidiaries — Habit Opco, LLC; CRC Health Corporation; and state-specific entities — are the named program sponsors on SAMHSA certifications, not Acadia Healthcare Company, Inc. This means an equity spinoff preserving legal entity continuity does not trigger SAMHSA re-certification or DEA re-registration. The CON landscape is more favorable than assumed: North Carolina eliminated CON requirements for chemical dependency treatment facilities in 2023 (Session Law 2023-7); Indiana fully phased out CON by 2019; Ohio requires CON only for nursing homes; Pennsylvania repealed CON in 1996. West Virginia remains the most restrictive (2007 moratorium keeping clinics at 9 statewide), but existing clinics are grandfathered under equity spinoff. The binding constraint is the debt covenant architecture: CTC subsidiaries are guarantors of the $550M Senior Notes and $650M term loan. Bondholder consent solicitation is required. SAMHSA workforce cuts (50% staffing reduction under current administration) create processing delay risk but not a legal barrier. BayMark's three PE-level ownership transitions since 2015 with zero documented DEA/SAMHSA disruption provide definitive industry precedent. The feasibility assessment is: Equity spinoff 72% overall feasibility; Asset sale only 30%. [Not captured in briefing: detailed state-by-state CON matrix for all 10 largest CTC states; West Virginia SB 204 (2025) analysis; complete CRC Health acquisition reverse-precedent timeline; SAMHSA budget cut operational impact assessment]

### Workstream 2: M&A Process Intelligence (L2 Deep Dive 2)

The advisory conflict map confirms that Goldman Sachs advises Acadia (confirmed conflict with any buyer Goldman represents); Goldman's equity arm simultaneously purchased 4.7M ACHC shares in Q4 2025 (merger arbitrage activity); Jason Bernhard's Lazard Executive Committee position creates a buy-side conflict signal; and Kirkland & Ellis has active relationships with KKR and Warburg Pincus (confirming neither is conflicted from bidding). Apollo/LifePoint, despite being the most strategically logical buyer, faces Fund IX timing constraints (past investment period), LifePoint's B-rated/overleveraged balance sheet, and the Goldman conflict. The most likely financial buyers are KKR and Warburg Pincus. The optimal transaction structure is a pre-negotiated definitive agreement announced simultaneously with committed financing (no public auction) due to 35.9% short interest. The most probable offer range is $30–40/share. The Athenahealth/Elliott timeline (May 2017 activist → November 2018 DA) maps to an expected Acadia DA in Q3–Q4 2026. ### Workstream 3: PLGL Claims Inventory & Reserve Adequacy (L2 Deep Dive 3)

The facility-by-facility inventory identified 10+ facilities with active litigation, 120–200+ named claimants, and three new catastrophic-risk facilities not previously analyzed: Lakeland Behavioral Health (32 plaintiffs, criminal convictions, "booty darts"), Detroit Behavioral Institute (40+ retained claimants, serial supervisor abuse), and CenterPointe Hospital (new 2025 filings). The SIR trajectory ($3M→$15M) was confirmed by CFO at March 2026 investor conference. The probability-weighted reserve gap estimate is $400–650M (revised upward from first-round estimate of $250–350M). Three consecutive years of adverse development constitutes a statistical pattern with <12.5% probability of occurring by random chance. The Pennsylvania lookback window (passed House in 2025, awaiting Senate) could retroactively open pre-2019 Southwood claims. UHS's Pavilion ($535M/$180M) and Cumberland ($360M) verdicts establish the industry severity ceiling. Chapter 11 risk becomes material in the catastrophic scenario (20% probability). [Not captured in briefing: complete Indiana SOL analysis; Rolling Hills Hospital historical exposure; New Mexico Desert Hills residual tail claims; Catholic diocese Chapter 11 structural analogy; asbestos reserve development pattern as long-tail analog]

### Workstream 4: Embedded EBITDA Deconstruction (L2 Deep Dive 4)

The $200M+ figure is overstated by 27–35% due to three independently verifiable factors. JV proportionality: 65–87% Acadia ownership on 80%+ of new beds creates ~20–25% NCI haircut on the bed EBITDA component. Medicaid credentialing delays: 6–8 month lag confirmed by management, extending breakeven from implied 24 months to 36–48 months; management confirmed "inside 5 years" with "no defined pace." SDP distortion: $28.5M non-recurring Tennessee catch-up in Q2 2025 inflates the baseline; $34M total non-recurring benefit confirmed by CFO. OBBBA phases down existing SDPs at 10%/year starting 2028. The $300M+ capex cut for 2026 — projects paused that "no longer project an acceptable return" — is the strongest implicit admission. The realistic shareholder-attributable, timing-adjusted embedded EBITDA is $105–135M. UHS behavioral health same-facility volume growth of only 0.7% in FY2024 demonstrates that mature operators struggle with behavioral health volume. [Not captured in briefing: NCI balance growth calculation ($930.9M→$1,045.6M); expansion bed vs. new facility bed disaggregation; OBBBA "good faith effort" and "completed preprint" definitional ambiguity; hospitality industry new-build ramp analogy; CMS IPF PPS FY2024 rule change for Medicare unit timing]

### Workstream 5: CTC Clinical Trial Platform (L2 Deep Dive 5)

The hypothesis is structurally sound but timeline-challenged. The GLP-1 pipeline is the key catalyst: Lilly's brenipatide is entering large studies for OUD in 2025–2026; NIH Phase II semaglutide study (NCT06548490) enrolling 200 patients across 3 sites. No for-profit OTP chain has built a pharma-facing research platform — BayMark ($750M revenue) has no research operations. The SMO market ($6.24B, 6.2% CAGR) is attracting PE consolidation: Genstar/Flourish, General Atlantic/Javara, KKR/Headlands. DCR's history — academic origin 1985, DaVita acquisition 1997, 25+ years to current scale — suggests realistic timelines are 7–10 years to meaningful revenue, not 3–5 years. The independent entity structure is essential: pharma sponsors will not place trials at an Acadia subsidiary during active DOJ investigation. Revenue model: $18–88M at Year 5–7 depending on scenario; standalone valuation $150–750M at SMO multiples. [Not captured in briefing: NIDA Clinical Trials Network 22-year history (101 studies, 650 publications); 42 CFR Part 2 research-specific consent requirements; MPACT multi-site OTP trial protocol (30 OTPs, 600 patients); DOJ clinical trial fraud enforcement focus; Acadia UNC CBOH academic partnership detail; Alkermes orexin receptor pipeline]

## 5. DATA GAPS & CAVEATS

### Critical Data Gaps (Highest Impact on Analysis Quality)

1. **Facility-Level JV Financial Data.** The single most important missing data point. Without individual JV ownership percentages (only a 65–87% range is disclosed), the embedded EBITDA proportionality adjustment has ±5% precision. Health system partner Form 990 Schedule R filings (available on ProPublica with 12–18 month lag) may partially fill this gap.

2. **PLGL Claims Database Composition.** Management has never disclosed the number of active claims, the distribution of claim severity, or the split between reported vs. IBNR reserves within the $153M. Our litigation inventory is built from public sources and is certainly incomplete.

3. **Take-Home Dose Penetration Rates.** Acadia has never disclosed what percentage of CTC patients are on 7-day, 14-day, or 28-day take-home dose schedules. This is the key revenue quality metric for the CTC segment. State-level OTP utilization reports in California, Ohio, and Tennessee may provide partial data.

4. **EHR Standardization Status.** The CIO confirmed "multiple systems." We do not know what percentage of the 277 facilities are on Netsmart myAvatar vs. legacy systems, or whether measurement-based care instruments (PHQ-9, GAD-7, COWS) are systematically captured.

5. **DOJ Investigation Scope and Timeline.** The precise subjects of the SDNY and W.D. Missouri grand jury proceedings, the timeline for resolution, and whether a DPA/NPA has been offered are all non-public. This is the single largest source of uncertainty in the entire analysis.

### Methodological Caveats

6. **The PLGL reserve gap estimate ($400–650M) is based on public-source litigation inventory that is certainly incomplete.** The true number could be higher (additional undisclosed pre-litigation claims) or lower (some publicly reported matters may settle for less than per-claimant benchmarks suggest).

7. **Valuation multiples for OTP platforms are calibrated against BayMark's marketed EBITDA ($75M), which has not yet cleared.** If BayMark fails to sell or clears below 8x, the CTC spinoff valuation thesis weakens materially.

8. **The take-private probability estimate (60%) is based on circumstantial signal analysis, not confirmed inside information.** The advisory retention, governance changes, and CEO compensation structure are all consistent with — but do not prove — an active transaction process.

9. **Two of eight L2 deep dives failed due to API credit exhaustion** (Sale-Leaseback Market Analysis and JV Model Analysis/OIG Exclusion Shield). The relevant strategic ideas are included but at lower depth than intended. The sale-leaseback idea in particular lacks detailed comparable transaction data and REIT counterparty analysis.

10. **This analysis relies entirely on publicly available information.** Internal management data, board minutes, DOJ correspondence, actuarial work papers, JV operating agreements, and EHR system performance metrics would materially improve every estimate in this document.
