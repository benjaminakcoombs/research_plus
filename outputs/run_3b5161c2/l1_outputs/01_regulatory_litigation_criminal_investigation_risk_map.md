#### **Comparable #1: Universal Health Services (UHS) — Behavioral Health Investigation**

 No criminal charges filed against the company or executives.

**Stock reaction post-settlement:** 
Investors reacted positively to the news of the settlement, with the company's stock up more than 10% since the announcement.


**Analyst Commentary on UHS/Acadia parallels:** 
The factual allegations in the Kachrodia case are similar to those made in a securities fraud case against Universal Health Services, Teamsters Local 456 Pen. Fund v. Universal Health Services, No. 2:17-cv-02817-LS (E.D. [Pa.]).


> **UHS Verdict for Acadia Sizing: The $117-127M UHS civil settlement represents the most direct analog for Acadia's core inpatient psychiatric admissions/LOS fraud allegations. UHS had no CTC analog.**

#### **Comparable #2: September 2024 Acadia False Claims Settlement**
*Prior FCA settlement covering 2014-2017 conduct*


In September 2024, the U.S. Department of Justice announced a $19.85 million resolution of False Claims Act allegations that Acadia submitted false claims to government healthcare programs for medically unnecessary inpatient behavioral health services and services that did not meet federal and state regulations.



Acadia admitted patients into inpatient behavioral health facilities who did not qualify, kept patients hospitalized when they no longer required inpatient care, and failed to develop or update individual treatment plans tailored to each patient's needs. The company was also found to have neglected to provide active treatment, including required individual and group therapy, did not ensure adequate staffing, and failed in discharge planning, all of which led to serious patient harm.


> **Critical note:** This $19.85M settlement covered 2014-2017 conduct and expressly did NOT foreclose investigation of more recent practices. The new investigation covers the period after this settlement, potentially 2018-present — a roughly 7-year period at issue. Revenue scale and bed count grew dramatically over this period, meaning the dollar value of potentially fraudulent billings is substantially larger.

#### **Comparable #3: Great Circle (Missouri Behavioral Health NPA)**


In January 2023, the USAO for the Eastern District of Missouri — the same district that issued a grand jury subpoena to Acadia — entered into an NPA with Great Circle, a Missouri-based nonprofit provider of behavioral health services to minor children in the custody of the Missouri Department of Social Services.



The US Attorney considered factors including: parties entered into a civil settlement agreement; Great Circle is a nonprofit and a criminal conviction could cause "hardship to innocent third parties"; Great Circle had no prior criminal history; Great Circle cooperated and made witnesses available; and Great Circle committed to a compliance program. The NPA, which carried a three-year term, did not impose a criminal penalty.


> **Relevance to Acadia:** Acadia is a for-profit public company with a *prior* FCA settlement (the 2024 $19.85M resolution) — meaning it does NOT qualify for the "no prior history" factor that benefited Great Circle. This likely forecloses an NPA without financial penalties and makes a DPA with substantial financial components more probable than a simple NPA.

#### **Comparable #4: DPA + FCA Civil Settlement Pattern (Modern DOJ Practice)**


Two recent cases suggest that the DOJ may be more open to DPAs as a vehicle to resolve FCA suits. In recent cases, a medical device company simultaneously agreed to settle an FCA case while entering into a DPA that included monetary penalties and an admission of guilt. A similar strategy was deployed when a specialty pharmacy agreed to settle an FCA case in exchange for, among other things, a DPA. These cases suggest a willingness on behalf of the government to consider DPAs in return for a corporate admission of liability to charges filed via criminal information as a means of resolving allegations of fraud typically addressed through civil FCA cases.


#### **Comparable #5: The "Global Resolution" Structure (Harvard Corporate Governance analysis)**


In light of the enormous potential collateral consequences of a conviction — most notably, the threat of exclusion from participation in state and federal health care programs — responsible businesses must insist on some protection for a parent company as part of a global resolution. These global resolutions typically involve a subsidiary taking a criminal plea, a settlement of a civil False Claims Act complaint, entry into a CIA, and a "side letter agreement" NPA with the parent company. The "side letter agreement" NPA provides the parent the necessary protection, and the CIA provides a basis for OIG-HHS to waive its permissive authority to exclude the company from participating in federal health care programs.


> **This is the most likely structural outcome for Acadia: a subsidiary (Lakeland Hospital Acquisition, LLC is already subpoenaed) takes a criminal plea or DPA, the parent gets a side-letter NPA, a large civil FCA settlement is paid, and a CIA is imposed on the enterprise. This structure protects Medicare/Medicaid participation rights while delivering DOJ the criminal resolution it needs.**

### 4B. SOUTHWOOD PSYCHIATRIC HOSPITAL LITIGATION


On June 2, 2025, five minors sued Acadia Healthcare, operating as Southwood Psychiatric Hospital, alleging sexual abuse at the hands of guards and counselors for decades. The suit also alleges that Acadia should have been aware the conditions at Southwood would lead to abuse based on related lawsuits of other Acadia-owned juvenile residential treatment facilities from 2025, 2024, and 2019.



According to the lawsuit, employees allegedly gave minors cigarettes, drugs, and alcohol in exchange for sexual favors, and staff allegedly threatened victims if they refused the abuse. One employee was accused of drugging a young girl and groping her, then allegedly forcing her to perform oral sex and engage in intercourse.



In Pennsylvania, the current statute of limitations for civil sex abuse claims is the age of adulthood plus 37 years, meaning survivors sexually abused as minors typically have until the age of 55 to file legal action, as long as they were born after November 26, 1989.


**Assessment of Southwood Exposure:** This is an extremely dangerous case for Acadia for several reasons: (1) Pennsylvania has one of the broadest child sexual abuse statutes of limitations in the nation, opening the door to dozens or hundreds of additional plaintiffs for conduct spanning "decades"; (2) the allegations include staff-directed, systemic abuse — not isolated incidents — which invites punitive damages; (3) the class of "awareness" allegations (citing prior Acadia cases) removes good-faith defenses; (4) it is in Pennsylvania federal court, a jurisdiction with experienced plaintiff's bar and large jury verdicts. Per-plaintiff exposure range: $5M–$50M; aggregate exposure for Southwood alone: $50M–$500M+, depending on the total number of claimants who emerge.

### 4D. INSURANCE RETENTION "DONUT HOLE" ANALYSIS

The shift from $3M to $15M self-insurance retention creates a critical exposure gap:

- **Pre-2022 exposure:** Any claim settled $3M–$15M was covered by insurance
- **Current exposure:** Any claim settled $3M–$15M is entirely self-funded by Acadia
- **For sexual assault claims:** Cost-sharing extends above $25M, meaning Acadia bears all costs up to $25M+ before insurance kicks in

With a 186% surge in claims and a portfolio that likely includes hundreds of individual claims in various stages of resolution, the aggregate "donut hole" exposure (claims in the $3M–$15M range that insurance previously would have covered) could be **$100M–$300M** over the 2-7 year settlement timeline that management has referenced.

The insurance market dynamics are telling: 
Acadia's CFO noted that "the magnitude identified in the final actuarial report exceeded our expectations," with elevated IBNR reserves reflecting increased claims activity and less favorable reinsurance coverage terms versus prior years.
 When insurers dramatically raise retentions and worsen reinsurance terms, it is typically because they have concluded that the underlying claims environment has fundamentally deteriorated — not that it will revert to prior norms.

The falsified records allegations create **FCA exposure specific to the CTC segment** that is analytically separate from the inpatient psychiatric fraud. ### 6B. STRATEGIC BUYER vs. PE BUYER ANALYSIS

**Strategic Buyer (e.g., UHS, Kindred, HCA Healthcare, or large health system):**

*Approach:* Strategic buyers have deeper management capacity to absorb a CIA and compliance infrastructure. They would seek:
- Pre-closing resolution of the DOJ investigation as a deal condition precedent, or
- A substantial indemnity escrow (15-20% of enterprise value) funded by seller
- Representation & Warranty Insurance (RWI) **will not cover** known government investigations; this is a hard exclusion. Strategic buyers bear this risk directly
- Post-closing regulatory certainty via CIA could actually be beneficial — it sets clear compliance parameters and may accelerate insurance market normalization
- Synergies from combining compliance infrastructure across a larger behavioral health platform can meaningfully reduce CIA costs

*Leverage:* Can use the pending DOJ investigation to negotiate significant price concessions (15-25% EV discount beyond standalone risk-adjusted DCF)

**PE Buyer:**

*Approach:* Private equity buyers face more constrained options:
- Exit timelines (typically 3-5 years) conflict with DOJ investigation resolution windows (2-4 years), creating timing mismatch
- PE funds cannot easily absorb post-exit indemnity claims without fund-level structural protections
- Leveraged buyout economics are severely challenged by the PLGL cost structure ($100-115M/year), investigation costs ($50-70M in 2025), and the settlement reserves that will require cash deployment
- Debt covenant risk: most LBO credit agreements contain compliance representation requirements that the pending criminal investigation would potentially breach at closing or thereafter
- RWI market: major RWI insurers have likely placed behavioral health facilities with active government investigations on their exclusion lists

*Realistic PE scenarios:* Continuation PE (existing management buyout/recap), carve-out of specific sub-segments (e.g., CTC business separately), or a SPAC/continuation vehicle structure

### 6C. TRANSACTION MECHANISMS FOR RISK ALLOCATION

**1. Indemnity Escrow Sizing:**
- Minimum adequate escrow for DOJ/FCA risk alone: **$400-500M** (18 months' investigation costs + most likely civil settlement range)
- Full contingent liability escrow (base case): **$800M–$1.0B**
- Practical challenge: At current stock prices ($22-25/share) and ~92M shares outstanding, market cap is approximately $2.0-2.3B. An $800M escrow would represent 35-40% of market cap — functionally precluding most transaction structures unless funded by debt
- *Recommendation:* Structured release escrow ($250M) covering defined triggers (DOJ resolution, securities settlement, catastrophic PLGL claims above $25M) with tail insurance coverage for residual exposure

**2. Representations & Warranties Insurance:**
- **Known Government Investigation Exclusion:** All RWI underwriters will exclude the pending DOJ/FBI/SEC investigations and all related claims
- **Sexual Abuse Tail Exclusion:** Post-Desert Hills, sexual abuse claims at behavioral health providers are either excluded or extremely high-premium
- **PLGL Exclusion:** Claims development on known PLGL matters will be excluded
- *Net RWI utility:* Covers operational reps (facility licenses, employment matters, routine contracts) but **not the material litigation risks**

**3. Earn-Out Structure:**
- Problematic if predicated on EBITDA metrics that will be depressed by investigation/PLGL costs through 2027-2028
- Could structure on post-CIA resolution milestones: e.g., earn-out triggered only if DOJ resolution results in civil-only settlement without criminal conviction

**4. Material Adverse Change (MAC) Clause:**
- Standard MAC clauses in healthcare M&A now routinely include government investigation carve-outs — seller will resist any MAC definition that captures pending investigation
- A criminal indictment (vs. investigation) would likely meet any MAC definition without carve-out

**5. Pre-Closing Conditions:**
- Most protective structure for buyer: condition closing on DOJ investigation resolution
- Acadia management/board will likely resist this as potentially requiring 2-3 year delay
- Alternative: Condition on no criminal indictment prior to closing + no materially adverse deferred prosecution agreement terms

**6. Corporate Integrity Agreement — Positive or Negative for a Buyer?**

A CIA, when properly structured, is **mixed but generally manageable** for a strategic acquirer:
- *Negative:* 5-year term typically; requires independent compliance monitor (cost: $2-5M/year); enhanced billing audits (independent review organization cost: $3-7M/year); mandatory disclosure of violations; compliance hotline and board-level oversight; mandatory training programs; potential 150% liability multiplier on future violations
- *Positive:* Provides regulatory certainty — Acadia is in a known compliance environment vs. unknown ongoing risk; CIA terms are public, allowing buyers to due-diligence the compliance burden; OIG historically allows CIAs to be assigned to successors in acquisitions; CIA provides a defense against future permissive exclusion for covered conduct; effectively immunizes the post-CIA period from "same conduct" claims
- *For a strategic buyer:* The CIA burden (estimated $15-25M/year in compliance overhead) is material but manageable against $3.3B revenue. The certainty benefit may outweigh the cost, particularly compared to ongoing open-ended investigation exposure

**7. Effect of Cumulative Settlement History on Negotiating Position:**

The company has now accumulated approximately $617M+ in settlements since 2019 (Desert Hills $400M, prior securities class action $179M, FCA $19.85M, WV Medicaid $17M) plus an additional $179M securities settlement announced November 2025. This creates a documented pattern that:
- Establishes that courts/juries will award large verdicts against Acadia
- Makes a "we're a good corporate citizen" argument to DOJ harder
- Reduces insurance market appetite (hence the retention shift from $3M to $15M)
- Will be used by DOJ to argue that prior settlements failed to deter conduct
- Prior securities settlement *was* $179M for a 4.5 year class period on mostly UK operational issues — the new case with direct FBI/DOJ investigation is likely to settle higher, not lower

**ANOMALY 1: The $19.85M September 2024 FCA Settlement and Grand Jury Subpoena Were Disclosed the Same Day**

The September 26-27, 2024 timeline is extraordinary. The DOJ announced a civil False Claims Act settlement for 2014-2017 conduct on the same day Acadia disclosed it had received a **criminal** grand jury subpoena from the Western District of Missouri covering current conduct. This is not coincidental — it strongly suggests that DOJ structured the prior-period civil settlement as a floor to preserve its leverage for the ongoing criminal investigation, and may have extracted certain factual admissions or document production commitments in the civil settlement that now strengthen the criminal case. 
The inspector general's chief counsel explicitly stated that the settlement does not impede investigators from exploring allegations regarding more recent activities.
 This is a textbook DOJ staircase enforcement strategy.

**ANOMALY 2: The 99.97-100% CARF Compliance Score Contradiction**


Acadia management cited that "across our 161 CTC Opioid Treatment Programs, we scored >99% against all 13 dimensions of quality as measured by CARF, a leading accreditation body for behavioral health facilities."
 This directly contradicts the NYT findings of systematic record falsification and billing fraud at CTCs. The resolution of this contradiction is significant for transaction purposes: either (a) the CARF audit methodology is deficient or subject to manipulation (the NYT evidence suggests staff were coached), or (b) the quality metrics measured by CARF do not capture billing fraud and record falsification. 
The NYT investigation found the for-profit company is accused of failing to provide counseling, falsifying records, and enrolling patients who aren't addicted to opioids.
 The near-perfect CARF scores are a management talking point that any sophisticated buyer's due diligence counsel should probe aggressively.

**ANOMALY 3: Insurance Market Effectively Repriced Acadia Out of Standard Coverage**

The shift from $3M to $15M self-insurance retention is not merely a financial detail — it is the commercial insurance market's verdict on Acadia's risk profile. 
For certain sexual assault claims, cost sharing can extend above $25 million.
 This means that for the category of claim (sexual abuse of minors/vulnerable adults) that has already produced a $400M+ settlement and threatens to produce more, Acadia bears the first $25M+ of every single claim. The insurance market has effectively determined that Acadia's sexual abuse tail is uninsurable at standard rates. This is a structural adverse development that cannot be reversed by management commentary about quality improvements.

**ANOMALY 4: The Q4 2025 Reserve Miss Pattern — Third Consecutive Year of Adverse PLGL Development**

Management guided full-year 2025 PLGL at approximately $54-67M entering 2025. The actual result was $115M. The reserve balance at year-end 2025 ($153M) reflects a $52.7M Q4 adjustment that was disclosed on December 2, 2025 — six weeks after the Q3 earnings call on November 6, 2025, which itself had lowered guidance. 
Acadia's CFO stated that "the magnitude identified in the final actuarial report exceeded our expectations."
 For the third consecutive year, the annual actuarial review produced a material negative surprise. In actuarial science, when annual development consistently exceeds expectations in the same direction, the underlying assumptions are systematically wrong — not randomly wrong. This pattern suggests the reserve model has structural flaws (likely: claims inflation factors too low, tail development factors too optimistic, severity factors understated for sexual abuse cases post-Desert Hills). **An independent actuary should be retained by a potential acquirer and given access to the claims database** — management's certified reserve figures should not be relied upon.

**ANOMALY 5: The "No Single State Exceeds 15% of Revenue" Defense Is Superficially Reassuring**


CEO Osteen noted that no single state represents more than 15% of revenue.
 While this is geographically diversified, it masks a concentration issue of a different kind: **methadone clinic market concentration**. 
In 21 states, at least 50% of all methadone clinics are owned either by private equity firms or by Acadia Healthcare.
 In many markets, Acadia IS the methadone treatment market. A license revocation in a state where Acadia dominates methadone treatment would have both revenue impact and potential antitrust/regulatory scrutiny of any attempt to re-enter via acquisition.

**ANOMALY 6: Behavioral Pattern Strongly Suggests Systemic — Not Isolated — Misconduct**

The most important analytical question for a transaction buyer is: *is this a few bad facilities, or is this the business model?* The evidence points strongly toward the latter:

- Multiple NYT investigations over two years, based on 50+ employee interviews across psychiatric hospitals and 60+ interviews at methadone clinics, document the same practices in different states, different facility types, and different management periods
- 
Acadia Healthcare lawsuits are not confined to isolated incidents or limited to one or two underperforming facilities; they form a troubling pattern that spans Acadia's extensive national network of residential and psychiatric treatment centers; these lawsuits allege a systemic failure to protect vulnerable patients.

- The Senate Finance Committee's characterization that problems "occur inevitably and by design" as the direct result of the business model is damaging precisely because it was said by a United States Senate committee following a two-year investigation
- 
Acadia has identified a "handful of underperforming facilities" where it is working to improve growth, with underperforming facilities tending to be correlated with intense local media coverage.
 The CEO's framing of quality problems as "media-driven" rather than operationally driven is a significant gap between management's characterization and what the evidence shows

- The UHS parallel is informative: UHS resolved its case for $117-127M (civil only, no criminal charges) and the criminal investigation "came to absolutely nothing." However, UHS's investigation involved a single category of conduct (medically unnecessary admissions/LOS) at behavioral health facilities, resolved under the previous administration, and UHS had no CTC analog and no sexual abuse wave of comparable scale. **Acadia's case is materially more complex, more multi-front, and covers more recent conduct.**

**ANOMALY 7: The "Voluntary Cooperation" Characterization vs. Grand Jury Reality**

Management describes cooperation with the DOJ as occurring on a "voluntary basis." 
The DOJ is allowing Acadia Healthcare to share information on a voluntary basis, according to its latest quarterly financial report.
 However, a grand jury subpoena is legally compulsory — it cannot be declined. The "voluntary" framing applies only to the SDNY request for information (which is a non-compulsory request). The W.D. Mo. grand jury subpoena is an involuntary legal process. Management's framing conflates these two distinct legal instruments in a way that minimizes the apparent severity to investors and the public.

**ANOMALY 8: Investigation Costs as an Unusual Financial Signal**


Acadia spent $31 million on costs related to government investigations in the first three months of 2025 alone; during the same period in 2024, it spent less than $500,000.
 A 60-70x increase in investigation-related legal costs in a single quarter is not "routine cooperation." This is the scale of spending associated with document collection across hundreds of facilities, privilege review, witness preparation, outside counsel retainer, document hosting, and potential e-discovery for millions of records. For context, UHS's CFO disclosed that UHS was paying "in excess of $10 million annually in legal fees" during its multi-year behavioral health investigation. Acadia's run rate in Q1 2025 alone was $31M — 3x UHS's annual rate. This suggests an investigation of materially greater complexity and evidentiary scope.

**ANOMALY 9: The Simultaneous CEO and COO Departure Is Highly Unusual**


Chris Hunter, who began his tenure as CEO in April 2022, is leaving the company and its board of directors.
 
Additionally, Acadia's chief operating officer, Dr. Nasser Khan, resigned in early November [2025].
 The simultaneous departure of the CEO and COO within weeks of each other — both who served during the class period of the new securities litigation and during the period under active DOJ investigation — is an acute red flag. In government investigations, departure of executives who oversaw the questioned period can signal: (a) cooperation agreements; (b) personal legal exposure prompting voluntary separation to reduce liability association; (c) board-directed separation in anticipation of a resolution that requires management change as a condition. None of these possibilities is benign for a potential acquirer doing representations and warranties diligence.

**ANOMALY 10: The "Pure Play" Framing as a Double-Edged Sword**


CEO Osteen described Acadia as a "pure play" behavioral health provider with diversified service lines.
 In the context of the investigation, "pure play" behavioral health means that 100% of Acadia's revenue is from the sector under investigation, with no diversified revenue from unimpeached business lines to sustain the company through a prolonged resolution period. Unlike a diversified health system that might absorb a behavioral health settlement without threatening enterprise viability, Acadia has no buffer. A $500M+ FCA settlement — entirely conceivable given the scope — would be approximately 15% of revenue and roughly 80-90% of annual EBITDA. This creates a genuine going-concern consideration in the adverse scenario, which any transaction financing provider must stress-test.
