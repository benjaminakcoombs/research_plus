Management's stated view: across 2023 through 2026, new facility builds represent over 2,000 beds and at least $150 million of future EBITDA at maturity. When startup losses are considered, the incremental opportunity exceeds $200 million, excluding expansion beds.



CFO Young explicitly confirmed at Leerink 2026: "$50,000 of EBITDA per bed" as the basis for the math, noting some facilities make more and some make less.


**Reconstructing the Math:**
- 3,000 new beds (2023–2026) × $50,000/bed = **$150M EBITDA at maturity**
- Plus: reversal of current startup losses (~$56M in 2025, declining to $47–53M in 2026) = **~$50M improvement**
- Total: **~$200M** gross incremental adjusted EBITDA opportunity

### The $200M — Five Critical Stress Tests

**Stress Test 1: JV Proportionality (Most Important)**

In 2025, 
Acadia added 778 beds through the opening of one wholly-owned facility and five JV facilities.
 If ~80% of new beds in 2025 are from JVs where Acadia owns ~50%, then Acadia's attributable share of the $150M maturity EBITDA is:
- Wholly-owned share: ~20% of beds = 30M contribution at 100%
- JV share at 50%: ~80% of beds × 50% ownership = $60M contribution
- **Total attributable to Acadia: ~$90M of the "maturity EBITDA" rather than $150M**

With startup loss reversal (~$50M), the truly attributable $200M becomes approximately **$140M** — a ~30% haircut. Management presents the gross $200M figure without explicitly breaking out the JV proportionality, which is a material framing issue.

**Stress Test 2: $50K/Bed Assumption Validity**


Young acknowledged: "We have facilities obviously that make more than that on a per-bed basis. We have some that make less."
 At the consolidated level, current EBITDA per bed on existing facilities is approximately $608.9M / 12,500 beds = **$48,700/bed** (2025 actuals). The $50K target is essentially "current performance sustained" — not aspirational. The embedded opportunity requires ramping new beds to match existing mature facilities.

Downside scenario: If Medicaid managed care utilization pressure persists and new facilities ramp at 75% of historic pace, the effective EBITDA per new bed reaches $37,500 — implying only $112M maturity EBITDA from 3,000 beds, with proportionality adjustment further reducing Acadia's share.

**Stress Test 3: New York Medicaid Structural Offset**


Young confirmed the New York Medicaid policy change represents a $25–30M EBITDA headwind in 2026.
 This directly reduces the net "embedded EBITDA" improvement from the $200M gross opportunity. Net embedded opportunity: **$200M − $27.5M = ~$172.5M** before JV proportionality adjustment.

**Stress Test 4: PLGL Trend**


Key PLGL figures for 2025: PLGL expense ~$116M vs. $54M in 2024 (+$62M); 168% increase in 2025 policy-year claim frequency; year-end 2025 PLGL reserve $153M.



CFO Young acknowledged: "This new claim volume, this 186% growth, most of that won't play out from either a settlement or a litigation standpoint in the courts for 2 to 7 years just because of the nature of how long it takes."
 The $200M claim is in "adjusted EBITDA" terms — which adds back certain PLGL reserve changes. But the ongoing $100–110M PLGL run rate for 2026 (vs. the pre-surge baseline of ~$40M) represents a **permanent structural cost increase of ~$65M/year** that flows through to cash EBITDA even if excluded from the "adjusted" figure.

**Stress Test 5: Ramp Timeline Realism**


Management says facilities typically reach target performance "inside 5 years."
 The 2023 cohort (595 beds) opens in 2026 to materially contributing, approaching maturity 2027–2028. The 2025 cohort (778 beds) may not be at target until 2029–2030. 
New additions concentrated at the end of 2024 were not expected to break even until early 2026.


**Summary Assessment:**

| Scenario | Gross $200M Opportunity | JV Adjustment | NY Medicaid Offset | Realistic Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Management case | $200M | None disclosed | None embedded | 2027 |
| Bull case | $175M | −15% (optimistic JV ownership) | −$20M | 2028 |
| Base case | $140M | −30% (50/50 JV mix) | −$27.5M | 2028–2029 |
| Bear case | $100M | −40% (Medicaid volume risk + JV) | −$30M + PLGL persistence | 2030+ |

**⚠️ CONCLUSION:** The $200M claim is arithmetically derived from the $50K/bed assumption across 3,000–4,000 new beds. It is directionally plausible but systematically overstated by management in presentation because it: (1) does not net out JV proportionality, (2) does not net out NY Medicaid structural headwind, and (3) uses "adjusted EBITDA" terms that exclude ongoing PLGL cash costs. The realistic net cash benefit to Acadia shareholders is **$110–140M** materializing over 2027–2029.


Leerink Partners independently validated the framework, estimating Acadia could achieve $600 million in EBITDA at the midpoint plus $200 million of embedded earnings from de novo facilities,
 confirming sell-side adoption of the management narrative — but noting: 
Leerink stated the behavioral healthcare provider "has been mismanaged for an extended period and has overspent for years" with margins declining meaningfully over a multi-year timeframe.

## NOTABLE ANOMALIES — CONSOLIDATED AND UPDATED

### Anomaly 1: The Goodwill Impairment ($996.2M) Arithmetically Implies Massive Internal Valuation Markdown


Loss on impairment was $1,006 million in Q4 2025; the non-cash impairment charge included a $996.2 million goodwill impairment.
 A $996M goodwill impairment — recorded less than 2 years into the most aggressive expansion program in company history — means Acadia's auditors concluded the business is worth materially less than the carrying value of goodwill on the balance sheet. The carrying value of goodwill was reduced by nearly $1 billion in a single quarter. At 12x EBITDA, this implies the Board recognized the business is worth approximately $83M less EBITDA than previously assumed — roughly one-third of the current "embedded EBITDA" opportunity. This is not a routine write-down; it is a formal acknowledgment that prior expectations for acquired and built assets were fundamentally wrong.

### Anomaly 2: Eating Disorder Exit — 180-Degree Reversal in 12 Months


As recently as June 2024, Acadia was actively expanding its eating disorder programming and planning additional eating disorder capacity while competitors were scaling back.
 
By October 2025, Acadia had shut down all eating disorder facilities from the CRC Health Group acquisition.
 This represents a complete strategic reversal in under 18 months — closing facilities that management was publicly planning to expand. The reversal was precipitated by eating disorder industry dynamics (payer rate pressure, labor intensity, declining commercial enrollment) but the speed of the reversal is anomalous. The COO who championed the eating disorder expansion, Dr. Nasser Khan, 
resigned from the COO role effective November 3, 2025,
 coincident with the closure program and guidance revision.

### Anomaly 3: The CTC Business Is Structurally Mispriced Within the Conglomerate

At estimated 28–30% EBITDA margins, $572M in annualized revenue, 76,000 patients in long-duration (>1 year) treatment, and capital-light owned real estate, the CTC portfolio is Acadia's highest-quality business unit. It is the nation's largest OTP chain with genuine competitive moat (DEA licensing barriers, state OTP certification, established patient relationships). Yet it trades inside a conglomerate priced at roughly 4x peak EBITDA (~$850M peak EBITDA / ~3.4B enterprise value), burdened by RTC litigation, government investigation overhang, and acute capacity startup losses. A standalone CTC entity — unencumbered by the legal and operational risks of the inpatient portfolio — might reasonably command 12–15x EBITDA, implying a standalone value of **$1.9–2.6B** for the CTC business alone (vs. Acadia's total equity market cap of ~$2.2B at current prices). This embedded value is almost entirely invisible to current investors.

### Anomaly 4: 186% Claim Frequency Surge — The Understated Tail Risk


Claim frequency increased 168–186% in 2025 policy year.
 
CFO Young acknowledged that most of this new claim volume "won't play out from either a settlement or a litigation standpoint in the courts for 2 to 7 years."
 This creates a massive legal liability that is: (a) not fully reserved on the balance sheet, (b) off-limits in strategic transaction planning because buyers cannot price an unresolved claim pile, and (c) growing — the 186% surge in frequency means claims filed in 2025 will become settlements and judgments in 2027–2031. The $153M PLGL reserve is almost certainly insufficient for a 186% frequency surge — reserves for sexual assault and patient harm cases often exceed $500K–$2M per claim in settlement value, implying even 300–400 incremental claims at an average of $500K = $150–200M of additional required reserves, potentially exceeding the current reserve balance.

### Anomaly 5: The Biggest Historical Settlement Was $400M — And Barely Discussed


In October 2023, Acadia settled three New Mexico (Desert Hills) lawsuits for $400 million.
 This is larger than all DOJ settlements combined. Yet it occurred largely without investor awareness of the magnitude of the RTC liability profile. The Desert Hills facility (New Mexico youth residential) is now closed, but the $400M settlement demonstrated that Acadia's liability exposure at RTCs can reach catastrophic scale. With 9 remaining RTCs, Senate investigation findings naming Acadia, and DOJ referral from Senator Wyden, the potential liability from remaining RTCs could materially exceed the current PLGL reserve structure.
