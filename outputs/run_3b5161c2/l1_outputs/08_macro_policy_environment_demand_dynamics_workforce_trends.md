**State-Directed Payment Grandfathering — Critical Near-Term Positive:**


OBBBA allows states to continue paying existing SDPs at current higher rates through the end of 2027, and then phases down those payments gradually — by 10% per year until they reach Medicare rates.



CMS effectively broadened this grandfathering, interpreting the 180-day window as "business days" rather than calendar days, extending it through March 27, 2026 instead of December 31, 2025 — allowing new and increased payments at the beginning of the year to pay far above Medicare rates.



Before OBBBA, Texas was approved to pay inpatient and outpatient providers SDPs totaling $6.5 billion. After OBBBA, CMS approved Texas' SDPs to increase to $9.1 billion.
 This front-loading of SDP increases before the phase-down begins represents a near-term positive for Acadia's operations in high-SDP states.

Acadia's partnerships with ECU Health, Tufts Medicine, Henry Ford Health, Geisinger, Ascension, Orlando Health, and SolutionHealth serve a dual function: (a) reducing capital requirements for new hospital construction, and (b) providing institutional credibility that has likely *shielded* Acadia from more aggressive regulatory action. A Medicare or Medicaid debarment action against Acadia — even temporarily — would be untenable for these nonprofit health system partners, creating alignment of interests in Acadia's compliance improvement. This "too interconnected to debar" dynamic is an underappreciated strategic protection.

**6. OBBBA's State-Directed Payment Grandfathering Creates a 2026 Phantom Tailwind**


Two states alone have increased their SDP rates by 50% since OBBBA passed, costing the federal government roughly $20 billion over ten years.
 States are front-loading SDP increases in the grandfathering window. Acadia's 2026 and early 2027 results will temporarily benefit from these front-loaded state SDPs before the phase-down begins in 2028. An M&A model that misreads 2026–2027 SDP revenue as a run-rate will overstate normalized EBITDA by $20–40M.

**7. 29% Short Interest Creates Structural Volatility Risk for M&A Process**


ACHC has 29% short interest, far above normal healthcare company levels.
 A strategic review announcement or M&A rumors would trigger a violent short squeeze, dramatically compressing any bid premium opportunity. An acquirer must be prepared for stock price volatility during a formal process that may complicate a negotiated transaction. A pre-agreed "go-shop" period or quiet negotiation (vs. auction) may be preferable.

**8. Tennessee Non-Expansion Protects Core Revenue from OBBBA Work Requirements — But Also Caps Medicaid Growth**

Tennessee (up to 14% of Acadia's revenue) has never expanded Medicaid. 
As of 2025, ten states have not expanded Medicaid: Texas, Florida, Georgia, Tennessee, Mississippi, Alabama, South Carolina, Wyoming, Kansas, and Wisconsin.
 This means Tennessee's patient population for Acadia is TennCare-eligible traditional Medicaid (disabled, children, pregnant women) — the very population that is categorically exempt from OBBBA work requirements. This is a meaningful structural protection for Acadia's largest single-state revenue base. The trade-off: Tennessee will never access ACA expansion Medicaid enrollment, capping Acadia's Medicaid population growth in its home state.

**9. CTC Business Is Strategically Separable — But Worth More Together**

The 178 CTCs (annualized ~$550–580M revenue, estimated ~$130–150M EBITDA, ~25% EBITDA margin) represent a structurally differentiated business from the inpatient psychiatric portfolio. CTC economics are: 
low capital intensity, low labor intensity, and having strong, predictable demand.
 The CTC network could theoretically be divested separately (to BayMark, PE, or a MAT-focused acquirer) to reduce DOJ investigation risk concentration, raise capital for debt paydown, and simplify Acadia's story. However, the regulatory cloud from the NYT/Hassan investigation would impair CTC sale proceeds significantly near-term.

**10. Behavioral Health M&A Market Is Healthy — But Acadia Is an Unusual Asset**


Across all behavioral categories, 2025 multiples have remained stable, with premium outcomes concentrated among scaled, multi-state platforms with accreditation, diversified payer relationships, and strong clinical governance.
 
Tech-enabled care, behavioral health and physician specialty platforms could see some of the most aggressive capital flows in years as acquirers look to scale models that can grow without adding labor.
 Acadia fits the "scaled multi-state platform" profile but fails on "strong clinical governance" — which is precisely what creates the discount. A strategic acquirer (hospital system, large PE fund with healthcare operations expertise, international health system, or payer-affiliated healthcare company) that can credibly commit to compliance infrastructure improvement is best positioned to close the valuation gap.

## TRANSACTION CONCLUSION

Acadia Healthcare represents a **fundamentally sound behavioral health franchise operating under an extraordinary cloud of legal, regulatory, and governance risk** that has compressed its valuation to historic lows. The core investment thesis — the largest pure-play U.S. behavioral health operator at 6.5x EBITDA with $200M of embedded earnings and secular demand tailwinds — is compelling on paper. The risks are well-known and concentrated:

**Bull case:** A strategic or PE acquirer with strong compliance expertise executes DOJ cooperation agreement, settles outstanding investigations for $300–500M (within modeled range), implements workforce and clinical quality reforms, captures $150–200M of embedded EBITDA as new beds ramp, and exits in 5 years at 9–10x EBITDA on $750–800M of normalized earnings for 3–4x MOIC.

**Bear case:** DOJ/FBI investigations expand into criminal indictment territory (not currently signaled but not ruled out), CTC fraud settlement approaches $500M+, OBBBA causes Medicaid enrollment loss beyond the behavioral health exemption's protection, and the $200M embedded EBITDA never materializes due to persistent workforce and licensure constraints. Company enters financial distress as contingent liabilities overwhelm cash generation.

**Base case:** Investigations settle for $200–350M over 2026–2028 via DOJ cooperation agreement, EBITDA recovers to $700–750M by FY2028, FCF turns meaningfully positive FY2026, debt falls to ~2.5x, and Acadia earns a re-rating to 8–9x. Total enterprise value of $5.5–6.8B supports equity value of $3.0–4.3B, representing 30–90% upside from current public market valuation.

**For an M&A transaction:** The transaction premium is bounded on the upside by the legal tail risk and on the downside by the activist shareholder base. A negotiated transaction at $30–35 per share (roughly $2.7–3.2B equity value, $5.1–5.7B EV, approximately 8.5–9.3x FY2026E EBITDA guidance midpoint) represents fair value with appropriate risk-sharing structures. Escrow of 10–15% of purchase price against legal contingencies is standard precedent for similar regulatory-risk transactions.
