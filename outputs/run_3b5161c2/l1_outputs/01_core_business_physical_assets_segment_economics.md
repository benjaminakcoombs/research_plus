**Occupancy — Mature vs. New:**

Occupancy at newer facilities is "behind," and Acadia has provided cohort curves comparing performance to history and expectations.
 
Once a facility reaches roughly 75%–80% occupancy, it becomes harder to match patient needs to available bed types, and adding beds can improve throughput.

Approximately 80% of acute admissions originate from professional referral sources such as emergency departments, police departments and schools.
 This referral dependency is critical to understand: new facilities must establish referral pipelines from scratch, which takes time and creates the occupancy lag. Mature Acadia hospitals with established ED referral relationships are dramatically more defensible businesses than the recent de novos.

**Staffing Model:**

Acute inpatient psychiatric facilities provide a high level of care in order to stabilize patients that are either a threat to themselves or to others. The acute setting provides 24-hour observation, daily intervention and monitoring by psychiatrists. These facilities provide evaluation and crisis stabilization of patients with severe psychiatric diagnoses through a medical delivery model that incorporates structured and intensive medical and behavioral care.

#### Patient Census


Through Acadia's network of 165 Comprehensive Treatment Centers across 33 states, they support more than 72,500 patients in treatment.

163 CTCs across 33 states were treating over 72,000 patients daily.

#### CTC Cost Structure

CTCs are fundamentally different from inpatient facilities:
- **Labor-light:** Typical CTC staff per location: 1 medical director (often part-time), 2–3 nurses, 5–8 counselors, administrative staff. Total FTEs per clinic: ~12–18.
- **Real estate:** Small footprint (2,000–5,000 sq ft), generally leased. 
Of 178 CTCs, 25 are owned properties and 153 are leased properties.
 The high lease proportion means minimal book value but ongoing lease obligations.
- **Medication cost:** Methadone is inexpensive ($0.25–$0.50/dose); buprenorphine/Suboxone is modestly more expensive but still low per-day cost relative to reimbursement.
- **DEA licensing:** Annual DEA registration required; this is a barrier-to-entry but not a meaningful ongoing cost.
- **Reimbursement:** CTCs bill under the Opioid Treatment Program (OTP) bundled payment system. Under Medicaid, the daily bundled rate varies by state ($15–$30/day in many states, but significantly higher in some). Under Medicare (added by the SUPPORT Act effective 2020), the OTP bundled payment was set at approximately $36–$38/day in the initial years. Commercial rates vary widely.


De novos in Acadia's CTC line of business continue to be a capital-efficient way to expand its footprint into new markets that are underserved.

## SECTION 3: THE $200M EMBEDDED EBITDA CLAIM — STRESS TEST

### The Claim


Debbie Osteen stated: "Relative to the start-up losses included in our 2025 results, the incremental EBITDA opportunity represented by the new facilities opened from 2023 through 2026 exceeds $200 million."


This is a carefully worded claim. It is not "$200M of additional EBITDA on top of today's results." It is "$200M incremental relative to the STARTUP LOSSES currently embedded in 2025 results." This means:

- If 2025 startup losses = ~$60M, and
- New facilities at maturity add some level of positive EBITDA, then
- The delta = (mature EBITDA contribution) – (negative startup loss) = total swing

### Quantifying the Math


CFO Young said: "Inside 5 years [to maturity]. It's 3,000 beds. That math makes it $50,000 of EBITDA per bed."
 — This is the most granular public disclosure. $50K EBITDA per bed × 3,000 beds = $150M of EBITDA at maturity from the new bed cohort.

### Occupancy Ramp Evidence


Occupancy at newer facilities is "behind," and Acadia has provided cohort curves comparing performance to history and expectations. He did not provide facility-by-facility occupancy details but said increasing inpatient occupancy is a primary focus.



Young attributed de novo underperformance to a combination of factors, including expectations that proved too aggressive, timing mismatches that drove staffing and fixed-cost ramp ahead of volume, and slower-than-expected licensure processes. **Stress Test — How Real is the $200M?**

The claim has three major risks:

1. **Ramp-rate risk:** If occupancy at 2023–2025 cohorts is running 12–18 months behind expectations, then the $150M EBITDA at maturity arrives in 2029–2030, not 2027–2028. 
Acadia expects full year 2025 startup losses to come in at $60 million to $65 million. Startup losses for the full year 2026 are expected to decrease modestly from 2025 levels, with a more material step-down expected in 2027. CapEx will step down meaningfully next year, but startup costs will decline less on a relative basis due to the large number of new beds coming online in 2025.


2. **JV dilution:** A meaningful portion of new beds (roughly 1/3–1/2) are in JV hospitals. If Acadia owns ~50% of those JVs, then the EBITDA flowing to Acadia's consolidated P&L from JV beds is proportionally reduced. The "$50K EBITDA per bed" figure may represent 100% of facility-level economics, not Acadia's net share.

3. **Medicaid headwinds eating into the ramp:** New York Medicaid changes, the OBBBA, and managed Medicaid tightening could impair target occupancy and ARPD assumptions embedded in the "mature state" model.

**Bottom Line:** The $200M claim is arithmetically reasonable if all 3,000 beds ramp to target within 5 years and macro/policy headwinds don't worsen. The realistic scenario is that $120–$160M of the $200M materializes by 2028, with the remainder depending on policy and execution. The $200M number includes the startup loss reversal ($60M), meaning "true" incremental run-rate EBITDA from mature operation is ~$140M — meaningful but not transformational at a $600M EBITDA base.

## NOTABLE ANOMALIES

The following observations represent the most significant surprises, contradictions, or material data points uncovered in this analysis:

---

**1. THE PLGL CRISIS IS A BUSINESS MODEL SIGNAL, NOT JUST A ONE-TIME EVENT**

The 186% increase in claims, self-retention rising from $3M to $15M, and reserves doubling to $155M are not coincidental. 
DOJ alleged that Acadia "admitted patients who didn't qualify for inpatient care and kept patients longer than necessary, violating patient care standards. Acadia also failed to provide adequate staffing, supervision, and treatment plans, resulting in patient harm, including assaults and elopements."
 This pattern — holding patients too long for billing purposes, understaffing, resulting in adverse incidents — is the root cause of the PLGL explosion. Until the business model is restructured (shorter, medically appropriate stays, adequate staffing), PLGL costs will remain elevated. The CFO's guidance of $100–110M for 2026 PLGL — compared to $54M in 2024 and just ~$20M in 2022 — represents a permanent structural cost increase of **~$80–90M/year** from 2022 norms. At Acadia's pre-PLGL margin of ~26%, this $80–90M drag alone explains ~200bps of margin compression. **This is the single most important anomaly in the business, and it is underappreciated by the market.**

---

**2. THE CTC DIVISION IS A HIDDEN JEWEL MISCLASSIFIED AS "CHALLENGED"**

The CTC segment grew 7.7% in Q3 2025 against a backdrop of specialty declines. With ~440 patients per clinic on long-duration methadone therapy, sticky Medicaid/Medicare reimbursement, capital-light structure (153 of 178 clinics are leased), and industry-leading scale across 33 states, 
the CTC expansion is described as a "high-margin Comprehensive Treatment Center network."
 Engine Capital's proposal to sell the CTC division at a private market multiple of 12–14x EBITDA would generate $1.7–2.0B in proceeds — potentially exceeding the entire company's current equity market cap. The anomaly: the market is pricing the CTCs as part of a "distressed operator," when the CTC business itself is not distressed at all.

---

**3. THE $200M EMBEDDED EBITDA CLAIM IS DILUTED BY JV MECHANICS**

Management presents the $200M as an aggregate opportunity, but approximately one-third to one-half of new beds are in JV hospitals where Acadia may hold only ~50% economic interest. 
The math is $50,000 of EBITDA per bed across 3,000 beds = $150M,
 and the $200M figure adds back startup losses. However, if 1,000+ of those beds are in JVs where Acadia receives 50% of earnings, the net attributable EBITDA is perhaps $120–$140M, not $150–200M. The $200M claim appears to reflect facility-level economics, not Acadia's consolidated share. **This is never directly clarified in public disclosures — a material ambiguity.**

---

**4. REAL ESTATE REPLACEMENT VALUE EXCEEDS ENTERPRISE VALUE**

With 91% of ~11,350 non-CTC beds in owned properties, at $400K construction cost per behavioral health inpatient bed in a purpose-built facility, the replacement cost of owned real estate alone is approximately **$4.1 billion.** Even at a conservative 20% discount to replacement (wear, market conditions), that's $3.3 billion of hard asset value. The company's recent enterprise value (debt of ~$2.9B + market cap of ~$3.0B at recent trading levels ≈ ~$5.9B EV) implies the market is valuing the operating business at approximately $2.6B after real estate — which at $601M EBITDA (2025) implies just 4.3x operating EBITDA for the ongoing businesses. This is a profoundly anomalous discount to sector comparables.

---

**5. PENNSYLVANIA IS A SINGLE STATE CONCENTRATION RISK THAT WAS INVISIBLE UNTIL THE NY MEDICAID CHANGE**

The New York Medicaid policy change — no payment for NY Medicaid patients treated out-of-state — creates a $25–30M annual EBITDA headwind concentrated entirely in Acadia's Pennsylvania specialty facilities. 
From essentially 8 facilities impacted down to 6 after consolidating two leased facilities.
 This cluster of specialty SUD facilities near the NY/PA border was apparently highly dependent on NY Medicaid patients — suggesting a geographic concentration risk that was never disclosed as material until the policy changed. **This raises a broader question: how many other cross-state Medicaid relationships exist in Acadia's portfolio that could be similarly disrupted by policy changes?**

---

**6. THE GOODWILL IMPAIRMENT IS A CONFESSION**


Loss on impairment was $1,006 million for Q4 2025, compared to $6 million in Q4 2024.
 A $996M goodwill write-down in a single quarter is management explicitly acknowledging that the growth investments of the Hunter era overpaid for assets or that the embedded value of the growth pipeline has declined below carrying value. This is consistent with Engine Capital's critique that "the market is not pricing in any profitable growth." The write-down removes the goodwill overhang but does not repair underlying operations. Notably, 
Engine Capital observed that "the market is not pricing in any profitable growth and is valuing the business at a significant discount to its replacement value, implying that growth is destroying value."
