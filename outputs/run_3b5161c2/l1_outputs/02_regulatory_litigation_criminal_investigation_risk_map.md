#### 1.A.ii — Core Allegations (What the Evidence Shows)

The NYT investigation established the operational predicate for the criminal inquiry:


Involuntary commitment laws say that someone can be held involuntarily against their will if they are an immediate threat to themselves or others. The NYT investigation, which was based on voluminous internal and external records and interviews with more than 50 current and former employees, found that even though those laws were meant for people who pose an imminent threat, Acadia was holding patients who did not appear to meet that legal standard — holding people who should not have been there for much longer than is allowed under these state laws.



Acadia can charge more the longer patients are there, and even one or two days can make a big difference. The company deployed a range of strategies to get insurance companies to cover longer stays: exaggerating patients' symptoms, tweaking their medication dosages and claiming patients need to stay longer, arguing that patients aren't well enough to leave because they didn't finish a single meal.



These strategies were pervasive in 12 out of the 19 states where Acadia operates inpatient psychiatric hospitals.



These extended stays, billed at rates as high as $2,200 per day, often continued until patients' insurance coverage was exhausted.



From 2019 to 2023, North Tampa filed more than 4,500 petitions to extend patients' involuntary stays, according to a Times analysis of court records.
 This is a quantifiable, court-record-documented pattern that provides criminal investigators with granular evidence.

Beyond involuntary detention, the CTC/methadone business faces separate fraud allegations: 
In December 2024, the New York Times published an investigative report, based on 60 interviews with current and former employees at Acadia's methadone facilities, that found that Acadia personnel frequently falsified medical records used for billing, and often gave methadone to people who did not have opioid addictions but were dependent on other drugs. The report also found the company's methadone counselors carry large caseloads, sometimes more than twice the limits set by state regulators.


Senate investigation letters reinforce falsification allegations: 
A former assistant medical director of an OTP in North Carolina alleges that Acadia systematically falsified records for at least a year beginning in September 2020, fabricating notes for certain patients.

#### 1.A.vi — Corporate Integrity Agreement (CIA) Analysis for Transaction Structuring

If a CIA is imposed (highly probable in any civil resolution scenario given the UHS precedent), the following operational constraints are typical for behavioral health CIAs:

- Independent compliance monitor (selected by OIG) reporting to OIG for 5 years
- Annual IRO (Independent Review Organization) review of billing claims
- Random clinical record reviews
- Patient safety monitoring programs
- Board-level compliance committee obligations
- Mandatory self-disclosure obligations

**For a strategic or PE buyer:** A CIA has a dual character:
- **Negative:** Adds $15–30M/year in compliance overhead; restricts operational flexibility; creates ongoing disclosure obligations; delays integration.
- **Positive:** Provides a defined regulatory compliance framework with bright-line rules, reducing ambiguity about what is permissible. Post-CIA companies often achieve better long-term payer relationships.

**Transaction structuring implication:** A CIA in place at time of transaction actually improves deal certainty for a buyer, as the residual regulatory risk is bounded and structured. A buyer acquiring Acadia prior to CIA resolution faces an unquantified compliance obligation. This argues for a **pre-closing resolution condition** or an **enhanced escrow sized to CIA compliance costs** if closing before resolution.

The New York Times found the for-profit company is accused of failing to provide counseling, falsifying records, and enrolling patients who aren't addicted to opioids.


**Business model threat:** Proposed legislation (MOTAA — Modernizing Opioid Treatment Access Act) would allow physicians to prescribe methadone outside of OTPs. 
In 21 states, at least 50% of all methadone clinics are owned either by private equity firms or by Acadia Healthcare.
 If methadone prescribing is deregulated, Acadia's 165-clinic CTC network faces structural obsolescence. This represents an existential strategic risk to the CTC business segment that is independent of fraud exposure.

VA investigations carry unique risk: the VA has its own Inspector General, its own prosecutor (DoD DCIS), and TRICARE is separately administered. The September 2024 DOJ settlement included TRICARE allegations, meaning the VA investigation almost certainly covers post-2017 conduct in a new enforcement window. Potential additional financial exposure: $20–100M for a TRICARE-specific settlement.

## NOTABLE ANOMALIES

### ANOMALY 1: The 99.97%–100% CARF Compliance Score vs. the FBI Investigation


Acadia stated that across its 161 CTC Opioid Treatment Programs, it scored >99% against all 13 dimensions of quality as measured by CARF, a leading accreditation body for behavioral health facilities.


This figure is cited in the same September 2024 press release disclosing the DOJ investigation — and in the same week that the NYT published its investigation finding systematic falsification of records at CTCs. **This is the single most analytically suspicious data point in the entire Acadia risk profile.** Possible explanations:

- CARF accreditation reviews are largely documentation-based and retrospective; they may measure what was *recorded* rather than what was *done* — making them perfect tools for an operation that allegedly falsifies records.
- If Acadia personnel were fabricating counseling notes (as the NYT alleged based on 60 employee interviews), those same fabricated notes would generate near-perfect CARF audit scores.
- CARF accreditation has historically been criticized as insufficiently rigorous for detecting systemic fraud. The accreditation body is a conflict-of-interest-adjacent organization funded by member fees.

**This is not exculpatory; it is potentially aggravating.** It suggests that compliance infrastructure may have been weaponized as a fraud concealment mechanism rather than a genuine quality assurance program.

---

### ANOMALY 2: The Absence of a CIA in the September 2024 Settlement

The September 2024 $19.85M FCA settlement conspicuously did not include a Corporate Integrity Agreement. 
In the UHS comparable settlement, UHS entered into a Corporate Integrity Agreement with the HHS-OIG for five years, with an independent monitor assessing UHS's Behavioral Health Division's patient care protections.


The absence of a CIA in Acadia's 2024 settlement — despite covering nearly identical conduct across an even larger facility network — is highly anomalous. **The most analytically consistent interpretation:** HHS-OIG declined to impose a CIA in 2024 because investigators were already actively developing a separate, subsequent, potentially criminal enforcement action covering post-2017 conduct for which a CIA would be premature.

---

### ANOMALY 3: The Insurance Retention Shift as an Insurer Vote of No Confidence

The increase of self-insured retention from $3M to $15M (and to $25M for sexual abuse claims) is not merely a financial metric — it is an external, market-based assessment of Acadia's underlying risk profile. Insurance carriers who face commercial incentives to provide coverage have effectively concluded that Acadia's liability risk is uninsurable at commercially reasonable premiums in the $3–15M per-claim range. This is an actuarial and market signal that is entirely independent of DOJ or congressional investigations.

**For transaction purposes:** A buyer is effectively acquiring a company that has been partially priced out of the professional liability insurance market. The structural implication is a permanent elevation in PLGL expense — potentially $50–60M above normalized industry levels annually — that should be capitalized as a perpetual liability in valuation.

---

### ANOMALY 4: The Pattern Question — Systemic vs. Facility-Level Misconduct

Management has consistently characterized problematic conduct as confined to a "handful of underperforming facilities." 
Acadia has identified a "handful of underperforming facilities" where it claims it is working to improve growth. The underperforming facilities tended to be correlated with intense local media coverage, rather than national scrutiny.


**The evidence does not support this characterization.** 
The NYT investigation found that the company deployed strategies pervasive in 12 out of the 19 states where Acadia operates inpatient psychiatric hospitals — exaggerating patients' symptoms, tweaking their medication dosages, arguing that patients aren't well enough to leave because they didn't finish a single meal.


The NYT's CTC investigation covered facilities in Oregon, North Carolina, and elsewhere — not isolated geographic clusters. The Senate Finance Committee's report covered multiple RTFs nationally. The FBI is interviewing former employees in both Georgia and Missouri — two geographically distant states with independent corroboration.

**The evidence is consistent with systemic, corporate-level design rather than facility-level idiosyncrasy.** This is the interpretation most damaging to Acadia's defense in both criminal and civil proceedings.

---

### ANOMALY 5: The "30-day Email Deletion Policy" and Document Retention Risk


In the prior securities litigation, Acadia attempted — and failed — to exclude from trial evidence that the company maintained a 30-day automatic email deletion policy for its U.S. operations.
 If this policy existed through the 2020–2024 class period and through the conduct now under DOJ investigation, it represents:

1. A potential obstruction of justice issue in the context of a grand jury investigation
2. A spoliation inference in civil litigation
3. An aggravating factor that would increase financial penalties in any DOJ resolution

This is a critical diligence item: buyers should confirm whether the email deletion policy has been suspended, when it was suspended, and whether any responsive documents were destroyed after the grand jury subpoena was received in September 2024.

---

### ANOMALY 6: CEO Turnover During Active Investigation


Debra K. Osteen was appointed CEO effective immediately, succeeding Chris Hunter, who departed the Company and its Board of Directors. Board Chairman Reeve Waud stated: "As Acadia continues to take decisive steps to optimize its growth investments and existing portfolio amidst ongoing macro headwinds facing many healthcare providers, the Board believes now is the right time to transition leadership."


CEO transitions during active federal criminal investigations are significant events. Chris Hunter was the CEO who presided over the period covered by the current DOJ investigation and who issued the September 2024 statement characterizing the investigations as routine. His departure may reflect: (a) personal legal exposure, (b) disagreement over cooperation strategy, or (c) genuine strategic redirection. Any of these has material implications for the investigation trajectory and for representations and warranties in a transaction.

---

### ANOMALY 7: The $200M "Embedded EBITDA Upside" vs. Volume Headwinds


Management reiterated the view that Acadia has roughly $200 million of "embedded EBITDA" opportunity within the 3,000 beds added in the 2023–2025 period.


However, 
in November 2024, the company cited media scrutiny as the cause of referral volumes to dip slightly, though volumes began to recover.
 The reputational damage from the NYT series, the FBI investigation disclosure, and the active congressional investigation creates a structural referral headwind — particularly for involuntary commitment referrals from hospital emergency departments (which are a primary growth lever for inpatient psychiatric facilities). The $200M EBITDA embedded upside is predicated on volume growth that may be systematically impaired by the investigation.

**This is a gap between management's characterization of the growth opportunity and what the evidence about referral dynamics suggests.** Any DCF valuation that accepts management's volume ramp assumptions without applying an investigation-related discount is likely to overstate intrinsic value by $500M–$1.5B.
