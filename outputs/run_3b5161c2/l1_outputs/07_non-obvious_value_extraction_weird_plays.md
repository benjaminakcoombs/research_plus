### The Workforce Crisis Creates the Opportunity


As of March 2023, 160 million Americans live in areas with mental health professional shortages, with over 8,000 more professionals needed to ensure an adequate supply.



While some researchers have estimated that the number of psychiatrists will begin to slowly expand in early 2025, they estimate that the shortage may not be completely resolved by 2050, and may still be short by over 17,000 positions.



Psychiatric mental health nurse practitioners (PMHNPs) can be leveraged in place of psychiatrists or used strategically to create a collaborative care team model, helping to meet rising demands and improve access to care. With rising mental health needs and persistent access gaps in psychiatric care, PMHNPs can help organizations strengthen their workforce while improving patient access.


### The HCA/Galen Playbook Applied to Behavioral Health


In 2020, HCA Healthcare expanded its commitment to education by becoming the majority owner of Galen College of Nursing. The partnership with Galen has increased access to education and provided nursing career development opportunities that positively impact patient care.



HCA Healthcare committed $300 million toward education and training efforts, including $200 million to expand HCA's Galen College of Nursing and approximately $136 million to open new HCA Healthcare Centers for Clinical Advancement, designed to bridge the gap between the classroom and the bedside.



More than 3,000 HCA Healthcare colleagues are enrolled at Galen College of Nursing. Since joining HCA Healthcare, Galen College of Nursing has announced 14 new campuses nationwide, with a combined enrollment of more than 12,000 students.


The logic that drove HCA's $300M investment in Galen applies directly to Acadia:

## PLAY #5: EMPLOYER-DIRECT BEHAVIORAL HEALTH CONTRACTING
### *"The Lyra Killer or Lyra's Best Partner — 'Acadia Complete'"*
**Feasibility/Impact Rank: #5 | Revenue Potential: $100–200M/year at Scale | Critical Missing Piece: Outpatient Layer**

---

### The Digital Behavioral Health Gap That Acadia Fills


Lyra Health is the leading provider of Workforce Mental Health benefits, serving more than 20 million people globally through direct employer contracts and more than 100 million through health plan and partner relationships.



About 5–10% of Lyra members need to be referred to higher acuity care.



Carrum Health, which offers value-based specialty care for employers, has teamed up with Lyra Health. The partnership integrates Carrum's specialty care services, including surgical, cancer care, and substance use disorder (SUD) treatment, with Lyra's behavioral health services. SUD patients receive a warm hand-off to Lyra's recovery programs.


This partnership reveals the gap: Lyra, with 20 million covered lives, has *already identified SUD treatment as the key high-acuity care gap in its network* and is solving it by partnering with Carrum. Carrum's network for SUD treatment is thin. **Acadia's 178 CTCs and 262 facilities are a purpose-built SUD/behavioral health network that Lyra is currently solving with a suboptimal partner.**

The natural Lyra + Acadia partnership: Lyra provides the digital front door (app-based screening, virtual therapy, coaching), and Acadia provides the **physical care network for everything Lyra can't manage virtually** — IOPs, residential treatment, inpatient psychiatric care, and methadone maintenance. The referral volume (5–10% of 20M = 1–2M members/year) could generate $200–500M annually in Acadia facility revenue.

### PEPM Model for Direct-to-Employer Contracting


Lyra Health's pricing operates on a custom business-to-business model, where employers pay a per-employee-per-month (PEPM) fee.



According to SHRM, the average cost of a traditional EAP per employee per month is between $0.75 and $1.50.
 Lyra charges significantly more — estimated at $3–15 PEPM depending on services. A full-continuum "Acadia Complete" product — outpatient telehealth through inpatient psychiatric care — could command $8–20 PEPM from self-insured employers with complex, high-acuity employee populations (healthcare companies, law enforcement, first responders, manufacturing with high substance use risk).

**Revenue model for direct employer contracts:**
- 50 employer clients × 10,000 average employees × $10 PEPM = **$60M/year**
- 100 employer clients at scale = **$120M/year**

The critical missing piece: Acadia lacks an outpatient therapy layer. For a PEPM product to work, Acadia needs virtual/outpatient therapy capacity that it currently doesn't own at scale. **Options:** (1) Acquire a telehealth therapy company, (2) partnership with Headspace Health, Spring Health, or BetterHelp as the outpatient layer, (3) build a lightweight telehealth outpatient offering specifically for the employer product.

## PLAY #6: STATE PSYCHIATRIC HOSPITAL MANAGEMENT CONTRACTS
### *"Government's Problem Is Acadia's Revenue — 'Acadia Government Solutions'"*
**Feasibility/Impact Rank: #6 | Revenue: $75–300M/year at Scale | Timeline: 36–60 months | Political Risk: HIGH**

---

### The Market Need Is Real and Growing


State institutions and community hospitals have closed inpatient mental health units, often citing staffing and financial challenges. For-profit companies are now opening psychiatric hospitals to fill the void.


Many states operate psychiatric hospitals under consent decrees, DOJ oversight, or active legislative pressure to improve quality. These states face three choices: (1) invest heavily in improving state-run facilities, (2) close them (reducing capacity), or (3) contract with a private operator for management expertise. Option 3 is the Acadia opportunity.

Acadia's compliance and regulatory infrastructure — forged under DOJ scrutiny, SAMHSA oversight, CMS certification, and Joint Commission review across 40 states — is more relevant to managing state psychiatric hospitals than virtually any competitor. Ironically, the federal investigations that have depressed Acadia's stock have also demonstrated that Acadia has the compliance infrastructure capacity to respond to government oversight at scale.

**Key states with documented state psychiatric hospital capacity/quality crises:** California (multiple consent decrees), New York (ongoing DOJ oversight), Oregon (Measure 110 OUD treatment crisis), Mississippi (longstanding DOJ oversight), Georgia (historical federal oversight).

### Revenue Model and Margin Profile

State psychiatric hospital management contracts typically include:
- **Base management fee:** 6–8% of facility operating budget
- **Performance incentive fees:** 2–4% tied to quality metrics, reducing lengths of stay, reducing seclusion/restraint use
- **Capital advisory fees:** For facility renovation or expansion projects

Average state psychiatric hospital operating budget: $30–80M/year
Management fee at 8%: $2.4–6.4M/year per facility

- 5 state management contracts: **$12–32M/year**
- 15 state management contracts: **$36–96M/year**
- 30 contracts (mature scale): **$72–192M/year**

**The margin advantage:** Government contracts have negligible bad debt, predictable payment, and no commercial payer negotiation complexity. EBITDA margins on management contracts (no capital employed, minimal liability) are 30–40% vs. 15–20% for owned facilities.

### The Political Risk Is Severe

The political opposition to private management of public psychiatric hospitals is real and well-organized. Labor unions (AFSCME, SEIU), patient advocates (National Alliance on Mental Illness), and progressive legislators routinely oppose privatization narratives. **Acadia, specifically, is politically toxic right now:** 
In June 2024, Acadia and fellow for-profit Universal Health Services were found by the Senate Committee on Finance and the Senate Committee on Health, Education, Labor, and Pensions to have endangered the welfare of children in their facilities. The committees' report stated the problems "occur inevitably and by design: they are the direct causal result of a business model that has the incentive to treat children as payouts."


**Mitigation strategy:** Acadia Government Solutions should be structured as a separate entity with its own brand identity, leadership, and governance — explicitly distanced from Acadia's acute inpatient operations and pediatric facilities. The management contract pitch should focus on Acadia's *outpatient* and *CTC* operational expertise (which has a cleaner regulatory record) and avoid positioning around the acute inpatient hospitals.

Sabra's portfolio includes 217 skilled nursing/transitional care facilities, 32 managed senior housing communities, 83 senior housing communities, **16 behavioral health facilities**, and 15 specialty hospitals.


16 behavioral health facilities vs. Acadia's 262. The gap between what Sabra owns and what Acadia operates represents the entire uncapitalized behavioral health real estate market.

Sabra's pullback is not a signal that behavioral health REIT value doesn't exist — it is a signal that **Sabra's opportunistic approach (repurposing under-performing SNFs) has run its course**, and that the next wave of behavioral health real estate value will be found in purpose-built assets owned by an operator-informed REIT that understands the behavioral health operational model.

Medical Properties Trust announced a $950 million investment into a behavioral health platform that includes real estate and an interest in the operating company.


MPT's $950M bet confirms that institutional REIT capital *wants* behavioral health exposure — it just hasn't found the right vehicle. Acadia's real estate portfolio could be that vehicle.

Acadia sells its owned real estate to a newly formed REIT entity (potentially retaining a 20–30% equity stake), leases the properties back under long-term triple-net leases, and the REIT then raises additional capital by acquiring behavioral health real estate from BHG, Crossroads, and other private OTP operators.

**Pro forma REIT capitalization:**

**For Acadia:** Balance sheet liberation of $1.5–2B cash, debt paydown (current long-term debt: 
$2.247 billion as of June 30, 2025
), and retained equity stake in a pure-play behavioral health REIT that appreciates as the asset class matures.

### ANOMALY #2: Acadia Is Actively Lobbying to Protect the CTC Monopoly — While Simultaneously Building the Infrastructure That Would Replace It


Acadia has actively lobbied against a bipartisan proposal in the U.S. Congress to expand access to methadone treatment by allowing opioid use disorder patients to pick up methadone at pharmacies, rather than going to clinics.



In 21 states, at least 50% of all methadone clinics are owned either by private equity firms or by Acadia Healthcare.


The MOTAA legislation would disrupt ~$560M of annual CTC revenue. If MOTAA passes, the CTC network's revenue model changes but its strategic value for the non-obvious plays actually *improves* — daily dispensing visits would be replaced by clinical management visits that are more amenable to research participation.

Acadia is fighting to protect the wrong thing. The CTC network's durable value is not the dispensing monopoly; it's the infrastructure.
