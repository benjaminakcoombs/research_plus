## PART IV: REGULATORY MOAT — COMPLETE ARCHITECTURE

### 4.1 The Full Certification Stack


The regulation preserves states' authority to regulate OTPs. Oversight of OTPs remains a multilateral system involving states, SAMHSA, the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS), and the DEA.


To dispense methadone for OUD, a facility must simultaneously hold:
1. **DEA Schedule II Narcotic Treatment Program (NTP) Registration** — requires physical inspection, secure dispensary, registered medical director, DEA compliance with 21 CFR Part 1300
2. **SAMHSA OTP Provisional Certification** — Form SMA-162, triggers state + DEA concurrent approval requirement; 
provisional certification is granted for up to one year, during which time the OTP must become fully accredited. SAMHSA will not grant provisional certification without state and DEA approvals.

3. **Accreditation by a SAMHSA-approved body** (CARF, Joint Commission, etc.) — must be obtained within the 1-year provisional window; 
certification shall be granted for a term not to exceed 3 years.

4. **State OTP Licensure** from the State Opioid Treatment Authority (SOTA) — varies dramatically by state

And in CON states, additionally:
5. **Certificate of Need approval** from state health planning board

### 4.2 State Moat Map — Specific CON and Moratorium States


Nineteen states and the District of Columbia restrict providers from opening new OTPs in some way: All 20 require a certificate of need. And Indiana limits the number of new facilities that can open. West Virginia is the most restrictive state, with a legal moratorium disallowing new OTPs.


Key states where Acadia operates behind CON moats:
- **North Carolina (10 CTCs)**: CON required; Acadia acquires existing licensed facilities
- **Tennessee (Acadia HQ + regional focus)**: Supplemental payment programs; high Medicaid penetration
- **Virginia, Ohio, Pennsylvania, Indiana**: CON or capacity-limiting frameworks


While revisiting these rules, state policymakers should also change other regulations that limit access to care, such as burdensome staffing ratios that make it hard for OTPs to operate. Colorado removed such requirements, and four new OTPs opened across the state last year, with as many as nine more expected in 2024.
 This shows that when regulatory barriers fall, supply responds quickly — a key risk for the moat thesis in deregulating states.

### 5.3 The Take-Home Dose Revenue Risk — Quantified

From NY State data: 
A full bundle for a week with qualifying services: $240.77 for Downstate NY OTPs. A medication-only rate for take-home weeks: $40.61 for all OTPs.
 This is an **83% revenue haircut** per medication-only week vs. full-bundle week.

If 30% of previously daily-visit patients migrate to 4-week take-home schedules:
- Full-bundle weeks per year: 40 (vs. 52 previously)
- Medication-only weeks per year: 12
- Revenue change: (40 × $240.77) + (12 × $40.61) = $9,638 vs. old 52 × $240.77 = $12,520
- **Revenue impact: -23% per migrated patient**
- System-wide sensitivity: If 30% of 76,000 CTC patients migrate → ~$40–50M revenue reduction annually

This is the single most underappreciated financial risk in the CTC investment case. 
Informants expressed concerns regarding the substantial difference between the full bundled rate and the medication-only rate, reporting that extended take-homes might not be sustainable under the two-tiered model. Informants believed that increased take-home medication had profound implications for the delivery of counseling services, the counselor workforce, and financial viability for OTPs.

### 9.1 OBBBA — Enacted Law, Not Proposal


The One Big Beautiful Bill Act of 2025 (OBBBA) was signed into law on July 4, 2025. The law includes significant funding cuts and policy changes to Medicaid and the Health Insurance Marketplaces.
 
The legislation cuts federal Medicaid funding by 15%, or $1 trillion, over 10 years. The CBO estimates that these cuts will result in 11.8 million individuals directly losing health insurance coverage under Medicaid.


**CTC-specific OBBBA exemption** (the bullish case): 
Work requirements under OBBBA specify mandatory exemptions for individuals with substance use disorder or a "disabling" mental disorder. Acadia management believes a "significant majority" of the behavioral health operator's populations will be exempt.


**OTP patient specific OBBBA protection**: 
In the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, individuals with a diagnosed substance use disorder are exempt from Medicaid requirements.


**But the supplemental payment risk remains real**: 
Acadia Healthcare projects that about $230 million of its revenue comes from supplemental payments. However, these reductions would not be required to be effective until fiscal year 2028.
 Supplemental payments are allocated across all service lines — CTCs benefit but are not the primary recipients (that's the larger-footprint inpatient hospitals).

**Acadia's payer mix context**:

The most strategically significant management statement on CTC disposition from the March 2026 investor conference: 
Osteen reiterated her view that methadone is "the gold standard." She characterized the segment as requiring low capital to grow and being less labor intensive than acute settings, and said the company believes it still has growth potential given ongoing demand. She added that the company would be open to 'another way' to bring value to shareholders, but said management currently likes the business and views it as part of the continuum of care.


"Open to another way" — this is notably stronger language than prior comments about the CTC segment being "part of the continuum." It reads as a board-directed signal that a CTC sale/spinoff is under active consideration as a path to unlock the conglomerate discount.

At $608.9M FY2025 EBITDA × 4.0x net leverage = **~$2.44B net debt** — consistent with the Yahoo Finance figures. 
During 2025, Acadia refinanced its capital structure with a new $1.0 billion senior secured revolving credit facility and debt maturing in 2033.
 The refinancing extended maturities, reducing near-term debt maturity risk.

## PART XII: CONSOLIDATED NOTABLE ANOMALIES AND RISK FLAGS

### 🚨 ANOMALY 1: The $996M Goodwill Impairment Is a Non-Cash Inpatient-Only Event


The non-cash impairment charge included a $996.2 million goodwill impairment charge.
 This is the single largest item driving the apparent GAAP loss. CTCs, built largely through low-premium tuck-in acquisitions (typically $1–2M each), carry minimal goodwill per facility. The impairment is entirely driven by revised long-term value estimates for the inpatient psychiatric hospital segment under the investigation cloud. This charge has no bearing on CTC operational or economic reality.

### 🚨 ANOMALY 2: Take-Home Dose Transition — Revenue Cliff Hidden in Disclosure


The full bundle for a week with qualifying services: $240.77 (Downstate NY); medication-only rate for take-home weeks: $40.61.
 A stable patient on 4-week take-homes generates 83% less revenue per week than a daily dosing patient. Since Acadia began disclosing CTC revenue only in Q4 2025, we cannot determine whether the 5% year-over-year revenue growth (decent) reflects flat patient volume with rising rates, or growing patient volume with partially offsetting take-home migration. Management has provided zero quantitative disclosure on take-home dose penetration rates across their CTC network.

### 🚨 ANOMALY 3: New York Medicaid Policy Change — CTC Exemption Not Fully Confirmed

The NY Medicaid out-of-state patient restriction applies to specialty facilities (eating disorder, SUD residential). 
The approximate 350 basis point headwind to same facility growth is from changes in the New York Medicaid program.
 
This growth is expected to be driven primarily by improved occupancy at ramping facilities, offset in part by an approximate 350 basis point headwind from certain Pennsylvania specialty facilities following changes in New York Medicaid policy regarding the provision of care at out-of-state facilities.
 CTCs operate within their licensed state and do not treat out-of-state patients in the same way — so this headwind should be **minimal for the CTC segment**. But it underscores how Medicaid policy changes in a single state ($25–30M EBITDA impact) can meaningfully affect Acadia's consolidated results.

### 🚨 ANOMALY 4: CEO Confirmed CTC Capital Efficiency — But Used the Language as Defense Against Spinoff


Osteen reiterated methadone is "the gold standard" and characterized the segment as requiring low capital to grow and being less labor intensive than acute settings. She added that the company would be open to "another way" to bring value to shareholders, but said management currently likes the business and views it as part of the continuum of care.
 This is simultaneously the strongest endorsement of the CTC investment thesis from management AND a resistance to separating it. Management's refusal to pursue the obvious value-creation path (CTC spinoff) in the context of a ~$1.34B conglomerate discount is a governance concern that the activist investors have correctly identified.

### 🚨 ANOMALY 5: BayMark On the Market — Second Failed Process Risk


This is the second go-around for Webster Equity Partners. The company previously tried to sell BayMark in 2019 but moved it to a continuation vehicle in 2021.
 BayMark's second sale process is now complicated by: (1) the data breach lawsuits; (2) OBBBA/Medicaid uncertainty; (3) the Congressional investigations into OTP chains generally; (4) the same take-home dose revenue risk. If BayMark fails to sell again or clears at a distressed multiple (below 8x), this would be a negative signal for OTP platform valuations generally.

### 🚨 ANOMALY 6: The MOTAA Remains The Existential Tail Risk


Senator Norcross proposed the Modernizing Opioid Treatment Access Act (MOTAA) to allow board-certified addiction doctors to prescribe methadone outside of the clinic system. Norcross stated: "We must end the monopoly on this life-saving medicine."
 
PE investors have engaged in lobbying efforts to preserve their exclusive rights to dispense methadone for OUD treatment and have opposed the MOTAA.
 With 
the DEA extending telehealth prescribing flexibilities around controlled substances for a fourth time at the end of 2025
, the regulatory direction of travel toward greater flexibility is clear, even if MOTAA itself has not passed. Each incremental flexibility reduces the uniqueness of the OTP dispensing monopoly.

### 🚨 ANOMALY 7: OTP Concentration Data Shows Vulnerability to State Policy Changes


PE firms acquired 67% of the OTP market in the median county with any acquisition, often through multiple acquisitions within the same county.
 High geographic concentration within counties means that a single state's regulatory or Medicaid policy change (as seen with NY) can disproportionately impact clinic networks with dense in-state footprints. Acadia has disclosed no single state represents more than 15% of total revenue, but CTC-specific state concentration is not disclosed.

### 🚨 ANOMALY 8: Acadia's Staffing Strategy Adds New Risk


Acadia Healthcare eliminated several locations in 2025, impacting about 400 jobs.
 Combined with 
the decision to close 5 facilities in 2025, including 4 specialty facilities and 1 acute care hospital
, Acadia is demonstrating portfolio rationalization — closing inpatient/specialty underperformers while growing CTCs. This is directionally correct but creates workforce and brand instability risk in markets where Acadia is a major behavioral health employer.
