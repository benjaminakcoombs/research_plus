### 🔴 FINDING 3: Section 232 Tariff Regime — Alumina NOT Covered, But the Tariff Architecture Creates a Structural Domestic Premium Anyway

**What Round 3 confirmed definitively:**


Since June 2025, the United States has imposed **50% tariffs** on steel, aluminum, and products containing steel and aluminum ("derivatives") from nearly all trading partners.



On June 3, 2025, President Trump issued a Proclamation increasing the Section 232 duties on imports of aluminum and steel from 25% to 50%. The Proclamation states that the previously imposed steel and aluminum tariffs "have not yet enabled these industries to develop and maintain the rates of capacity production utilization that are necessary for the industries' sustained health and for projected national defense needs." This change is set to take effect at 12:01 a.m. on June 4, 2025, and applies to all aluminum and steel articles, as well as derivative articles.


However — and critically — **alumina (HTS 2818.20) is NOT an "aluminum article" under Section 232**. It is a chemical intermediate. The Section 232 structure covers finished aluminum metal and downstream derivatives. The BIS inclusions process that added 400+ product codes in August 2025 covered aluminum *derivative* products — downstream of aluminum metal, not upstream.


On April 30, 2025, BIS published a rule implementing the resulting Section 232 Inclusions Process. Submissions are accepted in two-week windows three times per year. For more information on the requirements for filing, see: Adoption and Procedures of the Section 232 Steel and Aluminum Tariff Inclusions Process. Submitted Section 232 Inclusion Requests are posted publicly on Regulations.gov to Docket BIS-2025-0023.


**The indirect alumina pricing effect is powerful even without a direct tariff:**


Two primary aluminum smelters within the United States have closed since Proclamation 9704 was promulgated. In addition, U.S. primary aluminum production decreased by 30 percent from 2020 to 2024, and U.S. smelter capacity utilization was only 52 percent in 2024. Overcapacity for primary aluminum has harmed downstream aluminum producers, including producers of aluminum extrusions and aluminum sheet. To allow U.S. aluminum producers to restart production and to incentivize new capacity, additional adjustments to Section 232 tariffs on aluminum need to be made, including limiting exemptions and increasing the tariff rate.


The explicit policy objective — restarting U.S. aluminum smelting capacity — requires domestic alumina. The 50% tariff on imported aluminum makes domestically smelted aluminum competitive *only if* the alumina feedstock cost is also competitive. An EGA/Century Inola smelter sourcing alumina at $560/t FOB from Brazil (plus $50–80/t freight to Oklahoma = $610–640/t landed) faces a structural disadvantage vs. a smelter sourcing Brimstone alumina at $480–520/t with zero freight cost. That is a $90–160/t alumina cost advantage — directly translating to $180–310/t advantage in aluminum cost of production (since it takes ~1.93t alumina per tonne of aluminum).

**The "alumina tariff" risk is actually a future upside option:** If the BIS inclusions process eventually adds alumina to Section 232 scope — which is legally possible via Docket BIS-2025-0023 — Brimstone's domestic alumina would receive an immediate price floor at import parity plus tariff. At 25% tariff on imported alumina at $420/t FOB, the domestic price floor becomes $525/t; at 50% tariff, it becomes $630/t. These scenarios are not modeled as base case but represent meaningful upside option value.

**Net model impact:** Confirms the indirect tariff premium mechanism. Upgrades alumina price assumption by $20–30/t over Round 2 base case due to EGA/Century demand confirmation. Identifies alumina Section 232 inclusion as an unpriced upside option worth $10–25M in NPV.

# PART VIII: THE ASTM C150 MOAT — CONFIRMED FINAL ANALYSIS

## What C150 Requires


ASTM C150/C150M covers eight types of portland cement. The cement covered by this specification shall only contain the following ingredients: portland cement clinker; water or calcium sulfate, or both; limestone; processing additions; and air-entraining addition for air-entraining portland cement. Portland cement of each of the eight types must have specific chemical compositions including: aluminum oxide, ferric oxide, magnesium oxide, sulfur trioxide, tricalcium silicate, dicalcium silicate, tricalcium aluminate, and tetracalcium aluminoferrite.


## Amazon Validation — The Key Commercial Stress Test


In August 2025, Brimstone announced that Amazon had signed a commercial agreement to reserve annual volumes of Brimstone's portland cement and SCM from its forthcoming plant. This agreement builds on successful initial testing showing that Brimstone cement performs comparably well with the conventionally produced materials used in Amazon's buildings.


Amazon's construction procurement team validates materials against real structural performance requirements across billions of dollars of construction annually. "Performs comparably well" is engineering procurement language for specification compliance — not a marketing claim.

## Moat Duration Estimate

A competitor seeking to replicate ASTM C150 from a non-limestone feedstock would need: (1) a novel feedstock with correct Ca:Al:Si:Fe stoichiometry, (2) multi-year pilot testing, (3) independent third-party certification across multiple test cycles including three-day heat of hydration testing conducted at least once every six months, plus long-term strength testing at 1, 3, 7, and 28 days, and (4) customer qualification and offtake development. Minimum competitive replication timeline: **5–8 years from feedstock identification**.

## Strategic Value for Infrastructure Buildout


U.S. government agencies, including the U.S. Departments of Homeland Security, Treasury, Energy, and Commerce, have repeatedly recognized alumina as critical to U.S. strategic interests. Under the Trump Administration, the Department of Commerce issued a report highlighting the national security implications of foreign aluminum production.
 ASTM C150-certified cement from domestic sources is the cement specification required for all federally funded infrastructure — the same political tailwind that protects the alumina business also protects the cement business.
