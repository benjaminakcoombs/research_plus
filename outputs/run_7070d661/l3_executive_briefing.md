## 1. SITUATION OVERVIEW

Lunar Energy is a company at war with itself. It has built what may be the most valuable residential VPP software platform in North America — Gridshare, managing 650 MW and 150,000 devices across three continents — and then wrapped it inside a battery hardware business that has deployed 2,000 systems through 40 installers in a single state. The investor narrative says "manufacturing scale-up." The operational reality says "software company with a hardware distribution problem."

**The central tension is this:** Lunar has approximately 11–18 months of cash remaining at current burn ($8–12M/month against ~$100M in estimated reserves post-Series D), and the company's CEO has publicly stated it will "never raise again." [Source: Latitude Media, February 2026] The math does not work. Scaling from 2,000 cumulative units to 100,000 units/year by 2028 requires $230–450M in manufacturing CapEx and working capital that does not exist in the current capital structure. [L1 Agent: Financial Analysis] The DOE Loan Programs Office — the hypothesis for filling this gap — has been effectively gutted by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, with ATVM repealed and Title XVII credit subsidies rescinded. [L2 Deep Dive: DOE LPO Analysis] The company's hidden Series C ($130M, raised late 2023 at what Sunrun's $58.7M impairment charge confirms was a down round) suggests the valuation trajectory is fragile. [L2 Deep Dive: Series C Valuation Forensics; Source: Sunrun 2023 10-K]

**What makes this moment different:** Three structural shifts have converged to create a window that did not exist 18 months ago.

*First*, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act eliminated the Section 25D homeowner tax credit while preserving the Section 48E commercial ITC for battery storage through 2033 — creating a 7-year regulatory moat around the TPO/lease battery model that is Lunar's primary channel. [Source: Public Law 119-21, §70513; IRS Newsroom FAQ, 2025] Solar's 48E expires for new projects after 2027. Battery storage does not. This is the single most underreported regulatory advantage in residential clean energy.

*Second*, Tesla's brand deterioration has opened a once-in-a-decade installer recruitment window. EnergySage reports 68% of homeowners are specifically requesting Powerwall alternatives. [Source: EnergySage 18th Annual Report, 2025] Roth Capital's installer survey shows active defection from Tesla hardware. [Source: Roth Capital Partners Installer Survey, Q1 2025] Yet Lunar does not appear on any major "Tesla alternative" shortlist. FranklinWH — with 3,000+ certified installers versus Lunar's 40 — is capturing the defectors. Every week of delay is market share permanently lost.

*Third*, Lunar's Mexico-based battery module assembly — quietly disclosed on the company's website but absent from "proudly domestic content" marketing — is about to become an existential liability. The domestic content ITC bonus threshold rises from 40% to 55% after 2026. [Source: Treasury Notice 2025-08; RSM Tax Advisory] Mexico-assembled modules will almost certainly fail this threshold, costing Sunrun's TPO channel $2,500 per system in lost bonus credit. Simultaneously, this manufacturing configuration forfeits an estimated $20M/year in Section 45X advanced manufacturing credits at target production volumes. [L2 Deep Dive: 45X/Domestic Content Analysis]

The strategic question is not "What should Lunar build?" — the product is genuinely differentiated. The question is: **Can Lunar convert a 36-month window of regulatory advantage and competitive disruption into durable market position before its capital runs out and the window closes?**

### IDEA 1: THE GEORGIA MANUFACTURING MIGRATION — $76M/YEAR IN CREDITS HIDING IN PLAIN SIGHT

**THE INSIGHT:** Lunar's Suwanee, Georgia inverter assembly already qualifies for Section 45X advanced manufacturing production credits worth $624 per unit ($0.065/watt × 9,600W) — and there is no public evidence the company is claiming them. [Source: IRC §45X(b)(2)(B); L2 Deep Dive: 45X Analysis] Meanwhile, battery module assembly in Mexico categorically disqualifies from both 45X credits ($200/unit at 20 kWh) and will fail the rising domestic content ITC threshold ($2,500/unit value to TPO channel). Relocating module assembly from Mexico to Georgia unlocks a combined per-unit benefit of $3,324 ($624 inverter + $200 module + $2,500 domestic content preservation). At 100,000 units, this is $332M/year in total credit and channel value — the difference between a viable and unviable business model.

**THE EVIDENCE:** The 45X statute explicitly requires US production — no USMCA carve-out exists for Mexico. [Source: 26 U.S.C. §45X(d)(2); McGuireWoods Legal Analysis, 2024] Treasury's October 2024 final regulations confirmed battery module assembly constitutes "substantial transformation" eligible for the credit. [Source: Federal Register, Final 45X Regulations, October 2024] Enphase shipped 1.69 million US-assembled microinverters claiming 45X credits in Q4 2024 alone, establishing the operational precedent. [Source: Enphase Q4 2024 Earnings Release] FranklinWH began US battery pack production in Santa Clara in 2025, demonstrating feasibility for a comparable competitor. [Source: FranklinWH Manufacturing Page, 2025] 45X credits traded at 92–95 cents on the dollar in H1 2024, providing immediate cash realization. [Source: Congressional Research Service, 2024]

**THE PLAY:** (1) Immediately engage tax counsel to confirm whether current Suwanee inverter assembly qualifies for 45X — if yes, file retroactive claims for all units produced to date. Timeline: 30 days. (2) Commission a formal domestic content analysis under Notice 2025-08's distributed BESS safe harbor table. Timeline: 60 days. (3) Evaluate contract manufacturing options in Georgia for module assembly as a faster alternative to captive facility buildout (Enphase's model). Timeline: 90 days for partner identification, 6–12 months to first module off the line. (4) Begin supply chain mapping for non-Chinese LFP cell sources (SK On Georgia H2 2026, Samsung SDI, LG) in preparation for FEOC threshold tightening. CapEx estimate for module relocation: $15–29M. [L2 Deep Dive: 45X Analysis]

**THE MAGNITUDE:** Inverter 45X alone (claimable today): $6.2M/year at 10,000 units, $62.4M/year at 100,000 units. Adding module 45X post-relocation: +$20M/year at 100,000 units. Domestic content bonus preservation: $25M–$250M/year in channel value depending on volume. Cumulative 45X through 2032 phase-out: $400M+. Break-even on relocation investment: 7,400–12,000 units (achievable in first production year).

**THE RISK:** SK On Georgia LFP production doesn't begin until H2 2026 and is substantially committed to Flatiron Energy (7.2 GWh). [Source: SK On Newsroom, 2025] Lunar's 2 GWh/year target demand has no disclosed offtake agreement. Cell supply is the binding constraint on any Georgia module line. Mitigation: dual-source with a second LFP supplier and negotiate SK On allocation terms now, leveraging SK Group's equity stake.

**CONFIDENCE:** High on inverter credit (85%+). High on domestic content risk (85%+). Medium-High on module relocation feasibility (70%). Key uncertainty: cell supply availability post-relocation.

**KEY CITATIONS:** IRC §45X(b)(2)(B); Treasury Final 45X Regulations (Oct 2024); Enphase Q4 2024 Earnings; Notice 2025-08 BESS Safe Harbor Tables.

Lunar's battery-first, solar-agnostic architecture is uniquely positioned. Yet its marketing still leads with "solar battery system." The repositioning to "battery-first home energy system" opens two massive segments: (a) 600,000–900,000 NEM 2.0 solar homeowners in California who can add battery storage while retaining grandfathered rates, and (b) non-solar homeowners wanting backup power and VPP participation.

[Source: CPUC NEM Tariff Decision D.16-01-044, as modified] California has 1.5 million NEM customers. [Source: CPUC, NEM 3.0 Decision, 2022] NEM 2.0 battery attachment rate at original solar install was ~10–15%; the residual retrofit opportunity is 500,000–800,000 homes. Sunrun's storage attachment rate has already risen to 71% for new installs, validating the market pivot. [Source: Sunrun Q4 2025 Earnings Release]

**THE PLAY:** (1) Immediately update all marketing materials to lead with "battery-first home energy system" messaging. (2) Create a "Solar Retrofit" landing page targeting NEM 2.0 homeowners. (3) Negotiate expanded battery-only TPO terms with Sunrun leveraging the 48E differential through 2033. (4) Target the non-solar backup power segment in wildfire/PSPS-affected areas through utility partnerships. Timeline: messaging pivot in 30 days; product/channel adjustments in 90 days.

**THE MAGNITUDE:** NEM 2.0 retrofit TAM (California alone, 5 years): $300M–$2.2B at 5–15% capture rate. National battery-without-new-solar TAM: $1B–$7.5B. The addressable market potentially expands 3–5x from "solar buyers adding battery" to "all homeowners wanting resilience + savings."

**THE RISK:** FEOC compliance is the make-or-break variable. If Lunar's cells are FEOC-contaminated, the 48E ITC advantage disappears entirely. [Source: Baker Botts, Notice 2026-15 Analysis, February 2026] Mitigation: conduct immediate FEOC supply chain audit (see Idea 1).

**CONFIDENCE:** High on regulatory facts (95%+). Medium on market response (55%) — consumer awareness of standalone battery benefits remains low.

**KEY CITATIONS:** Public Law 119-21, §70513(a)(2)(C); IRS FAQ on Clean Energy Credits; CPUC NEM 2.0 Grandfathering Rules; Sunrun Q4 2025 Earnings.

This is not a theoretical risk; it is a compliance question with auditable answers that Lunar's own team should already know. The fact that CEO statements contradict website disclosures suggests either careless messaging or deliberate opacity. Neither is acceptable.

The management team is most likely underestimating the *interaction* among these risks: a cell supply compliance failure triggers ITC recapture → Sunrun stops ordering → Gridshare revenue collapses → cash runway shortens → no ability to raise → forced sale at distressed terms.
