Real-time telemetry must be reported at 2-second intervals. Device-level validation ensures the aggregated output matches actual device performance within ±10%. Qualification tests confirm the aggregation can deliver the energy or reserves it promises.

After the RIOO submission is complete, the QSE representing the load resource must set up Inter-Control Center Communications Protocol (ICCP) telemetry points. Owner or operator changes will follow the same 45-day timeline as submissions to register a new load resource.

## 7. SPECIFIC RESEARCH QUESTIONS — DETAILED FINDINGS

### Q1: ERCOT ADER Technical & Regulatory Requirements

**Registration Process (Step-by-Step):**


In addition to registering with ERCOT, parties looking to create an ADER and participate in the Pilot Project must also register with the PUCT using the registration form provided. Following registration as an ADER, if any changes occur regarding enrolled premises, that ADER participant is required to file an update with the PUCT.


The full registration sequence:
1. **Designate or become a QSE** (or partner with one): 
Designate a QSE responsible for market operations and financial settlement. The QSE Acknowledgement should be submitted to ERCOT before the RE starts the registration process in RIOO. If the QSE is not registered when ERCOT receives the request, the Production Load Date may be delayed.

2. **Prepare "Details of the Aggregation" (DOTA) form**: Lists all premises (ESI IDs) by load zone and DSP
3. **Secure DSP acknowledgment**: 
The DSP reviews the Details of the Aggregation for feasibility of participation on the distribution network. If the DSP has concerns, they will notify the QSE, and may reject premises for reasons of safety, reliability, or regulatory impediments.

4. **Submit RIOO application** with all required forms
5. **Set up ICCP telemetry**: 
After the RIOO submission is complete, the QSE must set up Inter-Control Center Communications Protocol (ICCP) telemetry points for each new load resource.

6. **Telemetry validation**: 
Real-time telemetry must be reported at 2-second intervals. Device-level validation ensures the aggregated output matches actual device performance within ±10%.

7. **Qualification testing**: 
Qualification tests confirm the aggregation can deliver the energy or reserves it promises.

8. **Dual PUCT/ERCOT registration**: File registration form with PUCT Project No. 53911

**Minimum aggregation size:** 
The aggregation must have the capability to provide at least 100 kW of response (demand response capability plus injection capability), and each Premise must provide 1 MW or less of response.


**Participation models available (Phase 3):**
- 
ALR model: Dispatchable, follows 5-minute basepoints like a generator or controllable load. NCLR model: Simpler, non-dispatchable, responds only when deployed for contingency reserves. This dual-path approach in Phase 3 allows both sophisticated and simpler aggregations to participate.


**Services eligible:** Non-Spinning Reserve (Non-Spin) and ERCOT Contingency Reserve Service (ECRS) — 
ADERs can earn from energy market participation and capacity payments in Non-Spin and ECRS markets.


**What Tesla and Sonnen did to qualify:**
- Tesla served as its own QSE (Tesla Electric is a licensed REP and QSE), enrolled its existing Powerwall customers, provided ESI IDs to ERCOT, and went through ICCP telemetry setup and qualification testing. Tesla's internal VPP software (distinct from Gridshare) directly reported to ERCOT's systems.
- 
Sunrun enhanced benefits through its partnership with Tesla Electric. Sunrun customers who enroll are participating in ERCOT's ADER pilot program.
 In Phase 1, the ADER required all premises to share the same LSE (REP). Tesla Electric served as both REP and QSE — a fully vertically integrated structure.
- Phase 3's NCLR model now allows greater separation: 
the NCLR model will allow third-party QSEs to aggregate >100 kW premises, even if the LSE is represented by a different QSE.


**Realistic timeline to first dispatch:** 4–6 months from initiating QSE partnership discussions to first ERCOT dispatch, assuming no DSP rejections.

### Q5: Landed Cost in Texas vs. Competitors

Lunar assembles systems in Suwanee, Georgia — approximately 800–900 miles from Houston and 1,000–1,100 miles from DFW or Austin. This is meaningfully closer than a California-based competitor (2,000+ miles) and represents a real logistics cost advantage.

**Estimated Texas landed cost analysis:**

| Cost Component | Estimate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Suwanee → Houston freight (per unit) | $150–$300 | Less-than-truckload; full truckload would be ~$80–$120/unit |
| Permitting (varies by city) | $200–$800 | Houston and DFW generally $200–$400; Austin $400–$800 |
| Inspection fees | $100–$300 | Texas has no statewide residential solar inspection mandate; city/county varies |
| Interconnection (TDSP application) | $50–$200 | CenterPoint, Oncor, AEP processes; relatively streamlined vs. California |
| Installer labor (Texas market rate) | $2,000–$4,000 | Lower than CA ($3,500–$6,000) due to labor costs |
| **Total installed premium vs. California** | **-$2,000 to -$4,000** | Texas has lower labor, permitting, and interconnection costs |


Lunar Energy's systems are priced at about a 10% premium to Tesla's Powerwall batteries
, so if Powerwall installs in Texas run $12,000–$16,000 (lower labor/permitting than CA), Lunar's Texas installed cost would be approximately **$13,200–$17,600** — before any ITC or domestic content bonuses. With the 30% ITC + 10% domestic content bonus = 40% credit, net consumer cost on a lease/PPA basis becomes dramatically more competitive.
