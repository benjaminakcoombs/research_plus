## 1. EVIDENCE FOR THE HYPOTHESIS

### A. The Sunrun Impairment: The Single Most Important Publicly Available Data Point

This is the smoking gun that has not been prominently analyzed in existing coverage:


During the fourth quarter of 2023, Sunrun recorded a $58.7 million non-cash charge related to its investment in Lunar Energy.
 
Net loss attributable to common stockholders was $1,604.5 million for the full year 2023. Pro-forma to exclude the non-cash goodwill impairment charge in Q3, and non-cash loss on an equity investment in Lunar Energy in Q4, non-GAAP net loss would have been $387.8 million or $1.79 per diluted share.


This is not a routine mark-to-market adjustment — Lunar Energy is a **private company** whose equity is only written down when management determines there has been an "other than temporary" impairment or when a new observable transaction provides a lower price reference. Under ASC 321 (equity method investments) or ASC 323, Sunrun would have needed a specific trigger — almost certainly **a new financing round priced below Sunrun's carrying value** — to take this charge in Q4 2023. The $58.7M write-down is directly consistent with a Series C closing in late 2023 at a materially lower price per share than the Sunrun/SK rounds.

Now translate the impairment into implied valuation: 
Sunrun currently owns approximately 37% of Lunar Energy and has preferential access to the technology being developed.
 If Sunrun wrote down $58.7M, and it held ~37% of Lunar, its total carrying value pre-write-down was something on the order of its pro-rata share of total equity value. Working backward: if Sunrun owned 37% and wrote down $58.7M, that implies a total equity impairment across all shareholders of approximately **$158M** (= $58.7M / 0.37). This is rough — the 37% figure pre-dates subsequent dilution, and the write-down may reflect only Sunrun's marked position on the specific shares it holds under its cost method — but the order of magnitude is consistent with a valuation compression from a high-water mark of well above $800M to something materially lower. (Note: Sunrun may have held fewer shares than 37% by the time of the write-down due to earlier dilution from Activate's Series C; the actual implied write-down would then be even larger on a per-company basis.)

### B. Round Structure Anomaly: Non-Disclosure of a $130M Round


The startup shared it raised a previously unannounced $130 million Series C and a $102 million Series D. The Series C was led by Activate Capital, while the Series D was led by B Capital and Prelude Ventures.



On February 4, 2026, Lunar Energy announced it raised $102 million in an oversubscribed Series D round led by B Capital and Prelude Ventures, following a previously unannounced Series C financing of $130 million led by Activate Capital, resulting in a total of $232 million in new funding.


This non-disclosure is extraordinarily unusual. $130M venture rounds are almost universally announced within weeks of closing, for the following reasons: (1) they signal competitive strength to customers and channel partners; (2) they aid talent recruitment; (3) they provide anchor references for future rounds; (4) they trigger LP reporting requirements for the lead investor. The only structural reasons to **not** announce include: (a) the valuation is embarrassing relative to prior rounds and would trigger customer/partner concern; (b) the terms include provisions that are opaque to the public (e.g., tranched disbursements conditional on milestones); or (c) the company is navigating a sensitive governance restructuring that public disclosure would complicate.

### C. The Valuation Language: CEO's Deliberate Hedging

The CEO's choice to describe Lunar as "approaching a $1 billion valuation" at the time of the **Series D** is highly informative. 
Led by former Tesla Energy executive, Kunal Girotra, Lunar was founded in August 2020 and has raised $300 million in funding over two rounds by Sunrun, the nation's leading residential solar and battery storage provider, and South Korea's SK Group.
 Given that those two early rounds alone totaled $300M — and a standard 1x+ liquidation preference structure would require at least $300M in returns to prior preferred before common holders see anything — describing the *combined* post-Series D company as "approaching $1 billion" at a total capital raised of $532M implies a post-money valuation only modestly above $800-900M. This is **far below** what a well-performing company with $300M in early strategic backing would command. It strongly suggests the Series C was a significant valuation reset.

### D. Activate Capital's Typical Check Size vs. Its Lunar Commitment


Activate typically invests $10–$30M into companies at the Series B to pre-IPO stages, focusing on investing in technology companies at the inflection point of their growth.


Activate's standard check is $10–$30M. The Lunar Series C commitment of **$130M** — leading the round — is 4–13x their typical investment. This suggests either: (a) Activate took an unusually large concentration bet (rare, and typically done only with commensurately attractive terms like a ratchet, multiple, or a very low entry valuation); or (b) a significant portion of the $130M was co-invested alongside other parties whose names have not been disclosed. Notably, only Activate Capital is named as the Series C lead; the press releases do not name other Series C investors separately from the combined Series C/D investor list. This is itself suggestive of a structured or tranched deal where protective provisions substitute for normal co-investor syndication.

### E. Macro Context: Cleantech Valuation Compression in 2023–2024


Last year showed signs of a slowdown in the sector, with median EV/Revenue multiple for Energy Storage & Battery Tech only reaching 2.1x in Q4 2023.
 
Over the past two years, the rapid expansion of cleantech manufacturing capacity has outpaced actual market demand. This imbalance triggered sharp price declines in 2023 and 2024, placing significant pressure on manufacturers' financial performance and balance sheets.


The 2023–2024 period was characterised by significant valuation compression across cleantech hardware companies. Any company raising capital in that environment — especially one that had taken large strategic dollars at peak 2022 valuations — would be exposed to meaningful markdown.

### F. Sunrun's Continued Participation as a Signal of Anti-Dilution Rights


The Series C and Series D funding rounds also included participation from DCVC, Piva Capital, Leitmotif, Sunrun, Itochu Corporation, and Q Capital Partners.


Sunrun participated in both the Series C and Series D despite having just written down its investment. This is *not* behavior typical of a passive strategic investor who simply trusts management. It strongly suggests Sunrun had contractual reasons to participate — most likely **pay-to-play provisions** requiring participation to maintain anti-dilution protections, or a **pro-rata right** that Sunrun exercised to avoid further dilution. Either mechanism is consistent with the hypothesis that the Series C triggered anti-dilution protections that Sunrun then needed to manage actively.

## 3. COMPARABLE EXAMPLES

### A. Northvolt (Battery Manufacturing, Down Round Dynamics)
The pattern of a large strategic round followed by a much smaller growth equity round with protective provisions, combined with delayed disclosure, was visible at Northvolt before its eventual restructuring. 
Swedish electric-vehicle battery manufacturer Northvolt raised $1.1 billion in a convertible note in July.
 Convertible structures (rather than priced rounds) are common tools when a down round is undesirable from a PR standpoint but economically necessary — Lunar's structure, if tranched or carrying ratchets, would be functionally similar.

### B. Cleantech "Zombie Unicorn" Pattern

Success for a venture-capital-funded startup means an exit through a profitable acquisition or a public listing, but those wins have been few and far between for cleantech. Even mature companies can eventually stumble. Amid high interest rates, frozen M&A markets, and a serious slowdown in public offerings, the opportunity for financial success has shrunk for startups.
 Multiple cleantech companies raised large strategic rounds in 2021–2022 at peak valuations, then raised growth equity in 2023–2024 at materially lower prices without publicly announcing the down round. The Lunar pattern fits squarely within this cohort.

### C. Anti-Dilution Mechanics in Practice

Broad-based weighted average adjustments dominate modern venture capital agreements due to their balanced approach. Full ratchet protections, though rare, are sometimes seen in high-risk deals but are generally avoided due to their harsh impact on founders and employees. In 2023, 60% of venture capital transactions used weighted average anti-dilution provisions, highlighting their status as the industry standard for balancing investor protection and founder equity.


If the Series C *did* trigger anti-dilution protections for Sunrun/SK, the statistically likely mechanism is **broad-based weighted average**, not full ratchet. 
The bottom line is that under most circumstances full ratchet anti-dilution protection will be completely waived, while weighted average is likely to be accepted.
 The reason Sunrun re-participated in both the Series C and Series D rather than simply triggering a full ratchet is itself consistent with weighted average protection, which is softer and allows co-existence with future investors.

### What Would Be Required to Confirm

To fully confirm the hypothesis, a diligent investigator would need access to the following — **none of which is currently publicly available**:

1. **Delaware Secretary of State:** Certificate of Incorporation and all Amendments/Restated Certificates for Lunar Energy Inc., which would show: (a) the name and authorized share count of each series of preferred stock; (b) the "original issue price" and "conversion price" for each series; and (c) any anti-dilution adjustment provisions written into the certificate. These are *not* available via the public ICIS search interface, but can be obtained by **ordering certified copies directly from the Delaware Division of Corporations** (nominal fee per page; ~5–10 business day turnaround).

2. **SEC EDGAR Form D:** Lunar Energy would have been required to file a Form D within 15 days of the first sale of securities in the Series C. 
SEC requires the notice to be filed by companies that have sold securities without registration under the Securities Act of 1933 in an offering based on a claim of exemption under Rule 504 or 506 of Regulation D. Commission rules require the notice to be filed within 15 days after the first sale of securities in the offering.
 A Form D discloses: the total offering amount, the date of first sale, the type of securities offered (e.g., "Equity"), and the names and CRD numbers of associated broker-dealers. It does **not** disclose price per share or pre-money valuation. However, the **date of first sale** would confirm when the Series C closed, and any amendments would signal tranching.

3. **Sunrun SEC Filings (10-K, 10-Q):** Sunrun's annual reports will contain additional details on its accounting treatment of the Lunar Energy write-down under ASC 321/ASC 323. Specifically, the notes to financial statements may disclose the method of accounting (cost method vs. equity method), the *trigger* for the impairment (observable price change, i.e., a new financing event), and the carrying value after write-down. This would narrow the valuation range considerably. Sunrun's most recent 10-K should be reviewed carefully.

4. **Secondary Market Platforms:** Forge Global, EquityZen, and Hiive occasionally post indicative bids/asks for private company shares, which would imply a price per share. No such listing for Lunar Energy was found in this search pass; direct outreach to these platforms by a licensed broker would be required.

5. **Delaware UCC Filings:** If Activate Capital or any lender took a security interest in Lunar Energy assets (e.g., as part of a venture debt tranche accompanying the Series C), a UCC-1 financing statement would be filed with the Delaware Secretary of State and is publicly searchable, revealing the secured party and collateral description.
