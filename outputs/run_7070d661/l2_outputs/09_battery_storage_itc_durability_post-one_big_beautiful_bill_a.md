## 1. EVIDENCE FOR THE HYPOTHESIS

### 1A. Legislative Confirmation: The Battery ITC/Solar ITC Asymmetry Is Real and Enacted Law

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) was signed into law on July 4, 2025 (Public Law 119-21). The differential treatment of battery storage vs. solar under Section 48E is unambiguous.

**Solar under 48E:** 
The technology-neutral investment tax credit under Section 48E and the technology-neutral electricity production tax credit under Section 45Y will no longer apply to solar and wind facilities placed in service after December 31, 2027.
 The only exception: 
wind and solar projects that begin construction on or before the date that is 12 months following the date of the Act's enactment (July 4, 2026) — under current guidance and subject to any changes to such guidance, wind and solar projects that begin construction in 2025 would need to be placed in service before the end of 2029.


**Battery storage under 48E:** Explicitly carved out. 
This phase-out only applies to wind and solar facilities, and the Act makes clear that the phase-out does not apply to the ITC with respect to energy storage technology, including energy storage technology that is placed in service at a wind or solar facility.


The storage credit timeline is confirmed by multiple authoritative sources: 
The phase-out of the Section 48E ITC for all other qualified facilities — including energy storage facilities — begins in 2034. Facilities that begin construction in 2034 will be eligible for 75% of the full credit amount, dropping to 50% for construction beginning in 2035, and phasing out entirely for projects beginning in 2036 or later.


In plain English: 
Solar and wind projects must now be completed by December 31, 2027, unless they meet safe harbor rules before July 4, 2026. Energy storage projects are eligible through 2033, with a ramp-down thereafter.


**On the Section 25D homeowner credit (the residential direct-ownership credit):** Also confirmed eliminated. 
P.L. 119-21, the FY2025 reconciliation law commonly known as the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, repealed the residential clean energy credit (RCEC) for expenditures made after calendar year 2025.
 This is directly confirmed by the IRS: 
the Residential Clean Energy Credit (25D) is not allowed for any expenditures made after December 31, 2025.


**Critical nuance on owned batteries under 25D:** The 25D elimination applies equally to homeowner-purchased battery systems. 
For owned battery storage systems (those paid for through cash or financed), homeowners would claim the Section 25D tax credit. Like solar installations, owned battery storage projects will no longer qualify for tax credits starting in 2026.
 This is a crucial refinement: **the battery ITC advantage under 48E accrues exclusively to commercial/TPO structures, not to homeowners who buy batteries outright.**

### 1B. TPO/Lease Structures: The 48E ITC Is the Surviving Vehicle


Early versions of the One Big Beautiful Bill closed off the 48E tax credit for residential solar and battery systems, but the Senate removed this language for the final version that became law.
 This was a pivotal legislative moment: 
in a minor win, the Senate also removed language that blocked access to the 48E tax credit for residential leases and PPAs. Trump signed this version into law on July 4, and residential leases and PPAs will have access to the 48E tax credit through the end of 2027.


For battery storage deployed through TPO (Sunrun's model): 
the "placed in service" deadline does not apply to energy storage projects in the changes to 48E, so this leaves great avenues for Virtual Power Plant Projects (VPP) or storage as a part of new solar or wind projects.


**The structural upshot** for Lunar/Sunrun: 
Third-party owned (TPO) systems — such as solar leases and power purchase agreements (PPAs) — still qualify for the 30% credit through Section 48E, provided they're placed in service by the end of 2027. This can make TPO the preferred option for some homeowners to access solar incentives after 2025.
 But for **battery storage specifically**, TPO systems can access the 48E ITC through 2033, without any placed-in-service deadline equivalent to solar.

**ITC credit stack:** 
Projects receive a 6% base rate that can be increased to 30% if they meet Prevailing Wage and Apprenticeships requirements (or exemption). In addition, the ITC rate can increase to 70% if it qualifies for adders such as Domestic Content, Energy Community, and/or Low-Income Communities.

## 2. EVIDENCE AGAINST THE HYPOTHESIS

### 2A. The Critical FEOC Problem: Chinese Battery Supply Chain Risk

This is the single most serious structural threat to the hypothesis, and it is **underweighted** in the original framing.


Passed on July 4, 2025, the legislation largely spares battery energy storage systems (BESS) from the credit reduction that wind and solar projects received. However, BESS developers do need to comply with heightened Foreign Entity of Concern (FEOC) material assistance requirements compared to other types of projects.


The problem: 
Chinese companies are particularly relevant because of the outsized role they play in the battery supply chain. Chinese companies control the processing of most critical battery minerals and the manufacturing of key components. For instance, giants like CATL and BYD alone command over half of the global battery market. Furthermore, China processes the vast majority of the world's battery-grade lithium, cobalt, and graphite, making it nearly impossible to build a battery today without touching the Chinese supply chain.


**FEOC thresholds for storage specifically are more demanding than for solar:** 
For storage projects, 55% of the value must be non-FEOC for projects beginning construction in 2026, increasing to 75% for projects starting in 2030.
 This compares to 40% for solar in 2026.

**The 10-year recapture risk for 48E is severe:** 
In the case of 48E specifically, if any of these payments are made within 10 years after the facility is placed into service, the taxpayer would be forced to pay back the entire value of the previously claimed tax credit.
 This recapture risk — if a battery system's supply chain later becomes FEOC-contaminated through replacement parts, warranty repairs, or sub-component sourcing — creates ongoing compliance exposure that is not present in solar panel installations (which have no moving parts).


The main concern is that Chinese-sourced batteries and sub-components could trigger tax credit clawbacks due to routine maintenance and warranty repairs after December 31, 2025.


**Critical implication for Lunar Energy specifically:** Lunar's battery hardware sourcing must be scrupulously documented. If Lunar's cells or sub-components come from CATL, BYD, Gotion, or other FEOC-linked suppliers, the 48E ITC availability for Sunrun's TPO deployments could be compromised — destroying the core financial thesis.

### 2B. The 25D Elimination Hurts Owned Battery Sales Too, Shrinking One Market Segment

While the hypothesis focuses on TPO, it's worth noting that the 25D elimination removes the 30% credit for homeowners who would buy (not lease) a standalone battery. This eliminates a customer segment and could actually dampen total battery market growth, partly offsetting the TPO advantage. 
The overarching effect of losing the solar tax credit will be that it will make solar energy less affordable for homeowners to purchase. We may see more companies turning to TPO products, but especially in recent months/years, some large TPO providers have been experiencing financial trouble.


### 2C. Solar TPO (48E for Solar) Survives Through 2027 — The Asymmetry Window Is Shorter Than It Appears

The hypothesis describes a "7+ year" battery-storage ITC advantage. But this requires careful unpacking. Solar TPO (48E for solar leases/PPAs) remains available through end-2027 — not already expired. 
The business-claimed 48E tax credit for residential solar leases, PPAs, and prepaid solar products remains in effect through the end of 2027.
 So the **net asymmetry period** where batteries-only get ITC but solar does not begins in 2028, not today. The period from now (early 2026) through end-2027 is a **transition zone** where both solar and storage TPO qualify, though storage qualifies further into the future.

### 2D. The Battery-Only Value Proposition Still Requires Customer Education

For non-solar homeowners, a standalone battery system (Segment A) offers backup power and VPP earnings but no solar bill reduction. The payback period for a standalone battery in a non-solar home is materially longer than for solar+storage. Without a dramatic increase in VPP compensation rates, the economics remain challenging outside of high-reliability-risk markets (wildfire zones, areas with PSPS events, etc.).

### 2E. TPO Company Financial Stress Is a Distribution Risk

Sunrun itself carries enormous financial leverage: 
net debt/EBITDA ~78× and interest coverage below 1× expose Sunrun to refinancing risks if credit markets tighten; large scheduled debt repayments could strain cash flow if cash-generation targets fall short.
 A Sunrun financial distress scenario — similar to Sunnova's collapse — would be existential for Lunar's primary distribution channel.

### 2F. The "Placed-in-Service" Cliff for Storage at Solar Facilities Needs Clarification

One open question: 
with the solar 48E commercial ITC expiring after December 31, 2027, the question arises of whether batteries paired with solar installed in 2028 and beyond can qualify for the 48E commercial ITC. Current speculation is a simple "no." There appears to be a direct attack on solar and wind, and all indications point to solar and wind costs being ineligible for the 48E ITC after the deadline, even when paired with storage technology.
 This matters for hybrid solar+battery deployments post-2027.
