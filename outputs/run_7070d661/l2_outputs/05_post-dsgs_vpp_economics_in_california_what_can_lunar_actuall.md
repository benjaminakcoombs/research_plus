# ANALYTICAL REPORT: LUNAR ENERGY'S $464/YEAR VPP EARNINGS CLAIM — POST-DSGS VALIDITY ASSESSMENT

**Prepared:** February 28, 2026
**Classification:** Regulatory / Commercial Risk Analysis

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The hypothesis is **well-supported and highly credible**. Lunar Energy's $464/year VPP earnings figure was a legitimate 2025 historical average — but it was generated largely under a program (DSGS) that California has effectively defunded for 2026. The replacement programs available to California residential battery owners collectively offer materially lower compensation, likely in the range of **$100–$250/year per device** under utility ELRP alone, potentially rising to **$150–$350** for customers who can access CCA programs or a truncated DSGS with severely constrained enrollment. The gap between the marketed figure and 2026 forward-looking reality is material, likely **40–65%**, and Lunar's use of this figure in its February 2026 Series D announcement — without DSGS defunding disclosure — raises serious regulatory, legal, and channel trust concerns.

## 1. EVIDENCE FOR THE HYPOTHESIS

### 1A. DSGS Is Effectively Terminated for 2026


California lawmakers stripped all funding from the state's Demand Side Grid Support (DSGS) program. Instead of expanding it, legislators cut its budget and deferred key decisions on future funding. The program will likely be unfunded in 2026, and battery resources will be "mothballed."



The 2025 California legislative session ended in disappointment for VPP proponents, as Governor Gavin Newsom vetoed several VPP-related bills and lawmakers didn't approve new funding for the existing program.



This leaves DSGS with a dwindling pool of previously committed funding that is very likely to be depleted this year. The remaining budget stands at about $64 million. DSGS would need at least $75 million more to continue operating in 2026.


Critically, the CEC program administrator signaled a survival strategy but not restoration: 
Program staff anticipates having sufficient funding for the 2025 program season incentive payout. Staff expects to propose continuation of the DSGS Program for the 2026 program season with modifications to align with the funding level projected to be available after the 2025 incentive payout. A workshop will be announced at a later date to present potential changes to the DSGS Program.
 This suggests a drastically scaled-down, not fully funded, 2026 iteration — if any.

### 1B. The $464 Figure Is a 2025 Historical Average, Not a 2026 Forecast


Last year, Lunar's AI-driven software earned customers an average of $464 by participating in a VPP program, and saved customers an additional $338 compared to a standard home battery operating mode.



Customers earned US$464, on average, by participating in VPP programmes in 2025.


The figure is unambiguously backward-looking — a 2025 performance metric that Lunar is projecting forward as a value proposition for 2026 enrollees, in a fundamentally changed policy environment.


Lunar's systems are primarily deployed through residential leases, meaning homeowners pay no upfront cost. The company's claim that customers earned an average of $464 last year from VPP participation is a crucial metric — it demonstrates the value proposition that makes this leasing model work.
 This makes the integrity of the figure doubly important: it is load-bearing for the financial architecture of Lunar's business model, not merely marketing copy.

### 1C. DSGS Was the Dominant Revenue Source Behind the $464 Figure


During the July 2025 mass VPP test, more than 88% of the megawatts came from home batteries formally enrolled in the DSGS program. Under DSGS, residential battery owners in California can be compensated for providing grid services — in the form of either demand reduction or output from onsite generation — during extreme events, to reduce the risk of rotating power outages.



One of DSGS's signature success stories is its program option for behind-the-meter batteries participating in VPP fleets. Launched just over two years ago in August 2023, the DSGS battery VPP already has over 720 megawatts of customer battery capacity enrolled.


This enrollment dominance by DSGS — at over 720 MW — dwarfs replacement programs, which are far smaller in scale and lower in payment rates.

### 1D. Replacement Programs Pay Materially Less

**PG&E Emergency Load Reduction Program (ELRP):**


ELRP participants earn $2 per kilowatt-hour for credited load reduction. For example, a homeowner with two IQ Battery 10s who selects a 20% Backup Reserve can contribute up to 16 kWh during an ELRP event.


But event frequency is the critical constraint: 
ELRP events last from one to three hours, and will be scheduled between 4 p.m. and 9 p.m. on days they occur. Between 20 and 60 event hours will be scheduled during each ELRP season.


Enphase, one of the largest ELRP aggregators, gives a frank earnings estimate: 
Many participants can expect to earn between $100 and $250 per year, though the amount you earn may differ.


**Residential Power Saver Rewards (ELRP A.6) — the residential variant — pays even less:**


Residential customers participating in Power Saver Rewards (ELRP A.6) are compensated at a prefixed compensation rate of $1/kWh for voluntary incremental load reduction. The residential Power Saver Rewards will sunset at the conclusion of the 2025 ELRP program year.


The residential component of the main IOU ELRP program is itself expiring.

**ELRP is also inherently unpredictable:** 
One element of ELRP that has allowed aggregators to scale participation is the requirement for IOUs to dispatch the program a minimum of 20 hours per season. The minimum dispatch hours are important because they allow an aggregator to present potential customers with some minimum expectation of compensation for enrolling in the VPP. Absent the minimum dispatch hours, there would be no way to estimate potential earnings, since ELRP is only dispatched during CAISO emergency events, which are unpredictable and do not always occur each year.


**DSGS Enphase earnings benchmark (for context):**

Practically speaking, NEM 1.0 and NEM 2.0 customers can expect to earn approximately $150–$200/year as part of DSGS.
 This was the *DSGS* rate — which is now the endangered/defunded program. ELRP earnings would be lower.

### 1E. Sunrun's Own VPP Offer to Customers Is ~$150/Battery


Sunrun had enrolled more than 56,000 customers with solar-battery systems to participate in California VPP programs as of May, the majority of them in DSGS. The company offered participants up to $150 per battery enrolled in the 2025 season.


Sunrun is Lunar's largest distribution partner and Gridshare customer. If Sunrun's own VPP offer to customers — under what was then the operational DSGS — was capped at $150/battery, the claim that Lunar customers earned $464 requires either: (a) Lunar's AI optimization is dramatically superior, (b) the figure reflects a select, higher-performing subsample, or (c) significant portions came from non-DSGS programs that may or may not be replicable. In any case, Sunrun's disclosed customer-facing figure ($150) is a strong floor reference and is itself derived from the now-defunded DSGS.

### 1F. Newsom Vetoed VPP-Supporting Legislation, Closing the Legislative Path


Governor Newsom vetoed three bills that aimed to boost the use of virtual power plants: AB 44, AB 740, and SB 541, which were passed by large majorities in the state legislature.



Edson Perez of Advanced Energy United called the vetoes "missed opportunities to save billions in energy costs by leveraging technologies all around us in our homes, garages and on our roofs," adding that "this policy whiplash undermines confidence across the sector, discourages the deployment of cost-saving technologies and drives away investments."


### 1G. No Successor Program Has Been Enacted


Since lawmakers did not act on the DSGS program during this year's session, Advanced Energy United said "the pressure is now on for the next session to produce meaningful results and close the policy gaps necessary to keep energy affordable and flexible enough to meet future needs."


The 2026 California legislative session began in January 2026. No DSGS successor bill has been enacted as of the date of this report (February 28, 2026). The CEC's own program FAQ signals only a possible, scaled-down continuation — not restoration to 2025 levels.

### 1H. Industry Warnings About Policy Whiplash and Investor/Installer Confidence Risk


Advanced Energy United stated that California will miss out on billions of savings, discourage private investment, and reduce grid efficiency as a result of the vetoes.



The decision to not renew DSGS risks ending the program altogether and may mean losing more than $200 million in energy cost savings for Californians, while extending the state's dependence on peaker plants to meet emergency power needs.

## 2. EVIDENCE AGAINST THE HYPOTHESIS (Mitigating Factors)

### 2A. Lunar Has a Diversified VPP Portfolio Beyond DSGS


Lunar's software now controls close to 150,000 devices in homes, including those of Sunrun customers participating in VPP programs in California, Hawaii, New England, and Puerto Rico. Lunar Energy plans to expand its VPP services this year, partnering with several of California's community choice aggregators and retail electricity providers working in competitive markets.


This geographic diversification means the $464 average may already incorporate earnings from non-California markets (e.g., Hawaii, New England, Puerto Rico) where program structures and compensation rates differ. If the $464 figure already reflects a multi-market average where DSGS is not the sole contributor, the post-DSGS hit to new 2026 California enrollees could be mitigated by national expansion.

### 2B. Lunar Is Actively Partnering with CCAs as DSGS Replacements


Peninsula Clean Energy (PCE) and Silicon Valley Clean Energy (SVCE) are jointly launching demand flexibility efforts, including a Distributed Energy Resource Management System (DERMS). The platform will enable participating customers to automatically shift daily electricity use — earning them direct bill savings, while also helping to reduce grid costs. PCE and SVCE have contracted with Lunar Energy for its Gridshare DERMS platform to connect, control and optimize devices.



PCE and SVCE aim to aggregate up to 25 megawatts of dispatchable capacity over the next five years from customer-sited resources via numerous programs and initiatives.


This is a real, active CCA partnership — but it is (a) small (25 MW target over 5 years), (b) nascent, and (c) yields unknown per-device compensation rates. It is not an adequate substitute for 720 MW of DSGS capacity.

### 2C. DSGS May Continue in a Scaled-Down Form in 2026

The CEC's own program administrator (Olivine) has signaled potential continuation: 
Program staff anticipates having sufficient funding for the 2025 program season incentive payout. In addition, staff expects to propose continuation of the DSGS Program for the 2026 program season with modifications to align with the funding level projected to be available after the 2025 incentive payout. A workshop will be announced at a later date to present potential changes.


If residual DSGS funding covers a smaller enrollment pool in 2026, Lunar's existing enrolled customers (prior to new entrants) could still earn some DSGS compensation — but the first-come, first-served nature of DSGS enrollment and the exhaustion of funds means new 2026 enrollees almost certainly cannot count on DSGS participation.

### 2D. CCA VPP Programs Are Growing and Could Partially Fill the Gap


Lunar Gridshare is used by California Community Choice Aggregators to develop and deploy new distributed power programs across the state.



Sunrun's earnings announcement touts 4 GWh of networked energy storage capacity across 18 VPP programs — enough to dispatch 425 MW of power to the grid at times of peak demand.
 This signals an aggressive expansion of the VPP ecosystem beyond DSGS.

### 2E. Tesla Confirms DSGS Was Still the Gold Standard as of Mid-2025


Tesla's VPP page states that on average, DSGS VPP customers can earn $350 per Powerwall during the summer. Program payments are determined based on monthly average performance multiplied by a monthly capacity payment.


The Tesla figure ($350/Powerwall from DSGS) provides a mid-market data point below Lunar's claimed $464. This $350 figure — from the program that is now defunded — is itself now the "historical" benchmark, not a forward-looking number.

### 2F. The $464 Figure Was Not Fabricated — It Was a Real 2025 Outcome


The company says Lunar System customers earned an average of $464 last year by participating in VPP programs, a figure it claims is $338 more than for those who own systems with "standard home battery operating mode(s)."


The figure represents genuine 2025 performance, with Lunar's AI optimization demonstrably superior to standard battery behavior. This is not a fabricated claim about past performance — the misrepresentation risk lies in prospective marketing to future enrollees and investors without DSGS defunding disclosure.

### 2G. Texas and Other State Markets May Sustain National Average VPP Earnings


By year's end, Lunar expects to have customers in several more states, such as Texas, which has become a major target for VPPs from major energy retailers, smart-thermostat providers, solar-battery installers, and manufacturers of standalone backup batteries.



Sunrun CEO Mary Powell highlighted the company's VPP partnerships with NRG and Tesla in Texas.


If Texas compensation rates are comparable to or higher than California's DSGS rates, Lunar's national average VPP figure could remain close to $464 even as California earnings drop — but this would not accurately represent what a California-specific new enrollee should expect.

### 3D. Solar Industry FTC Precedent — Savings Claim Enforcement


The FTC requires that savings claims be substantiated by competent and reliable evidence and accurately represent the typical consumer experience. This standard creates specific requirements for solar savings projections: Savings projections must use assumptions representative of the typical customer, not best-case scenarios. Using optimal installation conditions, highest utility rates, and maximum production estimates creates misleading projections.



State net metering changes require territory-specific savings claim accuracy. California NEM 3.0 reduced export compensation 75%, making historical savings claims misleading for new installations.
 This NEM 3.0 precedent is directly analogous to the DSGS defunding situation: a program change that renders historical earnings figures materially misleading for future enrollees.


In a prior FTC action, a court found that a company violated the FTC Act by deceptively selling products by misrepresenting their energy-savings capabilities.


### 3E. FTC-California Joint Action Against Ygrene — Energy Financing Misrepresentation


In July 2025, the FTC issued more than $2.9 million in payments to consumers harmed by Ygrene's false claims. The FTC and the State of California took action against home improvement financing provider Ygrene Energy Fund.


The Ygrene case involved misrepresentation of financial benefits to homeowners in energy improvement financing — a directly analogous fact pattern to continuing to market VPP earnings figures from a defunct program to homeowners enrolling in battery leases.

### 3F. California's Own CPUC Anti-Greenwashing Posture


The CPUC insisted that its NEM 3.0 decision to slash compensation for rooftop solar came with a silver lining — a financial incentive for households to add batteries. The CPUC stressed that this policy could help supply the state with far more batteries that can store excess solar power.



For an industry laying off thousands of employees in the face of steep dropoffs in customer demand for rooftop solar, the CPUC's latest decision adds insult to injury. Brad Heavner of CALSSA called it "a bait-and-switch, through and through."


The CPUC has a demonstrated appetite for scrutinizing cases where consumers are promised energy compensation benefits that were then reduced by regulatory action. "Bait-and-switch" characterizations of policy-dependent earnings promises are already in the regulatory lexicon.

## 6. REGULATORY AND LEGAL RISK ASSESSMENT

### 6A. Federal: FTC Act Section 5 — Unfair or Deceptive Acts or Practices


The FTC requires that savings claims be substantiated by competent and reliable evidence and accurately represent the typical consumer experience. Savings projections must use assumptions representative of the typical customer, not best-case scenarios.


Lunar's $464 figure, presented without temporal or programmatic qualification in February 2026 marketing and the Series D announcement, fails this standard under the following theory:

1. The figure was derived primarily from DSGS, which is defunded for 2026.
2. New 2026 enrollees — the intended audience for the marketing — cannot realistically expect to earn $464 from the same program mix.
3. The figure is presented as a forward value proposition, not a clearly labeled historical metric.

### 6B. California Consumers Legal Remedies Act (CLRA) / UCL

The California Unfair Competition Law (UCL, Bus. & Prof. Code §17200) prohibits "unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent" business acts. The Consumers Legal Remedies Act (CLRA) prohibits misleading representations about the "benefits" of goods or services. Both statutes have been applied to solar industry earnings misrepresentations.

The specific risk here: Lunar's battery is sold/leased to homeowners partly on the strength of the $464 VPP earnings promise. If enrollees in 2026 earn $150–$250 instead, and if Lunar has not disclosed the DSGS defunding, this creates a viable CLRA/UCL theory for affected homeowners — especially those in lease arrangements where the VPP earnings were part of the economic justification communicated by installers.

### 6C. Channel/Installer Risk: "Bait-and-Switch" Exposure

Installer trust is Lunar's most critical near-term commercial vulnerability. Installers pitch the $802/year combined proposition to homeowners during the sales process. If customers who signed up in early 2026 earn $150–$250 in VPP payments rather than $464, installers will face customer complaints and potential fraud liability for misrepresenting the economics. This creates a channel confidence problem at the precise moment Lunar is scaling from ~2,000 installed systems to a 20,000-unit/year target by end of 2026.


Lunar has installed systems in about 2,000 homes and businesses in California. The firm plans to increase its manufacturing capacity from about 10,000 battery systems per year today to 20,000 per year by the end of 2026 and to 100,000 per year by the end of 2028.


At this growth rate, tens of thousands of new homeowners will be sold systems in 2026 based on marketing materials that include the $464 figure. The systemic scale of potential misrepresentation grows with every installation.

### 6D. Investor/Securities Risk


Lunar's funding consists of $102 million from a Series D round led by B Capital and Prelude Ventures, alongside a previously unannounced $130 million Series C led by Activate Capital.


The $464 figure appeared prominently in the February 4, 2026 Series D press release. Investors who committed capital based on forward-looking VPP economics embedded in this figure — without adequate disclosure of DSGS defunding — may have securities misrepresentation claims if VPP revenues underperform materially. This risk is amplified if Lunar's financial model for its lease portfolio relies on VPP revenue to make the unit economics work.


The financial model adds another layer of complexity. Lunar's systems are primarily deployed through residential leases, meaning homeowners pay no upfront cost. The claim that customers earned an average of $464 last year from VPP participation is a crucial metric — it demonstrates the value proposition that makes this leasing model work.

## 8. STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS (For Disclosure Purposes Only)

While this report does not offer legal advice, the following corrective actions would materially reduce Lunar's regulatory, legal, and channel exposure:

1. **Immediately add temporal and programmatic qualification to all $464 references:** Label it clearly as "customers earned an average of $464 in 2025 under California's DSGS program, which has since been defunded. 2026 earnings will depend on replacement programs and are projected to be lower." This single disclosure substantially reduces FTC Section 5 and CLRA exposure.

2. **Publish a California-specific VPP earnings projection for 2026:** Model the realistic expected earnings under (a) ELRP only, (b) ELRP + CCA programs, and (c) any residual DSGS allocation. Share this with installers so they can accurately represent the economics in customer conversations.

3. **Accelerate CCA program partnerships:** 
Lunar's Gridshare platform will enable customers to draw new benefits from their devices, and PCE and SVCE aim to aggregate up to 25 megawatts of dispatchable capacity over the next five years.
 Accelerating this to a two-year timeline would more rapidly fill the DSGS gap with real compensation.

4. **Proactively support the 2026 California legislative session:** The pressure is now on the 2026 session to restore DSGS funding. 
Advanced Energy United said "the pressure is now on for the next session to produce meaningful results and close the policy gaps."
 Lunar, as a direct financial beneficiary, should be visible in Sacramento advocacy efforts. Doing so also demonstrates good faith to regulators.

5. **Reframe the value proposition around AI-optimized bill savings ($338):** This figure is independently defensible and is not contingent on DSGS. A shift in marketing emphasis from VPP earnings to bill optimization reduces dependence on program-contingent promises.
