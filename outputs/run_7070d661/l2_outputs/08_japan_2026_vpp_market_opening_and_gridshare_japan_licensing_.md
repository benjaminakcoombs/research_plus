## 1. EVIDENCE FOR THE HYPOTHESIS

ITOCHU Corporation confirmed in December 2025 that it reached an agreement regarding the participation of five new shareholders in its subsidiary GridShare Japan Corporation. The five participating companies are OMRON Social Solutions Co., Ltd., Kyushu Electric Power Co., Inc., Chubu Electric Power Miraiz Co., Inc., Tokyu Land Corporation, and Lunar Energy Inc. (U.S.). Together with these partners, ITOCHU aims to accelerate the expansion of GSJ's distributed energy platform service.


This is a high-confidence, primary-source confirmation of the December 2025 restructuring. Critically, 
Lunar Energy's latest investment was in GridShare Japan as part of a Corporate Minority stake, confirmed on November 28, 2025.


**Structural implication:** Lunar Energy Inc. (the US parent) holds the equity stake, while Lunar Energy Limited (the UK subsidiary, formerly Moixa) is the IP owner and technology licensor. These are two distinct legal entities. The licensing flow runs from Lunar UK → GSJ; the equity upside accrues to Lunar US via the GSJ stake. This bifurcation is analytically important: Lunar UK's licensing revenue and Lunar US's equity returns are separate value streams.


In 2018, ITOCHU acquired the exclusive domestic sales rights in Japan for the "gridshare" service through its investment in Moixa Technology, Inc. (currently Lunar Energy Limited, "Lunar UK"). In the same year, ITOCHU established GSJ and launched the sales of the Smart Star residential energy storage system.



Gridshare is described as "an AI-powered distributed energy platform developed by Lunar UK, consisting of software and a database that connect to and optimally control and operate geographically dispersed batteries, water heaters, EVs, and other devices via a network."


This confirms Lunar UK as the IP originator — meaning any licensing fees flow from GSJ back to Lunar UK, and the December 2025 restructuring did not extinguish this relationship. It deepened it.

### 1b. The 2026 Balancing Market Opening Is Structural and Confirmed — and GSJ/Gridshare Is Explicitly Positioned for It


Starting in 2026, residential distributed energy resources, such as those enabled by Gridshare, will be eligible to participate in Japan's transmission-level capacity market and balancing markets, unlocking new VPP revenue opportunities and accelerating the transition to a more distributed, flexible grid.
 This is from Lunar Energy's own press release in mid-2025, confirming METI's policy intent.


Starting in April 2026, the balancing market will undergo structural reforms aimed at improving liquidity and operational efficiency. Expanded participation means low-voltage resources such as household batteries and EVs will be allowed to join the market, provided device-level measurement is in place. This opens the door for distributed energy resources to play a bigger role in grid stability.



The push is reinforced by Tokyo's 2025 regulation requiring solar panels on new homes, and the launch of virtual power plant (VPP) programs — slated to begin in fiscal 2026 — that will let households sell surplus energy to the grid.


### 1c. GSJ/Gridshare Has Active Commercial Operations With Major Utilities — Not Just Trials


More than 12,000 TEPCO EP customer batteries are already on the Gridshare platform. Together with ITOCHU and Gridshare Japan, Lunar has delivered successful VPP trials to TEPCO EP, which helped set the stage for this commercial rollout, and a similar service for Tohoku Electric Power.


This is significant: Gridshare's TEPCO relationship has already transitioned from pilot to commercial deployment.

The Lunar team delivered six concurrent trials over an 8-month period with TEPCO, Chubu Electric, Kyushu Electric, Tohoku Electric, and Shizen Energy, as well as C&I aggregator Eneres. The trials aggregated over 700 ITOCHU Smart Star batteries into 8 VPPs dispatched by Gridshare for grid services.

On May 9, 2024, METI selected 13 battery aggregators under a JPY 9 billion (USD 58 million) subsidy program to support the integration of distributed storage into the national grid. The initiative targets over 1.1 GWh of aggregated battery capacity from residential, EV, and commercial sources, including major participants like ENERES, Next Kraftwerke Toshiba, and Mitsubishi Electric.



For the scheme 'Support for the introduction of energy storage systems for home, commercial and industrial use,' the Japanese government allocated around JPY 9 billion (US$57.48 million) from the FY2023 supplementary budget. The idea is that behind-the-meter (BTM) distributed batteries installed at homes, commercial premises and industrial facilities can be used to adjust supply and demand on the electricity grid.

Even after procurement volume reductions mid-year, BESS units continued settling at an average of JPY 10.84 per ΔkW/30mins in April 2025.

Even at 10–20% capacity factor utilization in balancing markets, a 70 MW fleet could generate JPY 1.3–2.7 billion/year (~$8–17M) in gross balancing market revenue — **before** operator fees, costs, and customer revenue shares.


METI's October 2025 proposal would reduce the price cap for premium balancing products from JPY 19.51 to JPY 7.21 per ΔkW/30min — a reduction of approximately 63% — from April 2026 onward.
 This is a critical downside risk (addressed in Section 2).

In the fourth auction held in FY2024 (for actual delivery in FY2028), the contracted supply capacity totalled 166.21 GW. The total contracted cost reached JPY 1.8506 trillion — up 41% from the third auction and a record high.


For the LTDA (long-term mechanism relevant to new BESS): 
The cap for batteries is 42,000–57,000 yen ($321–435) per kilowatt per year — almost four to six times higher than the UK's latest auction clearing price.
 However, residential aggregated resources in the capacity market (through the standard annual auction) would receive clearing prices closer to the ¥14,000–¥15,000/kW/year range observed historically, not the LTDA caps.

**Revenue model sketch (illustrative):** 70 MW fleet × ¥14,000/kW/year = ¥980M/year (~$6.3M) in gross capacity market revenue — entirely at the GSJ/fleet operator level, before any licensing fee extraction by Lunar UK.

### 1h. Japan's Residential Battery TAM Is Large and Growing Rapidly


Home lithium-ion battery systems in Japan generated USD 278.5 million in 2023 and could surge to USD 2.15 billion by 2030 — a CAGR of 33.9%.



In 2024, more than 3 million residential solar systems had been installed in Japan, paving the way for a substantial rise in demand for home battery storage.

A fleet currently at 35,000 units, in a market heading toward hundreds of thousands of annual installations, positions GSJ for fleet growth that would compound licensing revenue.

What can be inferred structurally: ITOCHU's original licensing deal was negotiated in 2018 when the fleet was zero, the VPP market was pre-commercial, and Japan's balancing market did not exist. If the licensing fee was set as a fixed annual fee or a low per-unit fee at that time and has not been renegotiated for value-sharing upon market entry, the hypothesis of underpricing is structurally plausible — but unverified.

### 2b. Residential DER Participation in Capacity Markets Faces Structural Hurdles


These batteries are currently unable to access grid services markets, which are limited to high-voltage assets.
 While the 2026 reforms change this in principle, the technical and regulatory pathway for low-voltage residential aggregation to qualify for capacity market auctions remains complex. 
Expanded participation of low-voltage resources such as household batteries and EVs in the balancing market requires device-level measurement to be in place.
 Device-level metering at 35,000 locations is a non-trivial implementation requirement.


The increased minimum capacity requirements in the LTDA may reduce participation by developers of smaller projects. The increased minimum capacity requirement has made it impossible for BESS projects with capacity of less than 30MW that did not succeed in the first auction to participate in the second.
 Residential aggregators must demonstrate that their aggregated fleet meets minimum capacity and reliability thresholds, creating compliance complexity.

### 2c. The Proposed 63% Balancing Market Price Cap Reduction Is a Major Headwind


From April 2026, METI's proposal would reduce the price cap for premium balancing products from JPY 19.51 to JPY 7.21 per ΔkW/30min — a reduction of approximately 63%. This harmonizes the cap with lower-value products, effectively removing the premium previously assigned to faster-response products.



The proposed price cap reduction may pose a challenge to BESS project economics in Japan, to the extent projects' economic viability depends on a premium in the fast-response balancing market.



METI's System Review Working Group meetings in October and December 2025 revealed opposition from market participants, who questioned both the necessity and prudence of the proposed reductions.
 The outcome is uncertain, but if implemented, balancing market revenue projections must be revised downward by roughly 50–60%.


These revisions may indicate a trend toward METI/OCCTO de-emphasizing fast reserve arbitrage BESS systems, and promoting the adoption of long-duration BESS providing stable long-term supply in the capacity market.
 Residential batteries (typically 9.8 kWh at 2 kW) are inherently short-duration assets, potentially better suited to balancing markets than long-duration capacity auctions — but the price cap cut directly targets their most lucrative market.

### 2d. LTDA 6-Hour Duration Requirement Excludes Residential Batteries


The draft LTDA Guidelines state that BESS systems would be required to meet a minimum six-hour continuous operation requirement.
 ITOCHU Smart Star batteries are 9.8 kWh at ~2 kW continuous — approximately 4.9 hours of discharge duration. They do not meet the 6-hour LTDA threshold, meaning they are categorically excluded from the highest-paying long-term capacity contracts.


In the upcoming third round of LTDA auctions, the minimum discharge duration requirement will rise from 3 hours to 6 hours.
 Residential aggregators will be limited to the standard annual capacity market, where clearing prices are substantially lower than LTDA awards.

### 2e. Lunar's Control Over Revenue Is Structurally Limited

The December 2025 restructuring explicitly positions Lunar as a **minority investor in GSJ** — not the operator. Commercial decisions, pricing of aggregation services, customer acquisition strategy, and revenue-sharing with battery owners are all determined by GSJ's management and board (controlled by ITOCHU as the majority stakeholder, with utilities as co-investors). Lunar UK's licensing revenue flows upward through a contractual mechanism that Lunar UK does not control commercially. If GSJ management chooses conservative market entry, if ITOCHU's board prioritizes customer relationship preservation over aggressive balancing market dispatch, or if licensing terms are renegotiated in ways unfavorable to Lunar UK, Lunar's realized revenue could fall well below the modeled potential.

### 2f. Customer Acquisition for VPP Has Proven Difficult in Japan


Despite a lot of interest in VPPs over several years, customer acquisition has proven difficult in Japan as it has in other markets.


While Gridshare manages 35,000 batteries, not all of these owners will consent to VPP dispatch, particularly for new balancing market programs. Opt-in rates, customer compensation structures, and battery owner willingness to accept dispatch instructions during extreme weather events (when reserves are most valuable) will constrain the effective capacity available for market participation.

### 2g. Competitor Activity in Japan's Residential VPP Space Is Growing


In September 2024, TEPCO, Tokyo Gas, and ENERES launched a pilot project integrating Kyocera's Enerezza home battery systems into Japan's power supply-demand adjustment framework, aggregating residential battery storage units for remote operation and dispatch. This marks a significant move in Japan's residential storage market, with major utilities formally recognizing home batteries as assets for grid management.



The commercial DR service launched in 2023 includes competing battery manufacturers Smart Solar Corporation, Nichicon Corporation, and Omron Social Solutions alongside GSJ
 — demonstrating that GSJ does not have a monopoly even within the Shizen Energy platform ecosystem.


The collaboration between Fujitsu and AutoGrid Systems to bring AutoGrid's VPP solution to Japan exemplifies the market's international competition dynamics.



METI and Mitsui have run a project to create a VPP of aggregated behind-the-meter storage using technology from US provider Stem Inc.
 Multiple well-capitalized international software players are targeting Japan's residential VPP market.
