## 1. EVIDENCE FOR THE HYPOTHESIS

### A. The Central Chemistry-Geography Mismatch Is Confirmed

The hypothesis's core premise — that SK On Georgia does not currently produce LFP cells — is **fully confirmed** by multiple independent sources. 
SK On will initiate supply of LFP batteries beginning in H2 2026 at SK Battery America (SKBA) in Commerce, Georgia, by converting part of its NMC EV battery production lines.
 This is explicit in SK On's own newsroom. 
The South Korean manufacturer will repurpose a portion of its electric vehicle battery production line at its Georgia plant to produce LFP stationary energy storage systems.
 As of this analysis (February 2026), commercial LFP production has not yet commenced at SKBA. Lunar's system, however, has been shipping since at least 2024. 
The batteries started shipping in California last year and come integrated with the company's Gridshare software.


This creates a hard chronological impossibility: **Lunar cannot currently be sourcing LFP cells from SK On Georgia**, because SK On Georgia has not yet produced LFP cells commercially.

### B. Lunar's Own Website Admits a Dual-Geography Cell Source


Lunar's website states: "We source battery cells for the Lunar System from the United States and Europe and we make our battery module just over the border in Mexico." The system is designed and engineered in Mountain View, California, and most non-battery components (inverter, Bridge, Maximizers) are manufactured in Suwanee, Georgia.


This is a **direct admission** that cells come from Europe — not exclusively the United States. Yet, in a contemporaneous media interview: 
CEO Kunal Girotra told Latitude Media that "Lunar Energy only contracts with battery manufacturers in the U.S.," framing this as the basis for IRA tax credit and domestic content bonus eligibility.


These two statements are **mutually inconsistent**. Cells sourced from Europe cannot be simultaneously sourced only from U.S. manufacturers, unless Girotra's claim is interpreted as referring to the contract counterparty's legal domicile (SK Group, a Korean conglomerate with U.S. entities) rather than the physical manufacturing origin. That interpretation, if adopted for IRA purposes, would be legally aggressive and potentially incorrect under domestic content rules.

### C. SK On Hungary Produces NMC Pouch Cells — Not LFP Prismatic

A critical finding undermining the "SK On Hungary LFP" scenario: 
SK On Hungary specializes in high-energy-density pouch cells for electric vehicles, featuring nickel-rich cathode materials for high energy density, specifically third-generation pouch cells with energy densities exceeding previous generations, incorporating nickel-rich cathodes.
 The Hungary facilities are NMC-focused EV pouch cell plants, not LFP prismatic cell plants. This is corroborated by industry chemistry analysis: 
SK On has specialized in pouch cells with NMC chemistry, and only as of late 2021 began developing LFP batteries for electric vehicles.



As part of the SK Group, the company has grown up exclusively with pouch cells — and more specifically with high-nickel cell chemistries. It was not until 2023 that the company presented its first prismatic cell at InterBattery and began production later that year.


This means: **SK On Hungary's current production is NMC pouch cells, not LFP prismatic cells.** If Lunar's system genuinely uses LFP prismatic cells and those cells come from SK On Hungary, that is technically implausible without an undisclosed LFP production line at one of the Hungarian facilities. The "Europe" language in Lunar's disclosure therefore points either to (a) NMC pouch cells from Hungary being used but marketed as LFP, (b) an unrelated third-party European LFP supplier, or (c) cells sourced from South Korea (SK On's home country) where LFP development is ongoing.

### D. SK On's LFP Capacity Is Almost Entirely Committed to Flatiron

Even for the post-H2 2026 period when SK On Georgia begins LFP production: 
SK On signed a multi-year BESS supply deal with Flatiron Energy, able to supply up to 7.2 GWh of BESS through 2030, valued at approximately $1.5 billion — the first major supply contract focused exclusively on stationary storage batteries since SK On's spin-off. To fulfill the contract, SK On will repurpose part of its EV battery production lines at SKBA in Commerce, Georgia.



Additionally, SK On has obtained the Right of First Offer from Flatiron for projects totaling 6.2 GWh, which are being developed in Massachusetts and other regions of the United States through 2030.


The Flatiron contract alone represents up to 7.2 GWh over four years — approximately 1.8 GWh/year. At Lunar's target of 100,000 units/year × ~20 kWh/unit = **~2 GWh/year**, Lunar's demand would essentially require *all* of SK On Georgia's nascent LFP capacity that is not already committed to Flatiron. There is **zero public evidence** of a separate Lunar Energy-SK On LFP cell supply agreement. The publicly disclosed SK-Lunar relationship is framed as an investor/strategic partner relationship, not a specific cell offtake contract: 
"SK plays partnership and supply, Sunrun plays partnership and demand," but how those partnerships may translate into active business relationships remains to be seen.


### E. No Publicly Disclosed Formal Cell Supply Agreement Exists

Despite extensive searching of SK Innovation/SK On investor materials, press releases, and trade media, **no formal cell supply agreement** between SK On and Lunar Energy has been publicly disclosed with volume, pricing, chemistry specification, or geographic origin details. The Flatiron deal — *SK On's first major ESS supply contract* — was described as a landmark. 
Valued at approximately $1.5 billion, the Flatiron agreement represents SK On's first major supply contract focused exclusively on stationary storage batteries since its spin-off from SK Innovation in 2021.
 This framing makes it inconceivable that a prior undisclosed Lunar supply agreement for LFP cells exists at any meaningful scale.

### F. The "United States and Europe" Disclosure Is a Red Flag for Domestic Content Claims

Lunar's CEO claims IRA domestic content bonus eligibility. 
To qualify for the domestic content credit, all steel or iron used in a structural function must generally take place in the U.S., and for manufactured products, a minimum percentage of the total cost must be mined, produced or manufactured in the U.S.
 Battery cells are the highest-cost component of any BESS. 
In many systems, cells themselves can represent 40–60% of the total cost.
 If cells are manufactured in Hungary (or South Korea), the domestic content percentage math is likely to **fail the threshold**, invalidating the domestic content bonus claim.

## 2. EVIDENCE AGAINST THE HYPOTHESIS

### A. CBInsights Description Suggests LFP Chemistry — But Is Unreliable


According to CB Insights, "the company manufactures its own lithium-iron-phosphate battery cells, which offer longer lifespans and improved safety characteristics compared to the nickel-manganese-cobalt chemistry used in many electric vehicles and competing home batteries."
 However, this description is factually incorrect (Lunar does not manufacture its own cells — it assembles modules from purchased cells), raising questions about the reliability of this source. It nonetheless corroborates the LFP chemistry claim from a secondary source.

### B. SK Group Is a Strategic Investor and Partner — Creating a Presumption of Supply


Since its quiet founding in 2020, Lunar Energy has raised two investment rounds led by Sunrun and SK Group.
 SK Group's equity stake creates a strong commercial incentive to supply Lunar. The strategic partnership could theoretically involve SK On Korea supplying cells that are not yet publicly disclosed. SK On has stated its LFP development ambitions: 
SK On is developing high-performance LFP batteries with high energy density and fast charging speed, based on its technological capabilities in high energy density NCM batteries.


### C. The "United States" Portion of the Claim Could Be Partially Accurate for Future Supply

Girotra's claim about U.S.-only contracts might be prospectively accurate: once SK On Georgia LFP production begins in H2 2026, Lunar could transition to a U.S.-sourced supply. The current "Europe" language may reflect a transitional period. 
Lunar is also investing in manufacturing capacity, with plans to open a domestic battery assembly facility in the southeastern United States by late 2026.
 This Southeast U.S. expansion could be timed to coincide with SK On Georgia LFP availability.

### D. A Modest Current Demand Level May Be Sourceable From Transitional SK On Supply


The company has installed its systems in about 2,000 homes and businesses in California, and plans to increase manufacturing capacity from about 10,000 battery systems per year today to 20,000 per year by end of 2026.
 At 10,000 units × 20 kWh = 200 MWh/year, the current demand is micro-scale relative to any major manufacturer's output. This volume could theoretically be sourced from small LFP prismatic cell lots from emerging European producers or even via spot procurement channels not tied to a major offtake agreement with SK On.

### E. No Direct Evidence of CATL Technology Licensing at SK On

There is no confirmed evidence that SK On's LFP technology is CATL-licensed, which would be the primary FEOC trigger for a Korean-produced cell. 
SK On signed an MOU with South Korean company L&F, a cathode materials producer, for supplying LFP cathode materials.
 L&F is a South Korean company (not Chinese), suggesting SK On is pursuing a Korean domestic LFP cathode supply chain that would not create FEOC exposure from that specific input. However, this MOU was signed in mid-2025 and does not resolve questions about what cathode sources are used in any current SK On LFP cell production.
