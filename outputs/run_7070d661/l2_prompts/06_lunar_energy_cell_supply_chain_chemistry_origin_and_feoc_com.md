You are a battery supply chain analyst and trade compliance investigator examining the origin and chemistry of Lunar Energy's battery cells, which appear to have significant factual inconsistencies in public disclosures.

CONTEXT:
Lunar Energy markets a residential battery system using what it describes as LFP (lithium iron phosphate) chemistry. The company identifies SK Group as its cell supplier and assembles battery modules "just over the border in Mexico," with system integration in Suwanee, Georgia. However, critical inconsistencies exist:

- SK On's two Georgia factories (Commerce, GA) produce NMC (nickel manganese cobalt) pouch cells for Ford F-150 Lightning and Hyundai EVs — not LFP prismatic cells for stationary storage.
- SK On's LFP cell production was announced to begin in H2 2026, primarily committed to Flatiron Energy Storage's 7.2 GWh offtake contract.
- Lunar's website states cells come from "the United States and Europe," while CEO Kunal Girotra has said the company "only contracts with battery manufacturers in the US." These statements appear mutually inconsistent.
- SK On does produce batteries at facilities in Hungary (Europe) and South Korea.
- At Lunar's current estimated demand (~200 MWh/year for ~2,000 units), they are a micro-customer with minimal pricing leverage.

The IRA's Foreign Entity of Concern (FEOC) provisions, which take effect for battery storage ITC eligibility, require that battery components not be manufactured by or contain critical minerals extracted/processed by FEOC entities (primarily Chinese companies). FEOC compliance is essential for ITC qualification.

HYPOTHESIS TO INVESTIGATE:
Lunar's current LFP cells are most likely sourced from either SK On's Hungary facility, an undisclosed non-SK supplier, or possibly a non-LFP chemistry — each scenario creating different risks around FEOC compliance, domestic content qualification, and supply continuity. At the 100,000 unit/year target (requiring ~2 GWh/year of cells), Lunar would need approximately 9% of SK On Georgia's total LFP nameplate capacity — a non-trivial allocation with no public evidence of being contracted.
