### D) Debt Capacity Analysis

#### Current Debt Position

No publicly disclosed venture debt, equipment financing, or credit facilities. Given the company's stage and investor quality, it is likely operating on equity alone. Wilson Sonsini as counsel suggests a sophisticated capital structure, but no debt instrument has been announced.

#### Available Debt Instruments

**Venture Debt ($20–50M):**
- **Available?** Yes, almost certainly. Post-Series D with $100M+ in cash, lenders like Hercules Capital, TriplePoint, or Western Technology Investment would offer 20–35% of the last equity round ($20–35M).
- **Cost:** Prime + 400–600 bps (approximately 8–11% all-in), with 1–2% in warrants.
- **Value:** Extending runway by 2–3 months at $9M/month net burn; modest benefit.
- **Risk:** Covenants triggered by missed revenue milestones could accelerate repayment at the worst moment.

**Equipment Financing (Sale-Leaseback):**
- Manufacturing equipment in Georgia and Washington could support $10–25M in secured financing.
- **Strategic benefit:** Frees capital for working capital while locking in equipment costs.
- **Structurally attractive** given the IRA investment tax credit eligibility.

**IRA Section 48E Investment Tax Credit:**

To support the economics of leasing arrangements, leasing companies look for products eligible for the Section 48E investment tax credit (ITC) and domestic content bonus, which require products not manufactured using material assistance from "foreign entities of concern" (FEOC). Lunar VP Ed Gunn noted the company is "proudly domestic content," meaning its products qualify as non-FEOC and can secure the ITC and domestic content incentives for third-party owners.


This is **potentially Lunar's most underutilized financial asset.** The Section 48E ITC provides a 30% credit (plus 10% domestic content adder = 40% total) to third-party system owners (e.g., Sunrun). If Sunrun deploys 10,000 Lunar systems at $15K ASP = $150M of systems, the ITC value is $60M. Lunar doesn't capture this directly, but it **dramatically reduces Sunrun's cost of capital** for Lunar hardware, creating a structural pricing advantage over FEOC-sourced competitors.

**IRA Section 45X Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit:**

The 45X credit provides $10 per kWh for modules with a capacity greater than 7 kWh, or $45 per kWh if the module doesn't use battery cells. The credit is available from 2023 to 2032 but phases down over time: 75% of its value in 2030, 50% in 2031, and 25% in 2032.



To qualify for 45X credits, manufacturers must produce components considered "advanced energy property," such as solar and wind components, battery storage, inverters, and certain critical minerals.


**Lunar's 45X eligibility is a potentially enormous and underappreciated cash flow source.** At $10/kWh for assembled modules ≥7 kWh (Lunar makes 15 kWh and 30 kWh packs):
- 15 kWh module: $150 credit per unit
- 30 kWh module: $300 credit per unit
- At 20,000 units/year (2026 target, blended), assuming 60/40 mix: **~$4.8M in annual 45X credits in 2026**
- At 100,000 units/year (2028 target): **~$24M/year in 45X credits**

These credits are **transferable** under the IRA, meaning Lunar can sell them for cash immediately rather than waiting for taxable income. At 100K units, this is a $24M per year non-dilutive cash flow source. **This may be the most important financial engineering opportunity Lunar is not adequately discussing publicly.**

**DOE Loan Programs Office:**
Highly applicable. Lunar's domestic manufacturing focus, grid resilience mission, and scale targets align perfectly with DOE LPO's Title XVII program (Innovative Energy Projects) and ATVM (Advanced Technology Vehicles Manufacturing, which has been expanded to include battery storage). DOE loans carry ~2–3% interest rates vs. 9–11% venture debt, representing a 6–8% annual savings on $100–200M of potential capacity. **If Lunar has not applied, this is a material oversight.** Peer company Redwood Materials secured $2B in DOE LPO financing; Form Energy received $30M in ARPA-E support.

#### Public Comparables Analysis

The **lesson for Lunar:** IPO into a bull cycle, not a trough.

**Stem Inc. (STEM) — The Cautionary Tale**

The company went public via a SPAC in April 2021—this fact alone speaks volumes about the quality of the business.
 
Stem grew revenues 10-fold from $36 million in 2020 to $462 million in 2023, but a large majority (~85%) of revenue was still derived from low-margin hardware sales.
 
The company listed on the NYSE via SPAC merger but received a stock exchange warning that its shares could be delisted after trading below the required minimum average share price of $1.00 for more than 30 consecutive days. At the time of writing, shares were trading at $0.46, 95.29% down from their $10.00 listing price.


**The Stem autopsy for Lunar:**
1. 
Stem was accused of overstating its software revenues by falsely claiming that 100% of its services revenue line was attributable to software revenues.
 **Lesson:** Lunar must be meticulous in segmenting hardware vs. software vs. VPP revenues for public market credibility.
2. 
Stem meaningfully missed its original 2023 revenue guidance of $550–600 million vs. $462 million actual.
 **Lesson:** Sandbagging guidance is essential pre-IPO.
3. 
Over three years post-SPAC, Stem's operations consumed a combined $414.7 million, or about three-fourths of the cash it raised through its SPAC merger.
 **Lesson:** Hardware businesses with software aspirations burn cash faster than software-native businesses.
4. The SPAC route itself was a problem: 
most bad cleantech ideas that received far too much money were funded by SPACs.
 **Lesson for Lunar:** Avoid the SPAC route entirely; pursue a traditional S-1 IPO.

## SECTION 4: CREATIVE FINANCIAL STRATEGIES

### A) VPP Fleet Project Finance / Securitization

**The math:** 
The company says Lunar System customers earned an average of $464 last year by participating in VPP programs.
 If Lunar captures 20% of this ($93/device/year) from 150,000 devices, annual VPP revenue = ~$14M. At 300,000 devices (2028), this grows to ~$28M/year.

**Securitization structure:**
- Pool the contracted VPP revenue streams (similar to how solar companies securitize PPA revenue)
- Underlying security: Contractual dispatch obligations with utilities/CCAs + performance history
- Expected rating: BBB–/BB+ (given performance track record but limited history)
- Achievable leverage: 5–8x the annual cash flow
- **Financing capacity at 300K devices:** $140–224M at 5–8x $28M ARR

**Precedents:** SolarCity securitized solar leases starting 2013, raising hundreds of millions at 4.5–5.5% rates. VPP revenue streams are arguably more predictable than solar leases (dispatch events are utility-directed, not weather-dependent). **This is a highly viable strategy for 2028+ as the fleet scales.**

### B) Manufacturing Equipment Finance (Sale-Leaseback)

Lunar's Georgia and Washington assembly facilities likely contain $20–50M in specialized equipment. A sale-leaseback with a specialty finance firm (e.g., Gordon Brothers, Hilco) would:
- Generate $15–40M in cash immediately
- Create a fixed operating lease (~$2–4M/year)
- Keep equipment in service; no operational disruption
- **Net present value of cash proceeds vs. lease cost: positive if Lunar's WACC exceeds the lease rate**

**Recommendation:** Execute $20–30M sale-leaseback in H2 2026 to extend runway.

### C) Strategic Partnerships for Capital Efficiency

**Gridshare SaaS Licensing (Non-Hardware Revenue):**

Sunrun leverages Lunar's Gridshare platform for its distributed power plants across a dozen markets. Lunar Gridshare is also used by California Community Choice Aggregators, and utilities and energy retailers in Europe and Asia similarly work with Lunar.


Lunar could license Gridshare non-exclusively to:
- FranklinWH, Generac, Sonnen battery manufacturers (non-FEOC competitors)

- Estimated market: 10 licensees × $2–5M/year = $20–50M in high-margin SaaS ARR

**Risk:** Reduces competitive differentiation of Gridshare for Lunar hardware; creates channel conflict with Sunrun. **Verdict:** Only pursue for non-competing geographies (EU, Japan) where Lunar hardware is not being sold.

**SK Group Manufacturing JV:**

 A formal JV—where SK invests capex in exchange for manufacturing economics—would:
