No public data exists on Lunar's warranty claim rate. The company promotes rigorous testing:

At 2,000 deployed units with presumably <2 years of field time, warranty events are likely minimal but growing as the fleet ages. Industry benchmarks for residential battery systems suggest a 2–5% first-year warranty rate, rising to 5–10% in years 3–5 for components. At $17,500 ASP, even a 3% warranty rate on 2,000 units implies **$1.05M in warranty exposure** — a manageable but growing liability.

There is no evidence of a formal refurbishment or recycling operation for returned units. At current volumes, returned systems likely go to engineering for root-cause analysis and are then disposed of. This will become a regulatory and ESG issue as the fleet scales.

### 5D. Inventory Assessment

At 10K/year capacity with ~2,000 units/year deployed, the company is operating at **~20% capacity utilization on the installed base side**, but this does *not* necessarily imply 80% inventory buildup. The gap between 10K capacity and 2K deployments reflects an **installer channel constraint, not a production overrun**. This is a critical distinction analyzed in detail in the Notable Anomalies section.

### ANOMALY 1: The 10K Capacity vs. 2K Deployment Gap — Demand Problem, Supply Problem, or Channel Bottleneck?

This is the single most important operational question about Lunar. The company has **stated capacity of 10,000 systems/year** but has deployed only **~2,000 systems** — a utilization rate of just ~20%. Three competing hypotheses:

**Hypothesis A — Installer Channel Bottleneck (Most Likely):**
The residential solar+battery installer ecosystem is the constraining factor. 
The company produces its equipment in California, Georgia, and Washington state and has installed it in about 2,000 homes and businesses in California.
 With operations **exclusively in California** as of February 2026, the installer channel is limited to California-licensed solar+battery contractors. Building an installer network from 40+ partners to hundreds is a 12–24 month process requiring sales headcount, training programs, and quality assurance. This is the most likely explanation.

**Hypothesis B — The Lease Model Transition:**

In most cases, the systems are now being deployed through residential leases, meaning they are installed at no upfront cost to homeowners.
 The transition from a cash-purchase to a lease model requires Sunrun and other TPOs to build out lease financing programs, which takes time to structure and deploy. This transition may have temporarily depressed unit volume in 2024–2025.

**Hypothesis C — Demand Shortfall:**
California's solar market experienced a significant downturn in 2023–2024 following NEM 3.0 implementation. 
California's 2023 cuts to rooftop solar incentives have led to a steep drop-off in installations in the country's leading market.
 If Lunar's systems require solar co-installation (the standard configuration), the NEM 3.0 shock may have suppressed demand independently of supply or channel constraints.

**Assessment:** All three factors are simultaneously operative. The gap is NOT primarily a manufacturing quality or product problem — field reviews are consistently positive. It IS a **channel scaling and market timing problem**, which is more solvable with the Series D capital.

### ANOMALY 2: Revenue Discrepancy ($35M vs. $67.9M)

As analyzed in Section 3, the $67.9M figure from RocketReach is almost certainly algorithmically estimated and not based on actual financial statements. 
Girotra declined to share the startup's revenues or expectations for profitability.
 The refusal to disclose revenue publicly, combined with the discrepancy between two data sources, creates **unnecessary information asymmetry** that investors should press to resolve before any secondary transaction or future fundraise. The lack of audited financial disclosure is standard for private companies but becomes a material risk factor given the scale of capital raised ($532M).

### ANOMALY 3: The "650 MW Under Management" vs. "2,000 Units Deployed" Discrepancy


With thousands of systems deployed and 650 MW of devices under management across multiple continents, Lunar is proving integrated hardware and software delivers value at scale.


**2,000 Lunar systems × 10 kW inverter = 20 MW of Lunar hardware.** The 650 MW figure is **32.5x larger than Lunar's own installed hardware base.** This means Gridshare is primarily managing **third-party batteries** — specifically Sunrun's 500+ MW fleet of non-Lunar batteries. 
Last summer, Gridshare aggregated a portion of Sunrun's 500-megawatt fleet of home batteries that participated in California's Demand Side Grid Support program.


This is actually a **competitive advantage** (Gridshare is hardware-agnostic and captures value from assets Lunar doesn't manufacture), but it also reveals that **Lunar's primary VPP value is currently as a software company, not a hardware company.** The hardware business is at ~3% of the managed VPP fleet. This inverts the typical cleantech company narrative.

### ANOMALY 4: The SK Group Supply Chain — FEOC Liability vs. Asset

The SK Group relationship is simultaneously Lunar's greatest supply chain advantage (FEOC-compliant Korean cells, strategic investor alignment, Georgia manufacturing infrastructure) and its greatest potential vulnerability:

1. **SK On (SK's battery subsidiary) has experienced significant financial difficulties**, reporting losses as the EV market slowed. If SK On faces financial restructuring, Lunar's cell supply chain could be disrupted.
2. The **nature of the SK manufacturing relationship in Georgia remains undisclosed** — whether this is a formal tolling agreement, a sublease, or a more informal arrangement. Without documented supply security, the Georgia manufacturing anchor is at risk.
3. 
Following the IRA's enactment, South Korea's LG Energy Solution, SK On and Samsung SDI, as well as Japan's Panasonic, all announced major investments to build a US EV battery supply chain ecosystem.
 SK's Georgia investment was primarily for EV cells, not stationary storage. Lunar's use of this infrastructure for stationary storage is opportunistic but potentially secondary to SK's core EV customer obligations.

### ANOMALY 5: The "Never Raise Again" Statement vs. Manufacturing Capital Requirements

The CEO's statement that 
"This capital helps us, in a big way, in achieving our goals of not having to raise ever again"
 is mathematically inconsistent with the publicly stated goal of reaching 100,000 units/year by end of 2028, which (as estimated in Section 4) requires **$230M–$450M in manufacturing capital investment beyond current available resources.** This is either:
- (a) Management expectation that debt/project finance replaces equity (possible, especially if DOE LPO financing is available)
- (b) A plan to partner with a large manufacturing entity that provides capacity in exchange for a commercial agreement (most likely SK Group)
- (c) An aspirational statement made in a fundraising context that should not be taken as a financial commitment
- (d) Evidence that the 100K unit target is aspirational marketing rather than a financial planning assumption

### ANOMALY 6: Sonnen as the Ghost Competitor

Sonnen, now a Shell subsidiary, operates in the same premium residential battery segment with a community/grid-sharing model. Its acquisition by Shell in 2019 at an estimated $500M–$700M valuation preceded Lunar's founding by a year. Shell's backing gives Sonnen effectively unlimited capital for distribution and international expansion. If Shell decides to aggressively invest in Sonnen's US residential market, Lunar faces a competitor with sovereign backing. This risk is entirely absent from Lunar's public communications.

### ANOMALY 7: The Gridshare Japan Investment — Strategic Signal or Distraction?


Lunar Energy's latest investment was in GridShare Japan as part of a Corporate Minority stake on November 28, 2025.
 A pre-revenue company burning $35M–$59M/year is making **outbound venture investments** in a Japanese GridShare spinout. This suggests Lunar views the international GridShare expansion as a separate value creation vehicle — but it also represents capital and management attention deployed outside the core US hardware business. At a startup burn rate, every dollar invested in GridShare Japan is a dollar not applied to Georgia factory expansion.

### ANOMALY 8: Domestic Content Claim Under Scrutiny


Lunar VP of Revenue Ed Gunn noted that the company is "proudly domestic content."
 However, 
sourcing FEOC-compliant materials for LFP components remains a critical challenge that must be addressed.
 The LFP cathode active materials supply chain runs predominantly through China. Even SK's Korean production may use Chinese-sourced lithium, phosphate, and other precursors that could trigger FEOC scrutiny under evolving Treasury guidance. The domestic content claim is strategically valuable but legally fragile, and its durability depends entirely on regulatory interpretations of FEOC that remain in flux.

### ANOMALY 9: The 100K Unit Target Has No Residential Battery Precedent


The startup plans to scale manufacturing from current levels to 20,000 battery units by end of 2026 and 100,000 units by end of 2028.
 
That's a fivefold increase in just over two years, reflecting both investor confidence and urgent market demand.


No residential battery manufacturer in the United States has ever reached 100,000 units/year. Tesla Powerwall, the market leader, reached approximately 50,000–80,000 units/year *globally* only in 2022–2023 — **seven years after launch**. Lunar is proposing to achieve this in **five years from launch (2022–2028), starting from a base of 2,000 units.** The closest analogy would be Enphase's microinverter ramp — but microinverters are dramatically simpler to manufacture than integrated battery-inverter systems.

**This target, while directionally appropriate as a strategic vision, is almost certainly unachievable as a specific time-bound commitment.** A more realistic scenario is 30,000–50,000 units/year by end of 2028, contingent on (a) new manufacturing facility construction beginning no later than Q3 2026, (b) SK or another cell supplier committed to 1+ GWh/year supply by Q1 2027, and (c) installer network expansion to 200+ certified partners by mid-2026.
