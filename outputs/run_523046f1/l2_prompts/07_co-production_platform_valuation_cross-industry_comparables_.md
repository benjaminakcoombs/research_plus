You are a valuation specialist with experience in multi-commodity processing platforms across the oil refining, copper/gold mining, and critical minerals sectors. You are investigating whether Brimstone Energy's "Rock Refinery" co-production model — producing cement, alumina, supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs), and potentially iron, magnesium, and titanium products from a single feedstock — warrants a platform valuation multiple rather than a single-product cement or alumina multiple. The difference could be 2–3x.

CONTEXT:
Brimstone Energy's CEO Cody Finke explicitly compares the company to oil refineries (ExxonMobil) and copper processors (Freeport-McMoRan), where a single raw material input is fractionated into multiple products of varying value, and co-product economics transform capital returns. Brimstone mines calcium silicate rock and processes it through an acid-leach system to produce:
- Portland cement (ASTM C150 certified, ~80,000 t/yr at Plant 1): Primary product by mass. ~$130–150/t.
- Smelter-grade alumina (~20,000 t/yr at Plant 1): High-value co-product. ~$400–500/t if SGA spec achieved.
- Supplementary cementitious materials (SCMs, pozzolans): Co-product. ~$50–80/t.
- Potential future products: iron oxide, magnesium oxide, titanium dioxide — all extracted from the same feedstock.

The key economic insight: mining, grinding, and acid-leaching costs are allocated primarily to cement (the highest-volume product). Alumina extraction is essentially a high-value byproduct at near-marginal cost — estimated $125–225/t vs. $560–585/t landed cost for imported alumina in Oklahoma.

Brimstone's Oklahoma Plant 1 capacity was revised downward from 140,000 t/yr cement (DOE March 2024 announcement) to 80,000 t/yr cement + 20,000 t/yr alumina (current specification) — a 43% reduction in cement output with alumina separately quantified for the first time. This could reflect either: (a) genuine engineering optimization toward higher-value alumina production; (b) capital-constrained downsizing after the $189M DOE grant loss; or (c) simple disaggregation of a figure that previously included alumina-equivalent tonnes.

Separately, Brimstone holds what may be the only ASTM C150 certification in the world for cement produced from non-limestone feedstock. ASTM C150 is the prescriptive standard required for all federally funded infrastructure (DOT roads, bridges, IIJA projects). This certification — obtained through independent testing by Twining Consulting — took years to achieve and represents a regulatory barrier to entry that competitors would need to replicate.

HYPOTHESIS TO INVESTIGATE:
A co-production platform that extracts 5+ products from a single feedstock, with a regulatory moat on its primary product (C150 certification) and a structural cost advantage on its highest-value product (alumina at marginal cost), should be valued as a multi-commodity refinery platform — not as a cement company or an alumina company. Oil refineries trade at 6–8x EBITDA; copper/gold miners with significant byproduct credits trade at 8–12x EV/EBITDA; critical minerals platforms (MP Materials, Lynas) trade at 15–25x. A generalist climate-tech investor valuing Brimstone as a "green cement startup" at 3–5x forward revenue misses the platform economics entirely. The correct comparable set includes Freeport-McMoRan (copper with gold/molybdenum byproducts), MP Materials (rare earths with multiple oxide products), and Phillips 66 (refining multiple petroleum products from crude oil).
