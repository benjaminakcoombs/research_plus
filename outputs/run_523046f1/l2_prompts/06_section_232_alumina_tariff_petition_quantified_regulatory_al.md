You are a trade policy economist specializing in Section 232 national security tariffs, with expertise in the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) inclusions process and the aluminum/alumina supply chain. You are investigating a specific, unfiled regulatory petition that could create a durable competitive moat for any domestic alumina producer — particularly Brimstone Energy.

CONTEXT:
On April 30, 2025, the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) published a final rule establishing a Section 232 Inclusions Process (15 CFR Part 705), which allows any interested party to petition for additional products to be added to Section 232 tariff coverage. Petitions are accepted in two-week windows three times per year. The current Section 232 tariff on aluminum is 25% (increased to 50% for some countries by Trump's March 2025 proclamation), but this tariff covers primary aluminum and certain downstream products — NOT alumina (HTS 2818.10.20, "Artificial corundum, whether or not chemically defined; aluminum oxide").

The United States imports approximately 1.1 million tonnes of alumina annually (per USGS 2024 Mineral Commodity Summaries), primarily from Australia, Brazil, and Jamaica. The only operating domestic alumina refinery is the Gramercy, Louisiana plant (formerly Atalco, now operated by Almatis/various owners), running at roughly one-third capacity. Brimstone Energy is the only announced project to produce alumina from a non-bauxite domestic feedstock, with a planned 20,000 t/yr initial capacity in Oklahoma.

If alumina were added to Section 232 coverage at the current 25% tariff rate (or 50%), the landed price of imported alumina would increase by approximately $100–200/t (from ~$405/t FOB to $505–605/t landed before transport). Any domestic producer would be tariff-immune, creating an instant and durable competitive advantage.

HYPOTHESIS TO INVESTIGATE:
A Section 232 inclusions petition to add alumina (HTS 2818.10) to tariff coverage is a low-cost, high-upside regulatory action requiring no Congressional legislation, no executive order, and no new appropriation. If approved, it permanently transforms the economics of domestic alumina production. The Aluminum Association, Century Aluminum, EGA, and potentially the DOD all have aligned interests in supporting such a petition (reducing dependence on imported alumina for domestic smelters). At Brimstone's planned 20,000 t/yr scale, a $100–200/t tariff-induced price advantage generates $2–4M/year in additional margin. At 10-plant scale (200,000 t/yr), this is $20–40M/year. No evidence exists that this petition has been filed.
