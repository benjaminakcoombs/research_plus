## PART V: BRIMSTONE ENERGY — DEFINITIVE ASSESSMENT

### 5.1 Technology — The Critical Asymmetry Between Cement and Alumina

Brimstone's Rock Refinery® simultaneously produces portland cement (ASTM C150), supplementary cementitious materials (SCM), and smelter-grade alumina from calcium silicate rock. 
The Brimstone process avoids the need to import bauxite by producing alumina from rocks that are abundant in the U.S. By replacing bauxite with carbon-free calcium silicate rocks, Brimstone aims to reduce the dependence of the U.S. alumina industry on imported bauxite. Brimstone's patented process utilises co-production to obtain various minerals from a single type of rock, thereby lowering expenses, streamlining logistics and minimising waste.


**The validation asymmetry — the most important finding in Round 3:**

For cement: 
A third-party lab has verified that Brimstone cement meets ASTM C150 standards for portland cement, the type of cement used in virtually all concrete construction today.
 Additionally, Amazon has signed a commercial agreement to reserve annual volumes of Brimstone's portland cement. This represents a full commercial validation cycle: independent lab verification → industrial customer testing → commercial offtake.

For alumina: **No equivalent validation exists.** Brimstone states that 
every step of Brimstone's process is commercially validated, but the company is the first to combine them in this way
 — meaning individual chemical steps may be validated, but the integrated system has not been validated. No independent SGA quality test has been published. No aluminum producer has signed any commercial agreement or LOI for alumina volumes. 
The company is collaborating with potential customers and conducting tests on its deeply decarbonised cement and supplementary cementitious materials — but not, based on any public disclosure, with aluminum smelter customers on SGA.


**The technical SGA specification challenge for Brimstone:**

Smelter-grade alumina quality is not a single number — it is a multi-dimensional specification covering: Al₂O₃ content (>98.5%), α-alumina content (≤10–15%), moisture content (<0.3%), specific surface area (45–80 m²/g), particle size distribution (median 70–90 μm), loss on ignition (<1%), sodium oxide content (<0.4%), silica content (<0.02%), iron oxide content (<0.02%), attrition index, and angle of repose for silo storage and pneumatic conveying. 
The quality of alumina depends on many factors such as the bauxite, the process design and the process operating conditions. Some quality parameters are more reliant on one or more of the aforementioned factors than others.


Brimstone's calcium silicate rock process produces aluminum hydroxide (Al(OH)₃) via a chemically distinct pathway from Bayer process gibbsite. The calcination step that converts Al(OH)₃ to α-Al₂O₃ (SGA) is standard — but the upstream precipitation chemistry, particle morphology, and impurity profile of Brimstone's aluminium hydroxide may differ from Bayer hydrate in ways that affect the final SGA product. This is not a theoretical risk — it is the core industrial chemistry question that any aluminum smelter operator (including EGA's technical team) would ask before signing a supply agreement.

The structure would be: EGA/Inola JV agrees to purchase X tonnes/year of SGA from Brimstone's Kiowa County plant, contingent on (a) Brimstone plant achieving financial close by [date], (b) SGA meeting EGA's EX technology quality specifications verified by independent third party, and (c) first delivery by [date]. This is a conditional offtake agreement — bankable as a supply option but not as a firm supply commitment.

## PART VII: REVISED STRATEGIC PATHWAY — THE ACTIONABLE FRAMEWORK

### 7.1 The Four-Gate Model

Brimstone's pathway to the Inola ecosystem runs through four sequential gates, each of which must be passed before the next is achievable:

**Gate 1 — SGA Quality Validation (Months 1–6): THE CRITICAL PATH ITEM**

This is the single most important action Brimstone must take, and the one with the least public evidence of progress. The cement ASTM C150 validation provides the template. Brimstone needs:

1. Produce SGA at pilot scale (≥100 kg batch) using the Kiowa County calcium silicate feedstock
2. Submit samples to an independent metallurgical laboratory (ideally RHI Magnesita, Norsk Hydro's Årdal research center, or EGA's own Al Taweelah technical lab) for full SGA specification testing against the Sandy/Floury alumina standard (ISO 806, relevant ASTM and CEN standards)
3. Publish the independent test report publicly

Without this, **no aluminum producer will sign any supply agreement or LOI, regardless of price.** The Amazon cement agreement model confirms this: Amazon signed *after testing outcomes*, not before. There is no shortcut to this gate.

**Gate 2 — Anchor Offtaker LOI (Months 3–12): Conditional on Gate 1 Progress**

The target is a non-binding but commercially serious Letter of Intent from the Inola JV entity (EGA/Century/Oklahoma Primary Aluminum LLC) for annual SGA volumes contingent on:
- Successful Gate 1 SGA quality validation
- Brimstone plant achieving financial close by a specified date
- Pilot operations confirming consistent SGA quality

The Amazon template (volume reservation following testing outcomes) is the exact structure to propose. The LOI need not commit EGA to purchase — it commits EGA to *consider* purchase if conditions are met. This is sufficient to unlock government and private financing.

**Gate 3 — Capital (Months 6–18): Conditional on Gate 1 + Gate 2**

With SGA validation (Gate 1) and an anchor LOI (Gate 2), Brimstone has the two ingredients required for a capital raise:

*Option A — DPA Title III Application:* The DoW just invested $150M in Atalco under DPA authority. A Brimstone application citing: (a) domestic feedstock independence (Oklahoma rocks, not Jamaican bauxite), (b) named offtaker (Inola JV), (c) alignment with December 2025 White House aluminum strategy, and (d) SGA quality validation has strong merit. Target: $100–150M in DPA Title III preferred equity.

*Option B — Industrial Series C:* A $75–150M Series C led by a Japanese trading house (Sumitomo, Mitsui, or Itochu — all active in U.S. critical minerals) or a defense-adjacent infrastructure fund, structured as convertible preferred with an attached 10-year alumina offtake option. The CFO hire (Tim Rens, "materials industry veteran") is specifically the right profile to execute this round.

*Option C — Century Aluminum Strategic Investment:* Century's strategic logic for a Brimstone investment is stronger than EGA's: Century needs to diversify Jamalco risk, reduce alumina import exposure, and demonstrate U.S. domestic supply chain for its ESG/DOE grant positioning. A $50–100M Century equity stake in Brimstone — with attached offtake option — is more consistent with Century's corporate behavior than an EGA/Mubadala stake.

**Gate 4 — Plant Construction and FEED Integration (Months 18–36): Conditional on Gates 1–3**

With capital secured, Brimstone must engage Bechtel at the FEED stage to specify infrastructure at Inola capable of receiving truck-delivered alumina from Oklahoma. This is an infrastructure engineering requirement, not a commercial negotiation — but it must be specified during FEED (currently underway) to avoid being permanently locked out of the Inola supply chain by barge-only infrastructure design.

## PART IX: FINAL CONCLUSIONS — THE DEFINITIVE VERDICT

**1. The smelter is being built. The infrastructure evidence is overwhelming.**


Just months after winning a $500 million grant from the Office of Clean Energy Demonstrations, Century Aluminum announced plans to construct and operate a new aluminum smelter in Inola, Oklahoma, with a production capacity of 500,000 tons per year of aluminum, including 20,000 tons of high-purity aluminum for defense applications.
 The subsequent scaling to 750,000 t/yr with EGA as 60% partner, Bechtel FEED engagement, PSO's $3.2B infrastructure commitment, and the February 2026 downstream fabricator agreement constitute an irreversible institutional commitment.

**2. The alumina supply question is the largest unresolved variable in the Inola project.**

No supply agreement has been announced across three research rounds. The most likely outcome is a Jamalco-anchored, Atalco-supplemented multi-source supply structure — but this has not been formally committed. Jamalco's three-year disruption pattern will force lenders to require backup supply arrangements, creating a structural opening that no other supplier can fill with the combination of price, logistics immunity, and feedstock independence that Brimstone could offer.

**3. Century's $500M DOE grant is paused, not canceled — but its transferability to Oklahoma is unresolved and politically complex.**


It is unclear whether the terms of Century's grant have changed under the Trump administration. But Century recently pointed to Oklahoma's abundant wind generation and solar power potential in explaining its decision to partner with EGA on a smelter near Tulsa.
 This ambiguity represents both a risk (Century's capital position) and an opportunity (Century's motivation to find cost-efficient alumina solutions that improve project economics).

**4. Brimstone's cement is proven; its alumina is not.**


A third-party lab has verified that Brimstone cement meets ASTM C150 standards for portland cement.
 This validation — and the Amazon commercial agreement that followed — proves the commercial pathway model works. The same model applied to SGA would unlock the entire supply chain opportunity. The absence of any SGA validation equivalent is the single biggest near-term action item, and the one most in Brimstone's control to resolve.

**5. The window is 12–18 months to FEED infrastructure lock-in; 24–36 months to project finance bankability.**

These are two different deadlines requiring two different actions:
- **FEED deadline (12–18 months):** Brimstone must engage Bechtel/EGA to specify truck-delivery-compatible alumina receiving infrastructure in the plant design. This is an engineering specification request, not a commercial negotiation. It must happen now, before FEED is complete.
- **Project finance deadline (24–36 months):** Brimstone must achieve Gate 1 (SGA validation) and Gate 2 (anchor LOI) to be eligible as a bankable supply source in the Inola project finance data room.

**6. Brimstone's irreplaceable competitive position is exactly one thing: domestic feedstock independence.**


The Brimstone process avoids the need to import bauxite by producing alumina from rocks that are abundant in the U.S. By replacing bauxite with carbon-free calcium silicate rocks, Brimstone aims to reduce the dependence of the U.S. alumina industry on imported bauxite.
 Atalco is domestically located but imports Jamaican bauxite. Jamalco is offshore entirely. EGA's Al Taweelah is UAE-based. Only Brimstone offers alumina produced from U.S. rocks, mined in the U.S., processed in the U.S., and delivered to a U.S. smelter — with zero dependence on any imported feedstock.

In the political environment created by the December 2025 White House aluminum strategy, Section 232 tariffs at 50%, and the DoD's own investment in domestic alumina (Atalco/DPA), this irreplaceable characteristic is worth a premium that cannot be quantified by landed cost alone. Whether Brimstone can survive long enough to prove it is the central uncertainty of this analysis — and the answer depends almost entirely on whether Gate 1 (SGA validation) can be achieved in the next 6 months on the roughly $15–20M of cash that likely remains in Brimstone's treasury.

The verdict: **Brimstone's pathway is technically coherent, strategically well-positioned, and politically timed correctly — but it is critically undercapitalized and has a technology validation gap that is closing the window faster than any competitive threat.** The next six months of Brimstone's decisions — specifically the initiation of SGA validation testing and a DPA Title III application — will determine whether this is a story of a missed strategic window or a transformational domestic supply chain development.
