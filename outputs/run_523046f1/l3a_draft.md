## I. The Opportunity

Brimstone Energy holds the only ASTM C150 certification ever issued for portland cement produced from non-limestone feedstock — the mandatory specification for all federally funded U.S. infrastructure.^1 Its co-production model simultaneously extracts alumina from the same rock, with Plant 1 targeting 20,000 t/yr at an estimated $480/t.^2 If that alumina meets smelter-grade specification, a critical-minerals platform multiple (12–15x EBITDA) applies; if it meets only chemical grade (~$200/t), the framework collapses to a cement startup at 7–8x.^3 That gap — $49M in NPV at Plant 1, $245M at platform scale — turns on one fact: after six years and $80M+ raised, Brimstone has published zero of thirteen standard SGA parameters for its alumina.^4 The non-obvious insight is not the risk — it is the timing. Sublime Systems, the only competitor with cement-major backing, has collapsed to ~28 employees with its Holyoke plant paused, stranding $75M in combined CRH and Holcim investment.^5 Both companies must now find a replacement green cement technology bet. Simultaneously, EGA and Century Aluminum are building a 750,000 t/yr smelter in Oklahoma — the same state as Brimstone's plant — requiring ~1.5M t/yr of alumina with no domestic supply agreement.^6 The first strategic investor to close on Brimstone captures the only C150-certified replacement asset and a domestic alumina supply option the market has not priced. Brimstone's Series B is structurally overdue; Crunchbase assigns a 76% probability of a raise within six months.^7 The window is Q2–Q3 2026.

## II. Company Situation

Brimstone Energy is a pre-revenue, venture-backed industrial technology company that has developed a patented process — the Rock Refinery® — to co-produce ASTM C150 portland cement, supplementary cementitious materials (SCM), and smelter-grade alumina from calcium silicate rocks rather than limestone. Founded in 2019 at Caltech, the company operates a laboratory in Oakland, California, is engineering a demonstration plant near Reno, Nevada, and has signed a letter of intent with Dolese Bros. Co. for feedstock supply from the Roosevelt quarry in Kiowa County, Oklahoma — the planned site of a ~$378M first commercial plant producing 80,000 t/yr of cement and 20,000 t/yr of alumina.^8

The company has raised more than $80M to date, with a $55M Series A in March 2022 co-led by Breakthrough Energy Ventures and DCVC, and participation from Amazon Climate Pledge Fund, Fifth Wall, Kajima Ventures (first disclosed October 2025), and 17 other investors.^9 No Series B has been announced. The last private equity round closed four years ago — an unusually long gap for a company at this capital intensity. CEO Cody Finke has acknowledged the fundraising environment is challenging but states the company "has investors in place to help it continue building."^10 The hire of CFO Tim Rens in October 2025 — who raised ~$20B across his career including $750M for Monolith Materials' first-of-kind plant, with confirmed banking relationships at J.P. Morgan and Goldman Sachs — is the clearest signal that a large raise is being actively prepared.^11

**Revenue today is zero.** At Plant 1 nameplate, estimated annual revenue splits approximately 44% from alumina ($9.6M), 51% from cement ($11.2M), and 5% from SCM ($1.0M).^12 This decomposition reveals a critical structural fact obscured by the "green cement company" label: at co-production cost allocation of ~$130–140/t, alumina's unit margin is an estimated $320–390/t versus near-breakeven margins on cement.^13 The alumina stream — not cement — generates the majority of the platform's economic surplus. The business model's viability therefore depends more on alumina specification and pricing than on cement.

The Amazon relationship is the most important commercial asset and the most complex structural feature. Amazon functions in four simultaneous roles: equity investor (Climate Pledge Fund), product testing partner (proprietary slab mix design testing through 2026–2027), anchor customer (non-binding volume reservation signed August 2025), and SCoBA founding member.^14 The reservation agreement is conditional on testing completion and commercial-scale production — it is not a binding take-or-pay. No ACPF portfolio company has ever been acquired through a change-of-control transaction, making Amazon's consent process entirely uncharted and likely unusually deliberate.^15

**What has changed in 12 months is almost everything.** The DOE OCED grant ($189M, selected March 2024) was cancelled in May 2025; only $8.7M of Phase 1 funding was disbursed.^16 The appeal is active, with Brimstone engaging congressional representatives, but no OCED grantee has won an appeal to date.^17 More critically, the IRA's loan authority for the DOE Loan Programs Office was repealed by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act in July 2025, eliminating what would have been the cleanest alternative financing route.^18 The entire ~$400M plant must now be financed privately — narrowing the financing menu to a large Series B, a strategic equity partner, project finance with an investment-grade sponsor guarantee, or acquisition.

Against this financing headwind, every competitive and commercial signal has moved in Brimstone's favor. Sublime Systems paused its Holyoke, MA plant and cut to ~28 employees by March 2026, stranding $75M in CRH/Holcim investment.^19 Brimstone secured the Dolese Bros. quarry LOI (June 2025), the Amazon reservation (August 2025), the Kajima Ventures investment (October 2025), and announced the "Fourth Great Refinery" product expansion in December 2025 — adding steel, magnesium, and titanium to its roadmap and framing a $2.4T addressable market.^20

**Where the narrative diverges from reality:** The December 2025 framing positions Brimstone as a multi-trillion-dollar industrial platform. But the Reno demonstration plant was still "currently being engineered" as of that announcement and had not broken ground.^21 The scale-up from bench-scale Oakland operations to a $378M commercial plant may represent a 40–80x factor with no intermediate demonstration of continuous integrated production.^22 The expanded product suite — steel, titanium, magnesium — has not been demonstrated at any scale. Brimstone correctly notes that "only process integration" remains to be de-risked, having used exclusively TRL 9 (commercially available) equipment at pilot scale.^23 That distinction matters — this is engineering risk, not chemistry risk — but it is real, and sophisticated capital will pressure-test it.

## III. Key Findings

**Sublime's Collapse Has Stranded $75M in Cement-Major Green Investment — Brimstone Is the Only C150-Certified Replacement**

**The Observation:** Sublime Systems went from 128 employees to approximately 28 in under four months.^24 Its $150M Holyoke, MA demonstration plant is paused indefinitely after the DOE cancelled its $87M OCED grant.^25 CRH holds equity (~$37.5M) with "priority site selection rights for full-scale plant development in key markets" and a binding offtake reservation.^26 Amrize (Holcim's U.S. entity) holds a convertible note and advance payments — structurally senior to equity in any wind-down — and has not disclosed an impairment charge in its FY2025 10-K.^27 Both companies' green cement technology bets are now effectively impaired. Separately, CRH completed 38 acquisitions in 2025 for $4.1B and maintains a ventures fund with an estimated $130–170M in remaining dry powder.^28

**The Deal Implication:** Brimstone's ASTM C150 cement drops into existing supply chains without modification — unlike Sublime's ASTM C1157 product, which is excluded from all DOT/AASHTO-governed infrastructure (~50–57% of U.S. cement demand) and requires specification-by-specification architect and engineer approval.^29 For CRH specifically — the largest road builder in North America — C150 is non-negotiable for its Ash Grove division. An investment of $75–125M at $450–550M pre-money, structured as equity plus binding offtake plus co-development rights, would replicate the Sublime deal anatomy with a structurally superior product. The first mover captures the replacement asset before the Series B closes.

**Key Uncertainty:** CRH's Sublime investment agreement may contain non-compete provisions. Until Sublime formally dissolves, these restrictions remain in force, potentially constraining CRH to a financial-only position.^30

---

**Six Years, Zero Alumina Specs Published — A $49M–$245M Diligence Question No One Has Asked**

**The Observation:** Brimstone claims "smelter-grade alumina" in every press release since January 2025, yet has published none of the thirteen standard SGA specification parameters — no Al₂O₃ purity percentage, no Fe₂O₃ or TiO₂ assays, no crystal phase XRD data, no BET surface area.^31 The cement product, by contrast, has been independently certified to ASTM C150 and tested by Amazon's construction team. The geological literature on Brimstone's Oklahoma feedstock — the Glen Mountains Layered Complex — describes the rock as "Fe-rich, Ti-rich, and P-rich biotite-bearing gabbros."^32 Published experimental data shows 93–97% titanium extraction from ilmenite under standard HCl leaching conditions.^33 Meeting SGA's TiO₂ specification of ≤30 ppm from feedstock containing 1.5–4% TiO₂ requires a decontamination factor of 500–1,300x — a level not demonstrated in any published non-Bayer alumina process.^34

**The Deal Implication:** If the alumina meets only chemical-grade specification, it is worth ~$200/t, not ~$480/t. Annual Plant 1 alumina revenue drops from $9.6M to $4.0M — a $5.6M/yr reduction with a 20-year NPV impact of ~$49M at 8% discount.^35 At five-plant scale, the gap reaches ~$245M.^36 More consequentially, the "critical minerals platform" narrative collapses — the difference between a 12–15x critical minerals multiple and a 7–8x cement multiple on the same EBITDA. An independent certificate of analysis with XRD phase confirmation and BET surface area would cost approximately $150K and definitively resolve this question.^37

**Key Uncertainty:** Brimstone may have achieved full SGA specification at pilot scale and is withholding publication as trade secret pending commercial plant construction. The company's willingness to hire a $20B-raise CFO and publicly commit to the alumina narrative suggests management expects the data to withstand diligence.

**EGA's 1.5M t/yr Alumina Demand Has No Domestic Supply Agreement — Brimstone Is 200 Miles Away**

**The Observation:** EGA and Century Aluminum signed a joint development agreement in January 2026 for a 750,000 t/yr primary aluminum smelter at Inola, Oklahoma — the first new U.S. smelter since 1980, with Bechtel engaged for FEED and the project announced as part of $200B in U.S.-UAE commercial agreements.^38 The smelter requires ~1.45M t/yr of alumina.^39 The U.S. has one operational alumina refinery (Atalco/Gramercy, LA) running at roughly one-third of its 1.2M t/yr capacity.^40 No alumina supply agreement for Inola has been publicly disclosed.^41 Brimstone's Plant 1 is 180–220 miles away and uses 100% domestic feedstock with zero dependence on imported bauxite. Neither company has publicly discussed a supply relationship.^42

**The Deal Implication:** Brimstone's 20,000 t/yr covers only 1.4% of Inola's demand — commercially negligible at Plant 1. The investment case is an option on scale: at 10 plants (200,000 t/yr), the estimated delivered cost advantage versus imported alumina is ~$200–300/t, generating ~$40–60M/yr in savings — a 20-year NPV of approximately $390–590M at 8% discount.^43 That figure requires 10 plants built over 10+ years, verified SGA quality, and sustained domestic alumina demand — each a significant assumption. But the entry point is actionable now: an EGA/Century equity co-investment of $50–100M in Brimstone is financially immaterial to a $4–5B smelter project but potentially existential for Brimstone's capital stack, and it secures a domestic alumina supply option no other company can offer.

**Key Uncertainty:** EGA's corporate history shows a preference for majority control of strategic assets, not minority stakes in pre-commercial startups.^44 Century Aluminum — which needs to diversify its hurricane-exposed Jamalco alumina supply — may be the more natural equity partner.^45
