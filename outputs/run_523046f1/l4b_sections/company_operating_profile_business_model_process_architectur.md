## II. Company Operating Profile: Business Model & Process Architecture

Brimstone's economic thesis rests on a single architectural insight: co-production from a shared feedstock makes the marginal cost of each output dramatically lower than any standalone production route. One mining operation, one grinding step, and a shared leaching process yield three saleable products — ASTM C150 portland cement, smelter-grade alumina, and supplementary cementitious materials — plus passively generated carbon credits. At Plant 1 nameplate capacity, this architecture produces an estimated $21.8M in annual revenue.^1 The cement product is independently certified and commercially validated. The alumina product — which carries 44% of that revenue at smelter-grade pricing — has zero independently validated specification data after six years of development and more than $80M in capital deployed.^2 The co-production cost allocation that underpins the unit economics thesis has not been independently verified. A buyer evaluating this business is pricing two things simultaneously: a certified cement product with demonstrated market acceptance, and an alumina co-product whose value is a function of product grade that no third party has confirmed.

### The Rock Refinery: Co-Production Economics Drive the Entire Valuation Thesis

The process is conceptually straightforward at an M&A-relevant level of abstraction. Brimstone mines calcium silicate rock — basalt, diopside, or anorthosite — from conventional quarries.^3 The rock is crushed and ground in a single grinding circuit, then subjected to acid leaching that separates the mineral components. Calcium compounds are routed through calcination and milling with gypsum to produce ASTM C150 ordinary portland cement — chemically and physically identical to conventional OPC produced from limestone.^4 Aluminum compounds are extracted from the same leachate and processed into alumina. The residual silicate fraction becomes supplementary cementitious material. Magnesium-bearing residues passively absorb atmospheric CO₂ over time, generating carbon credits at zero marginal operating cost.^5 The entire process uses commercially available industrial equipment — kilns, mills, leaching reactors, solid-liquid separators — rated at Technology Readiness Level 9, confining the residual scale-up risk to process integration rather than hardware invention.^6

Conventional cement manufacturing releases CO₂ from two sources: fuel combustion (addressable through electrification) and the chemical decomposition of limestone feedstock (CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂), which accounts for approximately 60% of cement's total carbon footprint and is chemically unavoidable in any limestone-based process.^7 Brimstone eliminates the second source entirely by substituting calcium silicate rock, which contains no bound CO₂. On renewable electricity, the result is zero-carbon cement; on the current U.S. grid mix, the emissions reduction is approximately 75%.^8 The feedstock substitution also delivers a raw material efficiency advantage: management states that the Rock Refinery process requires approximately seven times less rock per unit of combined output than the separate conventional processes for cement, alumina, and SCM production today.^9

The co-production architecture is the economic thesis, not a secondary benefit. CEO Cody Finke draws explicit analogies to two established industrial models: Freeport-McMoRan's copper mining operations, where gold and molybdenum are extracted as high-margin co-products from the same ore body, and ExxonMobil's petroleum refining, where a single crude oil input yields gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, petrochemicals, and lubricants — each priced independently in its own market.^10 In both models, the shared upstream cost (mining the ore, cracking the crude) is allocated across all products, making the marginal extraction cost of each co-product substantially lower than standalone production. Brimstone's process replicates this logic: the quarrying, crushing, grinding, and acid leaching costs are shared across cement, alumina, and SCM. The estimated marginal cost of alumina extraction — after allocating primary processing costs to cement as the highest-volume product — is $125–225 per tonne.^11 This compares to $560–585 per tonne for alumina produced through the conventional Bayer process and delivered to the U.S. interior.^12 The structural cost advantage of $200–400 per tonne on delivered alumina is embedded in the process architecture rather than contingent on any individual subsidy, tariff, or policy regime — a characteristic explored in the co-production valuation analysis within the Tension Point Analysis.

The company screened over 23,000 quarries across the United States before selecting the Dolese Bros. Co. Roosevelt quarry in Kiowa County, Oklahoma, as the feedstock source for its first commercial plant.^13 The quarry selection was driven by rock composition — specifically, the alumina content of the calcium silicate minerals, which directly determines the co-production yield ratio and the plant's revenue mix. A letter of intent for feedstock supply was signed June 30, 2025.^14

Plant 1 is designed to produce 80,000 tonnes per year of ASTM C150 portland cement and 20,000 tonnes per year of smelter-grade alumina, plus SCM and carbon credits.^15 Total capital expenditure is estimated at $378–400M.^16 The target is full production before 2030.^17 An important design evolution warrants attention: the original DOE OCED grant application (March 2024) specified 140,000 tonnes per year of cement capacity with no alumina separately quantified.^18 The current specification disaggregates the output to 80,000 tonnes cement and 20,000 tonnes alumina, a 43% reduction in stated cement tonnage. This redesign reflects either genuine engineering optimization toward the higher-value alumina product, capital constraint following the DOE grant cancellation ($189M, terminated May 2025), or reclassification of tonnage previously reported as cement-equivalent. Each interpretation carries different implications for unit economics and is analyzed further in the Tension Point Analysis.

The Plant 1 revenue build at base-case pricing is $21.8M annually:^19

| Product | Volume (t/yr) | Price ($/t) | Revenue ($M) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Portland cement (ASTM C150) | 80,000 | $140 | $11.2 |
| Smelter-grade alumina | 20,000 | $480 | $9.6 |
| Supplementary cementitious materials | ~15,400 | $65 | $1.0 |
| **Total** | | | **$21.8** |

Alumina carries 44% of Plant 1 revenue at the $480/t smelter-grade assumption. This concentration creates acute sensitivity to alumina product classification. If the alumina meets only chemical-grade specification — suitable for refractory, abrasive, and catalyst applications but not for aluminum smelter feed — the applicable price falls to approximately $200 per tonne.^20 Under this scenario, alumina revenue declines from $9.6M to $4.0M (20,000 t × $200/t), and total Plant 1 revenue falls to approximately $16.2M — a 26% decline driven entirely by the alumina product classification, with no change in volume, operating cost, or cement economics.^21 The $5.6M annual gap between $21.8M and $16.2M is the revenue expression of the alumina specification question analyzed in detail in the Tension Point Analysis and priced through the scenario analysis in the Valuation Architecture section.

Two caveats must accompany this revenue framework. First, Brimstone is pre-revenue; all figures are projected at Plant 1 nameplate capacity and have not been earned.^22 Second, the co-production cost allocation that produces the $125–225/t marginal alumina extraction cost has not been independently validated.^23 The theoretical economics of co-production are well-established in analogous industries (petroleum refining, polymetallic mining), but the specific cost allocation between cement and alumina in Brimstone's process — which determines whether each product stream carries a commercially attractive margin — rests entirely on management estimates. An independent engineering cost study is a standard pre-investment diligence requirement for any capital commitment at this scale.

### Brimstone Is the Only Advancing ASTM C150-Certified Green Cement Technology in the United States

The ASTM C150 certification, obtained in July 2023, is Brimstone's most commercially valuable asset — and the primary structural differentiator against every competing green cement technology.^24 For a non-technical reader, the distinction is this: ASTM C150 is the prescriptive standard that specifies the exact chemical composition, physical properties, and performance thresholds for ordinary portland cement. It is the standard referenced in virtually every U.S. building code, every state department of transportation specification, every federal infrastructure procurement document, and every structural engineer's mix design library. A cement product certified to ASTM C150 drops into 100% of the U.S. cement market — including the approximately 35–40% of demand that flows through DOT-specified and federally funded infrastructure projects — without specification changes, without project-by-project engineer approval, and without customer re-education.^25

This is the commercially decisive distinction. The alternative standard, ASTM C1157, is a performance-based specification that sets outcome thresholds (compressive strength, setting time, durability) without prescribing chemical composition.^26 A C1157 product addresses only the approximately 20–30% of U.S. cement demand where project specifiers have adopted performance-based procurement.^27 The remaining 35–40% of demand flowing through DOT-specified infrastructure — roads, bridges, and projects funded under the IIJA — requires C150 compliance per AASHTO specifications. A C1157 product cannot access this demand without multi-year specification change processes across 50 individual state transportation agencies. In practical terms, a C150 product can be sold everywhere; a C1157 product is excluded from the largest single category of U.S. cement consumption. Brimstone is the only company in the world to have achieved ASTM C150 certification for cement produced from non-limestone feedstock.^28

The certification was performed independently by Twining Consulting, a recognized third-party materials testing laboratory.^29 The Amazon testing program extends this validation into commercial application. In the initial phase, independent third-party tests — designed in collaboration with Amazon's concrete consultants — evaluated workability, compressive strength, and other properties of Brimstone's concrete materials against Amazon's proprietary slab mix designs for data center construction.^30 Following successful initial results, Amazon signed a commercial reservation agreement in August 2025 for annual volumes of portland cement and SCM from Brimstone's forthcoming plant.^31 The partnership then entered an expanded testing phase covering durability, sulfate resistance, aggregate reactivity, and other properties across a wider range of mix designs and applications, with testing continuing through 2025–2026.^32 As of February 2026, the expanded testing program was actively progressing.^33 Completion of this program — likely in the 2026–2027 timeframe — is the gate to converting the non-binding reservation into a fully binding commercial contract, a milestone with direct implications for project finance bankability as discussed in the Capital Structure analysis.

The competitive landscape has narrowed sharply in Brimstone's favor over the past twelve months. Sublime Systems — the only other venture-backed next-generation cement company with commercial-scale ambitions and more than $200M in cumulative funding — reduced its workforce from approximately 128 to ~28 employees between December 2025 and March 2026 and paused construction indefinitely on its $150M Holyoke, Massachusetts demonstration plant following the cancellation of its $87M DOE OCED grant.^34 Sublime's cement meets ASTM C1157, not C150.^35 CRH and Holcim/Amrize each invested approximately $37.5M in Sublime for equity and binding offtake — investments that are now functionally impaired.^36 The competitive implications of Sublime's collapse for the cement-major buyer universe are analyzed in the Tension Point Analysis. Summit Materials' calcined clay cement project, which held a $215M DOE OCED grant for a four-plant deployment, saw its grant cancelled in the same May 2025 rescission.^37 Heidelberg Materials' $500M DOE grant for carbon capture at its Mitchell, Indiana cement plant was similarly terminated.^38 Across the entire U.S. green cement technology landscape, Brimstone is the sole company that holds ASTM C150 certification, maintains an active commercial testing program with an investment-grade counterparty, and is advancing toward commercial-scale production. No other entity meets all three criteria.

In December 2025, management announced an expanded product roadmap under the "Fourth Great Refinery" framework, positioning Brimstone as a foundational industrial platform capable of co-producing steel, aluminum, magnesium, titanium, and other critical minerals from the same calcium silicate feedstock — expanding the stated total addressable market from approximately $400B (cement) to $2.4T (all building materials and critical minerals).^39 This narrative repositioning carries clear strategic logic: it broadens the potential investor and buyer universe beyond cement-sector participants to include critical minerals funds, defense-linked capital, and multi-commodity industrial strategics, and explicitly aligns with the Trump administration's March 2025 executive order on critical minerals production.^40

The roadmap must be evaluated with discipline. Steel, magnesium, and titanium co-production have not been demonstrated at any scale — bench, pilot, or otherwise — in any publicly available Brimstone communication.^41 Plant 1 produces only three products: portland cement, alumina, and SCM.^42 The "Fourth Great Refinery" framing represents management's stated strategic direction and pre-Series B narrative positioning, not validated chemistry or committed engineering. An acquirer or investor applying a critical minerals platform multiple should price demonstrated capability (cement, alumina, SCM) separately from aspirational capability (steel, magnesium, titanium) — a distinction quantified in the Valuation Architecture section.
