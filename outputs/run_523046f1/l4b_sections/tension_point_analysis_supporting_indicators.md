### The CFO's Career Is the Strongest Transaction Signal

Tim Rens is either the most precisely qualified CFO in American industrial technology or a serial participant in government funding failures — and the answer is the most consequential personnel diligence item in the Series B process.

Rens joined Brimstone on October 30, 2025,^1 six months after the company's $189M DOE OCED grant was cancelled. His immediately prior role was CFO of Monolith Materials, where he raised approximately $750M in total capital — including a $300M private placement in July 2022 with J.P. Morgan and Goldman Sachs as placement agents.^2 Monolith subsequently faced its own DOE crisis: the Department cancelled Monolith's $900M+ conditional loan guarantee under the Loan Programs Office, and the Wall Street Journal reported in September 2024 that the company was "running out of cash."^3 Rens departed during this period. He has now experienced DOE funding cancellations at two consecutive employers.

For skeptics, the pattern raises institutional credibility questions. For practitioners who have closed industrial project finance transactions, the pattern reads differently: Rens executed a $300M institutional placement under conditions structurally identical to Brimstone's current situation, with the same investment banks that would logically lead Brimstone's Series B. His investor network from the Monolith capital stack — including TPG Rise Climate, Warburg Pincus, and Decarbonization Partners^4 — maps directly onto the climate-infrastructure PE firms most likely to anchor a $100–200M+ raise. Across his career at CVR Energy (NYSE: CVI), Philadelphia Energy Solutions, and Monolith, Rens has raised nearly $20 billion.^5

The Monolith $300M placement is the operational template for Brimstone's Series B: a pre-revenue industrial technology company, government funding recently cancelled, first-of-kind plant financing required, JPM and Goldman as agents. Whether JPM's sustainable finance desk and Goldman's clean energy group view the parallel as "proven playbook" or "damaged goods" is determinable through a single channel check — and that assessment governs the process timeline, syndicate quality, and achievable valuation. As detailed in the management team analysis in the Company Operating Profile, the Rens hire is the clearest pre-transaction preparation signal in the entire company timeline.

**Monitoring trigger:** Engagement letter or mandate announcement by J.P. Morgan or Goldman Sachs for a Brimstone private placement; alternatively, any SEC Form D filing naming either bank as a broker-dealer of record.

### The Series B May Already Be Partially Committed

The observable pattern of Brimstone's capital formation activity over the past nine months is more consistent with a soft-closed first tranche awaiting a milestone trigger than with a fundraise that has not yet begun.

Four data points form the pattern. First, in September 2025, CEO Finke stated that Brimstone "has investors in place to help it continue building"^6 — language inconsistent with a company that has disclosed only $80M in cumulative fundraising against a $400M plant financing requirement. Second, Kajima Ventures appeared in the company's investor roster for the first time in the October 2025 CFO announcement, embedded within a broader press release rather than disclosed as a standalone funding event — and absent from all prior investor lists including the January 2025 alumina co-production disclosure.^7 Third, Finke secured a speaking slot at CERAWeek 2026 (March 23–27),^8 the institutional energy infrastructure investor conference where capital formation processes are launched. Fourth, a CFO with $20 billion in career capital raises was hired the same month Kajima's investment was disclosed.

Crunchbase's predictive model — assessing fundraising probability based on time since last round, sector, and comparable company trajectories — assigned a 76% probability that Brimstone would raise another round within six months of its assessment date.^9 No SEC Form D has been filed for Brimstone Energy, Inc. or Brimstone Commercial, LLC as of March 2026.^10

The absence of a Form D is itself informative. Under Regulation D, an issuer must file Form D within 15 days of the first sale of securities. Either no securities have been sold — meaning any committed tranche remains pre-close — or the round has been structured outside the Regulation D framework. Analysis suggests the most probable interpretation is a committed first tranche held open pending a specific milestone: Oklahoma site permitting, Reno demonstration plant construction commencement, or formal announcement of a strategic co-investor. If correct, the advisory window is not opening in Q2 2026 — it is closing. Any strategic investor or advisor not already in active dialogue with management is competing for allocation in an existing syndicate, not for a lead position in a new process. The transaction timing implications are developed in the Transaction Pathways section.

**Monitoring trigger:** SEC Form D filing by Brimstone Energy, Inc. or Brimstone Commercial, LLC — which would confirm round size, date of first sale, and number of investors within 15 days of close.

### Amrize Holds Debt, Not Equity — It Can Walk Away Cleanly

Amrize Industries' Sublime Systems position is structurally distinct from CRH's, and the instrument difference determines each company's freedom to engage with Brimstone.

Amrize's Form 10-Q filed with the SEC discloses its Sublime investment as "a convertible note and advance payments for future supplies"^11 — debt, not equity. In any Sublime wind-down or asset sale, Amrize's convertible note carries creditor priority over CRH's equity stake, meaning Amrize recovers principal before CRH recovers anything. The 10-Q language states Amrize "may participate" in additional Sublime funding rounds^12 — a permissive posture, not a committed one. The advance payments for future supplies represent a recoverable contract claim, not a sunk equity investment. Amrize can exit Sublime with its capital substantially intact and without the governance entanglement — board seats, co-development commitments, potential non-compete provisions — that constrains CRH, as analyzed in the product risk and competitive position section of this report.

This structural flexibility creates a paradox. Amrize can most easily pivot to Brimstone, yet it faces the most direct competitive conflict. Amrize's conventional cement capacity expansion in the Dallas-Fort Worth metropolitan area^13 places new volume into the same Southern U.S. market that Brimstone's Oklahoma plant would serve. A Brimstone equity investment by Amrize requires the board to address cannibalization risk explicitly — green cement competing with its own expanding conventional capacity in an adjacent geography.

CRH faces the inverse constraint. CRH holds equity in Sublime — estimated at ~$37.5M as part of the combined $75M round with Holcim^14 — with co-development commitments that may include exclusivity provisions covering C150-compliant green cement technology. CRH cannot invest in Brimstone without first resolving its Sublime contractual obligations. However, CRH has no conflicting conventional capacity expansion in the Oklahoma or Southern Plains market. Its Ash Grove subsidiary operates 12 U.S./Canada cement plants with geographic presence in the South-Central corridor,^15 and CRH's $2.1B acquisition of Eco Material Technologies confirms its appetite for cement technology transactions at scale.

Net assessment: CRH is the strategically superior Brimstone partner but the more legally constrained one. Amrize is the financially available partner with the clearest cannibalization tension. The buyer profiles in the Transaction Pathways section reflect this distinction.

**Monitoring trigger:** Amrize investor day (March 25, 2026) — any disclosure regarding Sublime Systems impairment testing, convertible note valuation adjustment, or alternative clean cement technology strategy signals Amrize's directional intent.

### The §45X Tax Credit May Not Apply — But the Purity Answer Is Diagnostic

The §45X Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit for critical minerals requires conversion to a minimum purity of 99% alumina by mass.^16 The commercial standard for smelter-grade alumina is ≥98.5% Al₂O₃.^17 The half-percentage-point gap between the SGA commercial threshold and the statutory tax credit threshold creates a diagnostic wedge with information value disproportionate to the credit's dollar magnitude.

The credit itself is financially modest at Plant 1 scale — approximately $600K–$1.2M per year at 10% of eligible production costs on 20,000 tonnes of alumina.^18 [Pokee estimate: 10% × estimated fully loaded production cost of $300–600/t × 20,000 t/yr; methodology consistent with the co-production cost allocation presented in the Valuation Architecture section.] The diagnostic value, however, is substantial. If Brimstone's alumina measures at 98.5–98.9% purity — meeting SGA commercial specification but falling below the §45X threshold — that result simultaneously confirms the product's commercial viability and reveals the precision boundary of the purification process. Conversely, purity exceeding 99% unambiguously confirms SGA quality and activates the tax credit. The purity answer, obtained at negligible cost through a standard Certificate of Analysis, resolves a significant portion of the alumina specification debate that anchors the entire valuation range.

A structural complication narrows the credit's utility regardless of purity. Section 45X transferability — the mechanism by which pre-tax-profit companies monetize credits through sale to tax-liable counterparties — expires December 31, 2027.^19 Brimstone's Oklahoma plant targets production "before 2030," almost certainly after the transferability window closes. A pre-revenue company generating §45X credits in 2029 or 2030 cannot monetize them without internal tax liability — rendering the credit worthless on a standalone basis.

The structural solution is a linked investment with a tax-liable critical minerals producer. Century Aluminum received a $75M direct payment for FY2024 §45X credits and carries a $173M receivable for combined 2023 and 2025 production.^20 A Century or EGA co-investment in Brimstone — or an alumina offtake structured to include tax credit pass-through provisions — would provide the internal tax capacity to absorb Brimstone's credits without reliance on transferability. This reinforces the strategic logic of the EGA/Century partnership analyzed in the demand architecture section. The §45X credit is not the reason for the partnership, but its monetization is an incremental structural benefit achievable only through it.

**Monitoring trigger:** Any Brimstone disclosure of alumina purity to the nearest tenth of a percent — whether in a press release, investor presentation, technical publication, or §45X eligibility determination request — resolves both the tax credit question and a material portion of the SGA specification debate.

### SCG Could Acquire Sublime's IP and Compress the Clock

Siam Cement Group — Thailand's largest industrial conglomerate and Southeast Asia's dominant cement and building materials producer — holds an early-stage investment in Sublime Systems predating the CRH/Holcim $75M round.^21 With Sublime reduced to approximately 28 employees as of March 2026 and the Holyoke demonstration plant paused indefinitely,^22 the company's primary residual value is its electrochemical process IP and patent portfolio. SCG has both the strategic interest in low-carbon cement technology for Southeast Asian markets — where the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism and emerging regional equivalents are creating demand — and the financial capacity to acquire that IP in a distressed or structured transaction.

The scenario where SCG acquires Sublime's IP creates a specific competitive dynamic for CRH and Amrize. Both companies lose their Sublime investments — CRH's equity becomes worthless; Amrize's convertible note recovers only partial principal in a fire-sale transaction — and simultaneously lose the technology option: the ability to license or deploy Sublime's electrochemical process in their own future plants. The green cement technology portfolio reverts to zero for both companies, with the only remaining ASTM-certified alternative being Brimstone.

If this dynamic materializes, it compresses the CRH and Amrize approach timeline from an open-ended assessment period to a hard deadline. Once SCG is known to be negotiating with Sublime's board, CRH and Amrize face a binary choice: secure a Brimstone strategic position before their last technology option disappears, or accept permanent exclusion from the next-generation green cement landscape. Preliminary indicators point to a timeline compression from "sometime in 2026" to Q2 2026 — particularly given that Sublime's workforce contraction from 128 to ~28 employees in four months^23 follows a trajectory consistent with a company preparing for an IP sale or wind-down rather than an operational restart. The buyer universe analysis in the Transaction Pathways section incorporates this competitive urgency as a potential timeline accelerant, and SCG is identified there as a named participant in the broader competitive landscape.

**Monitoring trigger:** Any change in Sublime Systems' board composition — particularly the addition of a director affiliated with an Asian industrial conglomerate — or the engagement of an investment bank or restructuring advisor, either of which would signal a formal process for the IP portfolio.
