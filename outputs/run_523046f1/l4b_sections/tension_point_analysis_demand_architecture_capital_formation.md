## III-B. Tension Point Analysis — Demand Architecture & Capital Formation

Three structural features of Brimstone's operating environment transform the company from a technology thesis into a financeable industrial project — and each carries an independent clock. The EGA/Century Aluminum smelter at Inola, Oklahoma creates a 1.05-million-tonne annual alumina deficit 180 miles from Brimstone's plant, with infrastructure design locking within months and no supply agreement disclosed. The federal government's simultaneous decision to maintain Century's $500 million OCED grant while cancelling Brimstone's $189 million award documents a policy incoherence that functions not as a grievance but as Brimstone's strongest political argument — and opens alternative financing pathways through the Department of Defense that did not exist under the original grant. Kajima Ventures' previously undisclosed investment signals a potential EPC relationship with Japan's largest general contractor, whose A+ credit rating could provide the sponsor guarantee that unlocks government debt on terms no private lender matches. Together, these findings answer three questions the technology analysis cannot: where does the demand come from, how does the plant get built, and who pays.

---

### The EGA/Century Smelter Creates a Structural Alumina Deficit 180 Miles from Brimstone's Plant

The first new primary aluminum smelter in the United States since 1980 is being developed within trucking distance of Brimstone's planned Oklahoma facility, and its alumina supply chain has not been secured. This geographic alignment — combined with the smelter's scale, the near-total absence of domestic alumina production, and irreversible utility infrastructure commitments already underway — creates a structural demand anchor for Brimstone's alumina that no other domestic producer can replicate.

EGA and Century Aluminum signed a joint development agreement in January 2026 for a 750,000-tonne-per-year aluminum smelter at Inola, Oklahoma, structured as a 60/40 joint venture.^1 At the standard 2:1 alumina-to-aluminum production ratio, the facility will require approximately 1.5 million tonnes of alumina annually.^2 No alumina supply agreement has been disclosed. EGA's existing eight-year Alcoa contract — covering up to 15.6 million metric tonnes of Western Australian alumina — explicitly serves UAE smelter operations, not Inola.^3 The United States has one operational alumina refinery: Noranda's Gramercy, Louisiana plant, running at approximately one-third of nameplate capacity.^4 After accounting for Gramercy's output and existing contractual commitments, the net domestic alumina deficit created by the Inola smelter exceeds 1.05 million tonnes per year.^5

The smelter's construction certainty is independently confirmed by the capital behavior of its regulated utility supplier. Public Service Company of Oklahoma has committed in excess of $3.2 billion in power infrastructure to serve the Inola industrial corridor: the acquisition of the 795 MW Green Country gas-fired generation facility, approval of the 995.5 MW Fuel-Free Power Plan adding nearly 1 GW of renewable capacity, and associated transmission investments.^6 Oklahoma Corporation Commissioner David publicly cited the EGA smelter as justification for the Green Country acquisition during regulatory proceedings, despite the smelter not appearing in PSO's formal legal filings and testimony.^7 A regulated utility subject to Oklahoma Corporation Commission rate review does not make $3.2 billion in capital commitments for a project that might not be built. These investments, combined with the state's $275 million incentive package and discounted power rate agreements, represent irreversible infrastructure sunk cost that would be stranded without the smelter.^8

Brimstone's Roosevelt quarry feedstock site — secured through the Dolese Bros. letter of intent in June 2025 — is located 180–220 miles from Inola by truck, within standard industrial alumina delivery range.^9 Plant 1's 20,000-tonne-per-year alumina output represents 1.3% of Inola's total requirement: insufficient as a sole source, but significant as a first domestic supply partner carrying a structural cost advantage embedded in the process itself.

The co-production cost advantage does not depend on any individual policy, subsidy, or tariff. At an estimated co-production cost of $125–225 per tonne — reflecting shared mining, grinding, and leaching costs allocated primarily to cement as the highest-volume product — versus imported landed alumina at approximately $460–500 per tonne in Oklahoma, the delivered cost advantage ranges from $235 to $375 per tonne.^10 At Plant 1 scale, the annual margin contribution from this advantage is $5–7.5 million [20,000 t × $250–375/t midrange].^11 At a ten-plant deployment producing 200,000 tonnes of alumina per year, the annual co-production premium reaches approximately $60 million [$300/t average cost advantage × 200,000 t], generating a net present value of approximately $589 million over twenty years at an 8% discount rate [$60M × 9.818 present value annuity factor].^12 These cost advantage figures feed directly into the scenario analysis presented in the Valuation Architecture section of this report; the co-production cost allocation methodology — which has not been independently validated — is addressed in the treatment of co-production economics in the Transaction Mechanics analysis.

**Execution mechanics and time-sensitivity.** Bechtel is conducting the front-end engineering and design study for the Inola smelter.^13 The FEED determines alumina receiving infrastructure design — specifying whether the facility accepts alumina by barge, truck, rail, or some combination. Standard FEED timelines for alumina receiving infrastructure indicate a design lock within 6–12 months of commencement.^14 If a domestic truck-delivery pathway is not specified during the FEED phase, the smelter may be designed exclusively for barge receipt of imported alumina — permanently excluding truck-delivered domestic supply from the physical plant layout. This is the most time-sensitive structural risk in Brimstone's demand thesis: not whether the smelter will be built, but whether Brimstone's delivery pathway will be physically accommodated in the final design.

Separately, EGA is organizing a $5 billion multi-tranche debt financing for Inola.^15 Standard smelter project finance requires lenders to verify alumina supply as a bankability condition. EGA must execute an alumina supply agreement before or concurrent with debt close. The window for Brimstone to enter the Inola supply ecosystem — before EGA locks in a long-term imported alumina contract that satisfies lender requirements — is narrowing on the same 6–12 month timeline as the FEED design lock.

**Confidence assessment.** Confidence is high that structural demand exists and the smelter will proceed — the $3.2 billion in PSO infrastructure commitments alone confirm this at the level required for transaction planning. Confidence is medium on whether Brimstone enters the supply conversation before EGA finalizes imported alumina arrangements. The "mine to metal" language in Brimstone's June 2025 press release — issued six weeks after EGA's original Oklahoma announcement, in the same paragraph referencing the smelter — is consistent with active positioning, but does not confirm direct commercial engagement.^16

### The Federal Government Simultaneously Funds the Smelter and Cancelled the Alumina Supply

The federal government is funding the construction of a facility that will consume 1.5 million tonnes of alumina per year while having cancelled the only announced domestic alumina production project in the same state. This policy incoherence is not a grievance — it is Brimstone's single strongest political argument and creates financing optionality through Department of Defense pathways that did not exist under the original OCED grant framework.

Century Aluminum's $500 million OCED cooperative agreement for the Inola smelter has been actively maintained through February 2026, with no indication of cancellation or reduction.^17 Brimstone's $189 million OCED grant — for an alumina production facility located in the same state, serving the same supply chain — was cancelled in May 2025; $8.7 million of Phase 1 funding was disbursed before termination, and an administrative appeal remains active.^18 The same administration's December 2025 White House aluminum strategy brief explicitly calls for developing the domestic aluminum supply chain.^19 The March 2025 executive order on critical minerals production classifies alumina as a critical mineral warranting accelerated domestic production.^20 The contradiction — fund the consumer of alumina, cancel the producer of alumina — is documented across government filings and executive orders issued within months of each other.

This documented incoherence creates alternative financing pathways through the Department of Defense's critical minerals infrastructure.

The DOD deployed $777 million through the Defense Industrial Base Cooperation mechanism in FY2025 alone, across 14 investments with individual awards ranging from $7 million to $400 million.^21 The MP Materials precedent establishes the operative template: $400 million in DOD equity investment combined with a $150 million Office of Strategic Capital loan, structured to secure domestic rare earth processing capacity.^22 Alumina — classified as a critical mineral under the same executive order that maintained Century's grant — fits within the DOD's stated priorities for supply chain de-risking. A DOD Industrial Base Fund equity investment of $80–200 million in Brimstone would fill 20–50% of the Plant 1 financing gap without diluting private equity at the rates a purely commercial round would require.^23 This pathway must be referenced alongside the transaction structures discussed in the Buyer Universe section of this report.

The Office of Strategic Capital offers loan terms that no private lender can match: U.S. Treasury rates (currently approximately 4.3–4.5%), tenors of up to fifty years, and loan-to-value coverage of up to 80%.^24 On $100 million of project debt, the interest rate differential between OSC Treasury rates and private project finance credit (typically SOFR plus 300–500 basis points) saves approximately $3–5 million per year in debt service.^25 The MP Materials OSC loan closed at $150 million, confirming that the program operates at the scale relevant to Brimstone's capital requirements. Separately, the policy incoherence provides direct political leverage through Representative Tom Cole, who chairs the House Appropriations Committee and represents the Oklahoma congressional district where both the smelter and the alumina plant would operate — a specific, fact-based argument for an appropriations rider or directed agency reconsideration.

**The binding constraint is the creditworthy sponsor guarantee.** OSC loan terms require a repayment guarantee from a creditworthy sponsor for pre-revenue borrowers.^26 Brimstone — pre-revenue, with no operating history and no investment-grade balance sheet — cannot access the OSC alone. This creates a structural dependency that governs the entire capital formation sequence: the best government financing requires the best private partner first. The optimal sequencing is strategic equity (providing the creditworthy sponsor), then OSC application (leveraging the sponsor's guarantee for government debt at Treasury rates), then construction financing (combining equity, government debt, and commercial debt into the complete capital stack). Reversing this sequence — seeking government debt before securing a sponsor — is structurally impossible under the program's terms.

**Confidence assessment.** Confidence is high on program terms, the MP Materials precedent, and the documented policy incoherence. Confidence is medium on timing: the first OSC NOFA application window closed February 3, 2025 — before Brimstone's CFO was hired — and the schedule for subsequent NOFAs has not been announced.^27 Confidence is medium on whether Brimstone's management team has formally engaged any DOD pathway. The hire of Tim Rens, whose career at Monolith Materials required navigating an identical DOE loan cancellation, is consistent with a management team that understands these mechanisms, but no public disclosure confirms active applications.

### Kajima's Quiet Investment May Solve the Creditworthy Sponsor Problem

Kajima Ventures' previously undisclosed investment in Brimstone is consistent with an emerging EPC relationship between Brimstone and Japan's largest general contractor — a relationship that, if confirmed, would provide the creditworthy sponsor guarantee required for government debt and collapse the financing complexity identified above. The evidence is circumstantial but directional, and the hypothesis is falsifiable against specific observable events.

Kajima Ventures appeared for the first time in the October 2025 Brimstone investor list, disclosed within the CFO hiring announcement; the name is absent from all prior investor disclosures, all public funding databases, and the January 2025 investor roster that accompanied the alumina co-production announcement.^28 The investment was not reported as a standalone funding event — it was embedded in an unrelated press release, a pattern of deliberate information management consistent with a commercially sensitive relationship. The gap between the last confirmed roster (January 2025, Kajima absent) and the updated roster (October 2025, Kajima present) establishes a nine-month window during which the investment occurred without public announcement.

Kajima Corporation is Japan's largest general contractor by revenue, reporting approximately $15 billion annually, with an A+ credit rating from Rating and Investment Information.^29 The company operates extensive process plant EPC capabilities globally — chemical plants, steelworks, and industrial processing facilities — with active U.S. operations through Kajima USA.^30 Kajima maintains a confirmed trilateral alliance with Bechtel, the same engineering firm conducting the FEED study for EGA's Inola smelter.^31 The pattern of an industrial construction firm taking an equity position in an early-stage technology company ahead of an EPC mandate is established: the EH2 hydrogen precedent, where a construction firm's venture investment preceded its role as commercial facility contractor, provides a direct structural analogy.^32

The timing reinforces the EPC interpretation. Brimstone's Reno demonstration plant was still "currently being engineered" as of December 2025, with construction not yet commenced.^33 The scale-up from Reno pilot throughput to Oklahoma commercial capacity — 80,000 tonnes per year cement plus 20,000 tonnes per year alumina, approximately 275 tonnes per day at nameplate — represents an estimated 40–80x factor in a single step, an aggressive trajectory that would benefit substantially from EPC contractor involvement during the engineering phase rather than solely at construction.^34 A Kajima equity position taken during engineering is consistent with an EPC contractor conducting technical due diligence on the process before committing to a fixed-price performance guarantee — standard practice in first-of-kind industrial facilities.

**Financial magnitude.** The significance of Kajima as EPC sponsor is measured not in investment dollars but in credit transformation. A Kajima Corporation EPC performance guarantee — backed by the parent's A+ credit rating and $15 billion revenue base — would satisfy the creditworthy sponsor requirement for both OSC loans and ATVM program financing.^35 This single corporate guarantee would accomplish what would otherwise require a cement major (CRH, Amrize) or an aluminum producer (Century, EGA) to provide. It decouples the creditworthy sponsor function from the strategic equity function, allowing Brimstone to negotiate strategic partnerships on commercial terms rather than from a position of financing dependency. The sequencing logic presented in the OSC analysis above — strategic equity first, then government debt — simplifies materially: Kajima's existing investment may already satisfy the sponsor requirement, compressing two sequential steps into one.

The relationship also opens commercial markets that no U.S. cement-sector partnership would access. Japan's Green Transformation policy framework mandates cement sector decarbonization with specific procurement incentives for low-carbon construction materials.^36 Kajima, as Japan's dominant contractor and a Brimstone equity investor, becomes the natural channel for Brimstone's technology into the Japanese and broader Asia-Pacific construction market — a geographic expansion that does not compete with any U.S. cement major's existing operations and therefore does not trigger the competitive tensions inherent in a CRH or Amrize partnership.

**Execution mechanics.** If the EPC hypothesis holds, the implications for the broader transaction architecture are material. Brimstone would not need a cement major co-investor to fill the creditworthy sponsor role, freeing CRH or Amrize to invest as strategic equity partners on pure commercial terms rather than as financing guarantors extracting commensurate governance and economic concessions. The capital stack could be assembled from parties with differentiated roles: Kajima as EPC guarantor and OSC sponsor, a cement major as commercial offtake partner and equity investor, DOD as co-equity through the IBF mechanism, and OSC as project debt provider. This layered structure mirrors the MP Materials capital formation, where DOD equity, OSC debt, and strategic partnerships each served distinct functions.

The Bechtel trilateral alliance is connective tissue worth noting. Bechtel is simultaneously conducting the Inola smelter FEED — where alumina receiving infrastructure is being designed — and allied with Kajima, the entity that may serve as Brimstone's EPC contractor. If Bechtel's engineering team is aware of both relationships, the truck-delivery pathway for Brimstone's alumina may already be under consideration within the Inola FEED process, which would address the infrastructure design-lock risk identified in the EGA demand analysis above.

**Confidence assessment.** Confidence is high that Kajima invested — the company name appears in an official Brimstone disclosure with no prior trace, establishing a new investment between January and October 2025. Confidence is medium on the EPC connection: the hypothesis is inferred from Kajima Corporation's corporate strategy, its construction capabilities, the Bechtel trilateral alliance, and the investor-as-builder pattern observed in comparable transactions — not from any confirmed EPC engagement. The hypothesis is falsifiable: a Brimstone EPC contractor announcement naming any firm other than Kajima would invalidate it; an announcement naming Kajima or a Kajima-Bechtel joint venture would confirm it. This is designated as a monitoring trigger in the Risk Architecture section of this report.

**Time-sensitivity across all three findings.** Three clocks run simultaneously, and their interactions define the advisory window. The Bechtel FEED design lock for Inola — determining whether truck-delivered domestic alumina has a physical pathway into the facility — occurs within 6–12 months. The next OSC NOFA, which would open the government debt pathway contingent on a creditworthy sponsor, has not been announced but is expected in 2026 based on the program's stated development trajectory. Kajima's investment terms — including any EPC-related provisions, exclusivity periods, or milestone triggers — will become visible through either a Brimstone EPC contractor announcement or a construction commencement disclosure for the Reno demonstration plant. The sequencing dependency identified across these three findings — demand anchor validates the alumina thesis, creditworthy sponsor unlocks government debt, government debt completes the capital stack — means that delays in any element propagate through the entire formation timeline. The capital structure window is not open indefinitely.
