## III-C. Tension Point Analysis — Transaction Mechanics & Regulatory Optionality

Three structural dynamics determine not just what Brimstone is worth but whether a transaction can close, who can close it, and what external catalysts could shift the economics between signing and funding. Amazon's simultaneous roles as investor, customer, tester, and procurement alliance member generate change-of-control consent rights that function as a structural veto on the buyer universe — certain buyer categories face blocking risk while others face a clear path. A Section 232 alumina tariff petition, unfiled as of this writing despite an established BIS process and aligned industry interests, would create a durable domestic price advantage that transforms Brimstone's multi-plant economics. And the co-production architecture — the fundamental design choice to extract cement and alumina from the same rock — determines whether this company commands a $120M technology-asset valuation or a $285M platform valuation. Each of these findings bridges directly into the valuation scenarios and buyer profiles that follow.

### Amazon's Four Roles Create a Structural Veto on the Buyer Universe

Amazon Climate Pledge Fund's standard protective provision architecture, combined with Amazon's three additional commercial roles at Brimstone, generates change-of-control consent rights that constrain which buyers can close a transaction. This is not a theoretical risk — it is a structural feature of every ACPF portfolio investment and the single most consequential gate in any Brimstone M&A process.

The evidence for Amazon's protective provisions rests on ACPF's disclosed investment architecture at Rivian, the only ACPF portfolio company to have filed an S-1 with full investor rights disclosure.^1 Rivian's registration statement reveals that Amazon held a 14.9% voting cap (the standard threshold below which a CVC avoids triggering Hart-Scott-Rodino and beneficial ownership reporting obligations), board nomination rights, anti-dilution protections, and change-of-control consent provisions.^2 ACPF's investment in Brimstone at Series A — alongside institutional co-leads Breakthrough Energy Ventures and DCVC — carries an estimated 80% or greater probability of including equivalent protective provisions.^3 Standard CVC deal architecture at this investment scale includes assignment restrictions on the portfolio company's material commercial agreements, meaning Brimstone's reservation agreement with Amazon is almost certainly non-assignable without Amazon's written consent.

Amazon's informational advantage compounds the legal provisions. Through its proprietary slab testing program — designed in collaboration with Amazon's concrete consultants at Twining Consulting to evaluate Brimstone's cement against Amazon-specific mix designs^4 — Amazon possesses performance data that no competing buyer can replicate without beginning its own multi-year testing program. Amazon knows the compressive strength profiles, workability characteristics, and durability parameters of Brimstone's cement in Amazon's actual construction applications. A competing buyer entering diligence starts from zero on product qualification; Amazon starts from two years of proprietary data. This asymmetry is structural and cannot be closed through a data room.

Amazon's founding membership in the Sustainable Concrete Buyers Alliance (SCoBA) adds a procurement governance layer distinct from its investor and customer roles.^5 SCoBA, administered by RMI and the Global Manufacturing Alliance, operates a collective procurement framework through which founding members coordinate demand signals and award early adoption commitments to qualifying cement producers. Amazon's SCoBA membership gives it influence over Brimstone's positioning in a multi-buyer procurement ecosystem — a form of soft governance that survives any change of control and reinforces Amazon's role as the company's primary commercial reference.

The financial magnitude of Amazon's consent rights is not measurable in dollars. It is a binary gate: Amazon consent determines which buyer categories can close a transaction. The consent probability varies sharply by buyer type.

| Buyer Category | Example | Competitive Conflict | Estimated Consent Probability | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cement major | CRH, Amrize, Heidelberg | High — acquirer controls Amazon's cement supply | 15–25% unencumbered; higher with conditions | Amazon loses procurement diversification; supply chain concentration risk |
| Aluminum-sector strategic | EGA, Century Aluminum | None — buyer wants alumina, not cement | 80–90% | Amazon's cement relationship continues uninterrupted |
| Financial sponsor | TPG Rise Climate, BEV Catalyst | None — no operating conflict | 70–80% with covenants | Consent conditioned on supply priority, reservation term maintenance |
| Government/DOD co-investment | OSC, DIBC | None | 85–95% | Non-control investment; Amazon retains all commercial rights |

A cement major acquisition presents the highest friction. CRH, which holds equity in Sublime Systems^6 and derives significant revenue from DOT-funded infrastructure projects through its Ash Grove division, would gain operational control over Amazon's cement supply relationship — creating the precise supply chain concentration risk that Amazon's SCoBA membership and diversified procurement strategy are designed to prevent. Amazon's most rational response to a CRH acquisition is to condition consent on binding supply commitments at pre-acquisition terms, board observation rights, and possibly a standstill on cement pricing. An aluminum-sector buyer, by contrast, triggers no competitive conflict: EGA or Century Aluminum would acquire Brimstone for the alumina stream, leaving the cement product and Amazon's reservation agreement undisturbed.

The most elegant transaction resolution — and the one most favorable for project finance bankability — occurs when Amazon converts its non-binding reservation agreement to a binding take-or-pay offtake as a condition of consent. A binding Amazon contract at investment-grade credit quality is the single most bankable commercial instrument available to any Brimstone project finance structure. An acquirer or strategic investor that negotiates this conversion as part of the consent process would simultaneously resolve the Amazon governance concern and strengthen the Plant 1 financing case. This dynamic is analyzed further in the transaction pathways discussion.

**Monitoring Trigger:** The next observable event is any SEC filing or press release from an ACPF portfolio company transaction that discloses ACPF's standard consent mechanics. Alternatively, any Brimstone Form D filing that lists Amazon Climate Pledge Fund as a participant in a new round would confirm the continuation of Amazon's investor role and its associated rights into the current capital structure.

### A Section 232 Alumina Tariff Petition Would Transform the Domestic Economics

Adding alumina to Section 232 tariff coverage — through an established BIS petition process that requires no Congressional action — would create a durable domestic price advantage for Brimstone's tariff-immune production. The mechanism exists, the filing cost is negligible, industry interests are aligned, and no stakeholder has filed the petition. This is an unclaimed regulatory option with asymmetric upside.

The Bureau of Industry and Security published a final rule on April 30, 2025 establishing a formal Section 232 Inclusions Process, accepting petitions to add new products to existing Section 232 tariff coverage in two-week filing windows three times per year.^7 The current Section 232 tariff on aluminum imports is 50%.^8 Alumina — the intermediate product refined from bauxite and fed into aluminum smelters — is not currently covered. The United States imports approximately 1.1 million tonnes of alumina annually, primarily from Australia, Brazil, and Jamaica.^9 A successful petition to include alumina (HTS 2818.10) as a covered derivative aluminum article would impose the existing 50% tariff on all imported alumina, creating an immediate price floor that domestic production sits beneath.

The legal durability of Section 232 has been dramatically strengthened by a development most market participants have not yet internalized. In February 2026, the Supreme Court struck down tariffs imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA), ruling that IEEPA's national emergency authorities do not extend to the imposition of import duties.^10 This ruling eliminated the executive branch's alternative tariff authority and confirmed Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962 as the sole durable legal basis for national security-justified tariffs. For any domestic producer seeking tariff protection, the implication is definitive: Section 232 is the only pathway that survives judicial review, and BIS's Inclusions Process is the only administrative mechanism for extending that protection to new product categories. Tariff strategies built on IEEPA authority — including several that had been applied to aluminum derivative products — must now be reconstructed through Section 232 or abandoned entirely.

The financial magnitude at Plant 1 is meaningful but not transformational. At 20,000 t/yr of alumina production, a 50% tariff on imported alumina at $460–500/t landed cost raises the effective import price by $230–250/t.^11 Brimstone's domestic production is tariff-immune. The resulting competitive advantage is approximately $200–300/t, or $4–6M per year at Plant 1 nameplate — a 20–30% revenue uplift.^12 At multi-plant scale, the calculus shifts decisively: ten plants producing 200,000 t/yr of alumina at a $200–300/t tariff-induced advantage generates $40–60M per year in additional margin, enough to fund plant construction from operating cash flow. The tariff scenario impact on the full valuation range is detailed in the Valuation Architecture section.

The coalition dynamics for filing the petition are unusually favorable. Century Aluminum — the U.S. partner in the Inola smelter joint venture — has publicly advocated for domestic alumina supply security and is actively building the largest new source of alumina demand in the United States.^13 EGA, Century's 60% partner, has identical interests in protecting the economics of the Inola investment from imported alumina price volatility. The Aluminum Association has historically supported domestic supply chain resilience, though its downstream fabricator members have mixed interests in higher alumina input costs.^14 A coalition petition — filed jointly by Century, EGA, and the Aluminum Association with Brimstone's domestic production as the named supply response — carries substantially more weight with BIS than a single-company filing. The next filing window opens approximately April 2026.

The petition itself costs essentially nothing to file. BIS review is discretionary — Commerce may decline to investigate — but the mere filing creates a signal to project finance lenders and strategic investors that a durable price floor may be established, de-risking Brimstone's multi-plant economics before any tariff is actually imposed. The strategic value of the petition exceeds the direct financial value of the tariff.

**Monitoring Trigger:** Any BIS filing in the Section 232 Inclusions Process docket by the Aluminum Association, Century Aluminum, EGA, or any alumina-consuming industry group. The next filing window is approximately April 2026. Absence of a filing through the April window would indicate either an awareness gap or active opposition from downstream aluminum fabricators.

### The Co-Production Architecture Determines Whether This Is a $120M Company or a $285M Platform

Brimstone's valuation hinges on whether a buyer models the company as a single-product green cement startup or a co-production platform with three distinct asset classes. The co-production architecture — the design choice to extract cement and alumina from the same rock through shared mining, grinding, and leaching operations — generates a structural cost advantage on alumina of $200–400/t below imported supply. This advantage is embedded in the process, not contingent on any individual subsidy, tariff, or policy. A buyer modeling only Plant 1 cement economics systematically undervalues the platform by 2–3x.

The redesign of Plant 1 makes the economic logic explicit. The DOE OCED grant application in March 2024 described a facility producing 140,000 t/yr of portland cement.^15 The current specification — 80,000 t/yr of cement plus 20,000 t/yr of alumina^16 — represents a 43% reduction in cement capacity with the simultaneous introduction of a higher-value-per-tonne product. This disaggregation reflects alumina's economics: at smelter-grade pricing of $480/t, a tonne of alumina generates 3.4x the revenue of a tonne of cement at $140/t.^17 The plant was rebalanced toward the product that carries the value, not the product that carries the volume.

The co-production cost structure explains why. In a conventional alumina refinery operating the Bayer process, production costs run $560–585/t on a landed basis in Oklahoma, reflecting bauxite mining, caustic soda consumption, energy-intensive digestion, and transoceanic shipping.^18 Brimstone's process extracts alumina as a co-product from the same rock already being mined and ground for cement production. The marginal cost of alumina extraction — the incremental acid consumption, separation, and calcination steps beyond the shared processing already required for cement — is estimated at $125–225/t.^19 The structural cost advantage is $200–400/t on a delivered basis, depending on the final process configuration and energy costs. This is not a function of any subsidy or tariff; it is a thermodynamic consequence of the co-production architecture, analogous to the economics that CEO Finke has drawn explicitly in public statements: Freeport-McMoRan's copper mines generate 15–25% of revenue from byproduct gold and molybdenum extracted from the same ore body, and Exxon's refineries produce dozens of distinct products from a single crude oil stream.^20

Brimstone's enterprise should be valued as three distinct assets, each requiring a different analytical lens:

**Process Technology IP** encompasses the ASTM C150 regulatory moat (the only certification for non-limestone-feedstock cement in the world), six filed patents,^21 trade-secret process integration know-how, and a proprietary feedstock selection database derived from screening 23,000+ U.S. quarries.^22 This asset is valued on option value and defensibility — the relevant comparison is a process technology licensor, not a commodity manufacturer. The ASTM C150 certification alone, which addresses 100% of the U.S. cement market including the ~35–40% that is DOT-specified infrastructure, is a regulatory barrier that no competitor has replicated.

**Domestic Alumina Supply Franchise** describes Brimstone's position as the only announced domestic non-bauxite alumina source in the United States, co-located 180 miles from the first new aluminum smelter built since 1980.^23 This asset is valued on strategic infrastructure premium — the relevant comparison is how the Department of Defense values MP Materials' rare earth supply chain position, not how the commodities market values a tonne of alumina. The EGA/Century smelter's 1.5M t/yr alumina requirement, with no disclosed domestic supply agreement, creates a demand anchor analyzed in detail in the demand architecture discussion.

**Oklahoma Plant** is the physical manufacturing facility: 80,000 t/yr cement, 20,000 t/yr alumina, ~$378–400M total investment.^24 This asset is valued conventionally — on projected EBITDA at nameplate capacity, discount rates, and replacement cost. At smelter-grade alumina pricing, Plant 1 generates estimated EBITDA of $7.6M at a 35% blended margin on $21.8M of revenue.^25 At chemical-grade alumina pricing, EBITDA falls to approximately $4.1M on $16.2M of revenue — a 46% compression driven entirely by the alumina product classification analyzed in the product risk section.^26

The three-asset framework produces a valuation range of $80–285M. At the floor ($80–120M), only the cement IP and Amazon reservation carry value — alumina is chemical-grade, no smelter demand is captured, and the plant is valued at 7x cement-only EBITDA. At the top ($~285M), the co-production platform is validated, EGA alumina demand is contracted, and the franchise premium on domestic alumina supply justifies a 15x blended EBITDA multiple plus scale-up option value. The methodology and scenario assumptions are detailed in the Valuation Architecture section, and the component inputs — $21.8M revenue, $7.6M EBITDA, $4.1M cement-only EBITDA — match the unit economics presented in the Company Operating Profile.

At scale, the co-production economics become transformational. Ten plants producing 200,000 t/yr of alumina at a $300/t average cost advantage over imported supply generate $60M per year in co-production alpha — recurring margin that a single-product cement company cannot access.^27 Capitalized over 20 years at an 8% discount rate, this stream alone carries a net present value exceeding the entire base-case Plant 1 valuation. The "Fourth Great Refinery" roadmap — extending the co-production logic to steel, magnesium, and titanium — further expands the theoretical platform value, but none of these additional products have been demonstrated at any scale.^28 The co-production cost allocation itself has not been independently validated; the $125–225/t marginal alumina cost is a company estimate, and the allocation methodology between shared mining/grinding/leaching costs and product-specific processing costs has not been reviewed by any third-party engineering firm.^29

A buyer or investor modeling only Plant 1 — a single facility producing 80,000 tonnes of cement and 20,000 tonnes of alumina — will arrive at a valuation reflecting a demonstration-scale manufacturing asset. A buyer modeling the platform — the process IP applicable to hundreds of quarry sites, the domestic alumina franchise embedded in a co-production architecture with structural cost advantages, and a multi-plant buildout trajectory — will arrive at a valuation 2–3x higher. The framing a buyer adopts is the valuation the buyer pays.

**Monitoring Trigger:** The first independently validated alumina production cost data from Reno pilot operations — or any engagement of a third-party engineering firm (Wood Group, KBR, Jacobs) to conduct a co-production cost allocation study — would confirm or invalidate the $125–225/t marginal cost estimate that underpins the entire platform valuation thesis. Absent independent validation, the co-production cost advantage remains a theoretical construct, not an engineering fact.
