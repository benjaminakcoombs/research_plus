## III-A. Tension Point Analysis — Product Risk & Competitive Position

Three findings define the technical and competitive risks embedded in Brimstone's product portfolio — and together they govern whether this company is valued as a critical minerals platform or a green cement startup. The alumina revenue stream, projected at 44% of Plant 1 revenue at smelter-grade pricing, rests on zero independently validated specification data across any of the thirteen parameters that aluminum smelters require for acceptance. The geological character of the Roosevelt quarry feedstock introduces a specific, quantifiable ceiling on achievable alumina purity that no public disclosure addresses. And the collapse of Sublime Systems, combined with Brimstone's singular ASTM C150 certification, creates a once-in-a-cycle replacement-asset premium — but the most strategically logical acquirer faces contractual constraints that less natural buyers do not. The combined effect is a 2–3x enterprise value swing, from approximately $80–120M to $230–285M, determined almost entirely by the resolution of a technical question that an acquirer could answer for $50–150K in independent laboratory work.

### The Alumina Revenue Stream Has Zero Independent Validation

**Hypothesis.** Brimstone claims to produce "smelter-grade alumina" (SGA) as a co-product of its Rock Refinery process.^1 If true, the alumina commands $400–500/t and positions the company as a domestic critical minerals producer with national security relevance. If the alumina meets only chemical-grade specification (CGA), it is worth $150–250/t, and the company is a green cement startup with a low-value byproduct. After six years of development and more than $80M raised, no independent data supports the smelter-grade designation.

**Evidence.** Commercial SGA must satisfy thirteen specification parameters to be accepted by an aluminum smelter operating Hall-Héroult electrolysis cells. These include chemical purity (Al₂O₃ ≥98.5%), impurity limits (Fe₂O₃ <200 ppm, SiO₂ <150 ppm, TiO₂ <30 ppm, Na₂O <4,000 ppm, CaO <400 ppm, P₂O₅ <10 ppm, ZnO <50 ppm, V₂O₅ <30 ppm), physical properties (BET surface area 50–120 m²/g, loss on ignition <1.0%, attrition index <7%), and crystal phase composition (γ/θ-Al₂O₃ dominant, ≥85% combined).^2 Brimstone has published zero of these thirteen parameters. Every press release since January 2025 uses the phrase "smelter grade alumina" without qualification, but no certificate of analysis, no third-party assay, and no smelter trial result has been disclosed.^3

The contrast with the cement product is instructive. Brimstone's portland cement was independently certified to ASTM C150 by Twining Consulting, then subjected to proprietary slab testing by Amazon's construction engineers against Amazon-specific mix designs — a two-stage independent validation process.^4 Customer engagement language consistently names "cement and SCM" when describing testing programs; alumina is never referenced in any testing disclosure.^5

The patent record introduces a more specific technical concern. Brimstone's core process patent describes calcination of aluminum sulfate (Al₂(SO₄)₃) to produce alumina — a fundamentally different precursor pathway than the standard Bayer process, which calcines aluminum hydroxide (Al(OH)₃).^6 Sulfate calcination at standard temperatures (~900°C) produces η-Al₂O₃ (eta-alumina), not the γ/θ-Al₂O₃ phase mixture that smelter-grade specification requires.^7 The crystal phase is not a cosmetic distinction. It determines BET surface area, hydrogen fluoride adsorption efficiency, and dissolution rate in molten cryolite — the three physical properties governing performance in a Hall-Héroult cell. Eta-alumina exhibits lower surface area and slower dissolution characteristics than gamma-alumina, rendering it functionally unsuitable for smelter operations even if chemical purity is achieved. Producing γ-dominant alumina from a sulfate precursor requires either specific precipitating agents (e.g., ammonium aluminum carbonate hydroxide intermediates) or precision multi-stage calcination control — process steps not described in the published patent.^8

No aluminum producer — not EGA, not Century Aluminum, not Alcoa — has signed any supply agreement, offtake reservation, or qualification testing protocol with Brimstone.^9

**Financial magnitude.** At Plant 1 nameplate capacity of 20,000 t/yr, the pricing differential between SGA ($480/t) and CGA ($200/t) produces approximately $5.6M in annual revenue variance.^10 Total Plant 1 revenue shifts from $21.8M at SGA pricing to approximately $16.2M at CGA pricing — a 26% decline driven entirely by alumina product classification.^11

The effect on enterprise value is not linear. Alumina grade determines both EBITDA and the applicable valuation multiple. At SGA pricing, Plant 1 projected EBITDA is $7.6M at a blended 35% margin; a "critical minerals platform" multiple of 12x yields $91M in operating value before strategic premiums. At CGA pricing, alumina contributes minimal margin; cement-only EBITDA falls to approximately $4.1M; a cement-technology multiple of 7x yields $29M before premiums.^12 When the ASTM C150 regulatory moat premium ($35–65M), EGA demand optionality ($25–50M), and scale-up option value are layered into the scenario analysis detailed in the Valuation Architecture section, the enterprise value range spans approximately $80–120M at the floor (CGA, cement-only thesis) to $230–285M at the base-to-bull case (SGA, critical minerals platform thesis) — a 2–3x swing.^13

Over a 20-year plant life at an 8% discount rate, the net present value of the alumina revenue differential alone is approximately $49M [$5M/yr × 9.818 present value annuity factor].^14

**Execution mechanics.** Resolving this question is neither expensive nor slow. A diligence program sufficient to confirm or disqualify the smelter-grade designation consists of four steps: (1) an independent laboratory certificate of analysis from pilot-produced alumina covering all thirteen SGA parameters, performed by a recognized minerals testing laboratory; (2) X-ray diffraction analysis confirming γ/θ-Al₂O₃ crystal phase dominance at ≥85% combined; (3) BET surface area measurement confirming 50–120 m²/g; and (4) a 1–5 tonne batch smelter trial at any operating aluminum reduction facility, confirming dissolution behavior in cryolite at operational temperatures. Total cost: approximately $50–150K. Timeline: 8–12 weeks from sample delivery.^15 Any investor, acquirer, or lender committing capital to the alumina thesis without this data is pricing an unverified claim.

**Confidence and gaps.** Confidence is high that the data gap exists — six years of public communications, patent filings, press releases, and investor disclosures contain zero SGA specification data points. Confidence is medium on whether this gap reflects a genuine technical barrier or a deliberate trade secret strategy. Brimstone may have achieved full SGA specification at pilot scale and elected to withhold publication pending commercial plant construction — a rational information management decision for a company negotiating with potential strategic partners. Alternatively, the alumina may achieve chemical purity (≥98.5% Al₂O₃) but fail the physical property requirements — wrong crystal phase, insufficient surface area — that determine smelter acceptance. This ambiguity cannot be resolved from the public record and is identified as a named information risk in the Risk Architecture section of this report.

### The Roosevelt Quarry Feedstock Sets a Ceiling on Alumina Purity

**Hypothesis.** Even if Brimstone's process chemistry is capable of producing smelter-grade alumina from an ideal feedstock, the specific geological character of the Dolese Bros. Roosevelt quarry in Kiowa County, Oklahoma — the confirmed feedstock source for Plant 1 — may impose an independent ceiling on achievable alumina purity. The minerals present in the Glen Mountains Layered Complex (GMLC) contain iron and titanium concentrations that require decontamination factors of 100–667x to meet SGA specification — factors that the published patent does not describe achieving.

**Evidence.** The Roosevelt quarry sits within the GMLC, a well-characterized geological formation in the Southern Oklahoma Aulacogen. Oklahoma Geological Survey Circular 82, in its treatment of the Southern Oklahoma Aulacogen, documents the GMLC as containing anorthosite with An₇₀ plagioclase — exceptionally alumina-rich and calcium-rich, representing the ideal feedstock for Brimstone's co-production process — interlayered with ilmenite (FeTiO₃) and magnetite (Fe₃O₄) as accessory minerals within formations the Survey describes as "iron-rich, titanium-rich gabbro."^16

The accessory minerals create the problem. Ilmenite dissolves at 93–97% efficiency under standard hydrochloric acid leaching conditions — the acid environment described in Brimstone's core patent.^17 Titanium from ilmenite enters the leach liquor alongside aluminum and calcium, contaminating the downstream aluminum recovery stream. SGA specification requires TiO₂ below 30 ppm and Fe₂O₃ below 200 ppm.^18 If the GMLC feedrock contains 2–6% Fe₂O₃ (consistent with published geological descriptions of ferrogabbro interlayers) and 0.5–2% TiO₂ (consistent with ilmenite accessory mineral content), the required decontamination factors are 100–300x for iron and 167–667x for titanium.^19

The Brimstone patent describes an acid leaching and precipitation process that separates aluminum from calcium through differential solubility, but it contains no dedicated titanium removal step.^20 Standard hydrometallurgical titanium removal from aluminum-bearing liquors requires either solvent extraction (expensive, complex, generates organic waste streams) or selective precipitation at tightly controlled pH — neither of which is described in the published patent.

One partial mitigant exists. Plagioclase dissolution under acid conditions generates amorphous silica gel, which can physically entrap and inhibit the co-dissolution of titanium-bearing mineral phases.^21 Under specific leaching conditions — low temperature, elevated acid concentration, short residence time — this silica gel inhibition effect could suppress titanium dissolution to levels consistent with SGA specification without a dedicated removal step. This suppression mechanism would need to be deliberately engineered into the process design, operated within a narrow parameter window, and validated at the specific Fe/Ti ratios present in the Roosevelt quarry rock. No public disclosure describes this engineering.

**Financial magnitude.** The pricing consequences are identical to those established in the preceding analysis: if the Roosevelt quarry's geological character constrains the alumina to chemical grade, Plant 1 alumina revenue falls from $9.6M to approximately $4.0M per year, total Plant 1 revenue compresses from $21.8M to approximately $16.2M, and enterprise value contracts by the same 2–3x factor.^22 The geological constraint, if binding, provides the mechanistic explanation for why the specification gap exists — it would indicate not merely a data disclosure gap but a reflection of a genuine feedstock limitation embedded in the site selection.

**Confidence and gaps.** Confidence is high that the geological risk exists. The GMLC mineral assemblage, the dissolution behavior of ilmenite under HCl leaching, and the SGA impurity limits are all well-characterized in published geological and metallurgical literature. Confidence is medium that this constraint is binding on Brimstone's actual process. Two factors introduce uncertainty. First, Brimstone screened over 23,000 quarries before selecting the Roosevelt site, and the selection criteria almost certainly included aluminum content and impurity profile — the company is unlikely to have selected a feedstock that makes its highest-value co-product infeasible.^23 Second, the silica gel inhibition mechanism could provide partial titanium suppression under conditions Brimstone's six years of process development may have engineered into the leaching step. Resolving this uncertainty requires either independent XRF analysis of run-of-mine Roosevelt quarry rock — a $5,000 engagement — or access to Brimstone's internal feedstock characterization data. The geological risk characterization is treated as a monitoring item in the Risk Architecture section of this report.

### Sublime's Collapse and the ASTM C150 Competitive Moat

**Hypothesis.** The operational collapse of Sublime Systems — the only other next-generation cement technology with strategic cement-major backing — combined with Brimstone's singular ASTM C150 certification, creates a replacement-asset premium that did not exist twelve months ago. The three cement majors that invested in Sublime must now either bridge a failing company or pivot to the only advancing alternative. Brimstone is that alternative. The first cement major to move captures the replacement asset; the second pays a premium or is locked out entirely.

**Evidence — Sublime's distress.** Sublime Systems reduced its workforce from approximately 128 employees to roughly 28 between December 2025 and March 2026 — a 78% reduction in four months.^24 The Holyoke, Massachusetts demonstration plant, a $150M facility, has been paused indefinitely following the cancellation of Sublime's $87M DOE OCED grant.^25 Sublime's CEO stated that the cancellation "created challenges in assembling our capital stack."^26 Local planning officials have stated publicly that no plant construction is expected "in the immediate future."^27

CRH and Holcim jointly invested $75M — approximately $37.5M each — in a combined equity and offtake transaction that included individually binding offtake reservations and co-development commitments for future plants.^28 CRH's stated rationale — that Sublime's "differentiated technology and ability to scale, combined with CRH's expertise and footprint, offers significant potential to dramatically decarbonize cement production" — now describes a paused company with fewer than 30 employees.^29

**Evidence — the C150 competitive moat.** Brimstone's ASTM C150 certification, independently verified by Twining Consulting in July 2023, is the single most commercially significant product distinction in the competitive field.^30 ASTM C150 is the prescriptive specification required for all federally funded infrastructure in the United States — DOT highway and bridge projects, IIJA-funded construction, and all federal procurement governed by AASHTO M85 standards. Sublime's cement meets ASTM C1157, a performance-based specification for which AASHTO has no equivalent, meaning it cannot be specified in state DOT projects without years of specification changes across 50 separate state transportation agencies.^31

The market access difference is quantifiable. Approximately 35–40% of U.S. cement demand is DOT-specified or federally governed, requiring C150 compliance.^32 At approximately $120–130B in total U.S. cement and concrete market value, the DOT-specified segment represents $40–50B per year in addressable demand that C150 serves and C1157 does not.^33 Brimstone's cement drops into 100% of the U.S. market without specification changes. Sublime's cement — even were the company operational — addresses approximately 60–65% of demand, limited to private-sector and non-DOT public construction. For CRH specifically, whose Ash Grove division derives significant revenue from DOT-funded infrastructure through 12 U.S. and Canadian cement plants, a green cement technology that cannot serve the DOT market has structurally limited value.^34

**Evidence — instrument structure and exit dynamics.** CRH holds equity in Sublime — a position that, in a wind-down, sits behind all creditors and is likely impaired to zero.^35 Amrize's Form 10-Q discloses its Sublime position as "a convertible note and advance payments for future supplies" — making Amrize a creditor, not an equity holder, until conversion.^36 In any Sublime wind-down, Amrize's note has priority over equity; its principal is potentially recoverable; and Amrize has no obligation to participate in future funding rounds. Amrize can exit the Sublime relationship and pivot to Brimstone without the governance complexity CRH faces.

**Financial magnitude.** The replacement-asset premium is the difference between acquiring Brimstone as one option among several and acquiring it as the sole remaining option. With Sublime paused, Brimstone is the only ASTM C150-certified, operationally advancing green cement technology in the United States.^37 The $40–50B per year in DOT-specified demand represents the structural basis for Brimstone's competitive moat — any cement major whose revenue includes significant DOT-funded infrastructure work requires a C150-compliant decarbonization pathway to meet tightening federal procurement carbon intensity standards.^38 The competitive dynamics shaped by this premium are reflected directly in the scenario analysis in the Valuation Architecture section.

**Execution mechanics — CRH is the optimal buyer and the most legally constrained.** CRH has the geographic proximity (Ash Grove's South-Central U.S. operations overlap with Brimstone's Oklahoma plant site), the financial capacity ($5B+ annual free cash flow, a $250M ventures fund), the precedent ($2.1B Eco Material Technologies acquisition in September 2025), and the strategic gap.^39 CRH is also the most legally constrained potential partner. Its equity investment in Sublime almost certainly contains contractual provisions standard in strategic venture deals: exclusivity periods, non-compete or non-solicitation clauses covering competing cement technologies, and co-development rights that remain in force until formal dissolution of the Sublime relationship or explicit contractual release.^40 Analysis suggests CRH cannot execute an operational partnership, co-development agreement, or offtake reservation with Brimstone without creating breach risk under the Sublime agreement — regardless of Sublime's operational status — until that agreement is formally terminated. Sublime has not filed for bankruptcy. No dissolution has been announced. The contractual provisions remain legally in force.

Amrize faces no such constraint. Its convertible note carries no co-development commitment, and as a creditor rather than equity holder, Amrize's exit from Sublime requires no board action and no IP release negotiation. Amrize can approach Brimstone immediately and cleanly.^41 Amrize does, however, face its own complication: its Dallas-Fort Worth conventional cement expansion competes directly with Brimstone's Oklahoma addressable market, creating a cannibalization tension at the board level that CRH — with no conflicting conventional capacity expansion in the same region — does not face.^42

The net assessment: CRH is the more natural long-term partner, but Amrize has the clearer legal path to move first. The optimal CRH entry structure — a passive, financial-only preferred equity investment through CRH Ventures, designed to avoid triggering non-compete provisions, with automatic upgrade upon Sublime dissolution — is detailed in the Buyer Universe and Transaction Pathways section.^43

**Confidence and gaps.** Confidence is high on Sublime's distress: workforce reductions, plant pause, and management statements are matters of public record. Confidence is high on the C150 competitive advantage: the specification framework, AASHTO requirements, and DOT market segmentation are structural facts. Confidence is medium on the specific terms of CRH's contractual provisions with Sublime — non-compete scope, exclusivity periods, and co-development rights are standard in strategic venture investments, but the specific language is private. The risk that Sublime revives through a $100M+ emergency bridge — eliminating the replacement-asset premium — is identified in the Risk Architecture section of this report.
