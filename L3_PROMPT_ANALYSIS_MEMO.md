## Part 1: Where v1 (ORIGINAL) Is Better Than v3

### 1.1 Section I Hook: Narrative Momentum and Surprise

v1 opens with a *story*, not a transaction summary. Its first sentence — "CRH and Holcim invested $75M in Sublime Systems in September 2024 — equity, binding offtake, and co-development rights for the next generation of green portland cement" — drops the reader into a specific event with named actors and a defined deal structure. The second sentence delivers a reversal: "Sublime has since collapsed to approximately 28 employees, paused its Holyoke plant indefinitely, and replaced its founder-CEO." By the third sentence, the reader has absorbed a $75M write-off and is leaning forward to learn what comes next.

v3's opening — "CRH paid $2.1B for Eco Material Technologies in September 2025 — the benchmark acquisition for cement technology platforms" — is factual and relevant, but it reads like a comp table entry. It establishes the valuation context before creating any tension. The Sublime collapse doesn't appear until the third sentence, and by then it's a supporting data point rather than the narrative engine.

The difference matters because v1's hook earns the second paragraph. By the time v1 introduces the alumina co-production thesis and the EGA smelter, the reader is already invested. v3 uses its second full paragraph to introduce the alumina certification gap — which is analytically sharper — but the path to that paragraph is less compelling.

Critically, v1 closes The Opportunity with a concrete catalyst timeline: "CEO Finke speaks at CERAWeek in Houston this week (March 23–27); Amrize holds its inaugural investor day March 25. The window is Q2–Q3 2026, and the catalysts are measured in days." This is the kind of sentence that makes an MD look at their calendar. v3's closing — "A strategic investor who resolves this single diligence question before the Series B close — likely by September 2026 — enters at pre-commercial pricing with a quantified risk-reward spanning approximately $80M to $285M in implied enterprise value" — is more analytically precise but reads like a valuation footnote rather than a call to action. The v1 closing is visceral; the v3 closing is cerebral. For a cold outreach deliverable, visceral wins.

### 1.2 Finding Format: "Key Uncertainty" vs. "Open Questions"

v1's findings use a three-part structure: **Observation → Deal Implication → Key Uncertainty**. The Key Uncertainty is a tight 1-2 sentence statement that names the single thing that would change the finding's conclusion. For Finding 1 in v1: *"Brimstone may have achieved full SGA specification at pilot scale and is withholding data as trade secret pending commercial plant construction. The information asymmetry (certified cement, silent alumina) is consistent with either a genuine technical barrier or deliberate commercial strategy."*

This is a well-calibrated hedge. It names the binary outcome, acknowledges both possibilities, and lets the reader form their own view. It's the kind of uncertainty statement a partner at a bank writes — not defensive, not evasive, just clear about what's known and what isn't.

v3's findings use **Open Questions** — a bulleted list of 2-5 discursive questions per finding. These are often individually excellent. The first Open Question under v3's Finding 1 is a standout: "The entire SGA thesis reduces to one ICP-MS measurement: what is the dissolved titanium concentration in Brimstone's leachate when Glen Mountains rock is processed under their conditions? If below 100 ppb, the SGA pathway is open; if above 1 ppm, it requires purification steps not described in any public document. This $50 lab test has a $125M+ valuation implication — and after six years of development, has never been disclosed. Why?"

That's a terrific question. But the format creates two problems. First, it dilutes density: v3's Finding 1 uses roughly 30% more words than v1's to make the same core point, because the Open Questions elaborate rather than compress. Second, it changes the document's register. The Observation and Deal Implication read like a partner memo; the Open Questions read like a diligence request list. The tonal shift is subtle but real — and it undercuts the authorial confidence that makes a cold outreach document persuasive.

### 1.3 Word Economy and Sentence-Level Density

v1 is tighter. Compare the Company Situation's treatment of the capital structure:

**v1:** "The capital structure underwent a seismic shift in 2025. In January, DOE finalized a $189M cooperative agreement, disbursing $8.7M for Phase 1. In May, the Trump administration terminated the remaining ~$180M as part of a systematic rescission of Biden-era clean energy awards. In July, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act repealed the IRA's LPO loan authority, closing what would have been the most viable alternative government financing path."

**v3:** "The capital structure is acute. The DOE OCED grant ($189M selected March 2024; $8.7M disbursed January 2025) was cancelled in May 2025; an administrative appeal and congressional engagement are active. The OBBBA's repeal of IRA loan authority closed the DOE LPO pathway on July 4, 2025."

v3 packs the same information into fewer words — but v1's version reads better. "Underwent a seismic shift" is unnecessary throat-clearing, but the chronological unfolding (January → May → July) gives the reader a sense of progressive loss that makes the "entire plant must now be privately financed" conclusion land harder. v3's compressed version is more efficient but less persuasive.

Elsewhere, though, v1 is genuinely more economical. v1's treatment of ownership — "Ownership is private and founder-led. The board consists of only two members — both founders — an unusual governance structure for a company with $80M+ from institutional investors that typically demand board representation" — is 36 words of setup that pays off immediately. v3 spends a full paragraph naming every investor from the Series A before getting to the governance point, which delays the payoff.

### 1.4 Tone: Opinionatedness and Authorial Confidence

v1 has a stronger voice throughout. The narrative-vs-reality paragraph in v1 ends with: "And on the alumina product that anchors the entire critical minerals narrative, zero of the thirteen standard smelter-grade specification parameters have been publicly disclosed after six years of development — a gap with transformational valuation implications explored below." The dash-led clause is a directorial comment. It tells the reader: we found something important, and we'll prove it.

v3's equivalent — "That asymmetry between certified cement and unverified alumina is the single most important fact about this company" — is actually more assertive in content, but it appears at the *end* of a long paragraph about the December 2025 "Fourth Great Refinery" announcement. The assertive sentence gets buried by context instead of standing alone as a section-ending hook.

### 1.5 Citation Style in the Body Text

v1 uses clean superscript numbers throughout the body (^1, ^2, ^3...) with a single Source Notes section at the back. The reading experience is uninterrupted. Every claim has a footnote, and the reader trusts that the notes will be there when they check.

v3 uses the same superscript style (it was instructed to) but adds a separate Calculation Notes section with Roman numerals (^i, ^ii, ^iii). This is analytically superior — it distinguishes third-party data from Pokee estimates — but it creates a more cluttered reading experience. When the reader encounters "^iii" in the valuation table, they have to decide whether to look for it in Calculation Notes or Source Notes. For an MD skimming a cold outreach PDF, this is friction.

## Part 2: Where v3 (CURRENT) Is Better Than v1

### 2.1 Sourcing Rigor and the Two-Category System

v3's most important structural improvement is the Calculation Notes / Source Notes separation. v1 blends Pokee estimates into the Source Notes (e.g., v1 Source Note ^10: "Pokee estimate: plant nameplate capacity × 2024–2025 published market pricing per product"). v3 isolates all estimates into a dedicated Calculation Notes section with Roman numerals and a mandatory `[Pokee estimate]` tag, with full calculation chains.

Compare v1's valuation methodology paragraph — a dense block of prose beneath the valuation table explaining methodology — with v3's Calculation Note ^iii, which runs to nearly a full page and decomposes every component: floor methodology (2.0–2.5× capital raised), base case sum-of-parts (Plant 1 operating value + C150 regulatory moat premium + Amazon offtake + EGA alumina supply chain optionality + multi-plant replication option, with dollar ranges for each), and bull case (projected EBITDA × critical minerals multiples, with named comparables and 10-year median ranges). An MD won't read the Calculation Notes on first pass, but a VP doing diligence will — and the v3 version is dramatically more auditable.

### 2.2 Cross-Referencing Between Findings

v3 is substantially better at connecting findings to each other. Finding 2 in v3 (CRH/Sublime) includes: "If the alumina product resolves as chemical-grade rather than smelter-grade (see Finding 1 above), the cement-major thesis actually strengthens — the valuation would compress to cement-technology multiples, making a CRH or Amrize entry less expensive while the C150 moat remains fully intact." This sentence connects the alumina specification question (Finding 1) to the CRH buyer thesis (Finding 2) in a way that creates analytical depth — the reader realizes the findings aren't independent, they form a system.

v1 has a comparable cross-reference in Finding 3: "Critically, this finding is contingent on Finding 1: if the alumina product is chemical-grade rather than smelter-grade, EGA's technical team will not accept a supply agreement at any price." But v1's cross-reference only points one direction (3→1), while v3's bidirectional linkage (1→3 and 2→1) creates a richer picture.

### 2.3 The Opportunity's Analytical Depth

v3's Section I is longer (two full paragraphs vs. v1's two shorter paragraphs) and introduces the alumina certification gap as the *structural* basis for the opportunity, not just one of several signals. v3's second paragraph: "The gap that creates the transaction: Brimstone's cement holds full ASTM C150 third-party certification — independently verified by Twining Consulting and tested against Amazon's proprietary slab mix designs. Its alumina co-product — projected at 44% of Plant 1 revenue at smelter-grade pricing — has zero independently validated specification data across any of the thirteen standard SGA parameters after six years of development and $80M+ raised."

This framing is more useful to a banker than v1's dual-thesis approach (cement replacement + alumina/EGA supply). v3 gives the reader one clear axis of analysis — the cement/alumina certification asymmetry — and shows how the entire valuation pivots on it. v1 tries to establish both the Sublime replacement thesis and the EGA alumina thesis simultaneously in Section I, which diffuses the hook.

### 2.4 Evidence Strength Signaling

v3 consistently distinguishes between documented facts and inferences in a way v1 does not. v3's Finding 2 (CRH): "CRH's contractual provisions — including any non-compete covering competing C150-compliant green cement technology — **remain legally in force**" is stated declaratively, supported by cited source. But the Key Uncertainty then correctly scopes what's unknown: "Whether CRH's Sublime agreement contains explicit non-compete language covering C150-compliant green cement, or merely operational co-development exclusivity. This distinction determines CRH's timeline to act."

v1's equivalent treatment is more assertive: "all contractual restrictions remain in force" followed by Open Questions asking "Does CRH's Sublime agreement contain non-compete provisions covering competing green cement technologies?" — but v1's body text states the restrictions exist as fact before the Open Questions admit the scope is unknown. v3 avoids this contradiction by qualifying "including any non-compete" — signaling that the existence of restrictions is documented but their precise scope is inferred.

### 2.5 Valuation Framework Sophistication

v3's valuation table is structurally superior. It uses three clear scenarios (Floor / Base Case / Bull Case) with named approaches: "Technology value + 7x cement-only EBITDA ($4.1M)" for Floor, "Sum-of-parts: core platform + C150 moat premium + scale-up option" for Base, and "15x blended EBITDA + validated scale-up option value" for Bull. Each row tells the reader *what assumptions are in and what's out*.

v1's table uses similar scenarios but the labeling is less clean: "Floor (technology option, pre-revenue)" vs. "Base (Plant 1 operating, blended)" vs. "Bull (SGA validated, EGA offtake, multi-plant)." The parentheticals try to do too much. More importantly, v1 doesn't explicitly state the approach/method for each scenario — it relies on the methodology paragraph below the table, which means the reader has to cross-reference to understand each row. v3 makes each row self-contained.

v3's sensitivity line is also better rendered: bold text, explicit walk from SGA to CGA, showing the EBITDA decline, margin compression, and enterprise value impact in one readable sentence. v1's sensitivity is embedded in the body paragraph of Finding 1's Deal Implication, not in Deal Context where it naturally belongs.

### 2.6 Precedent Transaction Table: Type Column

v3's comp table includes a "Type" column distinguishing M&A, Minority/Strategic, and Government transactions. v1's table has no such column. This matters because the table includes CRH/Sublime ($75M minority), DOD/MP Materials ($550M government), and DOD/Atalco ($450M government) alongside full M&A deals. Without the Type column, a reader could mistake a $450M government DPA for a market-rate strategic acquisition, distorting the implied multiple range. v3 prevents this confusion.

### 2.7 Buyer Universe Specificity

v3's buyer entries are more actionable. v3 introduces Breakthrough Energy Catalyst / TPG Rise Climate as a fourth buyer category with a specific deal path: "BEV already invested at Series A. Catalyst could lead a Series B without triggering Amazon M&A consent — avoiding the structural complexity entirely. CFO Rens' prior relationship with TPG Rise Climate (from Monolith Materials) provides a warm introduction."

v1's buyer universe covers CRH, EGA/Century, and Amrize — the same three as v3 minus the BEV/TPG path. The omission of a financial-sponsor path in v1 leaves a gap: an MD who isn't covering any of those three strategics has no reason to engage. v3 gives every reader a pathway.

## Part 3: Causal Tracing — Prompt Instructions → Output Qualities

### 3.1 Section I Hook Quality: "Value Tension" vs. "Reader's World"

**ORIGINAL L3a prompt (lines 55-56):** "Structure it as a value tension: '[Specific asset or business segment] is worth $X at [benchmark multiples]. [The company] trades at / is valued at $Y. The gap exists because [specific, named reason].'"

**CURRENT L3a prompt (lines 54-57):** "Open with the reader's world, not the company's product. Start with a market event, a recent transaction, a competitor collapse, or a regulatory shift that the reader already knows about — then reveal what this company uniquely offers in that context. The reader should recognize their own landscape in the first sentence and see something new by the third."

**Causal mechanism:** The ORIGINAL's "value tension" template naturally produces a numerical gap structure — which is what v1's hook delivers (Sublime's $75M investment → collapse → Brimstone as the replacement). The CURRENT's "reader's world" instruction produces a comp-table-first opening (CRH's $2.1B Eco Material acquisition) because the prompt explicitly says to start with "a recent transaction." The CURRENT instruction is *correct in principle* — starting with the reader's world is the right strategy — but its example list ("a market event, a recent transaction, a competitor collapse") steers the model toward a transaction benchmark rather than a narrative reversal.

The irony is that v1's Sublime collapse *is* the reader's world — CRH and Holcim's $75M loss is something any cement/materials MD already knows about. The ORIGINAL prompt's "value tension" format accidentally forced the model to find the most dramatic entry point, while the CURRENT's more sophisticated instruction steered toward a safer one.

### 3.2 Finding Format: Observation/Implication/Uncertainty vs. Observation/Implication/Open Questions

**ORIGINAL L3a prompt (lines 110-114):** "**The Observation** (3-5 sentences)... **The Deal Implication** (2-4 sentences)... **Key Uncertainty** (1-2 sentences): What could invalidate this finding. What would need to be verified in diligence. Be honest — intellectual honesty builds credibility."

**CURRENT L3a prompt (lines 112-118):** "**The Observation** (3-5 sentences)... **The Deal Implication** (2-4 sentences)... **Open Questions** (2-5 bullet points): Each finding closes with a bulleted list of questions that the research surfaced but could not definitively resolve from public data. These are NOT caveats or hedges — they are the most strategically interesting unresolved questions, framed to make the reader lean back and think. Write them in a narrative, discursive style..."

**Causal mechanism:** The word count budgets tell the story. "Key Uncertainty (1-2 sentences)" allocates ~30-50 words per finding to hedging. "Open Questions (2-5 bullet points)" in "narrative, discursive style" allocates ~150-250 words per finding. The CURRENT instruction explicitly asks for questions that would "spark a compelling conversation over coffee" — which is excellent for relationship-building but creates the tonal shift problem noted in Part 1. The model follows the instruction faithfully: v3's Open Questions are genuinely conversational and thought-provoking, but they bloat the findings section from ~1000 words (v1) to ~1400+ words (v3) while diluting the analytical authority of the document.

The CURRENT prompt's instruction that Open Questions are "NOT caveats or hedges" is well-intentioned but partially counterproductive. In practice, questions like "Has any aluminum smelter operator conducted qualification testing on Brimstone's alumina?" function as hedges regardless of framing — they signal that the author doesn't know the answer to a question that matters for the finding's validity. Calling them "strategically interesting unresolved questions" doesn't change their epistemic function.

### 3.3 Citation Handling: Inline vs. Dual-Numbering

**ORIGINAL L3a prompt (lines 176-183):** "Use superscript notation (^1, ^2) for inline citations with a source list at the end of each major section. Keep citations compact — this is a professional document, not an academic paper."

**ORIGINAL L3b prompt (lines 87-88):** "CITATION FORMAT: Use numbered footnotes (^1, ^2, ^3...) throughout the document body, with a 'Source Notes' section at the end listing each footnote. Do NOT use inline [Source: ...] citations."

**CURRENT L3a prompt (lines 180-195):** Introduces the two-category system (Arabic numerals for Source Notes, Roman numerals for Calculation Notes), the "two-category rule" ("Every number in this document falls into exactly one of two categories"), and the `[Pokee estimate]` tag requirement.

**Causal mechanism:** The ORIGINAL L3a actually suggests "source list at the end of each major section" — section-level endnotes — but the ORIGINAL L3b overrides this to "a 'Source Notes' section at the end listing each footnote." The model follows L3b, producing a single, clean reference section. The CURRENT L3a's dual-category system creates the Calculation Notes section that makes v3 more auditable but more cluttered. The CURRENT L3b's instruction to verify "Arabic and Roman numerals are used consistently" reinforces this, and the model faithfully produces the dual system.

The key prompt difference: the ORIGINAL system never asks the model to distinguish between sourced facts and estimates at the citation level. Everything goes into Source Notes, and estimates are identified by prefix ("Pokee estimate:") within the note text. The CURRENT system makes this distinction structural. The trade-off is precision vs. reading flow.

### 3.4 L3b Editorial Pass: What Each Version Checks For

**ORIGINAL L3b (lines 41-50):** Checks for narrative-vs-reality debunks, "OBBBA / macro headwind" paragraphs, stacking of transaction signals, and specific ownership percentages. The specific mention of "OBBBA" as a named policy is a strong forcing function — it makes the model look for a specific regulatory catalyst rather than generic "regulatory risk."

**CURRENT L3b (lines 57-61):** Adds "Is the title a story with stakes?", "Does the deal implication connect the insight to an opportunity, not just a risk?", the cross-section repetition check, and the Open Questions format check. It also adds a "narrative energy check" in the final read-through (line 104): "does the document still have momentum? Does it read like it was written by someone who found something genuinely interesting?"

**Causal mechanism:** The ORIGINAL L3b's specific checks (OBBBA, transaction signal stacking, ownership percentages) produce concrete improvements — they're forcing functions that make the model look for specific content. The CURRENT L3b's checks are more meta-level ("is the title a story with stakes?") and produce better structural quality but less content discovery. The CURRENT L3b's "narrative energy check" is a partial countermeasure against over-refinement — but it comes at the end of a seven-step editorial process, and by then the model has already done extensive rewriting.

The CURRENT L3b also includes line 124: "Note: This document will pass through an L3c coherency audit after your edits, which checks for internal contradictions, unsourced claims, and numerical consistency. Your goal is to produce a document so clean that L3c finds nothing to fix." This instruction is double-edged: it raises L3b's quality floor (the model knows it will be checked) but may also make L3b more conservative (the model knows aggressive claims will be scrutinized).

### 3.5 L3c Coherency Audit: Help or Hurt?

**L3c prompt, Check 2 (lines 29-37):** "For each finding, read the Observation and Deal Implication paragraphs, then read the Open Questions. Ask: does the body text state as fact anything that the Open Questions frame as unknown or unresolved?... Fix: Soften the body text to be consistent with the Open Questions."

**Causal mechanism:** This check is the most consequential L3c instruction. It creates a ratchet effect: if the Open Questions ask "does the agreement contain an explicit non-compete?", L3c will soften any body text that states "non-compete provisions remain in force." The direction of the ratchet is always toward less certainty — L3c never *strengthens* a claim to match the body text; it always softens the body to match the questions.

This produces the hedging pattern visible in v3's Finding 2: "CRH's contractual provisions — including any non-compete covering competing C150-compliant green cement technology — **remain legally in force**." The dash clause "including any non-compete" is the kind of qualifier that L3c would insert. It's technically correct but reads as less authoritative than v1's direct "all contractual restrictions remain in force."

L3c's Check 3 (unsourced factual claims) is unambiguously positive — it catches claims that slipped through L3b without sources. But Check 2's body-vs-questions consistency enforcement has a net *negative* effect on the document's rhetorical power, because it systematically reduces assertiveness wherever the Open Questions identify uncertainty — which is, by design, at the most analytically interesting points in the document.

### 3.6 Evidence Strength Ratings

**CURRENT L3a prompt (lines 206-212):** Introduces DOCUMENTED / CIRCUMSTANTIAL / ANECDOTAL evidence strength ratings from L2 deep dives, with specific language guidance for each level.

**ORIGINAL L3a prompt:** No equivalent instruction.

**Causal mechanism:** The CURRENT's evidence-strength system produces better-calibrated language in the body text — v3 consistently uses "analysis indicates," "evidence suggests," "consistent with" for inferential claims. v1 occasionally presents inferences as declarative statements (e.g., "Kajima Ventures — the CVC arm of Japan's largest construction conglomerate — entered the cap table in mid-2025, a strategic signal that the construction industry establishment is validating the technology" — the "strategic signal" characterization is inferential but presented without hedging).

The evidence-strength system interacts badly with the Open Questions format, however. When L3a writes a CIRCUMSTANTIAL claim with inferential language, and then L3c's Check 2 softens it further because an Open Question probes the same territory, the result can be double-hedged. The document loses conviction on precisely the claims that are most interesting.

## Part 4: Recommendations

### 4.1 Things to Take from the ORIGINAL System

**Recommendation 1: Restore the "Key Uncertainty" format, kill "Open Questions"**

*Current instruction (CURRENT L3a, lines 118):* "**Open Questions** (2-5 bullet points): Each finding closes with a bulleted list of questions... Write them in a narrative, discursive style — not as dry diligence items but as the kind of questions that would spark a compelling conversation over coffee."

*Proposed replacement:* "**Key Uncertainty** (2-3 sentences): The single most important unresolved question for this finding. State it as a testable proposition: 'If X, then the finding holds and the implication is $Y. If not-X, the finding changes to Z.' Name the specific data point, filing, or test that would resolve it. If one of the unresolved questions is particularly vivid or counterintuitive, render it as a question the reader wouldn't have thought to ask — the kind that makes an MD realize they don't know the answer either. This is not a hedge; it is the sharpest possible articulation of what's at stake."

*Target:* Restores word economy (~50-80 words per finding vs. ~150-250), maintains analytical precision, recovers the authoritative tone. Preserves the CURRENT system's best Open Questions (the ICP-MS measurement question, the FEED infrastructure deadline) by allowing 2-3 sentences instead of 1-2, but eliminates the bulleted-list format that turns findings into diligence checklists.

*Trade-off:* Loses the "conversational" quality that makes Open Questions engaging for relationship-building. The best individual Open Questions in v3 are better than anything in v1's Key Uncertainties. But the format as a whole costs too much in density and tone.

**Recommendation 2: Restore a "value tension" hook structure as an option within the "reader's world" framework**

*Current instruction (CURRENT L3a, lines 54-57):* "Open with the reader's world, not the company's product. Start with a market event, a recent transaction, a competitor collapse, or a regulatory shift that the reader already knows about..."

*Proposed replacement:* "Open with the reader's world, not the company's product. The strongest hooks start with something the reader already knows — a competitor collapse, a stranded investment, a regulatory shift, a recent transaction — and reveal what this company means in that context. Structure it as a value tension where possible: 'CRH and Holcim invested $75M in X. X has since collapsed. There is exactly one replacement asset, and it has no strategic investor.' The reader should recognize their own landscape in the first sentence, see something new by the third, and feel urgency by the last. A narrative reversal (invested → collapsed → replacement exists) is more compelling than a comp-table benchmark (CRH paid $2.1B for Y)."

*Target:* Preserves the CURRENT system's principle (reader's world first) while providing a stronger structural template. The added example steers the model toward dramatic entry points rather than benchmark transactions.

*Trade-off:* The "narrative reversal" template won't always be available — some companies don't have a Sublime-style competitor collapse to anchor the hook. The instruction should position this as the preferred structure, not the only one.

### 4.2 Things to Keep from the CURRENT System

**Keep: The two-category citation system (Calculation Notes + Source Notes)**

The dual-numbering system is v3's strongest structural improvement. It makes the document auditable at a level that builds credibility with a VP doing diligence. The recommendation is to keep it exactly as-is, including the Roman numeral / Arabic numeral distinction and the `[Pokee estimate]` tag.

**Keep: Evidence strength signaling from L2 ratings**

The DOCUMENTED / CIRCUMSTANTIAL / ANECDOTAL framework produces better-calibrated language. Keep the instruction as-is in L3a, but modify L3c's interaction with it (see below).

**Keep: Cross-reference instructions between findings**

The CURRENT L3b's instruction to check for cross-references between findings ("Is there a cross-reference between findings that the draft missed?") is directly responsible for v3's superior finding-to-finding linkage. Keep this instruction.

**Keep: Type column in precedent transaction table**

The CURRENT L3a's instruction to classify transactions as M&A / Minority-Strategic / Government prevents misreadings. Keep it.

**Keep: L3c Checks 1, 3, 4, and 5**

Internal contradiction detection, unsourced claim detection, numerical consistency, and source-claim alignment are all unambiguously valuable. These are safety-net functions that catch real errors without damaging the document's rhetoric.

### 4.3 Things That Need a New Approach

**Recommendation 3: Rewrite L3c Check 2 to prevent the certainty-ratchet**

*Current instruction (L3c, lines 29-37):* "For each finding, read the Observation and Deal Implication paragraphs, then read the Open Questions. Ask: does the body text state as fact anything that the Open Questions frame as unknown or unresolved?... Fix: Soften the body text to be consistent with the Open Questions."

*Proposed replacement:* "For each finding, check for logical contradictions between the body text and the Key Uncertainty. If the body states as documented fact something the Key Uncertainty identifies as unresolved, either (a) add the source that supports the body claim and adjust the Key Uncertainty to acknowledge the evidence, or (b) soften the body claim. The direction of reconciliation depends on the evidence: if the claim is DOCUMENTED per L2 evidence ratings, preserve the body and narrow the uncertainty. If the claim is CIRCUMSTANTIAL, ensure the body uses inferential language. Do NOT systematically weaken body claims — a document that hedges everything persuades no one."

*Target:* Eliminates the one-directional certainty ratchet. Currently L3c always softens toward the uncertainty; the replacement allows strengthening toward the evidence when the evidence warrants it.

*Trade-off:* Slightly higher risk of overconfident claims surviving to final output. Mitigated by L3c Checks 3 and 5 (unsourced claims and source-claim alignment), which still catch genuinely unsupported assertions.

**Recommendation 4: Add a word budget to the findings section**

*Current instruction (CURRENT L3a, line 93):* "Length: 800-1200 words total across exactly 3 findings."

*Proposed replacement:* "Length: 800-1000 words total across exactly 3 findings. Each finding should be 250-350 words: Observation (100-150 words), Deal Implication (80-120 words), Key Uncertainty (50-80 words). If a finding requires more than 350 words, something in the Observation is insufficiently compressed."

*Target:* Prevents the word-count creep that v3's Open Questions caused. The explicit per-finding budget forces the model to prioritize density.

*Trade-off:* May occasionally truncate findings that genuinely need more space. But the constraint is the point — the model should be selecting the most powerful evidence, not listing all the evidence.

**Recommendation 5: Add a "catalyst prominence" instruction to L3a, not just L3b**

*Current state:* The CURRENT L3b (line 103) checks for catalyst prominence: "Identify the single most time-sensitive catalyst in the document — the event with the nearest hard date. Is it positioned for maximum impact?" But this check comes after the document is already drafted, making it a retrofit rather than a structural element.

*Proposed addition to L3a, Section I instructions:* "The final sentence of Section I must name a specific, dated catalyst — an event with a hard calendar date within the next 90 days. 'The window is Q2–Q3 2026' is acceptable but inferior to 'Amrize's investor day is March 25 — one week from this analysis.' The reader should finish Section I knowing not just why this matters, but when."

*Target:* Forces the near-term catalyst into the hook, where v1 naturally placed it and v3 did not. This was one of v1's strongest features.

*Trade-off:* Not every company will have a hard catalyst within 90 days. The instruction should be framed as "if available" rather than mandatory, to avoid forcing artificial urgency.

**Recommendation 6: Remove the L3b instruction that references L3c**

*Current instruction (CURRENT L3b, line 124):* "Note: This document will pass through an L3c coherency audit after your edits, which checks for internal contradictions, unsourced claims, and numerical consistency. Your goal is to produce a document so clean that L3c finds nothing to fix."

*Proposed:* Delete this instruction entirely.

*Rationale:* This instruction makes L3b optimize for L3c's approval rather than for the reader's experience. It incentivizes defensive writing — the model knows aggressive claims will be scrutinized in the next pass, so it preemptively softens them. L3b should optimize for the MD, not for the fact-checker. Let L3c do its job independently.

---

## Summary of the System-Level Trade-off

The ORIGINAL system optimizes for **rhetorical impact**: narrative hooks, word economy, authorial confidence, clean reading flow. Its weakness is sourcing rigor and analytical precision — it occasionally presents inferences as facts and doesn't distinguish between sourced and estimated numbers.

The CURRENT system optimizes for **analytical credibility**: dual citation systems, evidence strength calibration, cross-referenced findings, auditable calculation chains. Its weakness is that these improvements come at the cost of density, tone, and narrative momentum — the document reads more like a compliance-reviewed research report than a partner-written deal memo.

The ideal system takes the CURRENT's citation architecture, evidence calibration, and cross-referencing, but wraps them in the ORIGINAL's narrative structure, finding format, and word economy. The six recommendations above are designed to achieve exactly that combination.
