# DELIVERABLE 1: COMPANY SITUATION MAP

## What Camellia IS

Camellia Plc is a 135-year-old, AIM-listed agricultural holding company that is, by almost any structural measure, an anomaly. It is the world's largest privately controlled tea grower (34,857 ha, ~76 million kg/year), operates 48,500 hectares of productive land across seven countries with 78,000 employees, runs 67+ processing facilities — and trades at a market capitalisation (~£130m) that barely exceeds its cash position (£124.7m). The market assigns approximately **zero or negative value** to the entire agricultural operation.

The company is controlled by the Camellia Foundation, a Bermuda-registered charity, which holds 56.45% through a chain running through a Swiss holding company (Camellia Holding AG). Only two of seven board directors are genuinely independent. The Chairman previously chaired the controlling Foundation. Executive compensation is ~95% fixed salary with zero equity linkage. The CEO owns 0.079% of the company. There are no LTIPs, no performance shares, and no disclosed bonus metrics. The previous chairman served for five decades.

Financially, Camellia sits on a fortress balance sheet: £124.7m net cash, £30.4m in listed equities, £31.3m in investment property, and £7.3m in heritage assets (at cost, almost certainly worth multiples). Total liquid non-operating assets of ~£194m exceed the market cap. But the underlying agricultural business has been **loss-making at the trading level for two consecutive years** (£10.3m loss in 2023, £5.5m in 2024, £9.6m in H1 2025 alone). EP Kenya is the only consistently profitable unit. India barely breaks even. Bangladesh and South Africa lose money. The 260p dividend reinstated for FY2024 is being paid from reserves, not earnings.

The portfolio is dominated by commodity tea sold at auction (72% of planted land), supplemented by macadamia (3,533 ha across Kenya/Malawi/South Africa), avocado (934 ha in Kenya, 356 ha developing in Tanzania), Brazilian arable (3,953 ha — quietly the highest-margin unit at ~27%), 1,710 ha of structurally uncompetitive rubber in Bangladesh, and two non-agricultural outliers: AJT Engineering (Aberdeen, first-time profitable in 2024 after a decade of losses) and Jing Tea (luxury branded tea, growing 22% but still loss-making).

## What Camellia SAYS It's Doing

CEO Byron Coombs (appointed September 2023) and CFO Oliver Capon (ex-Shell, appointed June 2024) launched the Value Enhancement Plan in May 2025 — the first serious strategic overhaul in decades. The VEP has three pillars: improve operating results (maintenance capex rising to £8–10m/year), reduce risk (dispose of high-risk or unpredictable assets), and invest in growth (£15–25m/year in organic and inorganic expansion). Total planned investment: £23–35m annually, up from just £9.5m in 2024. The VEP explicitly targets returning to the 5%+ return on capital needed to sustain the dividend.

Capital allocation priorities: fortress balance sheet first, sustainable dividends second, business investment third. A £18.9m tender offer at £54/share was completed in June 2025. The board says no further tenders are anticipated. Growth areas: Tanzanian avocado, macadamia expansion across three countries, Brazil diversification, and Jing Tea brand development. Further disposals of underperforming assets are explicitly envisaged — Indian tea estates (Chulsa and Leesh River already sold) are the clearest examples.

The narrative is one of disciplined operational turnaround combined with selective portfolio pruning and reinvestment. Management acknowledges the share price "remains of concern."

## What the ENVIRONMENT Is Doing to Camellia

The environment is hostile and deteriorating on almost every front simultaneously. This is the central finding of the research — not any single risk, but the **convergence of multiple active threats across every major geography at the same time**.

**Kenya** (the jewel of the portfolio, highest-margin operations): Land tenure is reaching a tipping point. The NLC has directed Kakuzi to surrender 3,200 acres. Over 200 squatters occupy 350 acres of EPK's Sitoi estate with political cover, costing ~$230k/month. Both James Finlay and Unilever/Lipton have exited Kenyan tea entirely — selling to Browns Investments, which is now consolidating into a mega-competitor with 87m kg/year capacity. Kenya's 2010 Constitution grants foreign companies no automatic lease renewal right. County governors are publicly demanding community equity stakes of 15–100% and land rate increases of 300–625%.

**Bangladesh** (largest employer, ~18,500 people): Elections are happening literally today (12 February 2026) in a contest between BNP and a Jamaat-led alliance. Tea worker wages ($1.43/day) are among the lowest globally and unsustainably low — correction is coming. Sylhet flooding has become near-annual. CMIP6 projections show extreme monsoon events quadrupling. FAO projects 40% tea yield declines by 2050. LDC graduation in November 2026 removes trade preferences.

**Malawi** (second-largest tea producer nationally at 42% of output): Currency has lost >70% since 2022. Parallel market premium hit 150%. IMF program terminated. Inflation at ~29%. Profit repatriation effectively blocked. Foreigners now barred from acquiring new land. Lujeri, the country's second-largest producer and Camellia's primary domestic competitor, has suspended all operations — creating both a risk (sector collapse signals) and an opportunity (consolidation).

**India** (largest revenue segment at £108m): Assam's November 2025 ceiling amendment strips labour-line land from estates and transfers it to workers at ~£6m total compensation for the entire sector. Gauhati High Court hearing set for 26 March 2026. The amendment could permanently fragment estate integrity.

**Regulation**: The EU Forced Labour Regulation (December 2027) applies to all products Camellia exports to Europe with no size threshold, and Camellia's settlement history makes it a plausible enforcement target. EU pesticide MRL tightening is persistent. EUDR covers rubber by December 2026. The UK's AIM IHT relief drops from 100% to 50% in April 2026, undermining one of the primary reasons for the AIM listing.

**ESG**: The 2021 settlements (£6.9m for killings, rape, and violence) left a permanent mark. A 2025 investigation found sexual abuse continues on Malawi estates despite remediation. Leigh Day retains the right to resume litigation. Wages across all geographies sit 40–63% below living wage benchmarks. No formal SBTi commitment. No standalone sustainability report. Minimal ESG rating coverage.

**Commodity prices**: Tea is in structural oversupply (global production doubled in 20 years). Mombasa auction average: $2.05/kg. Malawi tea fetches $0.95/kg. Macadamia prices recovering from 2022 crash but face oversupply risk from South African and Chinese planting surges. Rubber is in supply deficit — the one commodity tailwind — but Camellia's Bangladesh rubber is structurally uncompetitive at ~500 kg/ha vs Thailand's 1,800 kg/ha.

## What's CHANGED Recently

Three structural shifts make the next 12–18 months qualitatively different from the prior period:

**First, the buyer universe for Camellia's assets is narrowing.** Finlay and Lipton have exited Kenya. McLeod Russel is in insolvency. Quaero Capital has completely divested its 3.94% Camellia stake. Lujeri has suspended operations. The window for orderly exits from challenged geographies is closing. This is not a theoretical concern — it's observable in the shrinking list of credible counterparties for emerging-market agricultural estate transactions.

**Second, regulatory walls are closing in from multiple directions simultaneously.** The EU Forced Labour Regulation (December 2027), EUDR for rubber (December 2026), UK sustainability reporting (January 2027), incoming CSDDD cascade requirements, and Kenya's tightening land/tax regime are converging. Each individually is manageable; collectively, they represent a step-change in compliance burden for a 78,000-person operation across high-risk jurisdictions.

**Third, the competitive landscape has been fundamentally reshaped.** Browns Investments' consolidation of Finlay and Lipton estates creates a dominant Kenyan operator at ~87m kg/year. Kenya's NLC is weaponising the land framework against foreign holders. India's Assam government is fragmenting estate models. These aren't cyclical pressures — they represent structural shifts in the viability of the colonial-era plantation model that underpins Camellia's existence.

### Key Tensions Between Workstreams

The research reveals several places where the workstreams contradict or create irreconcilable pressures:

- **The VEP promises £23–35m/year in growth investment, but the most attractive geography (Kenya) faces existential land risk.** Investing heavily in a country where your lease may not be renewed and where squatters occupy your land with political cover is a contradictory strategy.

- **Management says "reduce risk" while the most obvious risk-reduction move (exiting Bangladesh) conflicts with being the country's largest tea producer** (~16% of national output, 18,500 employees). An exit would be politically explosive and practically difficult.

- **Carbon credit/ESG monetisation opportunities (~£8.5–16m/year potential) depend on the same land tenure that is being actively contested.** You cannot sell carbon credits on land you may be forced to surrender.

- **The VEP targets operational improvement, but the most impactful value-creation moves identified across workstreams are structural** (portfolio simplification, demerger, brand separation, land redeployment) — none of which appear in the VEP. The VEP is an operational plan for a company that needs a structural transformation.

- **The controlling trust's interests (stability, opacity, tax efficiency) are fundamentally misaligned with minority shareholder interests** (value realisation, governance reform, operating profitability). The VEP operates entirely within the trust's comfort zone — no structural changes, no governance reform, no performance-linked compensation. The discount may narrow marginally on operational improvement but cannot compress without structural change.

## #1. Kakuzi's Makuyu Land Is Worth More as Real Estate Than as Agriculture — But Requires a Political Solution, Not a Financial One

**TENSION:** Kakuzi's 14,000+ hectares at Makuyu sit ~75 km from Nairobi on the Thika Superhighway corridor. Adjacent land at Kenol trades at KSh 10–12m/acre ($80–96k). Converting just 5% (700 ha / ~1,730 acres) at current nearby values implies $138–166m — exceeding Camellia's entire market cap. Meanwhile, the NLC has ordered Kakuzi to surrender 3,200 acres and squatters invaded immediately after the ruling. Camellia is simultaneously sitting on one of East Africa's most valuable private land banks and facing the most acute threat of forced land surrender in its history.

**HYPOTHESIS:** The land claims and the development value are not separate problems — they are the same problem. A proactive community equity-sharing framework (modeled on the 15% structures used in Browns/Finlay transactions, or the Murang'a County industrial park model) could simultaneously resolve land disputes, secure lease renewals, and unlock phased real estate release on lower-agricultural-value parcels. The political cost of resisting is higher than the economic cost of sharing, and every year of delay reduces Camellia's negotiating leverage as political momentum builds against foreign landholders.

**CATEGORY:** STRUCTURAL UNLOCK

**POTENTIAL MAGNITUDE:** Transformational — the real estate option value alone could exceed the company's current market capitalisation.

**TIME HORIZON:** Near-term initiation (6–18 months to establish framework), Medium-term value realisation (3–10 years for phased release)

**LAYER 2 RESEARCH QUESTION:** What are the specific terms of the community equity and benefit-sharing agreements negotiated by Browns Investments/Finlays and Browns/Lipton in their 2023–2024 Kenya tea estate transactions? How did these structures resolve NLC objections and community land claims? What proportion of land was ceded, what governance structures were created, and can these be applied to Kakuzi's Murang'a situation? Additionally, what is the current land-use zoning classification for Kakuzi's estate parcels adjacent to the Kenol-Sagana highway, and what is the Murang'a County government's spatial development plan for the corridor through 2035?

## #2. Tea Extract Facility at EP Kenya: Proven Model Operating 15km Away, 4–12× Price Multiplication

**TENSION:** EP Kenya's five tea factories in Nandi Hills process leaf that flows to the Mombasa auction at $2.05–2.20/kg. Finlays' Saosa extraction facility, 15km away in Kericho, converts the same commodity tea into spray-dried extract at $8–25/kg and standardised EGCG/polyphenol concentrates at $50–200+/kg. When Finlays sold its Kenyan tea estates to Browns, it **retained the extraction plant** — explicitly signalling the extraction business is more valuable than the plantations feeding it. Camellia has the raw material, the processing infrastructure, the geography, and £125m in cash — but no extraction capability.

**HYPOTHESIS:** A £5–12m co-located extraction facility would be the single highest-impact capital deployment in Camellia's VEP budget. It redirects 2,000–3,000 tonnes/year from auction commodity into the $3–5 billion B2B ingredients market (RTD beverages, supplements, cosmetics) at 4–12× the current price per kg. The EU-Kenya EPA provides duty-free access. The buyer universe is identifiable (Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, Nestlé, supplement manufacturers). This is not a brand-building exercise — it's an industrial processing investment with proven unit economics operating in the same geography.

**CATEGORY:** OFFENSIVE OPPORTUNITY

**POTENTIAL MAGNITUDE:** Transformational — £5–15m/year at steady state with 25–40% gross margins, potentially exceeding EP Kenya's current total profit of £5.5m

**TIME HORIZON:** Near-term to initiate (feasibility in 6 months), Medium-term revenue (18–36 months to commission and ramp)

**LAYER 2 RESEARCH QUESTION:** What is the current capacity, product mix, customer base, and financial performance of Finlays' Saosa extraction facility in Kericho? What capital cost, permitting timeline, and utility requirements (water, power, waste treatment) would a comparable spray-drying and extraction facility require at EP Kenya's Nandi Hills location? Who are the top 20 B2B buyers of Kenyan tea extract globally, and what volumes and quality specifications do they require? Is there a used/refurbished extraction equipment market that could reduce capital outlay?

## #3. The Lujeri Collapse Creates a Once-in-a-Generation Malawi Consolidation Opportunity — But the FX Trap May Make It Worthless

**TENSION:** Lujeri Tea Estates, Malawi's second-largest tea producer (22% of national exports), suspended all operations in August 2025. Its UK parent PGI Group faces London High Court sexual abuse claims. Over 10,000 smallholders and estate workers are affected. EPM already controls ~38% of Malawi tea exports. Acquiring Lujeri would consolidate Camellia to ~60% of national tea exports — a dominant market position in a small but strategically positioned origin. But Malawi's kwacha has lost 70%+ since 2022, the parallel market premium hit 150%, profit repatriation is effectively blocked, and the IMF program terminated. Acquiring an asset you can't extract value from is not an acquisition — it's a capital trap.

**HYPOTHESIS:** Lujeri is worth acquiring only if three conditions are met simultaneously: (a) the purchase price reflects the kwacha's true black-market value, not the official rate; (b) Camellia negotiates a formal profit repatriation agreement with the Reserve Bank of Malawi as a condition of the transaction, leveraging the acquisition's importance to national food security and employment; and (c) the combined entity's market power enables Camellia to redirect Malawi tea from the Mombasa auction ($0.95/kg) toward direct bilateral contracts with European buyers at higher prices. Without all three, the capital is better deployed elsewhere. The IDC (Malawi's 26% partner in EPM) provides a potential facilitator for government engagement.

**CATEGORY:** OFFENSIVE OPPORTUNITY (conditional)

**POTENTIAL MAGNITUDE:** Major — transforms Malawi from a marginal contributor to a dominant market-position play, but only if FX risk is managed

**TIME HORIZON:** Immediate — Lujeri is in distress now; the window may close if a local buyer or impact investor (AgDevCo is active in Malawi) moves first

**LAYER 2 RESEARCH QUESTION:** What is PGI Group's current financial position and stated intention for Lujeri? What is the estimated asset value of Lujeri's estates, factories, and smallholder supply chain at both official and parallel FX rates? Has the Reserve Bank of Malawi granted any precedent profit-repatriation waivers or special arrangements for foreign acquirers of distressed assets? What terms would IDC and the Malawian government accept for a Camellia-Lujeri consolidation — specifically, would they support an EPM-Lujeri merger under EPM's existing corporate structure, and would they facilitate FX access in exchange for employment guarantees?

## #4. Duncan Brothers Bangladesh: Convert Loss-Making Tea + Idle Land into Solar + Tea Dual-Income Asset

**TENSION:** Duncan Brothers operates at a £2.3m annual loss. 45% of Bangladeshi tea estate land is unsuitable for cultivation. IDCOL (Bangladesh's leading renewable energy financier) is actively assessing tea estate land for solar installation. The Bangladesh government has explicitly endorsed using idle tea land for renewable energy. A 50 MW solar project at Bibiyana (Sylhet) was recently awarded at Tk 9.91/kWh. Duncan Brothers' 16 estates contain thousands of acres of uncultivable land generating zero revenue. The rubber operation (1,710 ha) yields ~500 kg/ha vs Thailand's 1,800 — structurally uneconomic. Today's Bangladesh elections may produce a more investor-friendly government (BNP is historically pro-business). The policy window for solar on tea land is open now.

**HYPOTHESIS:** Solar on uncultivable tea estate land and conversion of rubber land to fast-growing timber (with carbon credits during growth) can transform Bangladesh from Camellia's worst-performing unit to a dual-income operation. Even utilising 500 acres of unused land for solar would generate reliable revenue alongside tea production while reducing the estates' own energy costs. The rubber conversion eliminates ~£0.3–0.5m in annual losses while creating a new revenue stream. Combined, these moves could swing Bangladesh from -£2.3m to breakeven or positive within 3–5 years. Engagement with IDCOL should begin immediately after election results are known.

**CATEGORY:** OFFENSIVE OPPORTUNITY

**POTENTIAL MAGNITUDE:** Major — transforms the portfolio's weakest link

**TIME HORIZON:** Near-term initiation (6–12 months for IDCOL engagement and feasibility), Medium-term revenue (2–4 years to commission first projects)

**LAYER 2 RESEARCH QUESTION:** How many acres of Duncan Brothers' 16 estates are classified as uncultivable, and what is their solar irradiance profile? What are the specific terms of IDCOL's solar financing for tea estates — is it BOO (build-own-operate), BOT, or co-investment? What tariff structure and PPA duration would Duncan Brothers receive? What is the regulatory approval pathway for converting rubber plantation land to timber/forestry in Sylhet Division, and what carbon credit methodology (Verra, Gold Standard) applies to afforestation on former rubber land in Bangladesh?

## #5. Jing Tea Is a Luxury Brand Sitting on a Disconnected Supply Chain — Vertical Integration Could Create Camellia's Only Genuine Pricing Power

**TENSION:** Jing Tea sources exclusively from Asia (China, India, Sri Lanka, Japan, Taiwan) and serves luxury hospitality — Michelin restaurants, five-star hotels — with single-origin, single-garden teas and a growing sparkling tea range. Revenue grew 22% in 2024 but the business is still loss-making. Meanwhile, Camellia owns Castleton and Margaret's Hope in Darjeeling (among the world's most famous tea estates), 29 Indian gardens with Rainforest Alliance certification, and seven Kenyan tea estates. Jing does not systematically source from any of them. The branded tea margin is 36–47% gross (Dilmah) versus sub-5% for auction commodity. Camellia captures the upstream cost and risk but none of the downstream brand premium.

**HYPOTHESIS:** Connecting Jing's brand and distribution to Camellia's estate supply chain is the single cheapest way to create pricing power in the portfolio. A "Camellia Estate Collection" of single-garden teas from Castleton, Margaret's Hope, and Nandi Hills — sold through Jing's luxury hospitality channel — would command £50–200+/kg versus £1.50–2.00/kg at auction. The sparkling tea range (premium non-alcoholic beverage, one of the fastest-growing categories) should be aggressively scaled. Investment: £2–5m for product development, brand scaling, and digital DTC capability. This is the Dilmah playbook applied to an already-owned brand and already-owned estates.

**CATEGORY:** OFFENSIVE OPPORTUNITY

**POTENTIAL MAGNITUDE:** Major — £5–15m revenue within 5 years at 35–50% gross margins; more importantly, creates a narrative asset for the company's equity story

**TIME HORIZON:** Immediate — product development can begin within months; first revenues within 12–18 months

**LAYER 2 RESEARCH QUESTION:** What is Jing Tea's current customer list, revenue breakdown by channel (hospitality vs. retail vs. DTC), and the specific reason it does not source from Camellia's own estates — is this a quality/terroir decision, a commercial decision, or simply organisational inertia? What is the unit economics breakdown of Jing's sparkling tea range (COGS, retail price, margin, volume)? What would it cost to develop a "Camellia Estate Collection" line with packaging, certification, and distribution into Jing's existing hospitality network? What are the current DTC e-commerce economics for premium single-origin tea brands (customer acquisition cost, average order value, repeat rate)?

## #6. Carbon Credits: £0.5–3m/Year Sitting in 16,200 Hectares of Forest That Currently Generate Zero Revenue

**TENSION:** Camellia manages ~9,200 ha of indigenous forest and ~7,000 ha of commercial forestry across Kenya, Malawi, and Brazil. These forests sequester an estimated 52,000+ tonnes CO2e/year from forestry alone. At current voluntary market prices ($10–20/tonne for quality nature-based credits), this is £0.5–1.5m/year near-term, scaling to £1.5–3.0m/year by 2030. The cost to register under Verra VCS or Gold Standard is £100–300k. Competitors have not pursued this despite comparable land holdings. The revenue is real, the cost is minimal, and it simultaneously creates a "carbon-positive" narrative premium for every product Camellia sells.

**HYPOTHESIS:** This is the highest-return investment available to the company — £100–300k in registration costs against £0.5–3m/year in recurring revenue. Begin with Kakuzi's 1,255 ha and EP Kenya's 690 ha of managed forestry in Kenya, where carbon market infrastructure is most developed (Kenya is Africa's leading carbon credit country). Bundle with CCB (Climate, Community & Biodiversity) certification for a 30% price premium. The "carbon-positive macadamia" and "climate-neutral tea" positioning also directly addresses EU buyer ESG procurement requirements and strengthens the case for Jing Tea's premium pricing.

**CATEGORY:** OFFENSIVE OPPORTUNITY

**POTENTIAL MAGNITUDE:** Moderate as standalone revenue; Major when combined with its effect on product pricing and ESG narrative

**TIME HORIZON:** Near-term — project registration in 6–12 months, first credits in 18–24 months

**LAYER 2 RESEARCH QUESTION:** What specific Verra VCS and Gold Standard methodologies apply to Camellia's forestry types (indigenous conservation, managed eucalyptus/pine, tea agroforestry) across Kenya, Malawi, and Brazil? What are the actual sequestration measurement results from comparable projects in East African managed forestry? Which carbon credit project developers (e.g., Boomitra, Cropnuts) have worked with Kenyan agricultural estates, and what were their measurement, reporting, and verification costs? Is there a risk that Kenya's proposed national carbon credit framework (under development) could restrict private credit generation?

## #7. The AIM Listing Serves No One After April 2026 — Take-Private Economics Are Compelling

**TENSION:** Camellia moved to AIM in 2014 for IHT business property relief. In April 2026, AIM shares lose 100% BPR (dropping to 50% relief / 20% effective IHT rate). AIM has shrunk to 679 companies — its 30-year low — with 89 exits in 2024 alone. Camellia's free float is ~28% (~723,000 shares), average daily volume is ~4,100 shares (~£20k), and only two analysts cover the stock. Annual compliance costs are £500k–1m+. The listing provides essentially no capital-raising benefit, no liquidity, and no index inclusion. Quaero Capital (patient small-cap specialist) completely divested in 2025.

**HYPOTHESIS:** The loss of full IHT relief in April 2026 removes the primary structural reason for maintaining the AIM listing. A take-private at, say, 7,000p/share (roughly 60% of NAV but a 30–40% premium to the current price) would cost ~£51m to buy out the free float — easily funded from Camellia's £125m cash position. This would eliminate compliance costs, remove minority shareholder pressure, and allow the Foundation to manage the portfolio without public-market friction. The question is not whether take-private economics work (they clearly do) but whether the Foundation has the institutional motivation to execute. The February 2026 share buybacks at £41–48/share, completed at massive NAV discounts, mechanically transferred value from departing minorities to the Foundation — a pattern consistent with creeping privatisation.

**CATEGORY:** STRUCTURAL UNLOCK

**POTENTIAL MAGNITUDE:** Transformational for minority shareholders (30–40% premium); neutral-to-positive for the Foundation

**TIME HORIZON:** Immediate — the IHT trigger is April 2026, two months away

**LAYER 2 RESEARCH QUESTION:** What are the specific terms and beneficiaries of the Camellia Foundation (Bermuda)? What IHT obligations does the Foundation face post-April 2026 with 50% BPR versus the current 100%? Has the Foundation's board (which overlaps with Camellia Plc's board through Stephen Buckland and, historically, Simon Turner) discussed take-private scenarios? What premium to market price would be required to secure Rule 9/scheme of arrangement approval under the Takeover Code for an AIM company with a controlling shareholder? What are the recent precedents for AIM take-privates by controlling trusts or families?

## #8. Macadamia Shell Valorisation: 5,500–7,300 Tonnes/Year of Waste That Could Be Worth £1–3m/Year

**TENSION:** Camellia processes ~8,000–10,000 tonnes of macadamia nut-in-shell annually across three countries, generating 5,500–7,300 tonnes of shell waste. Currently used as low-value biomass fuel and road surfacing. Macadamia shells have a calorific value of 24.8 MJ/kg and produce activated carbon with surface areas exceeding 1,000 m²/g — among the highest of any biomass feedstock. Commercial activated carbon sells at ~$2,000/tonne. Biochar-based carbon removal credits trade at ~$187/tonne CO2e — among the highest-value credits in voluntary markets.

**HYPOTHESIS:** A £0.5–2m pyrolysis unit co-located at Kakuzi's cracking facility could convert shell waste into activated carbon ($160–300k/year from Kakuzi alone) and biochar (both a sellable product and a carbon credit generator). South Africa produces 65,000+ tonnes of shell nationally — Camellia could process third-party shells through its EPSA/Zemtac facility as well, turning a disposal cost into a processing fee business. The triple benefit: waste elimination, carbon credit revenue, and soil amendment for Camellia's own orchards.

**CATEGORY:** OFFENSIVE OPPORTUNITY

**POTENTIAL MAGNITUDE:** Moderate — £1–3m/year from activated carbon, biochar, and carbon credits

**TIME HORIZON:** Near-term — 12–18 months to install and commission a pyrolysis unit

**LAYER 2 RESEARCH QUESTION:** What is the actual current disposal cost and logistics for macadamia shell waste at each of Camellia's three cracking facilities (Kakuzi Kenya, EPM Malawi, Zemtac South Africa)? What are the capital costs, operating economics, and product specifications of pyrolysis systems for macadamia shells (e.g., Pyrocal, ECHO2)? Who are the buyers of activated carbon in East/Southern Africa, and what quality standards do they require? What is the certification pathway for biochar-based carbon removal credits under Puro.earth or the European Biochar Certificate?

## #9. The Assam Ceiling Amendment Could Create a Structural Opening for Estate Consolidation

**TENSION:** Assam's November 2025 amendment transfers labour-line land from 825 tea estates to workers at ₹3,000/bigha — widely below market value. All five major tea industry associations have filed writ petitions. The Gauhati HC hearing is 26 March 2026. If upheld, the amendment fragments estate integrity while maintaining statutory obligations for housing/amenities. Meanwhile, McLeod Russel (India's largest bulk producer) is in insolvency, with 33 remaining Assam estates potentially entering forced sale through NARCL. Goodricke is already India's second-largest tea producer.

**HYPOTHESIS:** Camellia faces a choice in India that the VEP hasn't articulated clearly. Option A: continue de-risking (selling marginal estates, as already happening with Chulsa and Leesh River) until India is a smaller part of the portfolio. Option B: take the opposite approach — use the Assam disruption and McLeod Russel collapse to consolidate at distressed prices, becoming the dominant Indian tea producer with sufficient scale to justify downstream investment (packet tea, instant tea, branded products through Goodricke). The current posture — neither committing to India nor exiting — is the worst option, because it incurs all the political and regulatory risks with insufficient scale to capture the upside. The 50% compulsory auction rule and Goodricke's existing branded tea business (volumes up 8% in 2024) and instant tea plant (volumes up 17%) provide the foundation for Option B.

**CATEGORY:** INFORMATION ARBITRAGE — the market prices India as pure risk, but the combination of McLeod Russel's collapse + rising auction prices (24% in 2024) + Goodricke's downstream capabilities creates a potential contrarian value opportunity

**POTENTIAL MAGNITUDE:** Major — a clear India commitment (either in or out) would reduce portfolio complexity and improve the equity story

**TIME HORIZON:** Near-term decision required (Gauhati HC ruling in March 2026 will clarify the ceiling amendment; McLeod Russel NARCL resolution in 2026 will determine estate availability)

**LAYER 2 RESEARCH QUESTION:** What is the specific impact of the Assam ceiling amendment on Goodricke's 12 Assam estates — how many hectares of labour-line land are affected, and what would the compensation be at ₹3,000/bigha? What are the exact estates, production volumes, and condition of McLeod Russel's 33 remaining gardens? At what price per kg of production capacity did Carbon Resources and M.K. Shah Exports bid, and would Goodricke face FDI regulatory obstacles (26% divestment requirement) in acquiring additional Indian estates? What is the total addressable market for Goodricke's branded packet tea and instant tea businesses, and what share of revenue would need to come from these channels to achieve 5%+ operating margin in India?

## #10. The Green & Gold Macadamia Dependency: Single-Agent Concentration Risk on 3% of Global Supply

**TENSION:** Camellia's entire macadamia kernel production across three countries is marketed exclusively through Green & Gold Macadamias, an Australian agency. Camellia brands its kernel as "Maclands" and describes it as "the largest macadamia brand in Africa." Yet the exclusive dependency on a single marketing agent for ~3% of global kernel production creates critical concentration risk — and it means Camellia has no direct customer relationships in its highest-growth crop.

**HYPOTHESIS:** Camellia should negotiate a phased restructuring of the Green & Gold relationship: retain the agent for commodity kernel export to established markets while building a direct sales capability for value-added products (roasted snacks, oil, flour) targeting Middle Eastern and Asian markets where Camellia already has tea trade relationships. Pakistan — Kenya's largest tea buyer at 35–40% of Mombasa auction volume — is a $5.8B snack market growing at 8–10%. ## #11. Kenyan Matcha: The Tariff Arbitrage Window Is Open Now

Risk is moderate — matcha production techniques are well-documented and Camellia already has agronomic expertise. First-mover advantage in "Kenyan matcha" could create a new category.

**CATEGORY:** OFFENSIVE OPPORTUNITY

**TIME HORIZON:** Near-term pilot (12–18 months for shade infrastructure and first experimental harvest), Medium-term commercial scale (3–5 years)

## #15. EU Forced Labour Regulation: Camellia's Settlement History Makes It a Probable Early Enforcement Target
