# Five moves to unlock value at Camellia Plc

**Camellia Plc sits on approximately £125M of net cash and 48,500 hectares of productive agricultural land across seven countries — yet trades at a 55–65% discount to its £348M book value.** The core problem is not the asset base but where Camellia sits in the value chain: selling commodity tea at Mombasa and Chittagong auctions for ~$2.10/kg, while branded buyers resell the same leaf at $14–17/kg retail — a 7× markup the company never captures. The five opportunities below, totalling £15–25M of investment against the company's £124.7M cash pile, could collectively add £20–40M of annual revenue and fundamentally reposition Camellia from loss-making commodity producer to integrated, margin-capturing agricultural platform. Each is mapped directly to assets Camellia already owns.

---

## Camellia today: a rich estate earning thin margins

Camellia's portfolio spans **34,857 hectares of tea** (India, Bangladesh, Kenya, Malawi), **3,533 hectares of macadamia** (Kenya, Malawi, South Africa), **934 hectares of avocado** (Kenya, Tanzania), **16,200 hectares of forestry**, and **1,710 hectares of rubber** (Bangladesh). The company operates **57 tea factories** and **three macadamia cracking plants**, and holds a 74% stake in BSE-listed Goodricke Group (29 Indian tea gardens) and 50.7% in dual-listed Kakuzi Plc in Kenya.

FY2024 revenue was **£262.2M** with a **trading loss of £5.5M** — an improvement from £10.3M loss in FY2023, but still operationally unprofitable at group level. EP Kenya is the only consistently profitable unit at **14.9% margin**; India barely broke even at 0.7%; Bangladesh lost 11.6%. The share price of roughly **5,000–5,900p** implies a market cap of **~£125–150M**, representing just **0.36–0.43× book value**. Net cash and liquid investments of **£124.7M** alone account for most of the market capitalisation.

The new management team under CEO Byron Coombs launched the Value Enhancement Plan (VEP) in May 2025, targeting £23–35M annual capital deployment — up from just £9.5M in 2024. The strategic direction emphasises operational improvement, risk reduction through selective divestiture, and growth investment including downstream activities. The board has the balance sheet, the mandate, and now needs the specific investment cases. Here are five.

---

## Opportunity 1: Build a tea extract facility in Kenya to capture the $3–5B ingredients market

**This is the single highest-impact opportunity.** The global tea extract market is worth **$3–5 billion** and growing at 7–8% annually, driven by RTD beverages, functional food ingredients, and dietary supplements. Finlays (Swire Group) already operates the Saosa extraction facility in Kericho, Kenya — proving the model works at origin. They recently sold their Kenyan tea estates to Browns Investments but retained the extraction plant, signalling that the extraction business is more valuable than the plantations feeding it.

The economics are compelling. Commodity CTC tea at Mombasa auction fetches **$2.05–$2.20/kg**. Standard spray-dried tea extract sells for **$8–$25/kg** — a **4–12× markup**. High-purity standardised extracts (green tea EGCG, polyphenol concentrates) command **$50–$200+/kg**. The capital cost for a mid-scale extraction and spray-drying facility is **$5–15M** (£4–12M), well within Camellia's VEP growth capex envelope.

EP Kenya's **five tea factories** in the Nandi Hills process leaf that currently flows straight to the Mombasa auction. A co-located extraction facility would divert a portion of production — perhaps 2,000–3,000 tonnes per year — into the ingredients supply chain. Customers would be B2B: RTD beverage companies (Coca-Cola, PepsiCo/KeVita, Nestlé), supplement manufacturers, and cosmetics ingredient buyers. This is not a brand-building exercise requiring consumer marketing spend; it is an industrial processing investment with identifiable buyers and proven demand.

The B2B model also creates optionality. Camellia could supply **cosmetic-grade green tea extract** (tea polyphenols market worth $474M, growing at 6.5% CAGR) and position for the **kombucha supply chain** ($2.4–4.9B market, 13–15% CAGR) by offering certified organic tea concentrates to kombucha producers like GT's Living Foods and Remedy Drinks. A single facility could serve food, beverage, supplement, and cosmetic channels simultaneously.

**Investment: £5–12M. Revenue potential: £5–15M/year at steady state. Margin: 25–40% gross. Payback: 3–4 years. Risk: Low — proven model operating 15km away at Finlays Saosa.**

## Opportunity 2: Monetise 16,200 hectares of forest through carbon credits

Camellia manages approximately **9,000 hectares of indigenous forest and conservation areas** plus **7,200 hectares of commercial forestry** (eucalyptus, pine, cypress) across Kenya, Malawi, and Brazil. These forests currently generate modest timber revenue but are otherwise treated as balance-sheet line items rather than revenue-generating assets. They represent one of the largest privately held forest portfolios in East Africa.

Voluntary carbon credit markets, while softened from 2022 peaks, still offer meaningful prices for high-quality projects. **Reforestation and afforestation credits trade at $14–$20/tonne CO2e**. Conservation-based credits with co-benefits (biodiversity, community impact) in Africa command **$14–$24/tonne**. Tropical forest sequesters **2.4–11.7 tonnes CO2 per hectare per year** depending on age and type.

A conservative calculation for Camellia's existing forests: 9,000 ha indigenous forest × 2.4 tCO2/ha/year × $16/credit = **$345,600/year** from conservation credits alone. Adding reforestation projects on marginal land — including the **1,710 hectares of loss-making rubber in Bangladesh** — could push annual carbon revenue to **$0.5–$3M** as new plantings mature. The setup cost for project registration under Verra VCS or Gold Standard is **$100,000–$300,000**, making this one of the highest-return investments available.

Beyond direct revenue, carbon credit registration creates a **narrative premium** for every product Camellia sells. "Carbon-positive" macadamia nuts, "climate-neutral" tea, and forestry-backed sustainability claims enhance market access and command pricing premiums from European buyers increasingly subject to ESG procurement requirements. This is the cheapest way to improve Camellia's story.

The Bangladesh rubber conversion is a critical sub-component. Rubber yields only **~500 kg/ha** versus Thailand's 1,800 kg/ha — structurally uncompetitive and operating below cost. Ceasing rubber operations saves annual losses while converting the land to fast-growing timber species (eucalyptus at **15–25 tCO2/ha/year** sequestration) creates a dual revenue stream: timber harvest at maturity plus carbon credits during growth. **Estimated annual loss elimination: £0.3–0.5M. New carbon + timber revenue: £0.2–0.5M/year, growing.**

**Investment: £0.1–0.3M for project registration; £0.5–1M for rubber-to-forestry conversion. Revenue potential: £0.5–2.5M/year, growing with credit prices. Payback: 1–2 years.**

## Opportunity 3: Produce Kenyan matcha and powdered green tea for tariff-advantaged Western markets

The global matcha market is experiencing explosive growth — **US retail sales rose 86% in three years** — yet supply remains concentrated in Japan and China, both of which now face significant US trade barriers. Japan faces **15% tariffs**; China faces **37.5%**. Meanwhile, supply shortages from poor Japanese harvests have pushed wholesale ceremonial-grade matcha to **$107–$164/kg**, with culinary grade at **$33–$53/kg**. Regular Kenyan CTC tea sells at **$2.10/kg** — the margin gap is staggering.

Kenya's highlands are physiologically suitable for shade-grown green tea. EP Kenya's estates in the **Nandi Hills at 1,800–2,100m** sit in the same elevation band as Japan's premium growing regions. Small Kenyan producers like Vobyl Tea are already making Kenyan matcha; Jenier World of Teas sells Kenyan white tea matcha powder from the same Nandi Highlands where Camellia operates. The proof of concept exists.

The required investment is moderate: **shade-growing infrastructure** (shade netting over select tea plots, $1,000–$2,000/ha), **de-veining and de-stemming equipment**, and **stone grinding mills or jet mills** ($0.5–$2M for commercial capacity). Total capex of **£1–3M** could yield a product selling at **$33–$53/kg for culinary/barista grade** — roughly **15–25× the current auction price** for the same leaf.

The strategic positioning would be "**African Origin Matcha**" or "**Kenyan Powdered Green Tea**" — differentiated from Japanese matcha but positioned as a premium, tariff-free alternative for the US market and a sustainable, single-origin option for EU markets. Initial target customers would be food service and food manufacturing (matcha lattes, ice cream, baking ingredients) rather than the ceremonial retail segment. Kenya already exports specialty tea — only **7,510 tonnes (1.26% of production)** — but it commands significant premiums, and powdered format multiplies value further.

Camellia's existing Jing Tea brand (luxury hospitality tea, revenue up 22% in 2024, approaching breakeven) could serve as the branded vehicle for premium retail sales, while bulk culinary-grade matcha flows through B2B ingredient channels.

**Investment: £1–3M. Revenue potential: £2–8M/year from converting 100–200 tonnes of green leaf. Margin: 40–60% gross. Payback: 2–3 years. Strategic upside: first-mover position in tariff-advantaged origin matcha.**

## Opportunity 4: Valorise macadamia shells into activated carbon and biochar

Across its three macadamia operations, Camellia processes approximately **8,000–10,000 tonnes of nut-in-shell (NIS) annually**. Given a kernel recovery rate of **27–33%**, this generates roughly **5,500–7,300 tonnes of shell waste per year**. Currently, shells are used primarily as biomass fuel for drying operations — a low-value application of a remarkably useful material.

Macadamia shells have a **calorific value of 24.8 MJ/kg** (comparable to coal) and produce activated carbon with **surface areas exceeding 1,000 m²/g** — among the highest of any biomass feedstock. Commercial activated carbon sells for approximately **$2,000/tonne**. Conversion yield from shell feedstock is 8–15%, meaning 1,000 tonnes of shell could produce **80–150 tonnes of activated carbon worth $160,000–$300,000**. Across Camellia's full shell output, the theoretical activated carbon revenue is **$0.9–$2.2M/year**.

**Biochar** represents an even more interesting pathway. The biochar market is growing at **11.1% CAGR** and biochar-based carbon removal credits trade at approximately **$187/tonne CO2e** — among the highest-value credits in voluntary markets. Pyrolysis of macadamia shells produces biochar that sequesters carbon while also functioning as a soil amendment for Camellia's own orchards and tea gardens. This creates a triple benefit: waste elimination, carbon credit revenue, and soil improvement.

A pyrolysis unit capable of processing 2,000–3,000 tonnes of shell per year costs **$0.5–$2M**. Co-locating at the existing Kakuzi cracking facility (Kenya) or EPSA facility (South Africa) minimises logistics. The South Africa location is strategically advantageous given the country produces **65,000+ tonnes of shell annually** from its national crop — Camellia could potentially process third-party shells as well.

Additional shell applications include **cosmetic exfoliant** (milled macadamia shell powder for scrubs and skincare products) and **silicon carbide nanoparticle production** for industrial applications, though these are smaller, more speculative markets.

**Investment: £0.5–2M per pyrolysis installation. Revenue potential: £1–3M/year from activated carbon, biochar sales, and carbon credits. Margin: 30–50% gross. Payback: 2–3 years. Strategic bonus: enhances sustainability credentials across all products.**

## Opportunity 5: Scale the Maclands consumer brand into Middle Eastern and Asian snack markets

Kakuzi already operates an **"ultra-modern" macadamia cracking facility** with 2,000 tonnes/year kernel capacity and launched a **macadamia oil extraction plant in June 2024** (1,000 litres/day). The Maclands brand sells roasted macadamias and macadamia oil into the Kenyan domestic market. But this is subscale. Roasted and flavoured macadamias retail at **$35–60/kg in Europe** — roughly **3–5× the wholesale kernel price** of $10–14/kg. The margin multiplication from raw kernel to consumer product is enormous, and Camellia already owns every step of the chain.

The untapped markets are not Europe or North America, where shelf space is dominated by established brands (Hawaiian Host, Mauna Loa, Golden Macadamias). Instead, **the Middle East and South/Southeast Asia** represent high-growth, underserved premium snack markets where Camellia already has trade relationships through tea exports. Pakistan — Kenya's largest tea buyer at 35–40% of Mombasa auction volume — is also a **$5.8B snack food market growing at 8–10% annually**. The UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Egypt are premium nut markets with high per-capita consumption.

An investment of **£2–5M** would scale Kakuzi's roasting and flavouring line, develop retail-ready packaging, and establish distribution in 3–5 target markets. Product range would include roasted/salted macadamias, flavoured variants (honey-roasted, chilli-lime, chocolate-coated), **cold-pressed macadamia oil** (food-grade at $25–50/litre retail; cosmetic-grade at $30–80/litre), and macadamia butter. The flavoured snack segment is particularly attractive because it transforms a semi-perishable kernel into a shelf-stable consumer product with **12–18 month shelf life**.

Camellia's three-country macadamia footprint (Kenya, Malawi, South Africa) provides supply resilience — crop risk is diversified across hemispheres and climatic zones. The company already uses **Green & Gold Macadamias** as export agent; the opportunity is to redirect a portion of kernel from commodity export into the higher-margin consumer product channel.

**Investment: £2–5M. Revenue potential: £3–10M/year at steady state. Margin: 25–40% gross on consumer products vs 10–15% on commodity kernel. Payback: 3–4 years.**

## The value chain arithmetic that makes the case

The fundamental strategic insight is that Camellia operates almost exclusively at the bottom of its value chains, capturing 8–18% of the final consumer price across both tea and macadamia. Moving even one step downstream transforms economics:

| Value chain stage | Tea ($/kg) | Macadamia ($/kg kernel equivalent) | Camellia's current position |
|---|---|---|---|
| Farmgate/auction | $2.05–$2.20 | $10–$14 | **Here** |
| Extract/processed ingredient | $8–$25 | $18–$30 (roasted) | Partially (Kakuzi oil) |
| Branded consumer product | $10–$17 (commodity brand) | $35–$60 (retail snack) | Minimally (Jing Tea) |
| Specialty/premium | $15–$100+ (single-origin) | $50–$80 (gourmet/cosmetic) | Barely |
| High-value extract | $50–$200+ (EGCG/matcha) | $25–$80/litre (oil) | Not yet |

Each of the five proposed opportunities moves Camellia rightward on this table. The combined investment of **£10–25M** sits comfortably within the VEP's stated £15–25M annual growth capex target and would be funded entirely from the £124.7M cash position without requiring debt.

## What comparable companies prove about forward integration

**Dilmah** (Sri Lanka) transformed from a single-origin tea estate into a branded product sold in 104 countries with **~$70M revenue and 39% gross margins** — versus the 10–15% margins typical of commodity tea operations. The key was packing at origin and building brand equity around the founder's personal story and single-origin quality promise.

**Zespri** (New Zealand) converted commodity kiwifruit into a **NZ$4B branded platform** by investing in proprietary varieties (SunGold commands $26/tray versus $9 for standard green), controlling quality at every point, and building consumer brand awareness. Zespri captures **~50% of global kiwifruit value on just 30% of volume**.

**Kenya Nut Company** demonstrates the integrated macadamia model — cultivation, processing, and branded export — and has attracted **$18.7M** in development finance from Proparco. Closer to home, Camellia's own **Kakuzi** is already executing a version of this playbook with macadamia snacks and oil, but at subscale.

The common thread: **patient capital deployed into processing, branding, and quality differentiation consistently generates 2–5× the margin of commodity production** in agricultural value chains. Camellia has the assets, the cash, and now the strategic mandate.

---

## Conclusion: where the board should focus first

The five opportunities rank by a combination of margin uplift, capital efficiency, and execution risk. **Tea extract (Opportunity 1) offers the highest absolute revenue potential** with proven precedent operating in the same geography. **Carbon credits (Opportunity 2) offer the highest return on investment** at minimal capex against assets Camellia already holds. **Kenyan matcha (Opportunity 3) offers the largest margin multiplication** per kilogram but carries higher execution and market-development risk. **Shell valorisation (Opportunity 4) creates revenue from waste** at modest investment. **Maclands consumer brand scaling (Opportunity 5) leverages existing infrastructure** toward proven premium markets.

The correct sequencing is to pursue Opportunities 2 and 4 immediately (low capex, quick returns), begin detailed feasibility and engineering on Opportunity 1 (highest impact), pilot Opportunity 3 with a small shade-growing trial at EP Kenya, and fund Opportunity 5 through Kakuzi's existing capital budget. Together, these five moves would shift Camellia from a company that grows commodities and waits for auction prices to one that controls the conversion of its agricultural output into products the market actually pays premium prices for. At a 55–65% discount to book value with £125M in cash, the board has both the obligation and the means to act.
