# Camellia Plc: comprehensive intelligence brief (Aug 2024 – Feb 2026)

**Camellia Plc is in the midst of its most significant transformation in decades.** Under CEO Byron Coombs, the company has sold its Bermuda insurance stake for $100 million, restarted dividends after years of suspension, launched a formal Value Enhancement Plan targeting £23–35 million in annual investment, and begun disposing of non-core Indian tea estates — all while trading at a persistent **60% discount to net asset value** (~4,900p vs ~12,360p NAV per share). Simultaneously, the competitive landscape in its core markets is being reshaped by Browns Investments' acquisition of both the former James Finlay and Lipton tea estates in Kenya, creating a new mega-competitor, while in Malawi, the country's second-largest tea producer has suspended all operations — presenting potentially the most significant acquisition opportunity in Camellia's orbit.

### 2. Corporate actions signal a new shareholder-return era

The period from December 2024 to June 2025 saw Camellia's most intensive capital return activity in its modern history. Three distinct mechanisms were deployed in quick succession: a **£9 million on-market share buyback** (launched December 2024, with shares purchased at £41–50), a **260p per share final dividend** (£7.1M total, payable July 2025 — the first dividend since its suspension), and an **£18.9M tender offer at £54 per share** (completed June 2025, with £11.6M actually tendered). Combined shareholder distributions totalled approximately **£30 million** in under seven months.

On the disposal front, Camellia exited its entire **37% stake in BF&M** (Bermuda insurance) for $100 million, its Bangladesh financial services associates for £7.9M, and two Indian tea estates (Chulsa and Leesh River) for a combined ~£3.9M. The BF&M sale alone added approximately £79M in gross proceeds to the balance sheet. Camellia now has **zero associate companies** for the first time, simplifying its structure dramatically.

The **Value Enhancement Plan** (VEP), announced 19 May 2025, represents the most important strategic document from Camellia's new management team. It targets annual growth capex of **£15–25M** (vs just £1.6M in 2024) plus £8–10M maintenance capex. Focus areas include water resilience, farm technology, factory efficiency, mechanisation, and crop diversification. The plan explicitly includes further disposals of "higher-risk assets" alongside this investment programme.

### 3. Operating developments across the estate portfolio

**Kenya (Eastern Produce Kenya and Kakuzi):** Two critical developments dominate. First, the **National Land Commission recommended that Kakuzi surrender approximately 3,200 acres** (26% of its productive estate) in Murang'a County to settle historical land injustice claims (announced November 2025). Kakuzi has filed legal proceedings to challenge this recommendation. Second, **over 100 residents have been occupying 140 hectares of EPK's Sitoi Tea Estate** in Nandi County since August 2023, with losses exceeding **KSh 150 million** (~£900K) over five months from August 2024. Court injunctions have gone unenforced by police. The broader context is troubling: the NLC ordered in April 2023 that lease renewals for all foreign tea companies in Kericho, Bomet, and Nandi counties be withheld until a resurvey is completed, and Kenya's 2010 Constitution mandated conversion of 999-year leases to a 99-year maximum for foreign-held land.

On the positive side, Kakuzi's macadamia operations surged — profit from macadamia leapt from **KSh 32M to KSh 319M** in H1 2025, benefiting from buoyant global demand and Kenya's reinstated ban on raw nut exports which routes volume to integrated processors.

**Bangladesh (Duncan Brothers):** Despite national tea production declining ~10%, Duncan Brothers achieved **record production of approximately 15 million kg** in 2024, demonstrating operational outperformance. MD Mustafizur Rahman has prioritised climate adaptation (12 rainwater reservoirs built) and mechanisation trials. However, the August 2024 political upheaval that toppled PM Hasina created temporary instability — the state-owned NTC's chairman fled and wages went unpaid for weeks across NTC's 12 gardens. Duncan Brothers appears to have navigated this period without major disruption.

**India (Goodricke Group):** The VEP's de-risking agenda is most visible here. Two estates were sold — Chulsa (INR 181M, March 2025) and Leesh River (INR 265M, July 2025). Crop diversification trials are underway on remaining estates (garlic, turmeric, ginger, livestock, mechanical plucking), suggesting Goodricke's long-term role in the group may be shifting away from pure tea.

**Malawi (Eastern Produce Malawi):** H1 2025 results showed improved performance from Malawi operations, though the broader environment is challenging. Eastern Produce Malawi is Malawi's largest tea exporter, accounting for **38% of national tea exports**, and also operates macadamia (1,496 ha) and forestry (1,113 ha).

## SECTION 2: INDUSTRY AND PEER ACTIVITY

### 4. Sector transactions are reshaping the competitive landscape

The most consequential transaction in Camellia's competitive environment is **Browns Investments' (Sri Lanka/LOLC Holdings) acquisition of both the former James Finlay and former Lipton/ekaterra tea estates in Kenya**. Browns purchased Finlay's nine estates (10,300 ha, completed November 2023, price KSh 3.1 billion / ~$24M) and Lipton's 11 plantations and 8 factories (completed December 2024, terms undisclosed). Browns is now the **single largest private tea estate operator in western Kenya**, producing approximately **87 million kg annually** across Kenya, Rwanda, Tanzania, and Sri Lanka — making it one of the world's largest tea exporters. This transaction represents the exit of two legacy British tea companies from estate ownership and the entry of aggressive Asian capital. Lipton (CVC-backed) has shifted to an asset-light model focused on blending, packing, and brand management.

Other notable tea M&A includes **TreeHouse Foods' $205 million acquisition of Harris Tea** (US private-label, January 2025, ~8.5x EBITDA) and **Rossell India's acquisition of Dhoedaam Tea Estate** in Assam (January 2025, 1.8M kg annual production). In India, **McLeod Russel's massive debt** (~Rs 1,461 crore across 10 lenders) was transferred to NARCL (India's national bad-bank) in March 2025 for Rs 700 crore — a 36% haircut to lenders. Both Carbon Resources and M.K. Shah Exports expressed interest in acquiring McLeod Russel estates, though no deal has closed.

In the broader plantation sector, **Socfin was taken private** in 2024 at €32.50/share (~€460M) by the Fabri family and Bolloré group amid escalating ESG controversies, including a Bloomberg investigation into sexual harassment across multiple plantations. **MP Evans acquired two Indonesian palm oil companies** from Golden Land Berhad in 2025, expanding its managed area to ~70,000 hectares, and delivered record profits — its market cap now stands at ~£826M, double the £415M KLK offered in its failed 2016 hostile bid.

No major macadamia M&A transactions were identified in the period. The sector remains fragmented, with significant impact investment flowing into smaller players: **Mirova invested $8.5M in Pamoja/TenSenses** (Kenya-Tanzania macadamia, targeting 13,000 farmers) and **Hivos-Triodos provided financing to LIMBUA** (German-Kenyan organic macadamia, 9,000+ certified smallholders).

### 5. Peer companies reveal a stressed but consolidating sector

**Williamson Tea Kenya** swung to a **net loss of KSh 166 million** in FY ending March 2025 (vs KSh 527M profit the prior year), citing oversupply, a strong shilling, and dry weather. The dividend was cut to KSh 10 from KSh 15. A profit warning was issued in May 2025. H1 FY2026 (to September 2025) showed some stabilisation with losses narrowing to KSh 10M. The company is pursuing cost reduction, bought-leaf quality controls, and specialty tea development ("Kenya Orthodox"). Market capitalisation stands at approximately **KSh 2.58 billion (~$20M)** — a fraction of Camellia's capitalisation.

**McLeod Russel** (India's largest bulk tea producer) remains in **severe financial distress**. FY2025 net loss was Rs 197.87 crore. Book value per share is negative (-Rs 17.55), debt-to-equity stands at 33.57x, and the auditors have flagged going-concern uncertainty. The company now operates just 33 estates in Assam and West Bengal (down from 64) after years of forced divestiture. A revised resolution plan was submitted to NARCL in June 2025, with a techno-economic viability study underway as of November 2025. **This restructuring leaves Camellia (through its 74%-owned Goodricke Group) as arguably the largest tea producer of Indian origin still operating at scale.**

**Sasini PLC** (Nairobi-listed, diversified Kenya agriculture) is actively pursuing M&A across East Africa in tea, coffee, and other crops, having commissioned a 4M kg/year macadamia processing factory. Its Mweiga Estate macadamia trees (planted 2017) produced their first significant harvest in 2025.

**SIPEF** (Euronext Brussels) reported a near-doubling of its operating result to KUSD 84,641 in H1 2025, driven by higher palm oil prices. Total equity reached nearly $1 billion. **REA Holdings** (LSE) redeemed all its sterling notes and extended dollar note repayments to December 2028, while achieving full RSPO certification.

**KTDA governance is under serious pressure.** A Tea Board of Kenya audit revealed KTDA-managed factories accumulated **KSh 26.06 billion in debt**, with three-quarters concentrated in West of Rift factories. Unauthorised inter-factory loans totalled KSh 10.36 billion. Farmer payments fell over 22% year-on-year. The government ruled out disbanding KTDA but announced comprehensive governance restructuring.

### 6. Commodity prices tell a story of structural oversupply in tea, recovery in macadamia

**Tea:** The Mombasa auction averaged **$2.05/kg in 2025**, essentially flat from $2.06/kg in 2024 but well below the February 2024 spike of $3.41/kg. Kenyan tea averaged $2.12/kg (January–August 2025), down 3.6% year-on-year, pressured by global oversupply of CTC black tea, the loss of Sudan and Iran as export markets, and a stronger Kenyan shilling (KSh 129/$ vs KSh 144/$ in 2024). Malawi tea fetched just **$0.95/kg** at Mombasa — among the lowest of all origins. The World Bank projects a **5% tea price decline in 2025**, followed by a modest ~2% recovery in 2026–2027. Global tea production has doubled in 20 years to approximately 6 billion kg, creating persistent structural oversupply in CTC grades. However, a **45-million-kg CTC tea deficit** emerged in 2025 as Kenya production fell 12.1% due to drought, Bangladesh declined ~10%, and Malawi contributed tea in only 11 of 51 Mombasa auction sales. If this deficit persists, it could support a price recovery.

**Macadamia:** Prices are **recovering from 2022 lows** when they halved due to post-pandemic inventory buildups. Industry-wide expectations are for a **10–30% price increase** in 2025 depending on grade and origin. South Africa dominates production (78,091 tonnes in 2024, forecast 90,000–95,500 in 2025), followed by China (~69,500 tonnes), Australia (~54,000 tonnes), and Kenya (~44,000 tonnes). China is both the world's largest buyer and increasingly a self-sufficient producer — a long-term structural risk for exporters. Kenya's government reinstated the ban on raw in-shell exports in November 2024, benefiting integrated processors like Camellia/EPK and Kakuzi by routing volume through domestic processing. The global macadamia market is valued at approximately **$1.6–1.8 billion**, projected to reach $2.8–3.3 billion by 2030–2033.

**Rubber:** Natural rubber is in its **fifth consecutive year of supply deficit** (2025 demand ~15.6M MT vs production ~14.9M MT). Prices reached approximately **$2,300–2,500/MT** in late 2025. Key structural drivers include aging tapper populations in Thailand and Malaysia, palm oil switching in Indonesia reducing rubber acreage, and EV tyre demand requiring ~15% more natural rubber. The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), covering rubber, creates a potential premium for compliant producers.

## SECTION 3: DISTRESSED AND OPPORTUNISTIC TARGETS

### 7. Distressed assets present real but geographically concentrated opportunities

**HIGHEST PRIORITY — Lujeri Tea Estates, Malawi (production suspended, August 2025)**

Malawi's **second-largest tea producer**, contributing **22% of national tea exports**, **suspended all tea production** in late July/early August 2025. Lujeri, owned by **PGI Group (UK)**, stopped buying from over 10,000 smallholder farmers who depended on the company, and its estate workers face unemployment. Management cited "global market dynamics and reduced receptivity to Malawi tea exports." PGI also faces London High Court claims filed in March 2021 by women workers alleging sexual abuse and harassment on Lujeri plantations (22 incidents documented, including 13 assaults and 10 rapes). If PGI seeks to exit, this represents the most significant identified acquisition opportunity for Camellia. **Eastern Produce Malawi already controls 38% of Malawi's tea exports; acquiring Lujeri would consolidate Camellia's position to approximately 60% of national tea exports.** No asking price is publicly known. Risks include the forex crisis (parallel market premium peaked at 150% in March 2025), Lujeri's reputational liabilities, and persistently low Malawi tea prices ($0.95/kg at Mombasa).

**Mt Kenya Tea Factory Company Ltd — in administration (September 2025)**

Mt Kenya Tea Factory Company Limited was placed under administration and receivership on 3 September 2025, along with Tecof Limited and Njeru Industries Limited. Joint administrators were appointed under Kenya's Insolvency Act, 2015, and creditors invited to submit claims. The scale and asset quality are unconfirmed, but the proximity to Camellia's Central Kenya operations makes this worth monitoring. This likely represents a small-scale opportunity relative to Camellia's portfolio.

**Distressed KTDA factories in western Kenya**

Multiple KTDA-managed factories in Nyamira County (Nyansiongo, Nyankoba, Sanganyi, Kibirigo, Gianchore) and Kericho/Bomet regions froze monthly payments to farmers at KSh 23–24/kg as of February 2026, citing "low tea absorption and subdued prices." Some factories, including Kiamokama and Rianyamwamu, paid bonuses as low as KSh 10/kg — half of the prior year. The KSh 26 billion system-wide KTDA debt creates opportunities for processing partnerships, bought-leaf arrangements, or management contracts, though direct factory acquisition may be structurally difficult given KTDA's cooperative ownership model.

**McLeod Russel's Indian tea estates — potential forced divestiture**

McLeod Russel's 33 remaining estates in Assam and West Bengal could enter forced sale if the NARCL resolution process fails. Carbon Resources expressed interest in 18 gardens at Rs 750 crore; M.K. Shah Exports offered Rs 550 crore. A TEV study was underway as of November 2025. These Assam estates would complement Camellia's existing Goodricke Group operations but would run counter to the VEP's stated goal of reducing risk through India de-risking.

**Bangladesh — structural opportunities in a fragile sector**

No confirmed tea estate divestitures by Indian or British parent companies were identified beyond Camellia's own financial services disposals. However, the sector has structural fragility: the **National Tea Company** (state-owned, 12 gardens) posted a net loss of Tk 26.66 crore and its leadership fled during the August 2024 political upheaval, making it a potential privatisation candidate under the Yunus interim government. Approximately **9,400 hectares of very old, uneconomic tea land** (yielding only 482–496 kg/ha vs the national average of ~1,528 kg/ha) exists across the industry, representing consolidation opportunity for operators willing to invest in replanting. The **United Group** acquired Moulvi Tea Garden (716 ha) in Sylhet, showing domestic buyers are active. Sylhet estate owners warned in early 2024 that the industry could "collapse" without intervention.

**South African macadamia grower exits**

Low macadamia prices in 2022–2024 caused some South African growers to exit, reduce inputs, or remove orchards. No specific distressed processing facilities were identified for sale, but the KwaZulu-Natal province is experiencing a notable transition from sugarcane to macadamia farming, creating potential acquisition opportunities among struggling transition-period operations. The macadamia sector remains highly fragmented across all producing countries.

**Supply chain distress — limited signals**

No specific tea packaging, logistics, or processing equipment companies were identified as being in distress in Camellia's key markets. The Dar es Salaam tea auction closed in July 2025 due to inadequate volumes (average only $0.90/kg vs Mombasa's $1.80/kg), reflecting the broader East African tea sector's challenges but not creating a direct acquisition opportunity.

### 8. Partnership and licensing opportunities span technology, carbon, and consumer brands

**Carbon credits represent the most financially significant partnership opportunity.** Camellia's estates contain substantial carbon sequestration potential. Macadamia orchards sequester approximately **17 tonnes of CO₂ per hectare per year**, and the company's 3,498 hectares of forestry (including 1,113 ha in Malawi) could qualify for agroforestry or reforestation credits. At current voluntary carbon market prices of **$15–30/tonne for agroforestry credits**, a 10,000-hectare estate portfolio could potentially generate **$750,000–$5.1 million annually**. Kenya is Africa's leading carbon credit country (~20% of the continent's total), with the world's first Climate Change Act enshrining carbon markets. Key certification partners include Verra (VM0042 methodology for improved agricultural land management), Gold Standard, and new entrants like Boomitra (satellite-based MRV, Earthshot Prize winner) and Cropnuts (Kenya, soil baseline carbon mapping). The Africa Carbon Markets Initiative forecasts Africa could tap a **$6–30 billion annual market by 2030**.

**Macadamia value-addition and plant-based dairy** offer significant margin expansion. The macadamia milk market is growing at **12.5% CAGR**, with brands like Milkadamia (12,000 retail locations in the UK/US), Califia Farms, and South Africa's Giraf expanding rapidly. Cold-pressed macadamia oil, butter, and paste are growing segments. Kakuzi has already diversified into these products. Camellia's integrated macadamia operations in Kenya, Malawi, and South Africa are well-positioned to supply bulk macadamia to these brands or develop own-brand value-added products.

**AgTech partnerships in Kenya** are immediately actionable. DJI and XAG drones are already being deployed on **Kericho County tea estates** for precision foliar spraying, reducing pesticide use by 30–50% and water consumption by up to 90%. Apollo Agriculture (AI-driven agri-fintech, $85.5M raised, 100,000+ Kenyan farmers served) and Pula Advisors (digital crop insurance, $20M Series B) could partner with Camellia on outgrower support programmes. Kenya's One Million Farmer Platform connects 1.1 million farmers through 24 AgTech startups.

**EUDR compliance for rubber** is both a regulatory obligation and a competitive advantage. Camellia's **1,710 hectares of rubber in Bangladesh** will require full compliance by December 2026 (geolocation data, due diligence statements, deforestation-free proof since December 2020). Estate-model production inherently simplifies traceability versus fragmented smallholder supply chains, positioning Camellia's rubber as a premium EUDR-compliant source. Technology providers include BanQu (blockchain for rubber), TraceX Technologies, and Preferred by Nature.

**Specialty tea premiums** are growing faster than the bulk market. The single-origin tea segment is expanding at **7.25% CAGR**, and Camellia's estate teas from Kenya, Bangladesh, and Malawi could command premiums in this segment. Chinese modern tea chains like CHAGEE (sourcing ~10,000 tonnes annually, expanding across 8 countries) represent new wholesale demand for estate-quality tea. The Coffee Bean & Tea Leaf sources directly from Kenyan estates "without middlemen" — a natural fit for Camellia's model.

## SECTION 4: UPCOMING CATALYSTS AND CALENDAR

### 9. Key events ahead will test the VEP and sector dynamics

**Camellia-specific catalysts:** FY2025 annual results are expected in **April–May 2026**, which will provide the first full-year view of VEP execution. The Kakuzi land claims legal challenge will progress through Kenyan courts, with any adverse ruling potentially affecting the company's NAV materially. The board recruitment for Rachel English's replacement as independent NED should conclude in early 2026. The ongoing share buyback programme (£9M authorised, partially deployed) may continue or be renewed at the 2026 AGM.

**Bangladesh elections** expected in **early 2026 (likely February)** represent the most significant macro catalyst for Camellia's Bangladesh operations. The BNP appears politically favoured; a stable new government could improve the investment climate and regulatory predictability for foreign agricultural companies. Conversely, a contested or prolonged transition could create operational risk.

**Kenya's regulatory agenda** includes the Tea Development Authority Bill (proposing to revert KTDA to a regulatory authority), the Strategic Tea Quality Improvement Programme, and ongoing NLC proceedings affecting all foreign tea estates in the Rift Valley. The macadamia seasonal harvesting ban runs from 1 December 2025 to 15 February 2026, after which the new season begins at a minimum farm-gate price of KSh 100/kg.

**EU Deforestation Regulation** compliance is required by **30 December 2026** for large operators. The European Commission must conduct a simplification review by **30 April 2026**, which could lead to scope amendments.

**Tea seasonality:** Kenya's main crop season runs March–August, with the heaviest flush in May–July. Bangladesh's main season is April–November. Malawi harvests October–May. The Mombasa auction operates year-round on a weekly cycle.

## Conclusion

Camellia Plc is executing a credible transformation under new management, but faces a paradox: its balance sheet has never been stronger (£124.7M net cash), yet its core operations remain loss-making at the trading level, and its most important geography — Kenya — presents escalating land tenure risk that could erode asset values regardless of operational performance. The VEP's ambition to deploy £23–35M annually in growth capex represents a genuine strategic pivot, but the gap between current trading losses and the investment required means patience is essential.

The **Lujeri Tea Estates suspension in Malawi** stands out as the single most actionable acquisition opportunity. Consolidating from 38% to ~60% of Malawi's tea exports would give Camellia significant market power in a small but strategically positioned origin. The **KTDA system's financial distress** opens processing partnership possibilities in Kenya, while **McLeod Russel's slow-motion collapse** could eventually release high-quality Assam estates — though this would conflict with Camellia's stated India de-risking strategy. In the macadamia sector, the absence of large-scale M&A creates a potential first-mover advantage for consolidation, particularly in South Africa where grower exits have created available acreage.

Three forces will shape the next 12 months: Kenya's land tenure resolution (existential risk), the CTC tea supply deficit's effect on pricing (potential tailwind), and the VEP's ability to convert Camellia's cash hoard into operational profitability (the central strategic question). The persistent 60% discount to NAV means the market assigns near-zero probability to full value realisation — creating either a deep value opportunity or a value trap depending entirely on management execution.
