# Camellia Plc: Anatomy of an Opaque Agricultural Holdco

**Camellia Plc (AIM: CAM) is a 135-year-old diversified agriculture company controlled by a Bermuda charitable trust holding 56.45% of shares, trading at a persistent ~60% discount to book value — with its market capitalization of ~£126 million barely exceeding its £124.7 million net cash position.** This means the market effectively values Camellia's 48,500 hectares of agricultural land across seven countries, its listed subsidiaries, and all operating businesses at approximately zero. The reasons are structural: a controlling trust that insulates management from market discipline, AIM listing with reduced governance requirements, persistent operating losses, serious unresolved human rights controversies, and a compensation structure with no meaningful performance linkage. A new management team installed in late 2023 has announced a "Value Enhancement Plan," but execution against this backdrop of entrenchment and opacity will determine whether the discount narrows or deepens.

---

## 1. Governance structure reveals deep entrenchment

**There is no "Boustead Trust" or "Boustead family."** Research across all available Camellia Plc filings, annual reports, and corporate materials found no reference to any Boustead entity. The controlling shareholder is **the Camellia Foundation**, a Bermuda-registered charity established by founder Gordon Fox, which holds its stake through a chain: Camellia Foundation (Bermuda) → Camellia Private Trust Company Limited (trustee) → **Camellia Holding AG** (Glarus, Switzerland) → **1,427,000 shares / 56.45%** of Camellia Plc. The nature and beneficiaries of this Bermuda trust are not transparently disclosed. A linked UK charity, also called The Camellia Foundation (formerly The Libra Foundation), receives funding from the same ultimate source.

The board comprises seven directors following Rachel English's departure in January 2026. Three are executive: **CEO Byron Coombs** (appointed September 2023; former Investment Director at Camellia and CEO of the Group's private bank Duncan Lawrie before its 2017 sale), **CFO Oliver Capon** (appointed June 2024; ex-Shell, Cambridge-educated chartered accountant), and **Director of Agriculture Graham Mclean** (appointed 2014; 25+ years with the Group). The four non-executives are **Chairman Simon Turner** (NED since 2020, Chairman since December 2023; previously Chairman of the Camellia Foundation itself), **Stephen Buckland** (appointed 2021; simultaneously a director of Camellia Holding AG and executive at the Camellia Foundation — explicitly classified as non-independent), and independents **Alec Hayley** (appointed July 2024; CFO background in agriculture commodities at ECOM Agroindustrial) and **Alison McFadyen** (appointed July 2024; 22 years at Standard Chartered, now Senior Independent Director).

The Chairman's independence is compromised by his prior role leading the controlling Foundation. Buckland's triple role — NED at the listed company, director of the controlling Swiss holding entity, and Foundation executive — creates an inherent structural conflict. **Only two of seven directors are genuinely independent**, though both were appointed as recently as July 2024 in what appears to be a belated governance refresh. The previous Chairman, **Malcolm Perkins**, served for approximately five decades (1972–2023) — an extraordinary tenure that epitomises entrenchment concerns. Former long-serving independent NEDs William Gibson and Gautam Dalal did not stand for re-election in 2022, and former CEO Tom Franks departed that same year.

**Major shareholders** beyond the controlling trust include **Nokia Bell Pensioenfonds** (Belgian pension fund) at **14.80%** and **XIX-Invest NV** (Belgian investment company) at **3.03%**. The free float is approximately **723,302 shares or ~28%** of issued capital. Only two analysts cover the stock. No activist investors are present — and with a 56.45% controlling block, any activist campaign would be structurally futile.

## 2. A decade of simplification, punctuated by one spectacular failure

Camellia's M&A record over the past decade tells a clear story: **net divestment of approximately £185 million**, transforming a sprawling conglomerate into a focused agricultural holdco. The major disposals include:

- **Duncan Lawrie private bank** (2016–17): Loan book to Arbuthnot Latham for £42.7m, asset management to Brewin Dolphin for £28m, total ~£71m gross
- **Horizon Farms, California** (2020): Sold to Maricopa Orchards for US$31m; cited climate risk as rationale
- **Associated Cold Stores & Transport** (2022–23): Sold to Constellation Cold Logistics for £16.5m plus £3.8m pre-completion dividend
- **BF&M Ltd, Bermuda insurance** (2023–24): 36.9% stake sold to Bermuda Life Insurance/Argus Group for **US$100m** (~£78.7m net, £10.7m profit) after a contentious 18-month process during which BF&M's own board implemented a poison pill
- **United Finance & United Insurance, Bangladesh** (2024): Sold via Dhaka Stock Exchange block trades for £7.9m at a £4.7m loss

Ongoing programmes of heritage collection disposals (~£7.9m cumulative), investment property sales (~£15.5m+ cumulative including London, Bristol, and Rio de Janeiro properties), and selective Indian tea estate sales (Chulsa for ~£1.7m, Leesh River) continue. Linton Park, the former headquarters estate, remains unsold despite being marketed since 2024.

Acquisitions were modest and mixed: **Jing Tea** (2018, luxury branded tea, still loss-making), **Bargang and Harchurah tea estates** in India (2018–19, ~INR 913m via Goodricke), **Tanzania greenfield land** (2018–20, avocado development), **Beja Farm** in South Africa (2019, macadamia expansion), and **Black Gold Oil Tools** (2018, for AJT Engineering). The catastrophic outlier was **Bardsley England** (2021), the UK's second-largest apple grower, acquired for a total investment of ~£27 million. Management predicted it would be "significantly earnings enhancing." Within three years, it was completely wound down at a total loss of ~£26m+ after failing to cope with labour cost inflation, energy prices, and insufficient retailer returns.

The pattern is unambiguous: **exit from financial services, non-core UK assets, and the US; selective disposal of marginal tea estates; investment in avocado and macadamia in East and Southern Africa.** Net effect: the Group is smaller (revenue down from ~£300m+ to £262m), much less diversified by business type, but now sitting on an enormous cash pile.

## 3. The Value Enhancement Plan promises transformation from reserves

New CEO Byron Coombs and Chairman Simon Turner inherited a company in strategic drift. Revenue had declined, trading losses persisted (**£10.3m in 2023, £5.5m in 2024, £9.6m in H1 2025**), dividends were suspended, and the NAV discount widened to ~60%. Their response came on **19 May 2025** with the **Value Enhancement Plan (VEP)**, the output of a comprehensive strategic review initiated in 2024.

The VEP has three pillars. First, **improve operating results** through better land and factory utilisation, farm technology, crop management, and logistics — with maintenance capex rising to £8–10m per annum. Second, **reduce overall risk** by disposing of operating assets with higher risk profiles or less predictable cashflows, diversifying across crops and geographies, and improving workforce management. Third, **invest in growth** through organic efficiency projects (solar, mechanisation) and "low-risk" inorganic investments in familiar crops, with **growth capex of £15–25m per annum** — a dramatic step-up from just £1.6m in 2024. Total planned investment: **£23–35m annually versus £9.5m in 2024**.

The explicit capital allocation framework prioritises a fortress balance sheet first, then sustainable dividends (260p/share, funded from reserves until operating profits can cover it), then business investment. The **260p dividend requires a 5%+ return on capital** — a target the company has not yet achieved. Management has moved to a single annual dividend (eliminating the interim) because most crop revenues fall in H2. A **£18.9 million tender offer at £54/share** (6.4% premium to last close) was completed in June 2025, returning ~£11.6m to shareholders. The Board stated it does not anticipate further tender offers.

Key growth areas include **avocado in Tanzania** (356 hectares planted, targeting 500+), **macadamia expansion in Kenya, Malawi, and South Africa** (~500 hectares of immature orchards maturing), **Brazil arable diversification**, and **Jing Tea brand development**. The VEP explicitly envisages further disposals of underperforming assets.

### Business segments and financial performance

Camellia's eight agricultural businesses span seven countries, managing approximately **48,500 hectares** of mature land. **Tea dominates** at 34,857 hectares (72% of total land), followed by arable (Brazil, 3,953 ha), macadamia (3,533 ha), forestry (3,498 ha), rubber (Bangladesh, 1,710 ha), avocado (934 ha), and blueberries (10 ha). The major operating entities are **Goodricke Group** (~73%-owned, BSE-listed; 29 gardens and 30 factories in India), **Duncan Brothers** (14 tea estates in Bangladesh), **EP Kenya** (tea and forestry), **Kakuzi** (50.7%-owned, NSE/LSE-listed; avocados, macadamia, tea in Kenya), **Eastern Produce Malawi** (74%-owned; tea and macadamia, producing 42% of Malawi's national tea output), **Eastern Produce South Africa** (macadamia and developing avocado), and a **Brazil** arable operation. Non-agricultural holdings include **AJT Engineering** (Aberdeen, achieved profitability for the first time in 10 years in 2024) and **Jing Tea** (London luxury branded tea, growing but loss-making).

Only Kenya and Brazil operations managed to avoid losses in both 2023 and 2024. Group revenue for 2024 was **£262.2 million** with a trading loss of **£5.5 million** (improving from £10.3m in 2023). The listed equity portfolio stands at £30.4m, investment properties at £31.3m, and heritage assets at £7.3m at cost.

## 4. Commercial relationships span auctions, exclusive agents, and government JVs

Camellia's tea operations (76.1 million kg annually across 68 estates) sell through a mix of public auctions and direct sales. Confirmed major buyers include **Unilever** (Duncan Brothers in Bangladesh and EP Kenya appear on Unilever's published global tea supplier list), **Taylors of Harrogate** (confirmed buyer of Malawi and Kenya teas with jointly funded community projects), **Tata Consumer Products** (second-largest buyer at Indian auctions, 20.7 million kg/year), and **Japanese buyers** for Goodricke's instant tea from the Aibheel plant — the world's first integrated instant tea facility. India's 2024 regulation mandating 50% compulsory auction sales has increased auction dependency, reducing pricing flexibility. Goodricke is the **second-largest tea brand by sales in India**, providing direct-to-consumer exposure through its retail packet tea business and six tea lounges.

For macadamia (~3% of global kernel production branded as "Maclands," described as "the largest macadamia brand in Africa"), the sole marketing channel is **Green & Gold Macadamias** of Australia — an exclusive agency arrangement that creates both a valuable distribution partnership and a single-point concentration risk. Kakuzi's avocado exports (the largest producer in East Africa, ~45% of Kenya's export volume) reach **14 countries**, though the 2020 human rights scandal led **Tesco, Sainsbury's, and Lidl** to suspend purchases. A network of **3,500+ smallholder outgrower farmers** supplies Kakuzi on contract. New markets being developed include China (GACC approved) and Malaysia.

The most strategically significant partnership structures are the **partially-owned listed subsidiaries**: Kakuzi Plc (50.7%, dual-listed NSE/LSE), Goodricke Group Ltd (~73%, BSE-listed), and Eastern Produce Malawi (74%, with the **Investment & Development Corporation of Malawi holding 26%** — the Malawian government's statutory investment vehicle). The IDC partnership provides political legitimacy for Camellia's largest Malawi operation. Kenya's Capital Markets Authority has investigated Kakuzi over **transfer pricing** concerns — related-party transactions between Kakuzi and Camellia Group fellow subsidiaries worth Sh369.4 million annually — and conflict of interest by the majority shareholder.

The company operates a **fortress balance sheet** with **£124.7 million net cash**, minimal debt (£9.8m loans plus £18.6m overdrafts at operating company level), and no disclosed group-level borrowings. The business model explicitly relies on "shareholder equity and retained earnings, with limited external borrowing." Banking relationships are local and working-capital focused at the operating subsidiary level; the nominated adviser and broker is Panmure Liberum, and the auditor is Deloitte LLP.

## 5. Compensation structure has no meaningful performance linkage

CEO Byron Coombs received **£716,232 in FY 2024** — approximately **95% fixed salary** with only ~5% in bonuses and zero equity awards. This compensation rose while the company reported a £5.5m trading loss and a £4.9m loss attributable to shareholders. **No formal Long-Term Incentive Plan exists.** No share option schemes or performance share plans operate. No disclosed KPIs drive bonuses. Director share ownership is negligible — the entire board collectively owns less than **0.15%** of shares, worth under £170,000 combined. Director of Agriculture Graham Mclean received £526,920, CFO Oliver Capon £308,100 (partial year), and Chairman Simon Turner £281,510. Non-executive fees range from £26,650 to £113,300.

Total board compensation of ~**£2.06 million** equates to ~1.6% of market capitalisation — elevated for a loss-making company. At a shareholder webcast in September 2025, when asked about adopting a formal share ownership requirement policy, CEO Coombs stated the Board "does discuss these matters" but made no commitment. The absence of any equity-linked compensation or formal performance metrics means management bears minimal personal financial risk from continued underperformance.

## 6. Human rights settlements and land disputes create ongoing operational risk

Camellia faces serious, partially unresolved stakeholder controversies across its African operations. In **February 2021**, the company settled two London High Court lawsuits for a combined **£6.9 million** without admitting liability. The first, brought by 85 Kenyans represented by Leigh Day, alleged that Kakuzi security guards committed **killing** (a 28-year-old man beaten to death for allegedly stealing an avocado), **rape**, violent attacks, and false imprisonment between 2009 and 2020. The Sunday Times broke the story in October 2020; BBC subsequently reported fresh claims. **Tesco, Sainsbury's, and Lidl** all suspended Kakuzi supply. The £4.6 million Kenya settlement included community infrastructure commitments, an independent human rights impact assessment, and a UN-compliant grievance mechanism. The second case involved **36 Malawian women** alleging systematic sexual harassment and "sex for jobs" exploitation on EPM tea and macadamia estates, settled for £2.3 million with safeguarding commitments.

Critically, **independent investigations suggest promised reforms have not fully materialised.** A 2025 investigation by More to Her Story and the Platform for Investigative Journalism found sexual abuse "remains widespread and unchecked" on Malawi tea estates despite settlements. ZAM Magazine reported that community benefits from the settlement largely failed to materialise. A Pulitzer Center-funded project continues documenting "Sex for Jobs in Malawi's Tea Sector." Camellia established a Sustainability and Safeguarding Committee (now chaired by NED Alison McFadyen), but this appears largely reactive.

**Land disputes are escalating.** At EP Kenya's Sitoi Tea Estate in Nandi County, over 100 residents from the Kimasas Farmers' Cooperative Society have been occupying 350 acres since August 2023, supported by a national lawmaker. EPK reportedly loses **~£200,000 per month**. The Kenya National Land Commission found the land belongs to the community; EPK contests this in court alleging forged evidence. Police have not enforced court injunctions. In January 2025, squatters attacked an EPK company vehicle and employees. Separately, Kakuzi faces constitutional petitions over colonial-era land injustice claims on its 42,000-acre Murang'a County plantation. These disputes reflect broader post-colonial tensions that threaten the foundational asset — land tenure — upon which Camellia's entire business model depends.

## GOVERNANCE & STRATEGY TENSIONS

**The NAV discount as structural indictment.** Camellia trades at ~£49 per share against estimated net asset value of ~£134 per share. The market capitalization barely exceeds cash on hand. This is not a temporary mispricing — it reflects rational discounting for: controlling shareholder entrenchment, AIM-level governance standards, persistent operating losses, zero performance-linked compensation, ESG controversies, and a free float so thin (28%) that institutional investors cannot meaningfully participate.

**The AIM listing serves the trust, not minorities.** Camellia moved from the premium Main Market segment to AIM in September 2014, explicitly citing inheritance tax (IHT) relief benefits — AIM shares qualify as "business property" for IHT purposes. This reduced governance obligations while primarily benefiting the Camellia Foundation's tax position. The company now follows the QCA Corporate Governance Code rather than the UK Corporate Governance Code, with less stringent independence and disclosure requirements.

**Chairman independence is compromised.** Simon Turner transitioned directly from Chairman of the Camellia Foundation (the controlling shareholder entity) to Chairman of Camellia Plc. His 2023 Annual Report letter referred to "the Camellia family" — language more suggestive of a Foundation steward than an independent public company chairman.

**Compensation misalignment is extreme.** Management earns rising fixed salaries during sustained losses with no equity skin in the game. The CEO owns 0.079% of the company. There are no LTIPs, no performance shares, no disclosed bonus metrics. This structure rewards tenure, not value creation.

**The VEP's ambitious capex targets face credibility questions.** Plans to increase total investment from £9.5m to £23–35m annually represent a 2.5–3.7x increase, funded initially from the cash pile. Yet the company has not achieved the 5% return on capital needed to sustain its own 260p dividend. The Bardsley England disaster — a £27m write-off three years after acquisition — demonstrates the risk when Camellia deploys capital. The VEP's promise of "low-risk inorganic investments in familiar crops" echoes the Bardsley rationale ("increase our focus on agriculture where we have deep skills").

**Disposal proceeds flow to shareholders, but operating performance does not improve.** The £185m+ of net divestments over a decade have funded a massive cash pile and capital returns, but the underlying agricultural businesses continue to generate trading losses. The simplification strategy has removed diversification buffers (insurance, banking) without yet improving core profitability.

**Human rights risks threaten the most valuable commercial relationships.** The Tesco/Sainsbury's/Lidl supply suspensions following the Kakuzi scandal directly impacted revenue from Camellia's highest-value crop (avocados). If independent investigations showing ongoing abuse in Malawi gain further media traction, additional customer boycotts could follow — and Taylors of Harrogate, the company's most engaged tea buyer, has particular reputational sensitivity to ethical sourcing.

**Land tenure is an existential risk with no clear resolution path.** The EPK Nandi County dispute and Kakuzi Murang'a claims reflect post-colonial grievances that cannot be resolved through litigation alone. With 48,500 hectares of land across seven countries — much of it held under colonial-era titles — Camellia's entire asset base depends on the ongoing validity of these tenures.

**Share buybacks at deep NAV discount mechanically benefit the controlling shareholder.** The tender offer at £54/share (versus ~£134 NAV per share) and on-market purchases at ~£41/share retired minority shares at a fraction of book value, mechanically increasing the Camellia Foundation's percentage ownership from ~52% to 56.45%. Each buyback below NAV transfers value from departing minorities to the remaining majority holder.

**Most valuable under-leveraged relationships.** Three stand out: (1) **Jing Tea**, a luxury branded tea company that could far more aggressively leverage Camellia's unique supply of premium Darjeeling, Assam, and Kenya teas for direct-to-consumer margin capture — yet remains loss-making after seven years of ownership; (2) **Goodricke's Aibheel instant tea plant**, the world's first integrated facility of its kind, currently selling predominantly to Japan but with untapped potential in the growing ready-to-drink and tea extract markets; and (3) **the Maclands brand**, currently sold exclusively B2B through a single Australian marketing agent, which could develop consumer-facing positioning given the premium nature of African macadamia and the brand's position as "the largest macadamia brand in Africa." The exclusive dependency on Green & Gold Macadamias as sole marketing agent for ~3% of global kernel production is both a strength (established distribution) and a vulnerability (single counterparty concentration).

**The fundamental, irreconcilable tension** is between a charitable trust that benefits from opacity, stability, and tax efficiency, and a public market listing that demands transparency, accountability, and value creation. The Camellia Foundation's interests are served by maintaining control, preserving the AIM listing for IHT benefits, and ensuring the agricultural operations continue regardless of returns. Minority shareholders need operating profitability, governance reform, and either a narrowing discount or a take-private at fair value — none of which the controlling shareholder has demonstrated clear incentive to deliver.
