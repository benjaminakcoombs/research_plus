# Camellia Plc: comprehensive operational and financial due diligence

**Camellia Plc (AIM: CAM) is the world's largest privately controlled tea producer, operating ~48,500 hectares of agricultural land across eight countries with roughly 78,000 employees — yet its market capitalisation of approximately £130m sits at barely 40% of book NAV and is roughly equivalent to its liquid cash alone.** This report maps the complete business at the facility level, quantifies financial performance, and identifies hidden asset value and anomalies that make Camellia one of the most unusual listed companies on the London market.

The company is controlled by Camellia Holding AG (Swiss entity, 56.45% of voting rights), itself controlled by the Camellia Private Trust Company on behalf of the Camellia Foundation. Headquarters are at Wrotham Place, Kent. The stock trades on AIM with approximately 4,100 shares changing hands daily — roughly £20,000 of liquidity. Management launched a Value Enhancement Plan (VEP) in May 2025, the first serious strategic overhaul in years, aimed at restoring profitability and narrowing the discount.

## 2. Physical assets and infrastructure at the estate level

This section maps what Camellia physically owns, country by country. The group's total mature planted area is **48,495 hectares**, but total land owned (including fallow, unplanted, and non-agricultural land) is substantially larger — estate boundaries in Kenya and Malawi encompass forestry reserves, conservation areas, roads, housing compounds, and buffer zones well beyond the planted footprint.

### India — Goodricke Group (29 tea gardens, 30 factories, 1 instant tea plant)

Goodricke is Camellia's largest revenue generator. Its **29 tea gardens and 30 factories** span four distinct regions across northeast India. Key named estates include:

**Darjeeling (5 gardens):** Margaret's Hope (houses the flagship Margaret's Deck tea lounge), Castleton, Badamtam (Fairtrade certified), Barnesbeg (bio-organic/IMO certified), and Thurbo. All five hold Rainforest Alliance certification. These are high-altitude orthodox tea estates producing some of the world's most prized single-origin teas. Darjeeling pricing rose **27% in 2024** but remains below cost of production.

**Assam (12 gardens):** Orangajuli, Nonaipara, Amgoorie, Borbam (est. 1865 — its first manager invented the CTC machine), Bargang, Dejoo, Harmutty, Sessa, Harchurah, Borpatra, Koomber, and Coombergram. All Rainforest Alliance certified. Assam tea prices rose **20% in 2024**.

**Dooars (several gardens):** CTC production for domestic market. Prices up **9% in 2024**. **Cachar:** Additional gardens (count not publicly disclosed).

Goodricke also operates a **packet tea business** (retail brands including the "Teapot" chain, volumes up 8% in 2024), an **instant tea plant** producing high-grade product predominantly for the Japanese market (volumes up 17%), and purchases green leaf from independent growers to supplement estate production. The Chulsa tea estate was disposed post year-end 2024 as part of the VEP.

**Data gap:** Individual garden hectarage, per-garden production volumes, factory capacities, and India total production volume for 2024 are not publicly disclosed. Total production was described as "down slightly" (~1%).

### Bangladesh — Duncan Brothers (16 tea gardens, 14 tea factories, 2 rubber processing plants)

All operations are located in the **Moulvibazar and Habiganj districts of the greater Sylhet region**. Duncan Brothers acts as managing agent for seven tea companies. Named estates include Hingajea. Bangladesh is distinctive because **100% of tea is own-estate** (no bought leaf), all sold through the Chittagong auction as CTC standard teas.

**Tea production (2024): ~15 million kg** — a record, achieved while national Bangladeshi production fell 9%. This means Camellia produces approximately **one-quarter of Bangladesh's entire tea output**. Pricing fell 7% and sits below cost of production.

**Rubber:** 1,710 hectares planted across 13 of the 16 tea gardens, processed at 2 on-site rubber processing plants. Sold to local buyers. Production volumes are not disclosed.

**Infrastructure is extensive.** Duncan Brothers estates function as self-contained communities: **26 garden-run primary schools plus 15 government-aided schools** (60 teachers, 3,250 students); the **Camellia Duncan Foundation School** (residential secondary, ~17 students/year, inaugurated 2004); and the **Camellia Duncan Foundation Hospital** (50 beds). Approximately **18,500 employees** work across the Bangladesh operations.

**Risk:** The government cancelled a lease on 512 acres at Chandpur tea estate for a Special Economic Zone, demonstrating land tenure vulnerability.

### Kenya — Eastern Produce Kenya (7 own gardens, 5 own factories, 2 managed factories)

EP Kenya's estates cluster around the **Nandi Hills** in southwestern Kenya, at 1,000–2,000m elevation, producing CTC tea year-round. The seven named estates are **Chemomi, Sitoi, Kibabet, Kapsumbeiwa, Kepchomo, Savani, and Kipkoimet**. EP Kenya also manages three third-party estates: Siret, Koboswa (owned by Katuzi Ltd), and Kibwari (owned by Kibwari Ltd).

**Production: ~30 million kg annually** (combined own-estate and outgrower), with bought leaf constituting roughly 30%. The 2024 crop was up 3% — the second highest on record. Principal market is the **Mombasa auction**, with exports to Pakistan and the UK.

Factory marks (identifying individual factory output at auction): Sokot, Kipkoimet, Kepchomo, Chemomi, Kamarya, Sitoi, Savani, Kibwari, Siret, Toyoi. A **solar project** operates at Kepchomo factory. The company is running a **replanting programme of 50 hectares per year**, shifting to superior clonal cultivars with significant yield improvement. A hybrid harvesting system combines people and machine technology. The estates support **14,000 outgrower farmers**.

**Sustainability metrics:** 2,000 hectares of biodiversity conservation; 87 rainwater harvesting systems; 72 sanitation blocks; carbon intensity halved through energy audits.

**Risk:** Land encroachment on Sitoi estate (a colonial-era dispute being pursued through Kenyan courts). Pricing deteriorated 12% in 2024 due to high Mombasa auction inventories.

### Kenya — Kakuzi Plc (diversified estate near Makuyu, ~65km NE of Nairobi)

Kakuzi is Camellia's most diversified single operation and is **dual-listed on the Nairobi Securities Exchange and AIM**. The estate sits at Makuyu in Murang'a County.

**Avocado (884 ha mature, expanding to ~987 ha):** The largest avocado operation in East Africa. Packed and shipped from an on-site packhouse to Europe, China, India, the Middle East, and Malaysia. Kakuzi was the first commercial exporter of African avocados to China (2022). Production fell 16% in 2024 due to excessive rainfall, and revenues dropped ~35% as Red Sea shipping disruptions extended transit times and damaged quality.

**Macadamia (1,032 ha — largest single planting in Kenya):** Processed on-site at an **ultra-modern cracking facility** (commissioned ~2019). Exported to Asia and the USA. Growing value-addition includes macadamia oil and roasted snacks for local market. 2024 saw production up **20%**, kernel sales up **72%**, and pricing up **28%** — though still below pre-Covid levels.

**Forestry (1,255 ha):** Produces treated pole products for Kenya Power (electric utility), fencing, construction timber, and heat-treated pallets. A forestry treatment plant operates on-site.

**Livestock:** ~4,000–4,420 head of grass-fed Boran cattle, plus goats and sheep. Sold through an on-site butchery and the **"Boran Barn" BBQ restaurant**. Halaal beef.

**Tea (510 ha):** Located at a separate Nandi Hills site. **Blueberries (10 ha):** Grown in pots under polytunnels; principal market is the Middle East.

**Water infrastructure is significant:** Kakuzi has built **13 million cubic metres of water storage capacity**, eliminating dependence on river water for irrigation.

**Human rights settlement:** Kakuzi settled claims in 2021 for **£4.6 million** relating to allegations of abuses by security guards. An Independent Human Rights Advisory Committee (IHRAC) was subsequently established.

### Malawi — Eastern Produce Malawi (15 tea gardens, 8 tea factories, 5 macadamia estates, 1 cracking facility)

Camellia owns 74%, with IDC holding 26%. EPM is the **largest producer of both tea and macadamia in Malawi**. Operations are split between the **Thyolo Highlands** (~50km SW of Blantyre) and the **Mulanje District** (~120km west of Blantyre, near the Mozambique border). Named estates include Limbuli, Chisambo, Phwazi, Ruo, Thornwood, Eldarado, Likanga, Minimini, Lauderdale, Esperanza, Glenorchy, Kasembereka, Mianga, Gotha, Makwasa, Nasonia, Kumadzi, and Masawa.

Tea production hit a **record in 2024, up 15%** year-on-year, driven by a long-running replanting programme to irrigated superior clonal cultivars. Virtually all tea is exported — principal markets are the **UK, South Africa, and the USA**. The macadamia cracking facility (one of the first in Malawi, commissioned 2001) handles the full chain from nursery to saleable kernel. 100% of macadamia is exported as processed kernel to **Japan and the USA**. Macadamia production crashed **57% in 2024** — a delayed consequence of Cyclone Freddy.

**Risk factors:** 52% wage increase following Kwacha devaluation (Q4 2023); ongoing hyperinflation; increasing social volatility ahead of September 2025 elections. Human rights claims settled for **£2.3 million** in 2021.

### South Africa — EPSA macadamia and EPC wine grapes

EPSA operates **1,005 hectares of macadamia** across estates including Maclands, Mambedi River (dam completed 2020), and Beja Farm (acquired 2019) in **Limpopo Province**. Processing occurs through the **Zemtac cooperative cracking factory**, in which Linton Park (Camellia subsidiary) holds a controlling interest.

Eastern Produce Cape operates an **80-hectare wine grape estate** at Wellington (Slangrivier Farm, purchased 1994). Wine production halted in 2020 due to Covid; grapes are currently sold to local wineries. The estate is being **offered for sale**.

### Brazil — Maruque Farm (4,393 ha arable, São Paulo state)

Purchased 1985. Bi-annual rotation of soya (summer) and maize/sorghum (winter), with wheat/barley/oats/beans in rotation. Total production ~38,500 tonnes per year. The farm is highly mechanised: **30+ central irrigation pivots** (telemetry-monitored), **6 GPS-driven harvesters** (18 employees harvest 120 ha/day of soya), multiple dams, and on-farm grain silos. **Two solar stations** are installed, with three more planned for total energy self-sufficiency. No-till agriculture since the 1990s. Won the 2024 Soybean Money Maker Championship (1st place Sustainability).

### Tanzania — EP(T) East Africa (development stage)

**356 hectares of avocado planted** at Mgagao Farm in Iringa (Southern Highlands), expanding ~100 ha/year toward a target of ~650 ha. The first 50 ha are commercially producing — first crop exported to India. A main dam is under construction (completion expected 2025). Losses of £1.6m in 2024 relate to uncapitalisable development costs.

## 3. Key input costs and procurement

**Labour is overwhelmingly the dominant cost.** With approximately 78,000 employees, the group's wage bill dwarfs all other input costs. Wages are largely negotiated through collective bargaining, with outcomes varying sharply by country: Bangladesh saw a 5% increase in 2024 (effective until 2026); Kenya (EPK) secured a 7% increase; Malawi imposed a **52% increase** following Kwacha devaluation; Darjeeling saw no increase in 2024. The cost structure is extremely labour-intensive — tea picking in particular has limited mechanisation in most of Camellia's geographies, though EPK has introduced a hybrid harvesting system.

**Fertiliser** is a significant cost across tea and arable operations. Brazil's soya rotation naturally reduces nitrogen fertiliser requirements. Precision agriculture and drone-based soil fertility mapping are employed at Maruque Farm. India and Kenya both use replanting programmes to superior cultivars, which require heavier fertiliser application in early years.

**Energy** represents a substantial cost in tea processing, which requires withering, rolling, oxidation, and drying — the drying phase in particular is energy-intensive. Tea factories primarily use **firewood/biomass** for thermal energy, supplemented by grid electricity and diesel generators. Solar installations are reducing costs at EPK's Kepchomo factory and Brazil's Maruque Farm. **Macadamia shells are used as factory fuel** (and for road surfacing), reducing timber demand.

**Transport and logistics** is critical for perishable crops. The Red Sea shipping crisis in 2024 extended Kenya-to-Europe transit times by approximately 45%, severely impacting avocado quality and Kakuzi's profitability. Tea moves primarily to Mombasa auction (Kenya), Chittagong auction (Bangladesh), and Kolkata/Guwahati auctions (India) by road and rail. Macadamia kernel ships from Mombasa to Japan and the USA.

**Currency exposure** is pervasive: BDT, KES, MWK, INR, BRL, and ZAR all feature prominently. The Malawi Kwacha devaluation was particularly impactful — the 52% wage increase it triggered essentially wiped out margins. Agrochemicals, packaging (tea chests, paper sacks, jute bags, avocado cartons), and replacement parts for processing equipment represent smaller but material costs.

## 4. Waste streams and byproducts

Tea processing generates **tea dust, fibre waste, stalks, and wastewater**. Tea dust has commercial value in lower-grade blended products and tea bags. Stalks and twigs can be pyrolysed into biochar (450–600°C). Factories also produce flue emissions from biomass combustion. In Bangladesh, invasive **water hyacinth** harvested from waterways is used as mulch — a dual benefit of controlling an invasive species and improving soil moisture retention. In Malawi, **Guatemala grass** (Tripsacum laxum) serves a similar mulching function.

Macadamia processing generates substantial byproducts at each stage. **Husks** (the outer green layer) are composted into mulch that adds nitrogen and aerates soil. **Shells** (the hard inner layer) are the most valuable byproduct: Camellia uses them as **factory fuel** (reducing timber demand and energy costs), as **road surfacing material** on estate roads, and at Kakuzi specifically for **biochar production** aimed at carbon sequestration and soil health improvement. Macadamia shells also have potential (though unconfirmed at Camellia) for activated carbon production and cosmetics applications. The shell-to-fuel-to-biochar pipeline represents a genuine circular economy within the macadamia operations.

Rubber processing produces **wastewater and latex residues**. Details on treatment and disposal are not publicly disclosed but would be subject to local environmental regulation and Rainforest Alliance certification requirements.

## 7. Capital allocation history over the past decade

Camellia's capital allocation over the past decade tells a story of a conglomerate gradually simplifying its portfolio — selling non-core assets while intermittently investing in agricultural expansion — but doing so slowly and with mixed results.

**Major disposals:** Duncan Lawrie private bank (2017, generating £20.3m in gains); GU Cutting & Grinding, BMT Great Yarmouth, and XiMo (engineering, 2018); Affish and Wylax (food service, 2018); Horizon Farms USA (2020); Abbey Metal Finishing (sold to GIL Investments, August 2021 — a world-class aerospace metal finishing operation with customers including Airbus, Boeing, BAE, and Rolls-Royce); ACS&T cold storage logistics (sold to Constellation Cold Logistics for **£16.5m**, January 2023 — operated 14 cold stores and 2 ambient stores across Grimsby, Wolverhampton, Tewkesbury, and Scarborough with a large LGV fleet); BF&M Ltd (sold for **US$100m**, November 2024); United Finance and United Insurance Bangladesh (sold 2024 at a £4.7m loss); and ongoing disposal of UK properties and collections.

**Acquisitions:** Jing Tea majority stake (~2018); Black Gold Oil Tools for AJT (~2018); Bargang and Harchurrah tea gardens in Assam for Goodricke (2019); Beja Farm macadamia in South Africa (2019); Tanzania farmland for avocado development (2019–2020); Bardsley England fruit farming (80% for £15.7m, 2021 — subsequently closed entirely in 2024, with total losses of ~£24m including restructuring costs, representing the most conspicuous capital misallocation in recent history).

**Capital returns in 2025:** The company returned **£18.9m to shareholders** through a combination of: a tender offer of up to 350,000 shares at £54.00/share (£11.6m actually tendered); an on-market buyback programme of £9.0m (commenced December 2024, completed June 2025, purchasing shares at £41–48.45); and the 260p ordinary dividend (~£6.6m). The Board has signalled no further tenders or buybacks. Total shares in issue fell from ~2,862,095 to **2,590,295**.

**Capex:** Capital expenditure was only £9.5m in 2024 — low relative to the asset base. The VEP announced in May 2025 targets a **step-change to £23–35m per annum** (2.4–3.7x the 2024 level), focused on water resilience, farm technology, factory efficiency, power supply, mechanisation, and crop diversification.

## Notable anomalies and observations

**The cash-exceeds-market-cap anomaly.** At end-2024, Camellia held £124.7m in cash, deposits, and money market instruments plus £30.4m in listed equities — a total of £155m in liquid financial assets. Mid-2025 market cap was approximately £130m. Adding investment property (£31.3m) and heritage assets (£7.3m at cost), total non-operating assets approached **£194m versus a ~£130m market cap**. The agricultural operations, AJT, and Jing are implicitly valued at **negative £60m or worse**. This is a strikingly wide gap, even accounting for the controlling shareholder discount and illiquidity.

**Bangladesh tea: 25% of national output from a single company.** Camellia produces approximately one-quarter of Bangladesh's total tea — an extraordinary concentration of market share. This confers significant local purchasing power (labour, transport, warehousing) but also creates a political dependency that may explain the government's willingness to cancel leases.

**Macadamia shells as a circular economy asset.** Camellia's macadamia operations generate shells that serve as fuel, road surfacing, and biochar feedstock. This byproduct stream has potential for further monetisation — macadamia shell activated carbon and cosmetic applications are growing markets. Kakuzi's biochar programme is particularly noteworthy for its carbon credit potential.

**Bardsley: a cautionary tale.** The 2021 acquisition of Bardsley England for £15.7m and its complete closure by 2024 — with cumulative losses approaching £24m — stands as a major capital allocation failure. It was Camellia's only recent attempt to expand into UK agriculture and it was abandoned within three years.

**The Linton Park valuation test.** If the Grade I-listed estate sells at or above its £32m guide price, this single asset will represent nearly 25% of the current market cap — providing a concrete marker of how deeply the shares trade below asset replacement value.

**AJT Engineering's decade of losses.** AJT lost money for ten consecutive years before finally reaching profitability in 2024. Its 70,000 sq ft of workshop space and 12 overhead cranes in Aberdeen represent significant physical assets that were persistently underutilised during the oil and gas downturn. The pivot toward renewables and hydro (plus the C-Dart proprietary marine mooring product) may have turned this business, but the long track record of losses raises questions about management's willingness to persist with underperforming assets.

**Heritage assets at cost = probable hidden value.** Collections of fine art, philately, and manuscripts dating to the 1860s and carried at £7.3m cost are almost certainly worth multiples of that figure. The sale of partial collection items in 2022 generated £3.6m in proceeds and a £1.4m gain, suggesting the accounting cost basis is far below market value.

**Labour intensity versus market cap.** Camellia employs approximately **78,000 people** with a market cap of ~£130m. This implies a market capitalisation per employee of roughly **£1,667** — an almost absurdly low figure that underscores both the labour intensity of tea agriculture and the severity of the market's discount.

**Free float trap.** With only ~723,000 shares in public hands, average daily volume of ~4,100 shares, and roughly £20,000 of daily trading value, Camellia is effectively uninvestable for any institutional fund of meaningful size. The controlling shareholder structure (56.45%) eliminates any takeover premium and renders activist campaigns futile. Quaero Capital's complete exit in 2025 may signal that even patient, specialist small-cap investors have concluded the discount cannot be narrowed.

**The VEP capex inflection.** The Value Enhancement Plan's proposed tripling of capex to £23–35m annually represents a significant strategic bet. If successfully deployed into water infrastructure, mechanisation, and crop diversification, it could transform the operating margin profile. If deployed poorly — as Bardsley suggests is possible — it could accelerate value destruction in a company already burning cash at the operating level. Monitoring where this capital is actually deployed, and what returns it generates by geography and crop, will be the single most important indicator for the investment case over the next three to five years.
