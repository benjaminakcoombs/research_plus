# Camellia Plc: a £262M agricultural empire at an ESG inflection point

**Camellia Plc (LSE AIM: CAM) faces a defining moment where its historical labor liabilities and environmental assets collide.** The UK-listed holding company — managing **48,495 hectares** across Kenya, India, Bangladesh, and Malawi — settled £6.9 million in landmark human rights lawsuits in 2021 involving allegations of killings, rape, and systematic violence by estate security guards. Yet the same sprawling landholding that generated these controversies now presents an estimated **£8.5–16 million annual revenue opportunity** from carbon credits, biodiversity credits, certification premiums, and regenerative agriculture positioning. Camellia's 78,000-person workforce operates in some of the world's highest-risk jurisdictions for labor exploitation, while its 9,200 hectares of indigenous forest and 5,586 hectares of commercial forestry function as significant carbon sinks. The company's trajectory depends entirely on whether management can transform a legacy of reactive crisis management into proactive ESG leadership — and the newly reconstituted board, installed through 2023–2024, has approximately 18 months before tightening EU regulations and buyer requirements make the cost of inaction irreversible.

---

## S — The social wound that still defines the company

### £6.9 million in settlements, zero admissions of liability

The most material ESG event in Camellia's history unfolded between 2019 and 2021. **Leigh Day solicitors** filed two landmark cases in the London High Court on behalf of workers and community members from Camellia's estates:

**Kakuzi Plc, Kenya (settled February 2021, £4.6 million):** Eighty-five Kenyan claimants alleged systematic human rights abuses by security guards at Kakuzi's 14,000-hectare estate in Murang'a County between 2009 and 2020. Allegations included the beating death of a young man in May 2018, the rape of ten women and girls (including two minors), false imprisonment, and violent suppression of community protests. The Kenya Human Rights Commission (KHRC) and SOMO (Netherlands) supported the case through a 17-year advocacy campaign. The settlement funded individual compensation, an Operational-level Grievance Mechanism (OGM), community access roads, and an Independent Human Rights Advisory Committee chaired by former Kenya Attorney General Prof. Githu Muigai. Camellia spent an additional **£5 million in legal fees** beyond the settlement.

**Eastern Produce Malawi (settled February 2021, £2.3 million):** Thirty-six Malawian women alleged rape, sexual coercion, and systematic "sex for jobs" practices by supervisors on tea estates in Mulanje and Thyolo districts. The settlement funded personal attack alarms, security cameras, a Women's Safeguarding Officer role, and an independently monitored OGM for gender-based violence grievances.

**Total cost to the group exceeded £10 million.** The Sunday Times exposé in October 2020 — headlined "Rape, beatings and death" — triggered immediate supply suspensions from Tesco, Sainsbury's, and Lidl. Kakuzi's profits fell 52.88%. Rainforest Alliance temporarily revoked Kakuzi's certification. Camellia issued no public apology, drawing sustained criticism from KHRC and SOMO.

### Remediation efforts face credibility questions

Camellia's post-settlement remediation has been substantive in design but contested in execution. The company established OGMs at both Kakuzi and EPM, launched the SHARP program (Sexual Harassment Awareness, Reporting and Prevention) at Kakuzi, hired external human rights consultancy Ibis for impact assessments, and elevated its Sustainability and Safeguarding Committee to board level in 2024.

However, a 2025 investigative report titled "More to Her Story" found that **sexual abuse continues on Malawi tea estates** despite the settlement and promised reforms. The Ethical Tea Partnership acknowledged these ongoing violations as "unacceptable." KHRC has described the Kakuzi OGM as having "many gaps" — limited to company discretion, lacking urgency, and operating as a purely internal framework rather than one grounded in Kenyan constitutional principles. One year after Kakuzi promised three access roads, only one had been opened. Some Malawian claimants reported that pledged community projects had not materialized years after settlement.

The International Union of Food Workers (IUF) has reported Malawi to the ILO alleging widespread gender-based violence in the tea sector. Leigh Day retains the contractual right to resume litigation if the OGM fails to meet standards, maintaining a latent legal threat.

### Wages remain 40–63% below living wage benchmarks

Camellia's wage challenge is structural and sector-wide, but no less material for being shared with peers. The company states it is "working toward a living wage for workers" but discloses no estate-level wage data.

| Country | Approximate daily wage | Living wage (monthly, Anker) | Gap to living wage |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bangladesh (Duncan Brothers) | Tk 170 (~$1.43) | No formal benchmark | Extreme — below $1.90 poverty line |
| India/Assam (Goodricke) | Rs 232 (~$2.75) | ₹15,112/month | ~60% gap (cash only) |
| Kenya (EP Kenya) | CBA-negotiated rates | KSh 34,604/month | ~62% gap |
| Malawi (EPM) | Above minimum wage | MWK 300,961/month | ~43% gap (2018 data) |

In-kind benefits — housing, healthcare, rations, education — partially close these gaps. Camellia operates hospitals, clinics, and schools across its estates; the Camellia Duncan Foundation Hospital in Bangladesh has 50 beds, and EPM's participation in the Malawi Tea 2020 programme reduced the living wage gap by 25% between 2015 and 2018. But **cash wages in Bangladesh tea remain the lowest of the country's 43 industrial sectors**, and 96% of Bangladesh tea workers lack formal employment status.

### Peer comparison reveals an industry-wide crisis with company-specific overlay

All major tea producers — James Finlay, Ekaterra, KTDA, McLeod Russel, Williamson Tea — pay wages set by government minimums or collective bargaining agreements that fall well short of living wage benchmarks. Finlays and Ekaterra both had their Kenya Rainforest Alliance certifications temporarily suspended in 2023 for social non-conformities, and Unilever itself settled a Leigh Day case in 2023 involving 79 Kenyan women alleging sexual harassment on tea estates. Gender-based violence is documented across the sector, not unique to Camellia.

What distinguishes Camellia is the **severity of the proven allegations** (killings, rape of minors), the **scale of the settlement** (£6.9 million), and the **ongoing credibility deficit** created by the absence of a public apology and contested implementation of remediation commitments. Camellia's Goodricke estates in India are relatively well-certified (Rainforest Alliance across all Assam and Darjeeling gardens; Ethical Tea Partnership membership), but the Kenya government's **2025 nationwide suspension of Rainforest Alliance certification** — citing costs outweighing benefits for farmers — introduces fresh uncertainty across the sector.

## E — Significant natural assets, limited disclosure

### Nearly 15,000 hectares of forest and conservation land anchor the environmental story

Camellia manages **48,495 hectares of mature agricultural land** across seven countries, dominated by tea (34,857 ha, 72% of total). The environmental narrative centers on the company's substantial forestry and conservation holdings:

- **Commercial forestry (eucalyptus, pine, cypress):** 5,586 ha including immature areas
- **Indigenous forests and conservation areas:** ~9,200 ha  
- **Kakuzi:** 1,600 ha of forestry (including 300 ha indigenous trees), representing 12% forest cover; captures **12 million cubic metres** of rainwater in 19 earth dams
- **EPM Malawi:** Operations at the foothills of Mount Mulanje, a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve candidate with high biodiversity significance

Camellia has commissioned an external study estimating carbon sequestration from its core crops and managed forestry estates. The company acknowledges these holdings "sequester significant amounts of carbon and act as an important carbon sink."

### Emissions disclosure exists but lacks specificity and ambition

Camellia monitors Scope 1 and 2 emissions but **specific tCO2e figures are not prominently published** in publicly accessible summaries. Scope 3 measurement began in 2023, with the company stating it would "consider Science Based Targets for emission reduction" once Scope 3 data is finalized. **Camellia has not formally committed to SBTi** and has set no specific net zero target date, though the aspiration of net-zero emissions across the value chain is articulated as one of five sustainability pillars.

Kakuzi has been notably more advanced, becoming the **first agricultural business in Kenya to publicly report Scope 1 and 2 emissions** (in 2022) and engaging the UK Carbon Trust for Scope 3 calculation. Kakuzi has reduced CO2 emissions by 74% through new cold room technology and targets 2% annual reductions. The subsidiary's ESG reporting now exceeds the parent company's in transparency and specificity.

Financial year 2023 was Camellia's first year of Climate-related Financial Disclosures (CFD), aligned with UK regulatory requirements. A dedicated CFD working group reports to the Audit Committee and SSC. The assessment focuses on the Agriculture division, which comprises **94% of revenue and nearly all Scope 1 and 2 emissions**.

### Climate vulnerability is an existential threat to the operating model

Camellia's estates face intensifying climate impacts. The Ethical Tea Partnership and Rainforest Alliance estimate that **up to 40% of global tea-growing areas** are at risk from climate change. Camellia's own reporting confirms the pattern — Darjeeling suffered severe drought, Dooars experienced damaging hail, Cyclone Freddy devastated Mulanje operations in 2023 (macadamia production dropped 57%), and Brazil's 2024 soya harvest was significantly impacted by drought and pests.

Adaptation measures are underway but nascent. These include water infrastructure expansion (Kakuzi plans 1 million additional cubic metres of storage), crop diversification into avocados and blueberries, replanting with climate-resilient tea cultivars (240 ha replanted in 2023), and new operations in Tanzania chosen specifically for climate resilience. The Jiko Kisasa program has fitted over 1,000 community households with energy-efficient stoves, reducing wood demand by 30%.

### Chemical use is managed but not fully transparent

Camellia publishes information on reducing chemical use, prioritizing organic fertilizers, composting, and indigenous technical knowledge. Kakuzi works with CABI and Kenya's PCPB on integrated pest management and targets a **50% reduction in herbicide usage**. India's operations note a "reduction in the number of permitted chemicals" in 2024. No specific public controversies around pesticide use were identified, though the company does not publish a full list of chemicals used or quantities applied.

## ESG as a revenue engine worth £8.5–16 million annually

### Carbon credits could generate £1.5–3 million per year by 2030

Camellia's combined forestry (3,498 ha mature) and perennial crop holdings present a credible carbon credit opportunity. Conservative estimates for tropical/subtropical managed forestry yield **12–37 tonnes CO2e per hectare per year**, while tea plantations with agroforestry sequester approximately 2–5 tonnes CO2e/ha/yr.

Applying mid-range sequestration rates across Camellia's portfolio produces an estimate of **52,470 tonnes CO2e/year from forestry alone**, plus potentially 25,000–35,000 creditable tonnes from tea and rubber agroforestry. At current voluntary carbon market prices of **$10–20/tonne** for quality nature-based solution credits (with prices projected to reach $20/tonne by 2030 per BloombergNEF), this translates to **£0.5–1.5 million/year near-term, scaling to £1.5–3.0 million/year by 2030**. Applicable standards include Verra VCS, Gold Standard, and Plan Vivo, with credits carrying CCB (Climate, Community & Biodiversity) certification commanding a 30% price premium. Project registration typically requires £100,000–300,000 in upfront measurement, reporting, and verification investment.

### Certification premiums represent the largest immediate opportunity

The most accessible revenue uplift comes from expanding sustainability certification coverage across Camellia's 90,000–100,000 MT annual tea production. **Rainforest Alliance premiums** run approximately $50/MT (combining the Sustainability Differential of $10/MT and Sustainability Investment of $40/MT). **Fairtrade premiums** are substantially higher at **$500/MT** but apply to smaller volumes. Organic tea commands **15–30% producer-level price premiums** above conventional auction prices, with Darjeeling organic particularly valuable.

If 50% of Camellia's tea production were sold as certified, the incremental premium income would reach **£2.25–2.5 million/year** from Rainforest Alliance alone. Fairtrade certification on even 10,000 MT would add **£4–5 million/year**. Certified macadamia nuts command **20–40% wholesale premiums** — meaningful for Camellia's 3,533 ha macadamia portfolio across Kenya, Malawi, and South Africa.

### Biodiversity credits and PES are nascent but strategically aligned

The voluntary biodiversity credit market was valued at $5.7 billion in 2024 and projected to reach $48.7 billion by 2034 (24.1% CAGR), though actual credit transactions remain minimal — only $325,000–$1.87 million in voluntary credits sold globally as of September 2024. Prices range wildly from $2 to $68,000 per unit depending on habitat and scheme. Camellia's estates near Kenyan wildlife areas and Mount Mulanje's biodiversity hotspot in Malawi are natural candidates. Near-term revenue is modest (**£50,000–300,000/year**) but bundling biodiversity with carbon credits could add a 30% premium, and Kenya is among ~20 countries developing national biodiversity credit frameworks.

Payment for Ecosystem Services programs in Kenya — such as the Lake Naivasha and Kikuyu Escarpment schemes — provide modest but growing revenue streams for watershed protection. EP Kenya's highland tea estates sit in major water catchment areas, positioning the company as both a potential PES seller and ecosystem steward.

### Regenerative agriculture offers brand positioning upside

The global regenerative agriculture market reached **$12.66 billion in 2024**, growing at 18.7% CAGR. Companies including Nestlé (CHF 1.2 billion commitment), PepsiCo ($216 million), and Patagonia Provisions have built premium brand positioning around regenerative sourcing. Consumers pay a **39% premium** for sustainably marketed products. Camellia's premium tea brand Jing Tea — described as "showing good growth" in recent annual reports — is an ideal vehicle for regenerative-branded offerings, potentially adding **10–25% price premiums** on value-added products.

### Green bonds and ESG capital could unlock strategic investment

The green bond market exceeded $6 trillion in cumulative issuance by 2024, with agriculture explicitly eligible under ICMA Green Bond Principles. Camellia's strong balance sheet (**net cash £124.7 million**) and diverse ESG-eligible assets — sustainable forestry, replanting programs, factory energy efficiency, water infrastructure — could support a **£20–50 million green or sustainability-linked bond**. Impact investment funds active in the sector include AgDevCo (Platinum-rated, active in Malawi), AATIF, and Finnfund (€88 million in sustainable agriculture investments).

## ESG risk: regulatory walls closing in from multiple directions

### Customer requirements are the most immediate commercial pressure

All major tea buyers now require Rainforest Alliance certification as a baseline sourcing condition. **Ekaterra** (Lipton, PG Tips) has achieved approximately 98% sustainably sourced tea. **Twinings** requires 100% Rainforest Alliance certified tea and applies its proprietary Community Needs Assessment framework — covering gender, health, land rights, and working conditions — to every garden it sources from. **Tetley/Tata Consumer Products** requires 100% Rainforest Alliance certification for international markets and targets 100% sustainably sourced critical raw materials by 2040.

All three companies are rolling out explicit gender-based violence prevention requirements and living wage progress expectations. Loss of Rainforest Alliance certification at any Camellia operation would effectively sever access to these premium buyers. The precedent is real — Tesco, Sainsbury's, and Lidl all suspended Kakuzi purchases within days of the 2020 Sunday Times exposé.

### EU regulations create compliance obligations on multiple fronts

The **EU Deforestation Regulation** directly covers Camellia's rubber (1,710 ha in Bangladesh) and forestry/timber products. Compliance requires geolocation data, proof of deforestation-free production, and due diligence statements by **December 30, 2026** for large operators. Camellia's long-established estates (130+ year heritage) carry minimal deforestation risk, positioning early compliance as a competitive advantage rather than a burden.

The **EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive** (CSDDD), published July 2024, may not directly capture Camellia under revised Omnibus I thresholds (€1.5 billion turnover required), but agriculture is classified as a high-risk sector. More critically, CSDDD will cascade through Camellia's customer base — European buyers in scope will impose supply chain due diligence requirements on Camellia as a supplier. Germany's LkSG and France's Duty of Vigilance Law create similar cascading obligations.

Under the **UK Modern Slavery Act**, Camellia publishes the required annual statement but faces inherently elevated risk given 78,000 workers across high-risk jurisdictions, extensive seasonal labor, and the demonstrated pattern of abuse documented in the 2019–2021 litigation.

### ESG ratings coverage is thin, but controversy flags are severe

Camellia's AIM listing and ~£130 million market capitalization place it largely outside major ESG rating coverage. No current MSCI ESG rating was identified, and Sustainalytics coverage appears outdated (last updated November 2019, before the Kakuzi controversy). This invisibility to ESG screeners is both a blessing and a liability — Camellia avoids formal downgrades but also cannot demonstrate ESG improvement to institutional investors. Any ESG screening applied would trigger severe controversy flags under human rights, UN Global Compact compliance, and norms-based criteria.

## G — Governance rebuilt but still proving itself

### Board renewal signals intent but legacy oversight failures remain recent

Camellia underwent near-complete board renewal in 2023–2024. A new Chairman (Simon Turner), CEO (Byron Coombs), CFO (Oliver Capon, ex-Shell), and General Counsel (Nischal Hindia, ex-Freshfields/Allen & Overy/Diageo) were appointed alongside two independent NEDs with directly relevant expertise — Alison McFadyen (ex-Standard Chartered Group Head of Internal Audit, now chairs the SSC) and Alec Hayley (30 years in Africa/Asia agriculture commodities, ex-ECOM Agroindustrial).

The **Sustainability and Safeguarding Committee was elevated to a Board Committee in 2024**, the strongest structural signal of governance improvement. It oversees environmental strategy, climate-related financial disclosures, and progress on ESG matters. However, the previous board's oversight failures — which allowed the conditions that produced £10 million+ in settlements and supermarket boycotts — remain a recent memory. The company complies with the QCA Corporate Governance Code (appropriate for AIM) rather than the more demanding UK Corporate Governance Code.

### Reporting quality lags sector expectations

Camellia published its first standalone ESG report in 2019 but has since integrated sustainability reporting into the Annual Report rather than maintaining a separate publication. **No GRI, CDP, or SASB reporting** has been identified. While CFD/TCFD-aligned climate disclosures are included in the Annual Report, the company does not publish estate-level wage data, specific emissions figures in accessible formats, certification coverage percentages, or quantitative safety metrics.

**Shareholder concentration poses a governance concern.** The Camellia Foundation holds 56.45% of shares, with UBS Asset Management at 33.55% and KBC Asset Management at 10.29%. One board member (Stephen Buckland) holds an executive position within the Foundation and is classified as non-independent. This structure limits minority shareholder influence and reduces market discipline on ESG performance.

## ESG strategic imperatives: five actions ranked by urgency and impact

**1. Publish a standalone annual sustainability report with quantitative targets — within 12 months.** This is the highest-leverage, lowest-cost action available. The report must disclose absolute Scope 1 and 2 emissions by subsidiary, progress toward living wage benchmarks using Anker methodology by country, certification coverage percentages by estate, safety incident rates, and grievance mechanism utilization statistics. Adopt GRI Standards as the reporting framework and submit to CDP. Commit to SBTi and set a specific net zero target date. Current reporting opacity is actively destroying value by preventing investors and buyers from distinguishing Camellia from worst-in-class operators.

**2. Accelerate living wage roadmaps with published timelines and milestones — begin immediately, 3-year milestones.** The 40–63% gap between estate wages and living wage benchmarks is the single largest social risk in the portfolio. Rather than waiting for sector-wide solutions, Camellia should publish estate-by-estate wage gap analyses (using Anker benchmarks), set measurable targets for closing gaps (including in-kind benefit valuation), and join the IDH living wage initiative. Prioritize Bangladesh and India operations where gaps are most extreme. This directly addresses buyer requirements — Ekaterra, Twinings, and Tata are all demanding living wage progress from suppliers.

**3. Register forestry and agroforestry carbon credit projects under Verra VCS with CCB certification — initiate within 6 months.** The estimated **52,000+ tonnes CO2e/year** from Camellia's forestry alone represents £0.5–3.0 million in annual revenue at current-to-projected prices. Begin with Kakuzi's 1,255 ha and EP Kenya's 690 ha of managed forestry in Kenya, where Plan Vivo and Gold Standard infrastructure is most developed. Budget £200,000–300,000 for upfront MRV investment. This converts a currently zero-revenue asset (conservation forestry) into a recurring income stream while simultaneously strengthening the company's environmental narrative and attracting ESG-focused capital.

**4. Commission an independent third-party audit of OGM effectiveness at Kakuzi and EPM — within 6 months.** The 2025 "More to Her Story" findings that abuse continues on Malawi tea estates despite remediation pledges represent an existential reputational threat. KHRC's assessment of the Kakuzi OGM as having "many gaps" and Leigh Day's retained right to resume litigation create ongoing legal exposure. An independent, publicly reported audit — conducted by a recognized human rights organization rather than a company-appointed consultancy — would either validate progress or identify failures before they become the next Sunday Times headline. Issue the public apology that KHRC and SOMO have repeatedly called for; the reputational cost of continued silence now exceeds any litigation risk from acknowledgment.

**5. Pursue EUDR compliance as a first-mover competitive advantage — complete by Q3 2026.** With the compliance deadline at December 30, 2026, Camellia's long-established estates and vertically integrated supply chain give it a natural advantage over competitors who must trace complex multi-origin supply chains. Invest in GPS-level geolocation mapping and digital traceability systems for rubber and timber operations. Market verified deforestation-free status to EU buyers as a premium sourcing proposition. Fewer than 30% of Fortune 500 companies can reliably trace materials to origins — Camellia can offer what most competitors cannot. Extend the compliance framework to tea proactively, anticipating potential scope expansion by the EU Commission.
