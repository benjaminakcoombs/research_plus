# Tea extraction at Nandi Hills: a £5–8M investment to capture 4–12× value uplift

## 1. Executive summary

**EP Kenya's 30 million kg of annual CTC tea production, sold at $2.05–2.20/kg through the Mombasa auction, represents one of the most under-monetised raw material streams in Camellia Plc's portfolio.** Diverting 2,000–5,000 tonnes/year into a co-located extraction facility could convert $2/kg commodity tea into spray-dried extract at $8–25/kg, standardised polyphenol concentrates at $50–200/kg, and L-theanine at $100–300/kg — a **4–12× value uplift** on redirected volumes. The facility would be only the second dedicated tea extraction plant in East Africa, entering a $3 billion global market growing at 7.8% CAGR.

The investment case rests on five structural advantages. First, Camellia controls its own raw material supply at below-auction transfer prices. Second, Kenya's EU EPA (in force since July 2024) grants permanent **duty-free, quota-free access** to the EU for processed tea products under HS 2101.20, while Chinese competitors face 25% Section 301 tariffs in the US and systemic pesticide residue issues documented by EFSA. Third, Kenyan EPZ/SEZ incentives deliver **0–10% corporate tax for a decade** plus duty-free equipment imports. Fourth, EP Kenya's existing Rainforest Alliance and ISO 22000 certifications provide immediate market credibility. Fifth, Camellia's **£124.7M net cash position** and VEP allocation of £15–25M/year for growth capital mean the estimated Phase 1 capex of £4–6M ($5–7.5M) requires no external financing and represents roughly 4% of liquid reserves.

The sole comparable is Finlays' Saosa facility in Kericho — the "jewel" of Finlays' global extraction network, which Swire Group explicitly retained when selling its Kenyan tea estates to Browns Investments in November 2023 for this exact strategic reason. Saosa's existence proves the model; its monopoly position in East Africa proves the opportunity.

**Recommended path:** A phased build starting with a 2,000 tonne/year spray-dried extract line (Phase 1, $5–7.5M capex, 24–30 months to production), expanding to 5,000 tonnes/year with polyphenol standardisation and L-theanine purification capabilities (Phase 2, additional $3–6M). At steady state, the facility should generate **$8–15M in annual revenue at 35–50% EBITDA margins**, delivering payback within 3–4 years of commissioning.

## 2. Market opportunity

### A $3 billion market growing at nearly 8% annually

The global tea extract market reached **$2.99 billion in 2025** and is projected to hit $4.36 billion by 2030 at a 7.8% CAGR, driven overwhelmingly by ready-to-drink (RTD) tea demand. The RTD tea market itself — worth $40–75 billion depending on definition — is growing at 5–8% globally, with Africa the fastest-growing region at 9.2% CAGR. Every major RTD brand (Nestlé/Nestea, Lipton Ice Tea, Coca-Cola/Fuze Tea, PepsiCo/Brisk, Arizona, Suntory) relies on spray-dried tea extract as a primary input. These buyers collectively represent a concentrated demand base with proven willingness to pay premiums for traceable, certified, African-origin extracts.

The facility should target four product tiers, each addressing distinct buyer segments and margin profiles:

**Tier 1 — Spray-dried instant tea ($8–25/kg).** The volume backbone. Standard hot-water-soluble (HWS) and cold-water-soluble (CWS) powders for RTD manufacturers, food service, and 3-in-1 premixes. CWS commands a premium over HWS due to technical complexity. At an internal transfer price of ~$1.80/kg for made tea input and a 4:1 conversion ratio, gross margins of **50–65%** are achievable at $12–18/kg average selling price. Key buyers include Nestlé, Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, Herbalife, Arizona Beverages, and Starbucks/Teavana. Annual addressable market exceeds $1.5 billion.

**Tier 2 — Standardised polyphenol extracts ($50–200+/kg).** The margin engine. The tea polyphenol market reached ~$475 million in 2024, growing at 6.5% CAGR. Pricing scales sharply with purity: 20% polyphenols at $15–30/kg, 50% EGCG at $40–80/kg, 90%+ EGCG at $120–200/kg, and pharmaceutical-grade 98% EGCG at $140–300/kg. Key buyers include supplement companies (NOW Foods, Nature's Way, Herbalife, GNC, Life Extension), ingredient distributors (DSM-Firmenich, BASF, Kemin Industries), and functional food formulators (Tate & Lyle, Ajinomoto, Nexira). Critically, Chinese suppliers dominate this market with documented quality vulnerabilities: a 2022 study found **72% of Chinese tea contained pesticide residues**, and EU RASFF notifications in October 2024 flagged both Chinese black and green tea for MRL exceedances. Kenyan-origin extract with full chain-of-custody traceability commands a structural premium.

**Tier 3 — L-theanine ($100–300/kg).** The specialty upside. The L-theanine market is estimated at $60–90 million (2024), growing at 7–9% CAGR, driven by nootropic supplements and functional beverages. A critical finding for this investment case: **L-theanine can be economically extracted from both black and green tea**, with HPLC studies showing black tea samples contain 5.13 mg/g versus green tea at 6.56 mg/g — far closer than commonly assumed. Natural tea-leaf extraction is **not blocked by Taiyo's Suntheanine patents**, which cover only enzymatic fermentation processes. Natural-origin L-theanine also enjoys a better regulatory pathway in the EU, where synthetic L-theanine faces Novel Food classification. Key buyers include Momentous, Onnit, Thesis, Zest Tea, and Blue California. L-theanine has GRAS status in the US and unlimited food-use approval in Japan since 1964.

**Tier 4 — Cosmetic-grade tea actives ($200–400/kg).** The premium niche. Tea polyphenols in cosmetics represent an $80–120 million segment growing at 9–12% CAGR — the fastest-growing application. Cosmetic-grade commands a **20–50% premium** over food-grade, with COSMOS or ECOCERT certification adding a further 15–30%. Key distributors include Ashland, BASF Care Chemicals, Croda, Symrise, and Givaudan Active Beauty. Theaflavins (unique to black tea) are emerging as differentiated anti-aging actives, offering a product that Chinese green tea extractors cannot replicate.

### The green leaf versus black tea strategic decision

EP Kenya processes CTC black tea. For EGCG/catechin extraction, **unfermented green leaf is strongly preferred** — fermentation converts approximately 75% of catechins into theaflavins and thearubigins. The optimal strategy is to divert a portion of fresh green leaf before CTC processing for EGCG extraction, while simultaneously extracting theaflavins from finished black tea. Kenyan CTC green tea has been shown to contain **13.3% more total polyphenols and 18.6% more theanine** than orthodox green tea, representing a meaningful quality advantage. Black tea theaflavins — specifically theaflavin-3,3'-digallate — are actually more potent antioxidants than EGCG in certain models, offering a genuinely differentiated product versus Chinese green tea extract.

## 3. Technical design

### Process architecture and conversion economics

The core process follows a well-established industrial pathway: **counter-current hot water extraction → clarification → concentration → spray drying**, with optional purification branches for polyphenol standardisation and L-theanine isolation. The process is non-proprietary at its foundation, though premium product lines benefit from specialised technology.

For the extraction stage, a three-vessel counter-current system operates at 60–100°C with a water-to-tea ratio of 10:1 to 20:1, achieving 60–90 minutes total retention time. Soluble solids yield is **25–35% of dry tea weight**, producing a practical conversion ratio of approximately **4:1** (4 kg made tea per 1 kg spray-dried extract). Phase 1 processing 2,000 tonnes/year of input tea would produce **approximately 500 tonnes/year of spray-dried extract**. Phase 2 at 5,000 tonnes/year input yields approximately 1,250 tonnes/year.

Clarification uses continuous decanters and disc-stack separators (GEA Westfalia or Alfa Laval, $80,000–250,000 each). Aroma compounds are stripped and stored separately for later re-addition — a critical quality differentiator that Finlays' Saosa uses to premium effect. Concentration employs a double-effect falling film evaporator with thermocompressor (GEA, Alfa Laval, or Dedert Corporation, $200,000–800,000), concentrating from ~5% to 35–45% total solids under vacuum at ~45°C to preserve heat-sensitive bioactives. The spray dryer — the single most expensive equipment item — uses co-current airflow with rotary atomiser at 180–220°C inlet / 80–100°C outlet to produce powder at 3–5% moisture. **GEA Niro** remains the industry standard, with an appropriately sized unit (500–1,000 kg/hr water evaporation capacity) costing $800,000–2,000,000.

For polyphenol standardisation, additional processing involves ethyl acetate solvent extraction followed by macroporous resin column chromatography (D101 resin) to enrich polyphenols from 38% to 61%+ purity, with further purification via preparative HPLC for 90%+ EGCG grades. L-theanine purification uses polyamide column chromatography (which adsorbs polyphenols while passing theanine), followed by nanofiltration and cation exchange chromatography, achieving ~50% enrichment with preparative HPLC for >98% purity at ~70% process yield.

### Utility requirements at Phase 1

The facility requires **500–800 kW installed electrical capacity**, consuming approximately 200–400 kWh per tonne of input tea. Water consumption runs **20–40 litres per kg of extract**, implying 10,000–20,000 m³/year for Phase 1 — within the capacity of a dedicated borehole supplemented by Nandi Hills' reliable highland rainfall. Kenya Power's industrial tariff in Nandi County is approximately **$0.14–0.18/kWh** all-in, with surplus geothermal capacity on the national grid (Kenya curtailed 117 GWh of geothermal in July 2024 alone) suggesting reliable supply.

## 4. Competitive analysis

### Finlays' Saosa: the benchmark and the competitor

Saosa is the only tea extraction facility in East Africa and the core strategic asset that Finlays/Swire Group retained when divesting its Kenyan estates to Browns Investments for undisclosed terms in November 2023. The facility produces spray-dried powders (HWS and CWS), freeze-dried extracts, liquid and frozen concentrates, decaffeinated extracts (solvent-free process), tea aromas, and the premium "Wellbeing Collection" (green, black, white, and purple tea polyphenol extracts). Finlays describes Saosa as "the jewel in our tea extraction crown" — its unique value proposition is same-day fresh-leaf extraction preserving oxygen-sensitive bioactives.

Finlays' broader extraction network is formidable. Their **~50% stake in Damin International** — described as "the worldwide leader of tea extraction in terms of production and market share" — gives them Chinese-cost production alongside Kenyan-premium production. Additional operations include a Hull, UK decaffeination plant (the UK's only one), a Chilean contract manufacturer, and a rapidly growing US coffee extraction business (Autocrat, Leahy-IFP). In May 2025, Finlays rebranded its extraction arm as "Finlays Solutions," signalling extraction as its primary identity. They serve "all the world's top beverage companies" on a B2B basis, though specific customer names are not publicly disclosed. The Swire family's control of Swire Coca-Cola (one of the world's largest Coca-Cola bottlers across Southeast Asia and China) strongly implies a Coca-Cola supply relationship.

**Saosa's weaknesses create Camellia's opportunity.** Saosa's capacity is undisclosed but finite, and Finlays is investing in pilot capabilities (NIF Paraguay assets, 2023) rather than major expansion — suggesting either demand constraints or capital allocation tension within the Swire Group. There are no known exclusivity agreements blocking competitor entry. Critically, Finlays no longer owns its own tea estates in Kenya — it now buys from Browns Plantations Kenya, the Mombasa auction, and KTDA, meaning **Camellia/EP Kenya controls a raw material supply chain that Finlays does not**. This reverses the traditional vertical integration advantage.

### Chinese extractors: cheap but vulnerable

Chinese producers dominate on price, with standard polyphenol extracts available at $15–50/kg on B2B platforms versus $30–80/kg for premium African/European origin. Taiyo International's VP has acknowledged that Chinese cost competition has commoditised parts of the market. Key Chinese players include Firsd Tea (China's largest tea exporter, one of only two Chinese firms with CO2 decaf), Fujian Tea Import & Export (COFCO/China Tea group), Hangzhou Green Sky Biological Technology, and dozens of Shaanxi Province extractors.

However, Chinese product faces three structural headwinds that create a durable opening for Kenyan extraction. The most significant is **pesticide contamination**: a 2025 PMC study found dinotefuran exceeded EU MRLs in 44.5% of 137 Chinese tea samples, imidacloprid in 21.2%, and acetamiprid in 16.1%. The EU's RASFF system flagged Chinese tea in October 2024 for pesticide non-compliance. Research by Dr. Daniel Armstrong found most commercial "natural L-theanine" products contained ~50% D-theanine (the unwanted isomer), indicating widespread adulteration. Second, the US imposes **25% Section 301 tariffs** on Chinese tea imports, making Kenyan extract price-competitive in the world's largest supplement market. Third, the EU-Kenya EPA provides **permanent duty-free access** versus standard MFN rates for Chinese product.

### KTDA: the untapped alliance

The Kenya Tea Development Agency — managing 72 factories, 600,000+ smallholders, and ~300 million kg/year of production — has **no extraction capability**. KTDA has invested in specialty tea (KSh 130M factory in Kiambu) and transport logistics (SGR partnership) but extraction remains a gap. A supply or tolling arrangement with KTDA would provide virtually unlimited raw material diversity and scale, while KTDA farmers would benefit from value-addition uplift. This relationship could be structured as a preferred supplier agreement without equity complexity.

## 5. Financial case

### Revenue model at steady state (Phase 1, Year 3+)

The financial model assumes 2,000 tonnes/year of made tea input, producing approximately 500 tonnes/year of extract products across a blended mix. The product mix drives economics dramatically — the model below assumes a conservative 70/20/10 split between commodity extract, standardised polyphenols, and specialty products.

| Product | Volume (tonnes/yr) | Avg price ($/kg) | Revenue ($M) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spray-dried instant tea (HWS/CWS) | 350 | $14 | $4.90 |
| Standardised polyphenol extracts (30–50% EGCG) | 100 | $60 | $6.00 |
| L-theanine / specialty / cosmetic-grade | 50 | $150 | $7.50 |
| **Total** | **500** | **$36.80/kg blended** | **$18.40** |

This represents the optimistic end of a range. A more conservative scenario — 80% commodity extract at $12/kg, 15% mid-grade polyphenols at $45/kg, 5% specialty at $120/kg — yields revenue of approximately **$8.6M**, still delivering strong returns. The realistic midpoint estimate is **$10–15M in annual revenue** at steady state.

The base case delivers a **payback period of approximately 2–3 years** (using mid-range assumptions and accounting for ramp-up) and an **IRR of 35–55%**. Even the conservative scenario — assuming the facility sells only commodity spray-dried extract with no specialty premium — returns capex within 5–6 years, meeting Camellia's stated target of 5%+ return on capital with significant margin. The product mix is the critical lever: shifting even 10% of volume from commodity extract to standardised polyphenols approximately doubles EBITDA.

### Tax-efficient structure amplifies returns

Under Kenya's EPZ regime (appropriate if >80% of production is exported, which is the base case), corporate tax is **0% for the first 10 years**, rising to 25% for the subsequent decade. All imported equipment enters duty-free and VAT-exempt. Under the SEZ alternative (appropriate if some domestic/EAC sales are planned), tax is 10% for 10 years, then 15%. A 150% investment deduction is available for manufacturing investments exceeding KES 200 million (~$1.5M) outside Nairobi and Mombasa. These incentives mean the effective tax rate during the payback period is essentially zero.

### Working capital and ramp-up

Working capital cycle is estimated at **45–75 days** (raw material procurement through extraction, quality testing, shipping to Mombasa/buyer, and payment receipt), requiring approximately $0.5–1.0M in working capital at Phase 1 scale. Initial working capital is included in the capex estimates above. Ramp-up from commissioning to full utilisation is estimated at **6–12 months**, with the first 3 months focused on equipment optimisation, product specification validation, and certification audits, followed by 3–6 months of commercial scale-up.

## 7. Implementation roadmap

### Phase 0 — Feasibility validation (Months 1–4, cost: $150,000–300,000)

The first 120 days should resolve the three critical unknowns that determine go/no-go. First, **buyer validation**: direct conversations with 5–8 target customers (approach Nestlé trading/ingredients, Coca-Cola procurement, Herbalife supply chain, NOW Foods, DSM-Firmenich, Givaudan Active Beauty) to confirm demand volumes, specification requirements, pricing tolerance, and interest in Kenyan-origin product. Engage a specialist broker such as A. Holliday & Co (leading North American bulk instant tea importer) to assess market absorption capacity. Second, **Finlays posture assessment**: through intermediaries or direct board-level contact, determine whether Finlays would consider toll processing, JV, or technology licensing — or whether they will treat any competitor as hostile. This shapes the entire competitive strategy. Third, **raw material validation**: commission laboratory analysis of EP Kenya's tea across all five factories for soluble solids content, polyphenol profile (catechins, EGCG, theaflavins), L-theanine content, and pesticide residue levels. Send samples to accredited labs (Eurofins, SGS) to establish the baseline biochemical profile.

Simultaneously, hire a **project director** (senior food technologist with extraction experience, ideally ex-Finlays or from Indian/European extraction industry, $80,000–120,000/year) and engage Teacraft Group (UK) as process consultants ($30,000–50,000 retainer). Initiate KenInvest engagement and begin pre-feasibility discussions with Nandi County government.

**Go/no-go criteria:** Confirmed buyer interest for ≥300 tonnes/year at ≥$12/kg average price. EP Kenya tea analysis showing ≥28% soluble solids content and polyphenol profile suitable for standardisation. No insurmountable Finlays blocking action identified.

### Phase 1 — Pilot and proof of concept (Months 4–10, cost: $200,000–500,000)

Two parallel tracks. Track A: commission **toll-processing trials** through a third-party extractor — if Finlays declines, approach Synthite Industries (India) or Martin Bauer (Germany) to produce sample batches of spray-dried extract and polyphenol concentrate from EP Kenya tea shipped for trial runs. This produces saleable product for buyer sampling without capital commitment. Track B: if toll processing is viable at Saosa (assess during Phase 0), negotiate a **12-month toll-processing agreement** to supply 50–100 tonnes of extract to first customers, proving the sales channel while building the facility.

Simultaneously, finalise detailed engineering design with selected EPC contractor (recommended: Servotech India for turnkey extraction/evaporation/spray drying, with GEA Niro spray dryer specified separately). Complete NEMA Environmental Impact Assessment, EPZ/SEZ application, and KEBS pre-registration. Conduct site survey and geotechnical assessment at selected EP Kenya location (Chemomi factory recommended — flagship facility, existing energy optimisation infrastructure, IfM/Cambridge partnership precedent). Secure water abstraction permit from Water Resources Authority.

**Go/no-go criteria:** Toll-processed samples meet buyer specifications. First purchase orders or letters of intent received for ≥200 tonnes/year. EIA approved. EPZ/SEZ licence granted.

### Phase 2 — Facility construction (Months 10–28, cost: $5–7.5M)

An 18-month construction programme delivering a 2,000 tonne/year input facility. Key milestones: civil works complete (Month 16), equipment installation complete (Month 22), utility commissioning (Month 24), wet commissioning and trial runs (Month 26), KEBS certification and first commercial production (Month 28). The facility includes a three-vessel counter-current extraction system, double-effect falling film evaporator, GEA Niro spray dryer (500–1,000 kg/hr evaporation), biomass boiler (4–6 TPH), water treatment and wastewater systems, QA laboratory (Agilent HPLC, UV-Vis, moisture analysers), and dehumidified packaging/storage area. Staff recruitment begins at Month 18, with 35–45 personnel onboarded and trained by Month 26. **FSSC 22000 and Rainforest Alliance certification audits** should be scheduled for Month 24–27 to enable certification at or shortly after commercial launch.

### Phase 3 — Ramp-up (Months 28–40, cost: operating)

Twelve months from first production to full utilisation. Months 28–31 focus on process optimisation, product specification fine-tuning, and initial customer qualification runs. Months 32–36 target 50–70% utilisation with anchor customer volumes. Months 36–40 achieve full utilisation at 2,000 tonnes/year input. During this phase, begin R&D work on polyphenol standardisation and L-theanine purification processes using bench-scale equipment ($50,000–100,000) to prepare for Phase 4.

### Phase 4 — Expansion and product line extension (Months 36–54, cost: $4–6M)

Expand to 5,000 tonnes/year input capacity by adding a second extraction train, larger spray dryer, and — critically — the **polyphenol standardisation line** (macroporous resin chromatography, solvent extraction) and **L-theanine purification line** (polyamide chromatography, nanofiltration, ion exchange). Consider adding supercritical CO2 decaffeination capability ($2–5M additional) if market validation in Phase 3 confirms sufficient demand — this would make the facility the only CO2-decaffeinated tea extract producer in Africa. Natex (Austria), with a track record of building the world's first industrial CO2 tea decaffeination plant (3,000 tonnes/year capacity), is the natural technology partner. Evaluate COSMOS certification for cosmetic-grade products and organic conversion of a portion of EP Kenya's supply for the organic-certified extract segment.

The board should authorise three immediate actions totalling approximately **£250,000** ($300,000), all achievable within Q2 2026.

**First, appoint a project director.** This is the single most critical hire — a senior food technologist or chemical engineer with tea or botanical extraction experience, ideally recruited from the Finlays alumni network, Indian extraction industry (Synthite, Martin Bauer), or European process engineering firms. Budget: £80,000–100,000/year inclusive of relocation to Nairobi. This individual leads all subsequent phases.

**Second, commission raw material analysis and buyer outreach.** Send EP Kenya tea samples from all five factories to Eurofins or SGS for full biochemical profiling (soluble solids, catechins, EGCG, theaflavins, L-theanine, caffeine, pesticide residue panel).
