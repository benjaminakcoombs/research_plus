# Camellia Plc: geopolitical risk assessment for the board

**Camellia faces an escalating cluster of land tenure, labor, and regulatory risks across its four core geographies that could force portfolio restructuring within 1–3 years.** The company's Kenya and India operations face the most acute near-term threats — active land invasions with political cover in Nandi County, National Land Commission directives to surrender 3,200 acres at Kakuzi, and Assam's November 2025 ceiling amendment stripping labour-line land from tea estates. Bangladesh's political transition is at a critical inflection point with elections on 12 February 2026, while Malawi's currency crisis has destroyed over 70% of kwacha value since 2022. The cross-cutting picture is no better: the EU Forced Labour Regulation (applicable December 2027) will impose product-level due diligence on every commodity Camellia exports to Europe, and tightening EU pesticide MRLs for tea create persistent compliance risk. The new management team's Value Enhancement Plan and ongoing asset disposals suggest the board already recognizes that portfolio rationalization is overdue — but the window for orderly exits is narrowing as country risks compound.

## 1. Kenya: colonial land legacy reaches a tipping point

**Overall risk: HIGH | Direction: DETERIORATING**

Kenya is Camellia's most exposed geography, with tea operations through Eastern Produce Kenya (EPK) in Nandi County and macadamia/avocado through Kakuzi Plc in Murang'a County. The core risk is not generic political instability — it is the specific, accelerating collision between constitutional restrictions on foreign land ownership and community mobilization over colonial-era dispossession.

### Land tenure is the existential risk

The 2010 Constitution (Article 65) automatically converted all foreign-held freehold interests to **99-year leases from August 2010**, and critically, Section 13(1) of the Land Act 2012 grants pre-emptive lease renewal rights **only to Kenyan citizens**. Foreign companies have no automatic renewal right. The National Land Commission has weaponized this framework: in November 2025, the NLC directed Kakuzi to **surrender 3,200 acres** to historical claimants, and separately issued Gazette Notice No. 4512 ordering all 999-year leases converted and **lease renewal withheld** pending agreements with county governments. Kakuzi has filed legal proceedings contesting the NLC's jurisdiction, but squatters invaded the farm immediately after the ruling, subdividing land and planting crops.

The EPK situation is more immediately damaging. Since August 2023, over 200 people — led by Nandi Hills MP Kitur — have occupied 350 acres of EPK's Sitoi Estate. Invaders are actively plucking Camellia's tea crop, causing **Sh30 million (~$230,000) in monthly losses**. Court injunctions have been defied. Occupiers have seized the Sitoi Airstrip and built permanent structures. In January 2025, squatters attacked a company vehicle and employees. The Kenya Tea Growers Association described this as a **"Zimbabwe-like illegal land invasion"** with "criminal gangs who appear to enjoy political cover." The UK FCDO acknowledged concern and contact with Kenyan authorities.

The broader pattern is unmistakable. Both James Finlay and Unilever/Lipton have exited Kenyan tea estates entirely — both selling to Browns Investments PLC (Sri Lanka) — suggesting the risk calculus turned negative for major multinationals. Kericho and Bomet county governors have stated they will use lease renewals as leverage for community equity stakes, dramatically increased land rates (proposed **300–625% increases**), and limits on mechanization.

### Political environment adds volatility

President Ruto's government remains fragile after the 2024 Gen Z protest movement, which permanently altered Kenya's political mobilization model. Raila Odinga's death on **15 October 2025** removed the dominant opposition figure, fragmenting the political landscape ahead of August 2027 elections. The **IMF terminated its $3.6 billion program in March 2025** over fiscal slippages, weakening the government's reform credibility. The Finance Act 2025 introduced a **5-year cap on tax loss carry-forward** (previously indefinite) — particularly punitive for agricultural companies with volatile earnings. Kenya's placement on the **FATF grey list since February 2024** and the EU's subsequent high-risk designation (June 2025) create banking friction for transactions involving Kenyan entities.

On the positive side, the **EU-Kenya Economic Partnership Agreement** entered force in July 2024, securing duty-free access for tea and macadamia exports. The KES recovered ~19% from its January 2024 low of 160/USD to ~129/USD, and Kenya's capital account remains liberalized with profit repatriation legally guaranteed under the Foreign Investment Protection Act — though the 15% repatriation tax on branch profits (Finance Act 2023) is a material cost.

### Top 3 Kenya risks

1. **Forced land surrender or lease non-renewal** — NLC directives, court challenges pending, political incentives align against foreign landholders
2. **Escalating estate invasions with political cover** — Sitoi losses ongoing at ~$2.8M/year; Kakuzi invasion post-NLC ruling; no effective law enforcement response
3. **Tax and regulatory tightening** — 5-year loss carry-forward cap, annual Finance Bill volatility, KRA transfer pricing investigation into Kakuzi (opened August 2022, unresolved)

**Key catalysts:** NLC Kakuzi legal proceedings (2026); Sitoi Estate court hearings (ongoing); 2027 election campaign season (intensifies late 2026); annual Finance Bill debate (May–August, protest risk).

## 2. Bangladesh: democratic transition meets structural fragility

**Overall risk: HIGH | Direction: UNCERTAIN (inflection point)**

Camellia operates in Bangladesh through Duncan Brothers, the country's **largest tea producer** (~16% of national output), with estates across the Sylhet Division employing approximately 18,500 people.

### Political transition at a critical moment

Bangladesh's general elections are scheduled for **12 February 2026** — effectively tomorrow — following the July 2024 ouster of Sheikh Hasina, who was subsequently sentenced to death in absentia in November 2025. The interim government under Nobel laureate **Muhammad Yunus** has overseen 18 months of political upheaval, with **401 political violence incidents in 2025** resulting in 102 deaths and 197 lynching/mob killings. The election is a bipolar contest between the **BNP** (led by Tarique Rahman) and a **Jamaat-led 11-party alliance** including student movement leaders. The Awami League has been banned and is not participating — raising legitimacy concerns.

The election outcome will determine the trajectory of IMF program compliance, FX policy, and the regulatory environment for foreign investors. The **$4.7 billion IMF program** (ECF/EFF/RSF combined) explicitly requires the new government's "full ownership." BNP is historically pro-business, which would be positive for foreign estates, but the party's capacity to govern after 15 years in opposition is untested.

### Economic fundamentals are recovering but fragile

The BDT has depreciated **42% against the USD since 2021** (from 86 to ~122.3 BDT/USD), though the rate has stabilized since Bangladesh Bank adopted a more flexible exchange rate regime in May 2025. FX reserves recovered significantly to **$33.18 billion gross** (December 2025) from below $20 billion at points in 2024, driven by $30.33 billion in remittances. However, the banking sector carries **35.73% non-performing loans** (September 2025), and GDP growth slowed to **3.97%** in FY2025 — the lowest since COVID.

**Profit repatriation** remains a material concern. The US State Department's 2025 Investment Climate Statement confirms the government "implemented measures to delay foreign currency payments and to slow or block foreign companies from repatriating profits." The acute phase appears to be easing as reserves recover, but approvals have taken over one year in some cases, and structural risks persist.

### Labour costs are unsustainably low — correction is coming

Bangladesh tea workers earn approximately **Tk 170–179/day (~$1.43)**, among the lowest tea worker wages globally — roughly half of Assam rates and one-quarter of Sri Lanka's. The gap between current wages and worker demands of **Tk 600/day** is enormous. Major strikes occurred in 2022 (150,000 workers, 19 days) and recurring work stoppages continued through 2024–2025. Duncan Brothers' estates in Moulvibazar and Habiganj sit in the heart of the protest zone. The incoming government will face immediate pressure to raise agricultural wages.

### Climate vulnerability is worsening in Sylhet

Flash flooding in the Sylhet Division has become **near-annual** — catastrophic events in 2022 and 2024, with 66% of Sylhet Division inundated in June 2024 and 6.25 million people affected. Bangladesh tea production fell approximately **10 million kg in 2024** versus the prior year's record. Duncan Brothers has begun adaptation (12 reservoirs, irrigation systems), but the Bangladesh Tea Research Institute reports yields at historic estates declining by ~110 kg/hectare/year. FAO projects tea cultivation areas globally could **shrink significantly by 2050**, with Bangladesh among the most affected.

**Bangladesh's LDC graduation in November 2026** will remove trade preferences covering ~70% of exports, adding macroeconomic pressure and potentially affecting tea export competitiveness.

### Top 3 Bangladesh risks

1. **Post-election political instability** — contested results, policy reversals, or military reassertion could destabilize the operating environment
2. **Labour cost correction** — the current wage floor is unsustainable; any significant increase compresses margins on already low-value bulk tea
3. **Climate-driven production losses** — Sylhet flooding is intensifying; tea yields declining structurally

**Key catalysts:** Election results (12 February 2026); new government's IMF review (Q2 2026); monsoon season 2026 (June–September); LDC graduation (November 2026).

## 3. Malawi: currency crisis erodes the value proposition

**Overall risk: VERY HIGH | Direction: DETERIORATING**

Eastern Produce Malawi (EPM) is Malawi's largest tea and macadamia producer, operating 15 tea gardens, 8 factories, and 5 macadamia estates across Thyolo and Mulanje.

### Macroeconomic crisis is acute

The Malawian Kwacha has lost **over 70% of its value since 2022** through a series of devaluations — 25% in May 2022, **44% in November 2023**, and further depreciation through 2024–2025. The parallel market premium reached **150% in March 2025**, implying black-market rates near MK4,000–4,375/USD against an official rate of ~MK1,750. **Inflation stands at ~29%**, public debt exceeds 90% of GDP, and the IMF ECF program **automatically terminated in May 2025** after no review was completed in 18 months. The country is classified as **"in debt distress"** with domestic interest payments consuming over 50% of tax revenue. FX reserves provide only ~2.3 months of import cover.

While EPM's tea exports earn foreign currency (a theoretical advantage during devaluation), **forex rationing** makes profit repatriation extremely difficult, and imported inputs — fuel, fertilizer, equipment — have become vastly more expensive. The new Mutharika government (re-elected September 2025) has pledged no further devaluation, but IMF pressure for a market-clearing exchange rate makes further adjustment near-certain.

### Land law changes create long-term tenure risk

A **2022 amendment to the Land Act** repealed Section 37, which had permitted foreigners to acquire 50-year leases. **Foreigners are now barred from acquiring land**, though existing leases are grandfathered and not retrospective. This means EPM's current leases remain valid but **cannot be transferred to another foreign entity** and face uncertain renewal terms — a structural constraint on exit options and long-term planning.

Over **20% of Malawi's population (~5 million people)** faces acute food insecurity following El Niño drought in 2024 and Cyclone Freddy's devastation in 2023. Political pressure to redirect cash-crop land toward food production is a latent risk, though no specific policy has been identified.

### Top 3 Malawi risks

1. **Currency collapse and forex rationing** — further devaluation near-certain; profit repatriation effectively blocked
2. **Macroeconomic crisis** — debt distress, 29% inflation, IMF program terminated, declining ODA
3. **Land tenure restriction** — 2022 amendment bars new foreign land acquisition; lease renewal terms uncertain

**Key catalysts:** IMF program renegotiation (ongoing); Reserve Bank of Malawi FX policy decisions (quarterly); monsoon/cyclone season (January–March).

## 4. India: Assam's ceiling amendment threatens estate integrity

**Overall risk: MODERATE-HIGH | Direction: DETERIORATING**

Camellia operates in India through **Goodricke Group Limited** (BSE-listed, ~74% owned), India's second-largest tea producer with **29 tea gardens and 30 factories** across Darjeeling, Dooars, Assam, and Cachar. India accounts for the Group's largest land area (**16,632 hectares**) and significant revenue.

### The Assam ceiling amendment is the headline risk

On **29 November 2025**, Assam passed the Fixation of Ceiling on Land Holdings (Amendment) Act, 2025, which **removes labour lines (worker housing) from the definition of "ancillary purpose"** for tea estates. The government will acquire labour-line land and grant titles (pattas) to tea workers across **825 tea estates** covering ~218,553 bighas. Compensation is set at **₹3,000 per bigha** — widely considered below market value, with total estimated compensation of only ~₹65.57 crore (~£6M). First-phase implementation in 103 estates began **January 2026**.

All five major tea industry associations have filed writ petitions in Gauhati High Court challenging the Act. The court listed petitions for **26 March 2026**, ruling any final action will be subject to the outcome. This directly affects Goodricke's Assam estates, potentially fragmenting estate integrity while maintaining statutory obligations for housing and amenities even after land transfer. CM Sarma has warned that state incentives (~₹150 crore/year to tea gardens) could be reconsidered if owners obstruct implementation. **Over 1.4 million residents are expected to benefit** — creating enormous political pressure to proceed.

India's FDI regime for tea remains uniquely restrictive: **100% FDI is permitted but only under the government approval route** (not automatic), with mandatory **divestment of 26% equity** to Indian partners within 5 years. Tea worker wages in Assam stand at **₹232–250/day** (~$2.75), with upward pressure from both CAG criticism (September 2024 report found wages "meagre") and political competition. The CAG notably found tea workers are **not classified under scheduled employments**, denying them minimum wage protections.

### Top 3 India risks

1. **Assam ceiling amendment** — labour-line land transfer at below-market compensation; estate fragmentation; Gauhati HC ruling (March 2026) is determinative
2. **Rising labour costs with political momentum** — wages revised from January 2026; CM Sarma using tea worker welfare as electoral tool
3. **FDI regulatory friction** — government-route approval, 26% divestment requirement, and restrictive land acquisition rules constrain strategic flexibility

**Key catalysts:** Gauhati HC hearing (26 March 2026); Assam Phase 1 implementation (Q1 2026); wage revision effective January 2026.

## 5. UK listing: AIM's structural decline raises strategic questions

**Overall risk: MODERATE | Direction: DETERIORATING**

Camellia is listed on **AIM** (not the Main Market as sometimes assumed), which provides a lighter regulatory regime but an increasingly inhospitable market environment. Only **679 companies** remain on AIM — the lowest in its 30-year history — with 89 exits in 2024 alone. With a **~28% free float**, only 2 analyst coverage, and persistent operating losses, the listing provides limited capital-raising benefit against **£500K–1M+ annual compliance costs**.

The most significant near-term change is the **April 2026 loss of 100% Inheritance Tax Business Property Relief** for AIM shares (reduced to 50% relief / 20% effective IHT rate). This directly affects the Camellia Private Trust Company structure controlling 56.45% of shares. Combined with the ongoing share buyback programme (tender offer at £54/share; on-market purchases ongoing), the trajectory appears consistent with eventual take-private, though no formal announcement has been made.

Kenya's **FATF grey listing** (February 2024) and subsequent EU high-risk country designation (June 2025) impose enhanced due diligence requirements on banking relationships involving Kenyan operations — an operational friction that increases compliance costs. No UK sanctions apply to any of Camellia's operating countries.

**ESG reporting burden is rising.** The FCA published consultation on 30 January 2026 proposing UK Sustainability Reporting Standards (aligned with ISSB) to replace TCFD rules. Mandatory reporting is expected for AIM companies from accounting periods beginning on or after **1 January 2027**. For a company operating across 8 countries with ~78,000 employees, Scope 3 emissions reporting will be particularly complex and costly.

## 6. Cross-cutting regulatory risks demand proactive compliance

### The EU Forced Labour Regulation is the most significant new threat

Regulation 2024/3015 (adopted November 2024, applicable **14 December 2027**) applies to **all products, all sectors, all companies regardless of size** placed on the EU market. Unlike the CSDDD, there is no revenue or employee threshold. Tea, macadamia, and rubber from all of Camellia's operating countries are in scope. Products found linked to forced labour will be **banned, withdrawn, and destroyed**. Given Camellia's settlement history (£6.9M in Kenya/Malawi human rights claims) and ongoing investigative reporting that abuse on Malawi estates may be continuing despite remediation mechanisms, this regulation represents **material commercial risk** requiring demonstrable, verifiable supply chain due diligence.

### EU pesticide MRLs are a persistent compliance challenge

The EU sets the world's strictest Maximum Residue Levels, with a default of **0.01 mg/kg** when no specific limit exists. **Anthraquinone** (MRL: 0.02 mg/kg for tea) is a particular problem — not from pesticide use but from combustion contamination during wood-fired drying, with **88% of samples** using pinewood exceeding the EU limit. New **neonicotinoid MRL reductions** (applicable 2026) based on environmental rather than solely health grounds represent a precedent-setting regulatory approach. German buyers often apply private standards at **30% of EU MRL**, creating even tighter effective limits.

### The EUDR covers rubber but not tea or macadamia

The EU Deforestation Regulation (as amended December 2025) applies to rubber from **30 December 2026** for large operators. Camellia's rubber operations in Bangladesh (~1,710 hectares) will require geolocation mapping, proof of no deforestation after 31 December 2020, and due diligence statements. **Tea and macadamia are not covered.** EU CBAM does not cover agricultural products directly, though fertilizer coverage (from January 2026) may increase input costs.

### Supply chain due diligence legislation is converging

The EU CSDDD (applicable July 2029) is unlikely to apply directly to Camellia given revised thresholds (>€1.5B turnover, >5,000 employees), but trickle-down requirements from in-scope customers will generate compliance demands. The UK Modern Slavery Act reform is pending, with a House of Lords committee recommending mandatory due diligence obligations. Agriculture is specifically flagged as high-risk. The US Section 307 of the Tariff Act (general forced labour import ban) applies globally to any Camellia products entering the US market.

### Three risks not currently in headlines that could materialize in 1–5 years

**First, Kenya county government lease extraction.** When tea estate leases eventually come up for renewal or regularization, Kericho, Nandi, Bomet, and Murang'a county governors have explicitly stated they will demand community equity stakes (15–100%), dramatically higher land rates, mechanization limits, and community development funding. The legal framework gives them leverage — foreign companies have no automatic renewal right. This is the slow-burn version of the acute invasion risk.

**Second, EU Forced Labour Regulation product seizures.** When enforcement begins in December 2027, Camellia's settled human rights claims and ongoing investigative reporting about Malawi estates create a trail of evidence that enforcement authorities could use to initiate investigations. Unlike the CSDDD, this regulation has no size threshold and applies product-by-product. A single seizure at an EU port would trigger customer reviews across the supply chain.

**Third, Assam political competition over tea worker welfare.** CM Sarma is using tea worker land rights and wages as electoral capital. The 2025 ceiling amendment could be the beginning, not the end, of state intervention in estate operations. If rival parties outbid on welfare promises, Goodricke could face progressively more restrictive operating conditions in Assam — its core Indian geography.

### Potential first-mover advantages

- **EU Forced Labour Regulation compliance**: Companies that build robust, verifiable due diligence systems ahead of December 2027 can position themselves as preferred suppliers to EU buyers who will face liability for non-compliant sourcing. Camellia's existing Operational Grievance Mechanisms and Independent Human Rights Advisory Committee provide a foundation that most competitors lack.

- **Kenya community equity partnerships**: Rather than waiting for forced concessions, proactively offering community equity stakes in Kenyan operations — modeled on the 15% structures used by Browns/Finlays — could secure lease renewal support and reduce invasion risk. The political cost of resisting is higher than the economic cost of sharing.

- **Macadamia and avocado pivot**: Kenya's macadamia and avocado operations (through Kakuzi) face the same land risks as tea but generate significantly higher margins. If land must be surrendered, concentrating remaining acreage on highest-value crops while reducing lower-margin bulk tea exposure would optimize returns per hectare of contested land.

## Conclusion: the portfolio needs surgery, not monitoring

The central finding of this assessment is that **multiple risks across Camellia's four core geographies are moving from latent to active simultaneously.** Kenya's land tenure regime is structurally hostile to foreign agricultural landholders and becoming more so. Bangladesh's political transition introduces a binary outcome that will clarify within weeks. Malawi's macroeconomic crisis is destroying the value of sterling-denominated returns. India's Assam amendment represents a direct transfer of estate assets to workers at below-market compensation. And the EU's incoming forced labour regulation will, for the first time, create product-level enforcement risk with teeth.

The new management team's Value Enhancement Plan and asset disposal programme suggest awareness that the status quo is untenable. The strategic question is no longer whether to rationalize the portfolio, but **which geographies and product lines to defend, which to negotiate controlled exits from, and how quickly** — before the window for orderly transactions closes. James Finlay and Unilever have already exited Kenyan tea estates; McLeod Russel entered insolvency. The buyer universe for emerging-market agricultural estates is narrow and shrinking. The company's **£124.7 million in net cash and investments** provides the financial flexibility to execute a strategic pivot — but only if decisions are made before country risks force them.
