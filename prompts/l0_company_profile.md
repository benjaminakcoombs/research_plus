Your job is NOT to analyze the company strategically — that comes later. Your job is to BUILD THE MAP. You need to understand what this company is, what it does, how it makes money, what it owns (tangible and intangible), who runs it, and what its environment looks like. Be exhaustive on facts. Be specific on operations.

## 1. IDENTITY, SCALE & OWNERSHIP

- Industry classification (GICS, SIC, or equivalent)
- One-paragraph plain-language description of what the company actually does
- Company archetype — classify as ONE of:
  - MANUFACTURER (makes physical products)
  - EXTRACTOR/GROWER (agriculture, mining, oil & gas)
  - RETAILER/DISTRIBUTOR (sells others' products)
  - PLATFORM/MARKETPLACE (connects buyers and sellers)
  - SAAS/SOFTWARE (sells software or digital services)
  - FINANCIAL SERVICES (banking, insurance, asset management)
  - INFRASTRUCTURE/UTILITIES (power, telecom, transport)
  - PROFESSIONAL SERVICES (consulting, legal, staffing)
  - CONGLOMERATE (multiple distinct businesses)
  - HEALTHCARE/PHARMA (drugs, devices, services)
  - MEDIA/ENTERTAINMENT (content, advertising)
  - REAL ESTATE (owns/operates property)
  - OTHER (specify)
- Scale indicators: Revenue, employees, number of facilities/locations, geographic footprint
- Key financial metrics: Revenue by segment (last 3 years), operating margins (gross, operating, net), balance sheet (net debt/cash, major assets/liabilities), free cash flow trend, capex by segment, dividend policy. ## 2. WHAT THE COMPANY ACTUALLY DOES

For each segment, map ALL forms of value the company holds — not just physical assets. Consider: physical assets and infrastructure, intellectual property, proprietary data and algorithms, brand equity, customer relationships and communities, workforce expertise, regulatory positions, and digital infrastructure. Many of the most valuable strategic ideas come from assets the company doesn't think of as assets.

Focus depth where it matters for THIS company. For a manufacturer, go deep on facilities, equipment, capacity, and waste streams. For a software company, go deep on the platform, data assets, network effects, and customer behavior. For a brand-driven company, go deep on brand equity, community, content, and customer loyalty. The dimensions below are guidance, not a mandatory checklist — spend your research time where the value actually lives.

For EACH distinct business segment or product line:

a) WHAT does this segment physically do? What is the product/service? Describe the actual operations — not the investor relations language, but what happens on the ground. What do employees do every day?

b) REVENUE MODEL: How does revenue come in? (Unit sales, subscriptions, commissions, licensing, contracts, auction, spot pricing?) What's the pricing structure? What are typical deal sizes?

c) KEY INPUTS & PROCUREMENT: What does this segment buy? Raw materials, components, energy, labor, technology, services? In what approximate volumes? Who are the major suppliers? Where does the company have meaningful purchasing scale?

d) PHYSICAL ASSETS & INFRASTRUCTURE: What does this segment own or lease?
   - Facilities (factories, offices, stores, warehouses, data centers, estates, processing plants)
   - Equipment and machinery (processing lines, vehicles, IT infrastructure, specialized tools)
   - Land (owned vs. leased, total area, location, current use vs. potential use)
   - Infrastructure (roads, power generation, water treatment, housing, clinics, schools — especially for remote operations)
   - Intellectual property (patents, trademarks, licenses, proprietary processes)

e) WASTE STREAMS & BYPRODUCTS: What waste, scrap, returns, or byproducts does this segment generate? What happens to them currently? (Sold, recycled, discarded, used internally?)

f) CAPACITY & UTILIZATION: What is the segment's maximum capacity? What's current utilization? What are the binding constraints on growth? Are there seasonal or cyclical patterns?

g) WORKFORCE: How many people? What skills? Where located? Labor intensity? Unionized? Key talent dependencies?

Focus on facts that would change how a strategic advisor thinks about the company. This section should give a research architect enough context to know which external forces deserve their own deep research workstream.

Include:
- Top 3-5 direct competitors per segment and key competitive dynamics
- Key regulations affecting the business (current and pending)
- Critical geographies and what operations exist in each
- Significant recent events (last 12-18 months): M&A, restructuring, management changes, financial performance shifts, operational events
- Key macro trends affecting the business (positive and negative)
- Geopolitical risks to operations
- ESG/sustainability posture: certifications, controversies, targets

## 4. STRATEGY, NARRATIVE & SELF-IMAGE

What has management communicated as the strategic direction? How does the company describe itself — to investors, to customers, to employees? What story does it tell about what it is and where it's going? Note any obvious tensions between this narrative and the operational reality documented above. How does the market perceive this company — and is that perception accurate?

Include:

- Capital allocation framework (if stated)
- Segments flagged for growth investment vs. divestiture

Based on everything above, what are the 5-10 most interesting, unusual, or strategically significant things about this company? What surprised you? What seems like it's hiding in plain sight? What would you want to investigate further if you had unlimited research time? What assets, capabilities, or relationships does the company seem to undervalue? These observations will be used to design targeted research workstreams — so be specific about WHAT is interesting and WHY.
