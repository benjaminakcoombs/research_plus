### STEP 2: BUILD CANDIDATE FINDINGS

A candidate finding can take two forms:

**(a) Compound thread** — multiple tension points combined into a single insight stronger than any individual TP. The connection between TPs should reveal a mechanism, constraint, or implication that is invisible from any single TP alone.

**(b) Standalone anchor** — a single tension point where the L2 evidence is so concrete and independently verifiable — committed infrastructure capital, signed agreements with public counterparties, regulatory filings establishing market size, published scientific data — that it stands as a self-sufficient argument. The test: would adding more TPs make the core evidence more persuasive, or would it bury the headline number in a more complex narrative? If the latter, the TP is strongest alone.

Both forms can produce world-class findings. Threading is a tool for amplifying insight, not a goal in itself.

**For compound threads, look for:**

- **Compound mechanisms**: Where TP-X establishes a condition and TP-Y provides the mechanism that satisfies it (e.g., "the financing architecture requires a creditworthy sponsor" + "this specific investor has an A+ credit rating and EPC capabilities" = a compound finding about the financing shortcut)
- **Constraint chains**: Where one TP's outcome narrows or expands the implications of another (e.g., "if the alumina is off-spec, the entire buyer universe from TP-4 collapses" — meaning the alumina finding and the buyer finding are linked)
- **Timing interactions**: Where multiple TPs converge on the same window or deadline, creating urgency that no single TP conveys alone
- **Elimination logic**: Where one TP rules out a path, making another TP's path the only remaining option (e.g., "Dolese can't be an equity sponsor" eliminates one creditworthy-sponsor candidate, elevating the remaining candidate)

**For standalone anchors, look for:**

- A TP with hard-number demand proof: committed capital, binding agreements, infrastructure build-out that independently validates market size or strategic position — where the evidence is so concrete that threading it with weaker TPs would dilute rather than amplify its force
- A TP where the L2 deep dive produced independently verifiable evidence (regulatory filings, public utility commitments, geological survey data) that no amount of analytical threading can improve

For each candidate (compound or standalone), write 2-3 sentences explaining why the finding works — either the connection between TPs and why the compound insight is stronger than either alone, or why the standalone evidence is compelling enough to occupy a finding slot without reinforcement.

### STEP 4: PORTFOLIO ASSESSMENT

You have drafted three findings. Now evaluate them *as a set* — not as individuals. Three strong findings that overlap or leave gaps produce a weaker document than two strong findings and one that fills a hole.

Run three diagnostics:

**Overlap test.** For each pair of findings (1↔2, 1↔3, 2↔3), ask: "If the reader has already absorbed Finding X, does Finding Y tell them something substantially new — or does it feel like an elaboration, a downstream consequence, or a restatement at a different altitude?" Pay particular attention to dependency: if Finding Y's conclusions change based on Finding X's outcome (e.g., "if the alumina is off-spec, this finding's buyer thesis collapses"), that's a signal they share a dimension. Some overlap is inevitable and healthy — the findings are about the same company. But if one finding is substantially downstream of another, it's using a slot on a dependent variable rather than an independent one.

**Gap test.** Ask: "What is the single strongest piece of L2-verified evidence in the entire research corpus that does NOT appear prominently in any of my three findings?" If the answer is a hard-evidence demand anchor (committed infrastructure capital, signed agreements, regulatory filings proving market size), that's a portfolio gap — the document proves the risk and the mechanism but doesn't prove the demand, or vice versa. A strong piece of evidence that's been "absorbed" into a finding's context paragraph rather than given a starring role may have been diluted. The test for lossy absorption: could a reader who skimmed only the three finding titles and first sentences reconstruct the absorbed insight? If not, it's been buried.

**Phone-call test.** For each finding independently, write one sentence: "An MD who reads this finding could call [specific client type] tomorrow about [specific actionable insight]." If you cannot write this sentence convincingly — if the finding requires the reader to already care about the company before it becomes actionable — the finding may be analytically strong but weak as cold-outreach material.

**If the assessment surfaces problems:** Revise your selection. You can swap a finding for a stronger candidate from the Step 3 matrix, split a compound finding into its strongest independent component, or promote an absorbed evidence anchor to a standalone finding. Write a short note explaining what changed and why.

**If the assessment confirms your draft:** State that the diagnostics passed and proceed.

A story with a reversal earns the second paragraph; a benchmark just sets context. The writer will develop it, but your editorial direction ensures the hook aligns with the finding selection.

## SELECTION PRINCIPLES

These are ranked. When they conflict, higher-ranked principles win.

1. **Non-obvious over well-known.** The document exists to show the reader something they haven't seen. A finding that every sector analyst would identify ("Sublime collapsed, creating an opportunity") is necessary context for Section II (Company Situation) but wastes a Finding slot.

2. **Deal-relevant over intellectually interesting.** Every finding must connect to a specific action a banker could take: approach a buyer, structure a deal differently, time an outreach, identify a financing mechanism. Pure analytical observations without deal implications belong in the research corpus, not the client document.

3. **Compound over simple — but not always.** A finding that threads 3 TPs into a single narrative ("the financing architecture runs through X because Y eliminates the alternatives and Z provides the mechanism") is often more valuable than a finding that presents a single TP. But a standalone finding backed by hard, independently verifiable evidence — committed infrastructure capital, signed agreements, regulatory filings — can outperform a compound finding that synthesizes softer signals. Compounding amplifies insight; it should not bury evidence. If the strongest piece of evidence in the corpus would be diluted by threading it into a multi-TP compound, let it stand alone.

4. **Inferential courage over defensive sourcing.** CIRCUMSTANTIAL findings — pattern recognition, inference chains, "notably absent" observations — are often the most valuable content. Do not penalize them in selection for being harder to source. The writing step will handle the appropriate hedging language.

5. **Portfolio completeness over individual strength.** The three findings should collectively give the reader enough to engage: a risk or opportunity the market has missed, a structural mechanism that makes it actionable, and evidence that the underlying demand or strategic position is real. If your three findings answer two of these but leave the third to the Company Situation section, you have a gap — the reader must do assembly work that belongs in the Key Findings. Evaluate the set, not just the individuals.
