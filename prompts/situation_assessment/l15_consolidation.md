DELIVERABLE 1: COMPANY SITUATION MAP

A concise (2-3 page equivalent) deal-oriented profile covering:

- **What {COMPANY_NAME} IS**: Business model, scale, physical footprint, revenue composition. What does this company physically own, operate, and sell? Be concrete — facility counts, geographies, headcount, revenue by segment with actual numbers or transparent estimates.
- **Who OWNS it and what that means**: Ownership structure, fund lifecycle position, capital structure, any visible transaction signals. If PE-backed: fund vintage, hold period, co-investors. If public: trading dynamics, activist interest, insider activity.
- **How it MAKES MONEY and how that's changing**: Revenue quality (recurring vs. non-recurring), customer/payer concentration, same-store vs. acquisition-driven growth decomposition, margin trajectory. This is what drives valuation.
- **What the ENVIRONMENT is doing**: Competitive dynamics, regulatory/reimbursement landscape, sector M&A activity, macro forces. Focus on factors that affect deal timing and valuation.
- **What's CHANGED recently**: Key events in the last 12-18 months that signal transaction activity or change the deal thesis. Management changes, operational improvements, advisor engagement, regulatory shifts.

Be opinionated. Synthesize, don't summarize. Where data from different workstreams conflicts, highlight the tension explicitly. Where the company's narrative doesn't match operational reality, say so — these gaps are often the most valuable insights.

DELIVERABLE 2: STRATEGIC TENSION POINTS

Identify 15-20 "tension points" — anomalies, contradictions, underappreciated dynamics, or non-obvious observations that an investment banker would want to bring to a client meeting. These are the "how did they know that?" moments.

Each tension point must follow this structure:

a) **THE OBSERVATION**: What the data says — with specific numbers, sources, and citations. This must be grounded in facts from the research, not speculation.

b) **WHY IT MATTERS**: What this implies for valuation, deal timing, buyer interest, or diligence risk. Connect the observation to a deal outcome. This is what makes it useful to a banker, not just interesting to an analyst.

c) **THE QUESTION IT RAISES**: What an acquirer, advisor, or investor would want to investigate further. This should be specific enough to guide diligence — not "is this a risk?" but "what would the CIM need to address to get a buyer comfortable?"

d) **CATEGORY**: Classify as one of:
   - **VALUATION DRIVER**: Changes how you think about the multiple — up or down
   - **DEAL TIMING SIGNAL**: Suggests urgency or patience in transaction timing
   - **BUYER THESIS**: Makes a specific buyer category more or less interested
   - **DILIGENCE FLAG**: Would surface in diligence and affect price or terms
   - **HIDDEN VALUE**: Asset, capability, or dynamic that the market or current owner undervalues
   - **INFORMATION ARBITRAGE**: The consensus view is wrong about something material

e) **POTENTIAL MAGNITUDE**: Transformational / Major / Moderate — with a sentence explaining why

f) **CONFIDENCE**: High / Medium / Low — and what drives your uncertainty

g) **L2 RESEARCH QUESTION**: The precise question a deep research agent should investigate next to validate or invalidate this tension point.

USE THESE FIVE FRAMES TO GENERATE TENSION POINTS:

1. **WHERE IS VALUE HIDDEN OR MISPRICED?** — Assets carried below market value, revenue streams valued at the wrong multiple, operational improvements not yet reflected in financials, data or platform value obscured by a services wrapper.

2. **WHERE IS THE GROWTH STORY MORE (OR LESS) THAN IT APPEARS?** — Same-store growth masking acquisition dependency (or vice versa), geographic divergence in performance, customer cohort dynamics that change the quality-of-earnings narrative, regulatory tailwinds/headwinds not priced in.

3. **WHAT SIGNALS TRANSACTION TIMING?** — Fund lifecycle math, management changes, operational cleanup patterns, advisor engagement, refinancing windows, regulatory deadlines, competitive pressure points.

4. **WHO WOULD WANT THIS AND WHY?** — Non-obvious buyer categories, specific platform gaps this fills, geographic or capability adjacencies, regulatory arbitrage opportunities, consolidation plays that only work with this specific asset.

5. **WHAT COULD GO WRONG IN DILIGENCE?** — Revenue quality issues, customer concentration risk, regulatory exposure, litigation overhang, management depth concerns, integration complexity, data/technology debt.

CRITICAL QUALITY STANDARD:

Do NOT produce generic M&A observations. Every tension point must reference SPECIFIC evidence from the research workstreams.

BAD: "The company faces competitive pressure that could affect valuation."
BAD: "The PE sponsor may be looking to exit given fund timing."
BAD: "Healthcare consolidation creates acquisition opportunities."

GOOD: "{COMPANY_NAME}'s same-store revenue growth of 12% masks a geographic divergence: the 8 clinics acquired in 2023 are growing at 22% while the legacy 15 clinics are flat. If you cannot make a tension point this specific, it probably isn't a good tension point.
