## WHAT YOU ARE OPTIMIZING FOR

**The reader test.** Before you change anything, read the L3a draft from top to bottom as if you are the MD receiving this cold. Ask yourself at every paragraph:

1. Am I still reading? If not, what made me stop? Cut or rewrite it.
2. Did I just learn something I didn't know? If not, this paragraph is filler.
3. Could I use this in a client conversation tomorrow? If not, it's too abstract.

**The VP test.** Now read it again as the VP who receives this from the MD with a note saying "take a look at this." Ask yourself:

1. Is the analytical work credible? Are the numbers sourced? Is the math shown?
2. Are there any claims I'd want to verify before putting my name on it? Flag and fix those.
3. Is there a connection between findings that the draft missed? Add it.

## SPECIFIC REFINEMENT TASKS

Work through these in order:

### 1. THE OPPORTUNITY (Section I)

This is the most important paragraph in the document. Pressure-test it:

- Does it open with a specific, quantified value tension? (Not a company description.)
- Does it contain at least two numbers that an MD wouldn't already know?
- Does it end with a clear statement of magnitude and time window?
- Is there a "management says X but the data shows Y" debunk that could be added? Check the research corpus — if there's a narrative-vs-reality gap (overstated growth, inflated EBITDA projections, misleading same-store metrics), weave it into the hook. This is often the sharpest thing in the document.
- Read the last sentence. Does it create urgency? If the MD could read this next month and feel the same way, the urgency isn't sharp enough.

### 2. COMPANY SITUATION (Section II)

This should read like a memo from the smartest analyst at the bank, not a Wikipedia overview.

- **Cut any sentence that a sector banker already knows.** "The healthcare sector is experiencing consolidation" — cut. "Behavioral health demand is growing" — cut. Every sentence must contain a SPECIFIC fact about THIS company.
- **Check the narrative-vs-reality paragraph.** Is the debunk quantified? "Overstated by approximately X%" is good. "May be overstated" is weak. If the research corpus has the math, show it.
- **Strengthen the "what's changed" section.** Look for transaction signals the draft may have underweighted: management changes, advisor retention, board additions, capex cuts, goodwill write-downs, activist engagement, insider transactions. Each of these is a data point. Stack them — the cumulative effect of 5-6 signals is more persuasive than any individual one.
- **Check for the OBBBA / macro headwind paragraph** (or equivalent sector-specific regulatory/macro risk). If the company has significant government payer exposure, regulatory overhang, or pending policy changes, this needs to be in the situation section with specific dollar impact. The L3a draft may have treated this as background — it should be foreground if it compresses the EBITDA base a transaction would be priced against.
- **Verify the ownership section names specific holders with percentages**, not just "institutional investors own a majority." Activist positions, short interest with days-to-cover, and insider transactions all matter for deal dynamics.

### 3. KEY FINDINGS (Section III)

You have exactly 3 findings. Make each one the best version of itself.

For each finding:

- **Is the title specific enough?** "The CTC Segment Is Legally Separable — and the Market Doesn't Know It" is a good title. "Regulatory Analysis" is not. The title should communicate the insight, not the category.
- **Does the observation paragraph have the strongest evidence from the research corpus?** Check the L2 deep dives — there may be a more powerful data point, a better comparable, or a more specific citation that the L3a draft didn't surface. Add it.
- **Does the deal implication include a dollar magnitude?** "$650M base case to $1.23B bull case" is good. "Significant value could be unlocked" is not. If the research has the math, show it.
- **Is there a cross-reference between findings that the draft missed?** Often findings interlock — e.g., a litigation reserve gap affects the viability of a separation, or a regulatory change creates urgency for a deal catalyst. If the findings connect, add a sentence making that connection explicit. This is the kind of insight that only emerges from reading the full document as a unit.
- **Is the key uncertainty honest and specific?** Vague hedging ("there are risks") hurts credibility. Specific uncertainty ("if the PA lookback bill passes the Senate, claims expand from dozens to hundreds") builds it.

After the three findings, verify the held-back teaser paragraph:
- Does it name specific topics from the remaining findings? (Not "additional analysis" but "real estate monetization mechanics with named REIT counterparties, a take-private signal timeline mapped against the Athenahealth/Elliott precedent...")
- Is it specific enough to create curiosity but not so detailed it gives away the content?

### 4. DEAL CONTEXT (Section IV)

- **Precedent transactions**: Are there 5-6 comps with disclosed metrics? Are the rationale columns specific (not "strategic fit" but "geographic fill-in in Southeast where target has 12 facilities")?
- **Valuation table**: Does it show 3-4 scenarios with clear methodology? Is there at least one sensitivity line?
- **Buyer universe**: Are there 3-4 buyers with SPECIFIC strategic rationale? Check the L2 research — are there details the draft missed? Geographic overlaps by state, recent conference attendance, advisor relationships, fund lifecycle timing. The best buyer entries read like mini-theses, not generic descriptions.
- **Held-back teasers**: Both the comp table and buyer universe should end with "The full analysis includes X more..." lines.

### 5. ABOUT THIS ANALYSIS (Section V)

- Is it under 200 words?
- Does it name Pokee AI?
- Does the second paragraph position Pokee's broader platform (not just this one report)?
- Does it end with contact info?
- Is the tone confident but not pushy? It should read like a firm you'd want to work with, not a firm that's desperate for the meeting.

### 6. CITATIONS AND SOURCING

- Scan every factual claim. Does it have a source? Flag any unsourced claims and either add the source from the research corpus or soften the language to "[Estimated based on...]"
- Are there any internal pipeline references (e.g., "L2 Deep Dive 4", "L1.5 Tension Point #9") that leaked into the client-facing document? Remove ALL of these. Replace with the underlying source.
- **CITATION FORMAT: Use numbered footnotes (^1, ^2, ^3...) throughout the document body, with a "Source Notes" section at the end listing each footnote.** Do NOT use inline [Source: ...] citations — these look like raw research output, not a client deliverable. Every footnote should cite the specific document (10-K, 8-K, earnings call transcript, analyst note, court filing) with a date.
- Are source notes at the end complete and using superscript notation consistently?

### 7. FINAL READ-THROUGH

Read the entire document one more time. Ask:

- **Length**: Is it 2,500-3,500 words? If over, what can be cut without losing insight? If under, is there a finding or deal context element that deserves more depth?
- **Tone**: Does every paragraph sound like it was written by someone who advises on billion-dollar transactions? No breathlessness, no hedging, no academic detachment. Confident, specific, direct.
- **Flow**: Does each section build on the last? Does the Opportunity hook pay off in the Findings? Does the Deal Context feel like a natural extension of the Findings?
- **The "close the PDF" test**: Is there any point where a busy MD would stop reading? If so, fix that section — cut it, sharpen it, or move it.
