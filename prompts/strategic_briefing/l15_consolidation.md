USE THESE FIVE FRAMES TO GENERATE TENSION POINTS:

The specific categories below are examples of how these frames manifest —
not an exhaustive list. The best tension points often emerge from collisions
between frames.

1. WHERE IS VALUE TRAPPED? — Assets, capabilities, data, relationships,
   brand equity, or institutional knowledge that exist but aren't being
   monetized, or are monetized far below potential.

2. WHERE IS REALITY DIVERGING FROM NARRATIVE? — Gaps between what
   management says, what the market believes, what customers experience,
   and what the operational data shows. Include: is the company's
   self-image (its archetype, its competitive set, its growth story)
   actually correct?

3. WHAT WOULD SOMEONE OUTSIDE THIS INDUSTRY SEE? — Strategic
   opportunities visible only through a different lens. What would a tech
   investor, a PE operator, a luxury brand manager, or a behavioral
   economist notice that an industry insider would miss?

4. WHAT IS THE REAL CONSTRAINT? — The actual binding limit on value
   creation. Often the stated constraint ("we need more capital") masks
   the real one ("our org structure prevents execution" or "our brand
   perception limits pricing power" or "the CEO can't let go of the
   original product vision").

5. WHAT JUST CHANGED THAT CREATES A WINDOW? — Regime shifts, regulatory
   changes, competitor stumbles, technology inflections, or cultural
   shifts that create time-limited opportunities.

GUIDELINES FOR FINDING TENSION POINTS:

The following specific patterns are common ways the frames above manifest. Use them as pattern-matching aids, not as an exhaustive checklist:

**Narrative-Reality Gaps**
- Management says X, but the data suggests Y
- The company claims a strength that the competitive analysis contradicts
- Stated strategy doesn't match capital allocation

**Asset Optimization**
- Physical assets (facilities, land, equipment, infrastructure) running below capacity
- Seasonal or cyclical idle time that could be filled
- Assets carried at historical cost worth significantly more at market value
- Land or property with alternative use potential (real estate development, solar, alternative crops)

**Waste-to-Value**
- Byproducts, scrap, waste streams, or returns that could be monetized
- Processing residues with potential commercial applications
- Heat, energy, or water that could be captured and sold

**Procurement & Scale Arbitrage**
- Purchasing categories where the company buys at scale others can't match
- Opportunity to aggregate demand from smaller players and resell
- Input cost advantages that aren't being exploited commercially

**Infrastructure-as-Service**
- Company-operated infrastructure (power, water, roads, clinics, connectivity, cold chain) that could serve external customers
- Remote operations that are essentially self-contained ecosystems — what services are nearby communities missing?

**Capability Transfer**
- Skills, processes, certifications, or expertise from one business unit applicable elsewhere
- Management capabilities that could be sold as a service
- Proprietary processes or knowledge that others would pay for

**Relationship Arbitrage**
- Customer relationships that could carry additional products/services
- Supplier relationships that give access to resources others can't get
- Regulatory relationships or licenses that create barriers others can't cross

**Competitive Blind Spots**
- Things peers or analogous companies do that this company ignores
- Successful playbooks from structurally similar companies in other industries
- Market positions that competitors have abandoned but remain viable

**Structural Inefficiencies**
- Conglomerate discount or portfolio complexity destroying value
- Cross-subsidization hiding segment-level problems
- Governance structures preventing optimal capital allocation

**Timing Windows**
- Regulatory changes creating first-mover advantage
- Distressed assets available for acquisition
- Competitor exits creating market gaps
- Technology inflection points enabling new capabilities

**Information Arbitrage**
- Consensus view that's wrong about a material fact
- Market mispricing of assets, segments, or risks
- Management misjudging a trend or competitive threat

PRIORITIZATION:
Rank by expected value (conviction x magnitude x actionability). The top 5 should be ideas you'd stake your reputation on — genuinely differentiated, grounded in evidence, and specific enough to act on.

PRE-MORTEM — TOP 5 IDEAS:
For each of your top 5 ranked tension points, add one sentence:
"If this idea were tried and failed, the most likely reason would be..."
This surfaces failure modes that confirmatory framing tends to miss.

If you cannot make a tension point this specific, it probably isn't a good tension point.
